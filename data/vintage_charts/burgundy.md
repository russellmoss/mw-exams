# Burgundy Vintage Chart 2000–2024
## For MW Blind Tasting: Vintage Deduction Reference

**Purpose:** Quick-reference for deducing vintage from what is in the glass. Organised for speed under exam conditions. Red = Pinot Noir (Côte de Nuits / Côte de Beaune). White = Chardonnay (Côte de Beaune, Chablis, Mâconnais).

**Research path:** Tavily was unavailable in this session (invalid API key). All data sourced via WebFetch from Berry Bros. & Rudd vintage chart (bbr.com/vintage-chart — the only publicly accessible scored chart retrieved), supplemented by established consensus from Decanter, Vinous, Allen Meadows/Burghound, and Burgundy Report (paywalled or 403-blocked; scores and descriptors derived from widely-cited published consensus, noted where). Growing season details draw on widely-published meteorological and harvest records for the Côte d'Or. Where season or sensory data cannot be confirmed to a specific retrieved URL, the entry is marked *(consensus)*.

**BBR scoring key:** 10 = outstanding, 9 = excellent, 8 = very good, 7 = good, 6 = average, 5 = below average (Source: bbr.com/vintage-chart, retrieved 2026-05-27).

## Deduction Shortcuts

These are the rules of thumb a blind taster can use under exam conditions to narrow vintage from structural evidence in the glass.

### For Red Burgundy (Pinot Noir)

**High acid + light body + red fruit dominant + no concentration**
→ Cool or difficult year: likely 2004, 2007, 2008, 2011, 2013, or 2021. If combined with very pale colour and green edge → 2004 or 2007. If pale but precise and fragrant → 2021 (tiny yields, frost year). If austere and demanding patience → 2008 or 2013.

**Deep colour + rich dark fruit + firm tannin + excellent freshness**
→ Warm, high-quality year: likely 2005, 2009, 2010, 2015, 2019, 2020, or 2022. Distinguish as follows:
- 2005: most concentrated + the longest finish of any year; structured tannin; can still be closed.
- 2009: most opulent and accessible of the warm years; rounder tannin; open now.
- 2010: more restrained than 2009; more structure; more age-worthy; less obvious.
- 2015: velvety and generous; round tannin; accessible; not as powerful as 2020.
- 2019: plush and rich; dark fruit + freshness; the "drinking well now and aging" sweet spot.
- 2020: the most powerful of the recent warm years; concentrated; still closed if young.
- 2022: similar to 2020 in power; very long finish; competes with 2005 for the decade.

**Medium ruby + charming red fruit + soft tannin + modest length**
→ Pleasant mid-tier year: likely 2002 (if elegant + bright acid), 2006, 2012 (if dense concentration), 2017, 2018, or 2023.

**Very dark, jammy fruit + low acid + high ABV + cooked/stewed note**
→ 2003. The heat wave year is nearly unmistakable: southern-style opulence without Burgundy freshness.

### For White Burgundy (Chardonnay)

**Very high acid + lean frame + mineral/chalky texture + razor-sharp finish + no fatness**
→ 2014 is the standout reference. If this profile is present, 2014 is the first call. Also consider 2008, 2013, 2021. Distinguish: 2014 has greater concentration at Grand Cru level; 2021 is lighter and more fragile.

**Rich, generous, stone fruit (peach/apricot) + good acid + toasted oak**
→ Warm-year whites: likely 2015, 2019, 2020, or 2022. Distinguish: 2020 and 2022 have the most concentration; 2015 is accessible and forward; 2019 sometimes lacks aromatic intensity.

**Ripe, tension, citrus + floral + excellent length**
→ 2002, 2010, or 2017. 2002 is more mineral and restrained; 2017 is more generous with tropical notes in warmer sites.

**Fat, low-acid, tropical + no backbone**
→ 2003. As for reds, the 2003 white Burgundy profile (if the wine has survived) is distinctive: heavy, flat, atypical.

**Classic, balanced, ripe apple + medium acid + moderate oak**
→ 2006, 2009, 2012, 2018, or 2023. These are the "good to very good" years without extreme stylistic signatures. Narrowing requires other clues (colour depth, length, oak regime).

### Cross-colour rules

1. **If the wine shows extreme acid with almost no fruit weight → frost or cool year.** For red: 2021, 2008, 2013. For white: 2014, 2021, 2013.
2. **If both colours from the same producer in the same year show rich, opulent style with low acid → 2003** (the one truly atypical year).
3. **The "warm trilogy" 2018-2019-2020** for reds all show depth and generosity. 2018 is slightly more restrained; 2019 is the most elegant; 2020 is the most powerful. In the absence of other clues, the exam question context (has the wine aged well? does it still need time?) can narrow it further.
4. **For whites, 2014 stands alone.** No other recent vintage produces the same combination of extreme acid backbone + concentration + purity. If the white Burgundy makes you reach for comparison to Chablis Grand Cru in tension — think 2014 first.
5. **Frost years (2016, 2021) produce tiny volumes.** The exam is unlikely to test severely frost-damaged village-level wines; if a Burgundy is presented as a quality benchmark, the frost year signal is less diagnostic. But if a candidate notices a fragrant, lighter-than-expected body on a Premier or Grand Cru → 2021 is plausible.
