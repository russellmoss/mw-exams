# Bordeaux Vintage Reference — Blind Tasting Deduction Guide

**Purpose:** Quick-reference for MW candidates deducing vintage from the glass. Focuses on what you *taste and see*, not encyclopedic harvest statistics.

**Sources:** Tavily was unavailable in this session (API key error). WebFetch was used against open-access sources; the majority of premium wine media (Decanter, JancisRobinson, Vinous, Wine-Searcher) returned 403/404 or paywall blocks. The sole external data point retrieved was Berry Bros. & Rudd's numerical ratings (1–10 scale) via thewinecollective.com.au/bordeaux-vintage-chart. All vintage character descriptions are drawn from the author's knowledge base (August 2025 cutoff), cross-referenced against the BBR scores and against widely agreed consensus positions across the critical press (Robert Parker/Wine Advocate, Neal Martin/Vinous, Jane Anson/Decanter, Jancis Robinson). Where assessments are contested, this is noted.

## Deduction Shortcuts

These are the blind-tasting rules of thumb for vintage deduction. Apply after you have established: this is Bordeaux (LB or RB dominant blend), approximate age from colour/tannin evolution.

### Rule 1 — The concentration/alcohol test (hot vs cool year)
- **Very deep purple, 14%+ alcohol, ultra-ripe dark fruit, low-ish acidity, plush tannins** → most likely a hot year: 2018, 2019, 2020, or 2022. Distinguish: 2022 is the most extreme (thickest body, earliest harvest), 2018 is very plush/velvety, 2019 is slightly more classical/structured than 2018, 2020 sits between them in style.
- **Deep but not extreme colour, high alcohol, great fruit/acid balance** → most likely 2009, 2010, 2015, or 2016. Distinguish: 2010 has the highest acidity and most structured tannins of the four; 2009 is more velvety; 2015 is accessible/charming; 2016 has the deepest colour and firmest tannin of the group.
- **Moderate depth, fresh acidity, structured firm tannins, classical Médoc character** → most likely 2005, 2008, or 2014. Distinguish: 2005 is now more open/developed; 2014 is leaner but very precise; 2008 has a slightly earthy/mineral character.

### Rule 2 — The acidity test (warm cool differentiation within top years)
- **High acidity + deep colour + long structure** = the fingerprint of 2010 and 2016 (the two "austere greats"). Both built for the long haul; tannins still firm even at 10+ years.
- **Low to moderate acidity + deep colour + opulent fruit** = 2009, 2018, 2022. All approachable younger. 2022 is the most extreme in alcohol.

### Rule 3 — The herbaceous/lean test (difficult years)
- **Red fruit, leafy/vegetal edge, lighter body, elevated acidity** → cool/difficult year: 2007, 2011, or 2013. 2013 is the leanest and most herbaceous. 2007 has more red fruit charm. 2011 is uneven.
- **Medium body, fresh but not thin, some structure** → rescue vintage: 2002, 2004, 2008, or 2014. 2014 is the standout of this group (looks and tastes closer to a great year than a merely good one).

### Rule 4 — The atypical profile (2003)
- **Jammy/cooked fruit, very thick body, very low acidity, extremely dark colour** → 2003 is the only vintage with this extreme heat-wave profile. Tannins can be almost porty. No other Bordeaux vintage since 2000 mimics this profile.

### Rule 5 — Left Bank vs Right Bank signals for vintage deduction
- If the wine is **Merlot-dominant** (RB: Pomerol, St-Emilion) and exceptional: 2000, 2009, 2010, 2015, 2018, 2022 all produced great RB. 2001 produced outstanding Pomerol despite being a weak LB year.
- If the wine is **Cabernet-dominant** (LB: Pauillac, St-Julien, Margaux, St-Estèphe) and exceptional: 2005, 2009, 2010, 2014, 2015, 2016, 2019 are the key years.
- **If LB excellent but RB disappointingly leaner** → consider 2002, 2008, 2014. These were Cabernet vintages.
- **If RB excellent but LB variable** → consider 2001, 2003, 2011.

### Rule 6 — Age crosscheck
Use tannin colour evolution and secondary character development:
- **Primary fruit dominant, tannins still assertive** → likely under 10 years from tasting date (so for a 2026 exam, likely 2017 onward).
- **Secondary character emerging (cedar, tobacco, leather, dried fruit), garnet/brick rim developing** → likely 10–15 years (2011–2016 bracket for a 2026 exam).
- **Fully tertiary, brick/tawny rim, soft tannins, dried/prune fruit** → likely 20+ years (2005 or older for 2026 exam).

### Rule 7 — The "modern" cluster vs "classical" cluster
- **Modern cluster** (riper, richer, more extracted, higher ABV): 2015, 2018, 2019, 2020, 2022. Common thread: very ripe dark fruit, low herbaceous character, polished tannins.
- **Classical cluster** (fresher, more structured, age-demanding): 2005, 2009, 2010, 2014, 2016. Common thread: firm tannins, good acidity, long aging curve.
- If the wine tastes "Napa-like" in its richness: lean toward modern cluster, especially 2018, 2020, 2022.
- If the wine tastes "traditional Bordeaux" with restraint and structure: lean toward classical cluster, especially 2016, 2010, or 2005.
