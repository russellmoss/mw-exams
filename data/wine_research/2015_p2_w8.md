# Brunello di Montalcino, San Polino, 2009. Tuscany, Italy

## Quick tasting profile
Medium garnet with slight orange-brick rim evolution (6 years of aging at exam time). Nose shows the characteristic Brunello intensity: dried cherry, pomegranate, tobacco, tar, dried rose, forest floor, leather, with secondary savoury complexity. The palate is full-bodied, with Brunello's signature fierce, drying tannins and elevated acidity — more austere and structured than Chianti Classico Sangiovese from the same variety. Long, complex finish.

## Vintage character (2009 Brunello di Montalcino)
2009 was a warm to hot vintage in Montalcino with low rainfall producing concentrated, ripe wines. Critics generally rate it highly (very good to excellent) though with less complexity than the exceptional 2010 or 2012 vintages. The wines show generous fruit and approachable tannins for Brunello — more accessible in youth than cooler, tighter vintages. At the time of the exam in 2015, these wines were in their earliest drinking window. (Source needed for specific 2009 Brunello vintage report URL.)

## Producer style
San Polino is a small, family-owned biodynamic estate in Montalcino, producing Brunello and Rosso di Montalcino from their single estate. The estate philosophy emphasizes farming in harmony with nature (biodynamic certification). The winery is boutique-scale with limited international distribution. Quality is consistently praised within the Montalcino community but the estate is not among the denomination's marquee names (compare La Pergola Torta, Biondi-Santi, Case Basse). Price positioning: mid-tier within Brunello di Montalcino (GBP 40-70 retail approximately). (Source: sanpolino.it — identity, philosophy confirmed; technical details source needed.)

## Why it's in this exam
Placed alongside Chianti Classico (w7) to test identification of Brunello di Montalcino's characteristic differences from regular Chianti Classico Sangiovese: greater structure, aging complexity, more prominent acidity and tannin, longer finish. The small producer (San Polino) rather than a famous name (Biondi-Santi, Ciacci Piccolomini) is a typical MW approach — testing knowledge of the appellation style rather than just brand recognition.
