# Dom Pérignon, Moët & Chandon, 2009. Champagne, France.

## Quick tasting profile
Bright gold with fine, persistent bead. By 2018 (approximately 9 years old, just at first plénitude) the nose shows fresh-toast brioche, hazelnuts, ripe yellow apple, stone fruit, and a hint of cream. 2009 was a very ripe, warm year — expect higher sugar accumulation and a rounder, more opulent style than the 1999. Palate: generous, creamy, full mousse, good length. Fresher and more primary than the 1999 (w1) but already showing development.

## Technical specs
- Grape variety: Pinot Noir and Chardonnay blend (proportion undisclosed; 2009 likely Pinot Noir-dominant given the warm vintage)
- Vineyard: Multiple Grand Cru sources across Champagne; not disclosed
- Vinification: Méthode champenoise; 8-10 years lees aging before first release c.2017
- Elevage: Extended bottle aging; low dosage brut
- ABV / RS / TA: 12.5% / brut / medium-high

## Vintage character (2009 Champagne)
2009 was a warm, ripe Champagne vintage — one of the most opulent of the 2000s decade. High potential alcohol, lower natural acidity, generous fruit. The wines tend to be broader and more approachable young, with a Burgundian richness. Dom Pérignon's 2009 rosé was described as "the current release" as of September 2024, confirming the prestige reputation of this vintage. (source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dom_Pérignon_(wine))

## Producer style
Dom Pérignon is Moët & Chandon's prestige cuvée (LVMH). Exclusively vintage-dated; only released in declared years. The three-plénitude concept governs release strategy. 2009 was the sixth-oldest release in the "current" lineup as of 2024. Production: 5+ million bottles/year across vintages. Market positioning: luxury tier, £150+ per bottle at release. (source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dom_Pérignon_(wine))

## Why it's in this exam
The direct pairing with 1999 (w1) is one of the most challenging comparative exercises in the MW exam. The candidate must: identify both as vintage Champagne from the same house, assess the maturity difference (primary vs. secondary character), comment on vintage style differences (1999: elegant/rounded; 2009: opulent/rich), and make quality judgments. Market positioning, prestige cuvée characteristics, and the plénitude concept are all testable angles.
