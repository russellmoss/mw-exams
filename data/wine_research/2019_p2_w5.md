# Santa Maria Pinot Noir, Au Bon Climat, 2016. Santa Maria, California, USA

## Quick tasting profile
Translucent medium ruby with red-brick rim. Nose of ripe red cherry, cranberry, dried strawberry, cola, subtle earthy undertones and very restrained oak — minimal oak signature, characteristically Burgundian in approach. Palate is medium-bodied with bright acidity, silky tannins, and a food-friendly, elegant structure. Long, spice-tinged finish. One of California's most Burgundian-style Pinot Noirs.

## Technical specs
- Grape variety: 100% Pinot Noir
- Vineyard: Sourced primarily from Bien Nacido Vineyard (and potentially Los Alamos/other Santa Maria Valley sites — source: Wikipedia Au Bon Climat)
- Soils: Sandy loam over calcareous subsoil; cool, fog-influenced marine climate
- Vinification: Jim Clendenen (founder) famous for Burgundian approach — whole-cluster inclusion possible; native yeast; open-top fermenters; minimal intervention

## Vintage character (2016 Santa Barbara/Santa Maria)
2016 was a relatively cool year in Santa Maria Valley, bolstered by marine influence from the Pacific. Wines show good natural acidity and aromatic elegance. Yields were moderate and quality high — vintage is considered one of the better recent expressions of Santa Maria Pinot, balancing concentration with freshness.

## Producer style
Au Bon Climat (ABC) was founded in 1982 by Jim Clendenen (d. 2021), a pioneer of Burgundian-styled Pinot Noir and Chardonnay in California. Exclusively produces Pinot Noir, Chardonnay, Pinot Blanc and Pinot Gris from Santa Barbara County sites (source: Wikipedia). ABC is widely credited with putting Santa Maria Valley on the world fine wine map. Style is deliberately anti-interventionist and Burgundy-inspired — understated, food-friendly, restrained versus California hedonism. Market position: premium but accessible ($35–65 range for the Santa Maria bottling).

## Why it's in this exam
Au Bon Climat Santa Maria Pinot Noir is a classic MW benchmark: California Pinot Noir showing Burgundian elegance rather than New World excess. Candidates must distinguish this from Burgundy itself (higher ABV, riper fruit) and from more exuberant California Pinot styles (Russian River, Sonoma Coast). The 13.5% ABV and cool-climate profile are key blind tasting signals.
