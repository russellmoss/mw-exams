# Château Coutet, 2016

## Quick tasting profile
Medium to deep gold; elegant and aromatic Barsac botrytis nose — lighter and more citrus/floral in character than Sauternes' weight, showing lime marmalade, apricot blossom, ginger, honey and waxy citrus peel; the Sauvignon Blanc component contributes a lively zest against the Sémillon richness and botrytis sweetness. Full-bodied (13% ABV), concentrated, sweet but with excellent balancing acidity; Barsac's characteristically lighter texture than Sauternes; long, complex finish.

## Technical specs
- Grape variety: 75% Sémillon, 23% Sauvignon Blanc, 2% Muscadelle (Source: Wikipedia)
- Vineyard: 38 hectares in Barsac; Premier Grand Cru Classé (1855 classification)
- Vinification: Multiple tries (passes) through the vineyard for selective botrytis harvesting; whole-cluster pressing typical; barrel fermentation in French oak
- Elevage: New and used French oak barrels; typically 12-18 months
- ABV / RS / TA: 13% / very sweet (botrytis-concentrated; approx 120-150 g/L RS) / high acidity
- Historic château: founded 1643; Thomas Jefferson praised it circa 1788 as "the best Sauternes originating from Barsac" (Source: Wikipedia)

## Vintage character (2016 Sauternes/Barsac)
2016 was an excellent Sauternes-Barsac vintage with good botrytis development — autumn conditions with morning mists and warm afternoons created ideal noble rot. After the exceptional 2015, the 2016s are regarded as slightly more restrained but elegant, with excellent freshness and aging potential. Château Coutet in 2016 is expected to be an elegant, age-worthy wine at the Premier Cru tier.

## Producer style
Château Coutet is one of Barsac's three Premier Grands Crus Classés (with Climens and Doisy-Daëne). Located in Barsac on the left bank of the Ciron, the château features a medieval tower dating from the 13th century. Owned by the Baly family since 1977, Coutet also produces a second wine (Chartreuse de Coutet) and the ultra-rare Cuvée Madame in exceptional years. Thomas Jefferson identified it as the finest Barsac circa 1788. (Source: Wikipedia)
