# Pinot Gris, Vendanges Tardives, Rotleibel de Rorschwihr, Rolly Gassmann, 1996

## Quick tasting profile
Rich, mature late-harvest Alsace Pinot Gris with honeyed, quince, pear and smoky spice notes, likely carrying both botrytic richness and tertiary complexity. The palate should be concentrated and sweet but balanced by extract and sufficient acidity, with a long, evolved finish.

## Technical specs
- Grape variety: 100% Pinot Gris
- Vineyard: Rotleibel lieu-dit in Rorschwihr
- Soils: Red marl with gravel and loess according to regional site references
- Vinification: Late-harvest selection under Vendanges Tardives rules; botrytis and surmaturité are both plausible
- Élevage: Source needed
- ABV / RS / TA: 12.5% / high residual sugar; later Hachette note cites 92 g/L on the 2017 release / Source needed

## Vintage character (1996 Alsace)
By 2011 this bottle was already deeply mature, so the exam value is not about youthful fruit but about recognizing the aged sweet Alsace profile. Candidates needed to read sweetness, varietal breadth and tertiary evolution correctly without defaulting to Sauternes or Tokaji.

## Producer style
Rolly Gassmann has long been associated with ambitious terroir bottlings and a rich, age-worthy style. Rotleibel is one of the domaine’s better-known Rorschwihr sites, and the Vendanges Tardives bottling is a classic example of Alsace sweetness framed by site identity rather than simple confection.

## Why it's in this exam
This is the sweet, mature counterweight in the first-question Alsace trio. It expands the candidate’s definition of “same region” by forcing recognition that Alsace can produce dry aromatic Muscat, mature dry Riesling and fully sweet late-harvest Pinot Gris within one set.
