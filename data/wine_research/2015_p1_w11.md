# Condrieu, Domaine Barou, 2013. Rhône, France

## Quick tasting profile
Deep gold; intensely perfumed nose of white peach, apricot, violet, dried apricot and anise — Viognier's signature floral-stone fruit profile. Palate is full-bodied and rich with silky texture; naturally low acidity characteristic of Viognier (a key tasting discriminator). Dry but with an opulent, almost unctuous mouthfeel. Finish shows peach and floral persistence. Around 30,000 cases produced annually from all Condrieu producers — tiny appellation (source: Wikipedia Condrieu AOC). Domaine Barou is a smaller producer within the appellation; less commercially visible than Guigal, Vernay or Cuilleron.

## Technical specs
- Grape variety: 100% Viognier — the only permitted grape in Condrieu (source: Wikipedia Condrieu AOC)
- Vineyard: Condrieu spans approximately 202 hectares across seven communes on the Rhône's west bank south of Vienne; "low yields of small berries" from the traditional Condrieu clone of Viognier (source: Wikipedia Condrieu AOC)
- Soils: Decomposed granite and gneiss on steep terraces; shallow soils demand low yields and produce intensely concentrated fruit
- Vinification: Typically partial barrel fermentation (some new oak) and/or full stainless steel depending on producer style; most Condrieu undergoes full malolactic fermentation to soften naturally low acidity; some producers use 10–25% new French oak (Source needed: confirmed Domaine Barou production details)
- Elevage: Short — 6–9 months typically; Condrieu intended for early consumption (floral aromatics fade with age)
- ABV / RS / TA: 14.0% / dry / low acidity (characteristic of Viognier; Condrieu "naturally low acidity of Viognier presents winemaking challenges"; source: Wikipedia Condrieu AOC)

## Vintage character (2013 Northern Rhône)
2013 was a cool, late vintage in the northern Rhône — challenging for Viognier, which requires warmth to fully develop its floral-stone fruit character. Yields were reduced by disease pressure and difficult flowering. However, careful selection in 2013 could yield Condrieu with good natural acidity (higher than in warmer years) that may have extended the wine's fresh aromatic window. At 14% ABV, Domaine Barou's 2013 is at the lower end of typical Condrieu ripeness, suggesting a relatively restrained, food-friendly style. ## Producer style
Domaine Barou is a small, family-run estate within the Condrieu AOC. It is considerably less prominent internationally than the appellation's most famous producers (Guigal, Georges Vernay, Yves Cuilleron, André Perret, François Villard). The choice of Domaine Barou by the MW examiners is deliberate: it tests whether candidates can identify Condrieu/Viognier from intrinsic sensory qualities rather than recognising a famous label. Producer information is limited in publicly accessible sources. (Source needed: Domaine Barou producer information — not found during research.)

## Why it's in this exam
W11 is part of Q3's six single-variety flight. Condrieu from Viognier is a classic MW-level identification challenge — distinctive (peach, apricot, violet, low acid, full body) but potentially confused with New World Viognier or Gewurztraminer (both intensely aromatic). The key discriminators: (1) Viognier's stone-fruit/violet versus Gewurztraminer's lychee/rose; (2) Condrieu's granite-mineral quality; (3) Viognier's low acidity versus Riesling or Sauvignon Blanc. The "potential for further ageing" answer is important here: most Condrieu experts recommend drinking within 2–4 years (Wikipedia: "Viogniers more than three years old tend to lose many of the floral aromas"), though premium examples may hold 4–8 years. At the time of the exam (2015, wine is from 2013), this is a 2-year-old wine right in its optimal drinking window.
