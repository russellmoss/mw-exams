# Dom Pérignon, Moët & Chandon, 1999. Champagne, France.

## Quick tasting profile
By 2018 (19 years old), this is deep gold with persistent, fine bead. Nose: complex secondary aromatics — truffle, mushroom, toast, fresh-bread autolysis, dried citrus peel, and a honeyed richness. The 1999 is a mature expression. Palate: full and round, creamy mousse, integrated dosage, long savoury-toasty finish. This is Dom Pérignon at its "first plénitude" peak or beyond. The contrast with the younger 2009 (w2) is the entire exercise.

## Technical specs
- Grape variety: Pinot Noir and Chardonnay blend (proportions vary by vintage; all vintages are assemblage of the two varieties)
- Vineyard: Multiple Grand Cru sites across Champagne; precise sources not disclosed by Moët
- Vinification: Traditional méthode champenoise; 8-10 years minimum aging before release (Dom Pérignon was released c.2007-2009)
- Elevage: Extended time on lees in bottle creates autolytic complexity; disgorged to brut dosage
- ABV / RS / TA: 12.5% / brut (low dosage) / high

## Vintage character (1999 Champagne)
1999 was a large-volume vintage (record worldwide shipping was achieved that year, partly owing to millennium celebrations). Quality was good to very good, with ripe, generous fruit and adequate acidity. The 1999 DP is considered a successful wine — a comfortable, round vintage rather than a pinnacle year. By 2018 it has evolved significantly from its primary fresh fruit phase into complex secondary maturity. (source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dom_Pérignon_(wine); en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Champagne_(wine_region))

## Producer style
Dom Pérignon is the prestige cuvée of Moët & Chandon (LVMH). It is always a vintage wine — released only in declared years — and always a Pinot Noir/Chardonnay assemblage. The wine follows a three "plénitudes" maturation concept: the first plénitude at release (approximately 8-10 years), second at P2 release (~20 years), third at P3 (~30+ years). Both 1999 and 2009 vintages are in the documented release list. Production is estimated at 5+ million bottles per year. Market positioning: luxury prestige, £100-200+ per bottle at release. (source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dom_Pérignon_(wine))

## Why it's in this exam
Paired directly with the 2009 (w2) to test the candidate's ability to distinguish a mature Champagne from a younger one, and potentially to comment on vintage character differences. The question almost certainly requires the candidate to sequence the two wines, discuss maturity indicators in sparkling wine, and comment on quality. Dom Pérignon is also a market-positioning question: prestige vs. supermarket entry-level.
