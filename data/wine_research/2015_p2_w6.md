# Fleurie, Bouchard Pere et Fils, 2013. Beaujolais, France

## Quick tasting profile
Pale-to-medium ruby with a vivid, translucent appearance. Nose is aromatic and lifted: fresh red cherry, raspberry, violet, iris, strawberry, with the signature floral character that gives Fleurie its reputation as the "Queen of Beaujolais." Light-bodied with silky, virtually imperceptible tannins, medium-low acidity, and a relatively short, delicate finish. No oak influence discernible.

## Technical specs
- Grape variety: 100% Gamay (Gamay Noir a Jus Blanc)
- Vineyard: Fleurie AC, northern Beaujolais on decomposed granite (Fleurie's granite soils produce the most floral, delicate of the ten crus)
- Soils: Decomposed blue granite; some pink-tinged sandy granite
- Vinification: Semi-carbonic maceration or whole-berry fermentation; short maceration to preserve freshness and aromatics
- Elevage: Stainless steel or older large wood; minimal or no new oak aging; released early
- ABV / RS / TA: 12.5% / dry / Source needed
*(12.5% is characteristic for Fleurie versus heavier southern crus like Moulin-a-Vent or Morgon.)*

## Vintage character (2013 Beaujolais)
2013 was a challenging vintage across France with frost, hail, and rain reducing yields in Beaujolais. Resulting wines were more structured and concentrated than normal for the appellation, with better-than-expected aromatic complexity but lower volumes. For a Fleurie from this year, expect slightly firmer structure than typical but still the characteristic aromatic freshness. By 2015 it would be fully ready and showing primary fruit at its peak. ## Producer style
Bouchard Pere et Fils (see also 2015_p2_w1) expanded beyond Burgundy to source wines from Beaujolais as part of their negociant portfolio. As a large-scale negociant they source Fleurie from contracted growers in the appellation. The wine is positioned at the accessible end of the Bouchard range — entry-level commercial pricing as an introduction to Beaujolais crus under the Bouchard label.

## Why it's in this exam
Placed to test identification of Gamay in a paper otherwise dominated by Pinot Noir, Nebbiolo, Sangiovese, Tempranillo, and Merlot/Cabernet blends. The 12.5% ABV, light structure, and floral Gamay character should signal the variety clearly. The producer (same Bouchard) as w1 may have been a deliberate test of producer recognition. Fleurie is the standard Beaujolais cru benchmark at MW level — it appears frequently as the "aromatic light red" curveball.
