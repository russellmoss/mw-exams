# Puligny-Montrachet, Jean-Marc Boillot, 2012

## Quick tasting profile
Pale to medium gold; nose shows green apple, white peach, subtle hazelnuts, light vanilla oak and wet stone minerality. Palate is medium-full bodied, with less weight and concentration than the Grand Cru Bâtard (W3); still barrel-fermented but tighter in texture, with lively acidity and a clean Côte de Beaune character. Finish is of medium length with white fruit and gentle oak spice. Classic village-level Puligny-Montrachet: elegant rather than monumental.

- Vineyard: Village-level Puligny-Montrachet parcels; the commune's Grand Crus adjoin the best village-level sites

## Vintage character (2012 Puligny-Montrachet)
2012 was a low-yield year in Puligny-Montrachet following spring frost and summer hail. The wines tend toward the tighter, more austere end of the spectrum compared with 2009 or 2010, but with good natural acidity and genuine terroir expression. Village wines from 2012 may have needed more bottle age to show at their best; at the time of the MW exam (2015, served 3 years post-vintage) they would have been in a somewhat closed phase but structurally sound. ## Producer style
Jean-Marc Boillot is a high-profile Burgundy vigneron based in Pommard, son-in-law of the late Etienne Sauzet. He set up his own domaine in the late 1980s after departing Sauzet, and has since built a reputation for polished, internationally styled whites and reds. His white Burgundy portfolio includes Puligny-Montrachet village, several Puligny Premiers Crus (La Truffière, Champ Canet, La Garenne, Combettes, Referts), Bâtard-Montrachet and Chevalier-Montrachet Grand Crus. The style is unabashedly modern: good new oak influence, barrel fermentation, full MLF, generous bâtonnage. Wines tend toward the richer, more textural end of village and Premier Cru Puligny. Commercially well-placed; internationally distributed. (Source needed: producer website/tech sheet; general reputation from MW study knowledge.)

## Why it's in this exam
W4 closes the Burgundy whites quartet in Q1. At village level (vs. W3's Grand Cru Bâtard), it tests whether candidates can detect quality hierarchy through weight and length rather than style (both are oaked Côte de Beaune Chardonnay). The pairing of W3 and W4 from the same general area (Puligny-Montrachet) and the same vintage, combined with W1 and W2 from Chablis, makes Q1 a classic white Burgundy hierarchy exercise. Boillot's richer-than-average village style may blur the line slightly with inferior Premier Cru from more restrained producers.
