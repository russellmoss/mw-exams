# Bandol, Château de Pibarnon, 2013. Provence, France.

## Quick tasting profile
Deep garnet. Nose: Mourvèdre's signature character — dark berry (blackberry, damson), garrigue (rosemary, thyme, lavender), smoked meat, and a savoury leathery note. Pibarnon's hilltop 300m Mediterranean site adds a herbal freshness and Mediterranean sunshine quality. Palate: full-bodied, structured tannins (Mourvèdre demands minimum 18 months oak), dry, with a long spicy savoury finish. At 14% ABV and 5 years old in 2018, the wine is still developing but approachable.

## Technical specs
- Grape variety: Mourvèdre dominant (minimum 50% required by AOC; Pibarnon typically uses a higher percentage); secondary grapes Grenache, Cinsault
- Vineyard: Château de Pibarnon at ~300m elevation, overlooking the Mediterranean near La Cadière-d'Azur; terraced restanques (terraces); limestone soils; hillside site providing both diurnal variation and sea breezes
- Vinification: Traditional Bandol methods; extraction of tannins and colour from Mourvèdre
- Elevage: Minimum 18 months in oak (AOC requirement for Bandol red); Pibarnon typically uses large old wood (foudres); no new small barriques typically
- ABV / RS / TA: 14% / dry / medium-high

## Vintage character (2013 Bandol/Provence)
2013 was a challenging vintage in Provence due to wet, cool conditions — lower ripeness than hot years like 2007 or 2009. For Mourvèdre, which requires full phenolic maturity, 2013 was a difficult year and some wines show a harder, more angular tannin structure. Pibarnon's elevated hillside position and careful selection would mitigate some vintage weakness. By 2018 the wine is still relatively young for serious Bandol. ## Producer style
Château de Pibarnon was purchased by Catherine and Henri de Saint Victor in 1977 after a "fateful lunch" in the region. Located at the highest point in Bandol (~300m), the estate overlooks the Mediterranean. Pierre Casamayor (Revue du Vin de France) described the Mourvèdre here as showing "charming and velvety tones" that reveal "the expression of a unique terroir." The philosophy is hedonistic but complex — wines that are "full of light" and accompany food. (source: pibarnon.com)

## Why it's in this exam
Bandol Mourvèdre is one of the most challenging identification exercises in Paper 3: a full-bodied, structured, garrigue-dominated French red that is not Côtes du Rhône (no Grenache dominance), not Roussillon, and not from any AOP the candidate associates with obvious Mourvèdre. The 18-month oak requirement and the Provence coastal context (garrigue aromas, Mediterranean warmth at 14%) are the key identifiers. Placed alongside the lighter Birichino Cinsault (12.5%) and the Mustiguillo Garnacha (14%), the structural contrast is deliberate.
