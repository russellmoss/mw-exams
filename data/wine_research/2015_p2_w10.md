# Barolo DaGromis, Gaja, 2010. Piemonte, Italy

## Quick tasting profile
Medium garnet with moderate orange-brick evolution (5 years at exam time). Nose is classic Barolo: dried cherry, tar, rose petal, tobacco, eucalyptus, leather, with the Gaja house signature of more refined, less rustic tannins than traditional producers. The palate is full-bodied, with substantial but polished tannins, elevated acidity, complex secondary notes emerging. A prestigious, long-lived Barolo. 2010 is an exceptional Barolo vintage.

## Technical specs
- Grape variety: 100% Nebbiolo (Barolo DOCG mandates single variety)
- Vineyard: DaGromis is sourced from two principal sites: Serralunga d'Alba (Rennina vineyard) and Serralunga (Marenca and Rivette). Serralunga's Helvetian soils produce powerful, structured, tannic Barolo.
- Soils: Serralunga d'Alba: compact, nutrient-poor Helvetian marl (Helvetian formation — Elveziano in Italian), giving great concentration and aging potential
- Vinification: Gaja uses rotary fermenters for extraction in some wines; short maceration with temperature control; this gives the polished, less rustic tannin profile associated with the "modernist" school
- Elevage: French barriques (small barrels) and larger barrels; Gaja pioneered small barrique use for Nebbiolo in the 1980s; DaGromis uses a proportion of French oak barriques — more new oak than traditional large Slavonian botti approach
- ABV / RS / TA: 14% / dry / Source needed
*(Note: DaGromis was introduced in 1988 as Gaja's second Barolo label, separate from the single-vineyard Sperss and Conteisa.)*

## Vintage character (2010 Barolo)
2010 is widely regarded as one of the greatest Barolo vintages of the modern era: a cool, late season following frost risks in spring, producing wines of exceptional concentration, tannic structure, and aromatic complexity. The wines are long-lived with classic terroir expression. At 5 years in 2015, the 2010 Barolo would still be unyielding and tannic — typically these wines need 10-15 years from release. Compared to the riper, earlier-drinking 2011, 2010 is the more structured, austere vintage. ## Producer style
Angelo Gaja is arguably the most famous Piedmontese wine producer, having revolutionized Italian fine wine from the 1970s onwards by introducing French barrique aging and low-yield viticulture. Family winery in Barbaresco. Gaja produces single-vineyard Barbaresco (Sori San Lorenzo, Sori Tildin, Costa Russi) and entered Barolo in 1988 via the Serralunga estate. DaGromis is the more accessible and higher-volume Barolo compared to the prestigious single-vineyard Sperss and Conteisa. Market position: ultra-premium Italian wine; DaGromis is typically USD 80-120 at retail, making it the entry point into the Gaja Barolo range. Gaja labels his single-vineyard Barolos as "Langhe DOC" due to the addition of Barbera in some years, though this policy has evolved. DaGromis remains DOCG.
