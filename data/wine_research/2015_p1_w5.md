# Chenin Blanc, Tierhoek, 2012. Piekenierskloof, South Africa

## Quick tasting profile
Deep gold, possibly amber highlights if older-vine or exposed to some barrel work. Nose shows tropical fruit (guava, pineapple), quince, yellow peach and honey with a waxy, lanolin quality that signals old-vine Chenin. Palate is full-bodied with 14% alcohol clearly felt; texture generous and round. Acidity present but less prominent than Loire Chenin; long, rich finish. South African old-vine bush vine Chenin character, contrasting sharply with the leaner, more mineral Loire expression of W6.

## Vintage character (2012 Piekenierskloof / South Africa)
2012 was a relatively balanced vintage in South Africa's cooler elevated regions. Piekenierskloof's altitude moderates temperatures significantly versus the Swartland or Stellenbosch floor. The 2012 growing season generally produced wines of good concentration with retained freshness in the Western Cape. Old-vine Chenin from Piekenierskloof in 2012 would show good density of fruit and natural acidity. (Source needed: specific 2012 South Africa vintage report.)

## Producer style
Tierhoek is a small, quality-focused estate in Piekenierskloof, owned by Stefanie and John de Beer. The property is known for old bush vine Chenin Blanc planted on granite-derived soils at altitude. The estate is biodynamically farmed and has developed a reputation among wine cognoscenti for producing distinctive, site-expressive Chenin Blanc that combines South African tropical richness with genuine structure and longevity — positioned as a counter-narrative to the dominant Swartland-style Chenin. Commercial positioning: boutique/artisan sector, relatively low volume. (Source needed: Tierhoek website and producer information unavailable during research.)

## Why it's in this exam
W5 pairs with W6 (Vouvray Marc Brédif, Loire) in Q2, which states "Wines 5–6 are both made from the same single grape variety, but come from different countries." This is a classic Old World/New World Chenin Blanc comparison. The South African Piekenierskloof expression (fuller, tropical, higher ABV at 14%) contrasts with the Loire Vouvray (leaner, higher acid, more mineral, lower ABV at 12.5%). Key exam discriminators: ABV (14% vs 12.5%), tropical vs mineral/honeysuckle fruit, body and acid balance. MW candidates must identify Chenin Blanc from both expressions, then separate origin.
