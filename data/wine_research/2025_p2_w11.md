# Beaujolais Villages Le Perreon, Domaine de la Madone, 2023

## Quick tasting profile
Bright ruby-purple. Expect a lifted, crunchy Gamay profile rather than extraction: raspberry, red cherry, blossom and mineral notes, with juicy acidity and light-to-medium body (source: https://www.northberkeleyimports.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2016/11/MADONE_beaujolais_perreon1.pdf). In blind terms it should feel more serious than generic Beaujolais Nouveau because of altitude, vine age and granite tension, but still resolutely fresh and drinkable.

## Technical specs
- Grape variety: 100% Gamay (source: https://www.northberkeleyimports.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2016/11/MADONE_beaujolais_perreon1.pdf)
- Vineyard: Estate vineyards in Le Perreon on steep hillsides at roughly 1,600-2,000 feet (source: https://www.northberkeleyimports.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2016/11/MADONE_beaujolais_perreon1.pdf)
- Soils: Mostly pink decomposed granite, compared by the importer to Fleurie-like soils (source: https://www.northberkeleyimports.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2016/11/MADONE_beaujolais_perreon1.pdf)
- Vinification: Hand-harvested; indigenous yeasts; fermented in cement tanks (source: https://www.northberkeleyimports.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2016/11/MADONE_beaujolais_perreon1.pdf)
- Elevage: Aged in tank; bottled unfined and unfiltered (source: https://www.northberkeleyimports.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2016/11/MADONE_beaujolais_perreon1.pdf)

## Vintage character (2023 Beaujolais)
Inter Beaujolais described 2023 as a bright, energetic year shaped by heatwaves, storms, and alternating warm and cool periods, but with fruity, colorful, well-balanced Gamays and a relatively generous crop (source: https://www.beaujolais.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2023/10/CP-Vignoble-beaujolais-Millesime-2023_EN-1.pdf). That matters here: the wine should show ripe fruit and charm, but still preserve the freshness and drinkability the region wanted from 2023.

## Producer style
Domaine de la Madone is run by Olivier and Bruno Bererd and is positioned by North Berkeley Imports as a high-altitude, old-vine Beaujolais specialist rather than a mass-market source (source: https://www.northberkeleyimports.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2016/11/MADONE_beaujolais_perreon1.pdf). The style is classic and minimally dressed up: native yeast, cement, no oak signature, no fining or filtration, and a focus on site expression from Le Perreon's granitic slopes.

## Why it's in this exam
This belongs to 2025 Paper 2, Question 3, the all-European five-variety set. It is a strong examiner choice because it tests whether the candidate can call Gamay from structure and fruit profile, then place it in Beaujolais rather than Pinot Noir, lighter Loire Cabernet Franc, or a simple carbonic red from elsewhere.
