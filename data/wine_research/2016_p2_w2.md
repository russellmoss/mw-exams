# Clos de Rochegrès, Château des Jacques / Domaine Louis Jadot, 2014

## Quick tasting profile
Deeper and darker than the Lapierre Morgon, with black cherry, violet, cracked pepper, and a more obviously structural frame. The palate should show firmer tannin, more extraction, and a slightly more Burgundian, cellar-built shape, with Gamay fruit carried by savoury grip rather than overt carbonic juiciness. In a blind lineup this reads as a serious, ageworthy cru Beaujolais with more power than charm-first immediacy.

## Vintage character (2014 Beaujolais)
2014 gave balanced, classically proportioned cru Beaujolais with fresher acids and cleaner fruit than the preceding year. In Moulin-a-Vent that often translates into floral perfume over a firmer tannic spine, supporting medium-term ageing and stronger resemblance to red Burgundy in structure. ## Producer style
Château des Jacques has been part of Louis Jadot since the mid-1990s and is one of the clearest examples of cru Beaujolais made with a consciously Burgundian mindset. Single-vineyard bottlings, structured extraction, and elevage choices aimed at ageing potential make the wines more architectural than many easy-drinking Beaujolais examples. That stylistic seriousness explains its usefulness in MW practical comparisons.

## Why it's in this exam
Paired with Morgon in 2016 Paper 2 Question 1 as a same-region, same-grape comparison. It lets the examiner test whether the candidate can separate two cru Beaujolais styles and articulate why Moulin-a-Vent often shows more tannin, structure, and ageing ambition.
