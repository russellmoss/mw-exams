# Fine Ruby Port, Cockburn's, NV

## Quick tasting profile
Deep ruby. This should be obviously fortified but youthfully fruit-led rather than oxidative: cherry, raspberry and plum fruit, soft sweetness, moderate tannin and a straightforward, commercial finish (source: https://www.cockburns.com/files/wine/2_cockburn-s-fine-ruby-port_file1727101795.pdf). In blind tasting the key call is young ruby Port, not LBV, Vintage or Tawny.

## Vintage character (NV / category character)
This is non-vintage, so the important exam context is the style category. Decanter's Port guide describes ruby Port as the youthful, fruit-driven end of the Port pyramid, in contrast to oxidative wood-aged tawny styles (source: https://www.decanter.com/learn/wine-terminology/vintage-port-283115/). Cockburn's own material matches that: bright fruit, minimal oxidative development, and short wood ageing to preserve youthful red-fruit character (source: https://www.cockburns.com/wine/cockburn-s-fine-ruby-port/2).

## Producer style
Cockburn's, founded in 1815, sits within the Symington family stable and positions Fine Ruby as an approachable, flexible house style rather than a connoisseur bottling (source: https://www.cockburns.com/files/wine/2_cockburn-s-fine-ruby-port_file1727101795.pdf). Commercially it is broad-market and easy to understand, which is precisely why it works well in a mixed-bag exam flight.
