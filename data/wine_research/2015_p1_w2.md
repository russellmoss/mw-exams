# Chablis Grand Cru Vaudésir, Domaine Louis Michel et Fils, 2012

## Quick tasting profile
Pale gold with green-gold rim; more depth than village Chablis. Nose shows green apple, lemon zest, white peach, chalk, and a distinctive flinty/iodine minerality. Palate is fuller than village tier — more extract, deeper texture — but retains the quintessential steely, high-acid Chablis backbone. No new oak (Louis Michel never uses oak). Saline, mineral finish with considerable length. Textbook unoaked Grand Cru Chablis.

## Technical specs
- Grape variety: 100% Chardonnay
- Vineyard: Vaudésir is one of seven Grand Cru climates in Chablis, on the southwest-facing hillside above the town; sits between Les Preuses and the unofficial La Moutonne designation
- Soils: Kimmeridgian limestone, clay, and fossilised oyster shells — the defining terroir feature of Chablis Grand Cru (source: Wikipedia Chablis Grand Cru)
- Vinification: 100% stainless steel fermentation and aging; no malolactic fermentation; this is the Louis Michel house signature — no oak at any level, even Grand Cru (Source needed: producer website unavailable)
- Elevage: Stainless steel; extended on fine lees; bottled after ~12 months

## Vintage character (2012 Chablis)
2012 in Chablis was a difficult but rewarding year. Spring frost damage and summer hail reduced yields sharply; a warm, dry September allowed full phenolic ripeness. Grand Cru sites, with better drainage and exposition, were less affected by disease pressure than village parcels. The resulting wines are concentrated, taut, and marked by excellent natural acidity — classic Chablis structure suited to medium-term aging. The combination of lower yields and good ripeness gives the 2012 Grand Crus above-average concentration. (Source needed: specific 2012 Chablis vintage meteorological data.)

## Producer style
Domaine Louis Michel et Fils (Jean-Loup Michel) is among the most celebrated names in Chablis, and the reference point for the unoaked, pure-terroir philosophy. Unlike contemporaries such as William Fèvre or Brocard who use oak on Grand Cru wines, Louis Michel uses no wood at any level. The entire range — from village Chablis through Premier Cru to all three Grand Cru holdings (Vaudésir, Grenouilles, Les Clos) — is vinified and aged in temperature-controlled stainless steel. This approach makes Louis Michel wines almost unmistakably identifiable: crystalline, minerally focused, with no oak or toasty interference. The domaine is organically inclined though not officially certified. Commercial positioning is mid-to-upper premium (Grand Crus ~£50–80 on release). ## Why it's in this exam
W2 is the Grand Cru foil to W1 (village Chablis). Both are from Louis Michel's no-oak camp, so the blind tasting challenge is recognising the hierarchy through weight, texture, and extract rather than through oak differentiation. Vaudésir is also specifically valuable for MW candidates to recognise as a top-tier Chablis Grand Cru — the question (Wines 1–4 same region same vintage, identify specific origin and discuss quality with reference to region) requires correctly placing this wine within the Grand Cru tier of Chablis.
