# Riesling Silberberg de Rorschwihr, Rolly Gassmann, 2021

## Quick tasting profile
Golden yellow with youthful reflections. Expressive nose of citrus, spice, and ripe/dried fruit notes with a hint of botrytis influence. The palate is complex and medium-bodied with refreshing, racy acidity (10.11 g/L TA) balancing 16.9 g/L residual sugar — reads off-dry but structured. Long, mineral-driven finish with limestone-derived tension. At 13.5% ABV this sits at the richer end for 2021 Alsace Riesling but below Rolly Gassmann's usual power levels.

## Technical specs
- Grape variety: 100% Riesling
- Vineyard: Silberberg lieu-dit, Rorschwihr (not a Grand Cru — Rorschwihr contains no Grand Cru sites)
- Soils: Muschelkalk limestone (pre-Jurassic fossilised shell deposits), partially decarbonated by acidic granite runoff from surrounding slopes
- Vinification: Gentle whole-bunch pressing with 12–15 hours skin contact in the press to maximise aromatic extraction. Must settled, then slow fermentation with indigenous yeasts (4–6 months depending on terroir). Noble rot welcomed at harvest. (source: Liberty Wines)
- Elevage: ~11 months on fine lees in large used oak tonneaux (25–100 hL capacity, some over 200 years old). Natural stabilisation, bottled autumn following harvest.
- ABV: 13.5% / RS: 16.9 g/L / TA: 10.11 g/L
- Closure: Natural cork
- Drinking window: Now–2030 (source: The Wine Society)

## Vintage character (2021 Alsace)
A challenging but ultimately excellent vintage. Frost in April and record June rainfall (125mm) caused ~50% crop loss across most vineyards (half frost, half mildew). A saving Indian summer in September rescued quality. The resulting wines are drier than usual with elevated, ripe acidity and lower alcohol — Zind-Humbrecht averaged just 25 hL/ha for AOC and 19 hL/ha for Grand Cru. Comparable to elements of 2019, 2017, and 2010. The high natural acidity and tension make this a strong cellaring vintage despite small volumes. (source: Zind-Humbrecht vintage report)

## Producer style
Domaine Rolly Gassmann, based in Rorschwihr since 1661, is one of Alsace's most singular producers. Run by Pierre Gassmann (third-generation since the 1967 Rolly-Gassmann marriage), the domaine farms 50 hectares across ~70 plots using biodynamic principles (since 1997). The house style is unmistakable: late-harvested, rich wines with more residual sugar than the Alsace norm, balanced by vibrant acidity and extended lees ageing. They hold four years' stock before release — over one million bottles in cellar — selling primarily to top restaurants and private clients (only 20% exported). Liberty Wines is the UK importer. Production: ~333,000 bottles annually across 40 cuvees. All lieu-dit wines are hand-harvested and vinified separately in large old oak. Rorschwihr has no Grand Cru, so the lieu-dit bottlings (Silberberg, Kappelweg, etc.) are the top tier. (sources: wineanorak.com; Liberty Wines; The Wine Society)

## Why it's in this exam
Wine 8 in 2025 Paper 1, Question 3: "Wines 7–9 are from the same country and different single grape varieties." Paired with Muscadet (Melon de Bourgogne, Loire) and Vouvray Demi-Sec (Chenin Blanc, Loire), this Riesling represents France's diversity of white grape varieties and regions. The question asks candidates to identify origin closely and comment on production method, style, quality, and maturity — testing whether candidates can distinguish Alsace Riesling's off-dry richness from Loire's contrasting styles (lean Muscadet vs. textured demi-sec Vouvray). The 13.5% ABV and Rolly Gassmann's characteristically generous RS make this a useful test of whether a candidate can correctly place an off-dry, rich-styled Riesling in Alsace rather than misreading it as German or New World.
