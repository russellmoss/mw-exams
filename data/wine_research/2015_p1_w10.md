# Gewurztraminer, Domaine Trimbach, 2012. Alsace, France

## Quick tasting profile
Deep gold; intensely aromatic nose dominated by lychee, rose petal, Turkish delight and ginger spice — Gewurztraminer's unmistakable varietal signature. Palate is full-bodied, rich and broad; Trimbach's house style is actually notably drier than many Alsace producers — their Gewurztraminer is made to be genuinely dry (not off-dry). Low acidity (characteristic of the variety). Long, perfumed finish. No new oak (Alsace does not use new oak for these wines). Visually deep gold; phenolically extracted from the pink-to-red grape skin.

## Technical specs
- Grape variety: 100% Gewurztraminer
- Vineyard: AOC Alsace; Trimbach sources from Ribeauvillé area, Haut-Rhin; Gewurztraminer accounts for 18.6% of Alsace vineyard area (source: Wikipedia Alsace wine)
- Soils: Diverse — granite, gneiss, schist, clay-limestone typical of Alsace's varied geology (Source needed: specific Trimbach vineyard parcels)
- Vinification: Temperature-controlled stainless steel fermentation; no new oak; limited or no MLF to maintain freshness; Trimbach's approach emphasises balance ("Balance, balance and balance" — Pierre Trimbach, source: Trimbach website); dry fermentation to near-zero residual sugar
- Elevage: Stainless steel or large older wood (foudres); bottled in classic Alsace flûte bottle (legally required; source: Wikipedia Alsace wine)
- ABV / RS / TA: 14.0% / dry (Trimbach's style; residual sugar very low despite aromatic richness) / low to medium acidity (Gewurztraminer has characteristically lower acidity than Riesling; Source needed for exact figures)

## Vintage character (2012 Alsace)
2012 in Alsace was a mixed vintage — early frosts reduced yields and a changeable growing season created variable results across producers and sites. However, warm, dry conditions through harvest allowed good ripeness accumulation. Gewurztraminer in 2012 Alsace can show generous body and aromatic concentration, with the variety's naturally low acidity making careful harvest timing critical to prevent flabbiness. Trimbach's dry style benefits from harvesting at optimal maturity rather than waiting for late-harvest richness. (Source needed: specific 2012 Alsace vintage data.)

## Producer style
Maison F.E. Trimbach has been a family-owned winery in Ribeauvillé since 1626 — 13 generations and one of Alsace's oldest houses (source: Trimbach website). Pierre Trimbach's stated philosophy: "Balance, balance and balance" (source: Trimbach website). The house is known internationally for its rigorously dry style — in sharp contrast to many Alsace producers who leave noticeable residual sugar. This makes Trimbach wines atypically dry for the category; their Gewurztraminer at 14% ABV is genuinely bone-dry or close to it, where competitors may show 12–20 g/L RS. Signature wines include Clos Sainte-Hune Riesling (arguably Alsace's finest white), Cuvée Frédéric Emile Riesling, and Gewurztraminer Seigneurs de Ribeaupierre. Commercial positioning: internationally distributed, broad quality range from entry to prestige; benchmark house for serious Alsace.

## Why it's in this exam
W10 is part of Q3's six single-variety flight. Gewurztraminer is one of the most identifiable white varieties — its lychee/rose/spice aromatic profile is unmistakable — making it relatively "easy" in blind identification terms. The challenge for MW level is: (1) identifying Alsace vs other Gewurztraminer regions (Germany, Italy/Alto Adige, New World); (2) recognising Trimbach's unusually dry style when the variety is typically associated with off-dry or sweet expressions; (3) commenting intelligently on "potential for further ageing" — Trimbach Gewurztraminer can develop over 5–8 years in good vintages. The Q3 "six single different grape varieties" question tests breadth across white wine types; Gewurztraminer as the most obvious aromatic may be the "giveaway" to anchor the flight.
