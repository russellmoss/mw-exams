# Chardonnay, Yellow Label, Wolf Blass, 2009

## Quick tasting profile
Riper and broader than the Chablis in the same set, with peach, melon and tropical fruit, plus obvious creamy/oaky shaping. The palate should be soft, accessible and commercial in style, aiming for immediate appeal rather than severe minerality.

## Vintage character (2009 South Australia)
2009 in South Australia was warm and naturally gave full-flavored fruit. In a major brand Chardonnay this would be expected to translate into ripe stone-fruit character, softer acidity and a broad palate that can comfortably carry visible winemaking input.

## Producer style
Wolf Blass Yellow Label is a flagship commercial range built around consistency and broad market appeal. The wines are intentionally polished and generous, making them useful exam foils when the candidate needs to distinguish commercial New World Chardonnay from more site-driven or artisanal examples.

## Why it's in this exam
This is the lowest-complexity Chardonnay in Question 2 and likely the commercial benchmark/foil. It helps the examiners test whether a candidate can discuss oak and malo intelligently while still distinguishing quality level inside a same-variety set.
