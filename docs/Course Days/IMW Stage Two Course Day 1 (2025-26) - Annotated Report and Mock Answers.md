# IMW Stage Two Course Day 1 (2025-26) - Annotated Report and Mock Answers

## Brief Report

Day 1 is a white-wine focused practical. It is mostly about:

- fast family recognition on aromatic and high-acid whites
- same-variety / cross-origin comparison
- quality and commercial calibration within a single grape family
- using maturity and winemaking to separate wines that are otherwise close

Best study tools:

- `outputs/study_diagrams/p1_whites.md`
- `outputs/study_diagrams/variety_cards.md`

Overall, this is a strong day for the white decision tree. The main weakness is Question 1, where the wines are intentionally diverse and the tree is forced to identify four different families rather than one neat branch.

### Q1 Pre-glass thinking

The study diagrams help less here than on later questions. The useful move is to stop looking for one shared grape and instead map the wines by aroma intensity, texture, and acid shape:

- Tokaji dry Furmint should feel linear, mineral, and volcanic
- Santorini Assyrtiko should feel saline, taut, and dry
- Torrontes should be the most perfumed and floral
- Hunter Semillon should be restrained, lemony, and low in obvious oak

### Q2 Pre-glass thinking

This is a classic Chardonnay comparison. Use the white diagram to get to Chardonnay first, then ask:

- which wine is most oaked?
- which wine is most maritime or mineral?
- which wine is most developed and premium?
- which wine is the most commercial or entry-premium?

### Q3 Pre-glass thinking

The Riesling branch should be easy to keep alive on the tree. After that, the useful distinctions are:

- dry vs sweet impression
- mature vs youthful
- Alsace weight vs German tension
- premium benchmark vs commercial dry Riesling

## Mock Answers

### Question 1

**Classifier summary:** `F4 independent_breadth`

**Mock answer:**  
Wine 1 is Furmint from Tokaj, Hungary. It is a dry, high-acid, mineral style with very good freshness and good commercial potential in the premium niche. Wine 2 is Assyrtiko from Santorini, Greece. It is tense, saline and concentrated, with very good to excellent quality and clear premium positioning. Wine 3 is Torrontes from Mendoza, Argentina, with a little more aromatic lift and a slightly more commercial, value-driven profile. Wine 4 is Hunter Valley Semillon from Australia, dry, low-ish alcohol and very restrained, with excellent food-pairing potential and strong medium-term ageing capacity.

**How I would have used the trees:**  
I would not try to force all four wines into one family. I would first identify the white-wine family, then let the tree tell me whether I was in volcanic/mineral, aromatic, or classic dry semillon territory. Once the grape families were narrowed, I would use acid, perfume, and texture to confirm the specific origins.

### Question 2

**Classifier summary:** `F1 same_variety_cross_origin`

**Mock answer:**  
All four wines are Chardonnay. Wine 5 is Hemel-en-Aarde Chardonnay, the most premium and most obviously serious in the set, with partial malolactic fermentation, oak ferment, and strong ageing potential. Wine 6 is Casablanca Chardonnay from Chile, the freshest and most commercial wine here, unoaked and cleanly made. Wine 7 is Marlborough Chardonnay from New Zealand, more oaked and more ambitious than Wine 6, with clear premium intent. Wine 8 is Langhe Chardonnay from Piemonte, a restrained Italian expression with good quality and strong regional identity. The quality ranking is likely W5 and W7 at the top, then W8, then W6, though W6 remains the most commercially accessible.

**How I would have used the trees:**  
I would go straight to the Chardonnay branch on the white diagram, then use oak, MLF, and ripeness to separate the four wines. The diagram is best for confirming grape family, not for ranking quality.

### Question 3

**Classifier summary:** `F1 same_variety_cross_origin`

**Mock answer:**  
Wines 9 and 10 are both Riesling, but W9 from Alsace is the more complex and premium wine, with greater depth, more textural weight and stronger cellaring potential. W10 from Pfalz is more straightforward and commercially accessible, with a lighter, cleaner dry style. Wines 11 and 12 are both Riesling too, but in a different stylistic register: W11, the Mosel Kabinett, is more delicate and more obviously sweet, while W12 from Washington is dry, commercial and comparatively simple. W11 is the higher quality wine on complexity and definition, while W12 is the more approachable market wine.

**How I would have used the trees:**  
The Riesling branch is the key. Then I would split by sweetness impression, alcohol, and maturity. The white diagram is helpful, but the decision matrix is only truly reliable once acid/sweetness balance is confirmed in the glass.

## Decision Matrix Accuracy

- Q1: moderate. It gets you into the right white families, but not all four wines with high confidence.
- Q2: good. Chardonnay is one of the most reliable branches.
- Q3: good. Riesling is another strong branch, especially when acid and maturity are obvious.

Overall, Day 1 is a solid application day for the white tree. It is best on Questions 2 and 3, and most limited on Question 1 because that question intentionally prevents the tree from doing too much work for you.
