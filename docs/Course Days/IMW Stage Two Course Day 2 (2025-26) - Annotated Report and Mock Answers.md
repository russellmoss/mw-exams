# IMW Stage Two Course Day 2 (2025-26) - Annotated Report and Mock Answers

## Brief Report

Day 2 is the most red-wine focused of the three course days. It tests:

- light to medium-bodied red identification
- same-variety / cross-origin comparison
- same-country breadth
- quality ladders and commercial positioning

Best study tools:

- `outputs/study_diagrams/p2_reds.md`
- `outputs/study_diagrams/variety_cards.md`

This is a good day for the red decision tree. The main limitation is that Question 3 contains a broad US set, so the tree helps more with family recognition than with exact regional placement.

### Q1 Pre-glass thinking

The tree is useful, but only if you keep the red families broad:

- Morgon Gamay should be the lightest and most floral
- Agiorgitiko should be ripe, supple and more Mediterranean
- Blaufrankisch should show pepper, high acid and firmer tannin

### Q2 Pre-glass thinking

This is a classic Pinot Noir comparison. The main task is to decide whether each wine is:

- Old World or New World
- cool-climate or moderate-climate
- more commercial or more fine-wine

### Q3 Pre-glass thinking

The best approach is to classify the broad US red family first:

- Zinfandel = ripe, plush, often sweet-fruited
- Napa Cabernet = structured, powerful, expensive
- Oregon Pinot Noir = lighter, cooler, more perfume-driven
- Petite Sirah = dense, dark, tannic
- Cabernet Franc = lifted, herbal, medium-bodied

### Q4 Pre-glass thinking

Cabernet Sauvignon is the anchor. The useful distinction is not only grape, but maturity:

- W8 is likely the more classic and slightly more developed wine
- W9 should be younger, firmer and more modern in construction

## Mock Answers

### Question 1

**Classifier summary:** `F4 independent_breadth`

**Mock answer:**  
Wine 1 is Gamay from Morgon in Beaujolais, France. It is carbonic or semi-carbonic in feel, with fresh red fruit, floral lift and strong drinkability, and has very good commercial potential in the specialist red-wine segment. Wine 2 is Agiorgitiko from Nemea, Greece. It is riper, rounder and more softly structured, with good quality and strong regional identity. Wine 3 is Blaufrankisch from Burgenland, Austria. It is the most peppery and structural of the three, with high acidity and good quality, but still a relatively niche commercial proposition.

**How I would have used the trees:**  
I would first identify the red family shape, then use fruit profile, tannin and acidity to separate the wines. The red tree is less about exact origin here and more about getting into the correct structural neighbourhood quickly.

### Question 2

**Classifier summary:** `F1 same_variety_cross_origin`

**Mock answer:**  
All four wines are Pinot Noir. Wine 4 from the Loire is likely the most direct and lightly structured. Wine 5 from Alsace is probably more commercial and straightforward, with modest complexity. Wine 6 from the Ahr is the most serious and ageworthy, with the strongest oak regime and the highest level of site-specific ambition. Wine 7 from Mornington Peninsula sits in the premium New World zone, with good fruit purity and careful oak use. Quality is highest in Wine 6, then Wine 7, then Wine 4, then Wine 5, although Wine 4 may be more interesting than its price suggests.

**How I would have used the trees:**  
I would stay on the Pinot Noir branch and then use colour intensity, perfume, tannin fineness and oak to separate the regions. This is a good example of a question where the tree narrows the family very well, but the glass still has to do the final work.

### Question 3

**Classifier summary:** `F2 same_origin_comparative`

**Mock answer:**  
The wines are all from the USA, but they represent five very different red-wine identities. McManis Zinfandel is the most commercial and the ripest, with obvious fruit sweetness and easy appeal. Heitz Cabernet Sauvignon is the most classic and expensive, with greater structure, depth and ageing potential. Lingua Franca Pinot Noir is the most perfumed and fine-boned. Bogle Petite Sirah is the darkest, densest and most tannic. Matthiasson Cabernet Franc is the most lifted and aromatic, with fine structure and a more specialist premium audience. The set is really about recognising that the USA can deliver both commercial and benchmark expressions in several red families.

**How I would have used the trees:**  
I would use the red tree to sort the wines into broad families, then switch to structural cues. This is where the variety cards help most, because the question is broad rather than regionally tight.

### Question 4

**Classifier summary:** `F1 same_variety_same_country`

**Mock answer:**  
Both wines are Cabernet Sauvignon from Australia. Wine 8, Wynns Black Label from Coonawarra, is the more classic and slightly more mature wine, with cassis, regional mint/eucalypt and strong structure. Wine 9, Leeuwin Prelude from Margaret River, is younger, more polished and probably the more ambitious wine in terms of fruit concentration and modern premium positioning. Both are good to very good wines, but they play different roles: Wynns is a benchmark regional classic, Leeuwin is a more modern premium Cabernet with stronger freshness and probably greater immediate appeal.

**How I would have used the trees:**  
I would move straight to the Cabernet branch and then use maturity and regional style to separate the wines. The matrix is good here because Cabernet has strong structural cues, and the age gap is meaningful.

## Decision Matrix Accuracy

- Q1: moderate. The tree will identify the red families, but the exact countries are not automatic.
- Q2: good. Pinot Noir is a strong branch and the quality contrast is readable.
- Q3: moderate to good. It is broad, but the families are familiar and the USA frame helps.
- Q4: good. Cabernet Sauvignon is one of the cleaner red decision-tree routes.

Overall, Day 2 is a decent test of the red matrix. It is strongest on Questions 2 and 4, and most limited on Question 3 because the set is intentionally broad and does not reward overfitting to a single red-region branch.
