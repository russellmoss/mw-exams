# **Master of Wine — Practical Papers**

*Question & Wine Compilation, 2015 – 2025*

*Source: Institute of Masters of Wine examination papers*

## **How to use this document**

Each exam year is presented in reverse chronological order (most recent first). Within a year, each practical paper (Paper 1, Paper 2, Paper 3\) is shown in full: all questions exactly as worded by the IMW, followed by the complete wine list for that paper.

Wines are numbered 1–12 per paper. Question stems reference these wine numbers directly (e.g. "Wines 1–4 are from the same single grape variety"). The wine numbers shown beside each question in this document indicate which wines from the paper's list are assigned to that question.

Theory papers are not included — this compilation covers only the three practical (blind tasting) papers from each exam.

Use the space beneath each question to annotate the examiners' intent: what skill is being tested, what the wines have in common, and what distinguishes them.

Lastly, it is testing the ability to perceive quality differences, not across wines, but within a category (e.g. sparkling), and once again who would buy this wine, where they would be (channel and country) and for what reason.

Wines 1-2, 3-4 and 5-6 are pairs. Each pair is made from the same single grape variety.

For each pair:

a) Identify the grape variety with reference to both wines. (3 x 10 marks)

b) Compare and contrast the quality, maturity, and capacity to age. (3 x 20 marks)

For each wine:

c) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (6 x 10 marks)

*Notes / Examiner intent:*

*This tests the ability to identify specific grape varieties.  More importantly, the ability to know which varieties are made in vastly different quality levels (e.g. grand cru white burgundy vs. entry level chardonnay).  Within that, whether the wine is built for agebility or immediate consumption).  These will be more commonly known wines, so the question also expects the test taker to know where these specific styles of each wine are made, and why.*

### **Question 2   *(Wines 7–9)***

Wines 7-9 are each from different single grape varieties.

For each wine:

a) Comment on the method of production. (3 x 7 marks)

b) Comment on the style, quality, and commercial position. (3 x 12 marks)

c) Identify the grape variety and origin as closely as possible. (3 x 6 marks)

*Notes / Examiner intent:*

This tests the ability to identify how wines are made (e.g. with wild yeast, or in oak, or with reductive or oxidative winemaking).  Secondly, what the style is, and if the test taker can discern which of these random wines are better.  With these less common wines, the question asks how the wines would be sold and to whom.  Lastly, what the grape is and where it is from.  The fact the the grape and origin are last and are only worth 6 marks suggests that the will be not mainstream wines, and that the guessing of a specific grape is less important than understanding how it is made and how good it is objectively.

This tests both the ability to know a variety of white blends, and which countries might make multiple styles of white blends.   The question also tests the ability to discern oak, and how the oak is used (fermented in, vs aged in, size, age of barrels etc).  Lastly, the style and how it is positined commercially.  Note that in this case the origin and production take preference over the other three.

This tests the ability to correctly identify high quality wines that are highly typical for their style.  These are generally recognizable wines. Then, it tests the ability to discern the quality level of wines that will most likely have a range (e.g. rated growth bordeaux vs. generic bordeaux).  Lastly it tests the knowledge of vintages, which is a combination of testing how old the wine is and knowledge of whether a specific vintage was hot or cold.
