# Proposed annotation for 2016 Paper 2 Question 4

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 8 and 9 come from the same region.

For each wine:

a) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (2 x 9 marks)

b) Discuss quality within the context of the specific region. (2 x 9 marks)

c) Discuss the key winemaking techniques used to arrive at this style. (2 x 7 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W8: Côte Rôtie, La Barbarine, Domaine Gangloff, 2011. Rhône Valley, France. (13%)
- W9: Châteauneuf du Pape, Clos des Papes, 2011. Rhône Valley, France. (15%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of the Rhône Valley and specifically your ability to distinguish northern from southern Rhône. "Same region" with two clearly different reds on Paper 2 is pointing almost certainly at the Rhône, which uniquely spans both a northern section (Syrah-based, granite, cool, perfumed) and a southern section (Grenache-dominant, garrigue, warm, blended) within the broad "Rhône Valley" designation. The same basic structure applies to France more broadly (e.g., two Languedoc AOCs) but the northern/southern Rhône contrast is the most iconic at MW level.

The key differentiation: Côte-Rôtie at 13% ABV is Syrah-dominant (can include up to 20% Viognier), concentrated but lifted and perfumed, granite-driven, expensive and prestigious. Châteauneuf-du-Pape at 15% ABV is Grenache-dominant, broad, warm, garrigue-inflected, blended from multiple varieties — a completely different style despite both being labelled Rhône. The 2% ABV difference alone is a major blind tasting signal.

Winemaking sub-question wants: Côte-Rôtie = 100% Syrah typically, steep granite slopes, destemming vs whole-bunch a key choice, new oak use (Gangloff uses around 40% new); CdP = Grenache-dominant multi-variety blend, galets roulés soils, typically large old foudres or older barrels, high natural alcohol, less new oak. Quality question wants you to place each within its appellation's hierarchy — Gangloff is a cult producer, Clos des Papes is one of CdP's most respected names.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - "Same region" = Rhône is the obvious region where two very different sub-appellations share a valley name
  - Two very different ABVs (13% vs 15%) expected from these different sub-regions
  - Quality and winemaking both worth significant marks
- Universe I considered:
  - Rhône: northern (Syrah-Côte-Rôtie) vs southern (Grenache-CdP) — perfect
  - Bordeaux: possible but already used in Q3 of same paper
  - Languedoc: could have two different AOCs but less iconic
- Rule-outs:
  - Bordeaux: already featured in Q3
  - Italy: no single "region" encompassing such stylistically distinct sub-appellations
- Conclusion: Rhône Valley — Côte-Rôtie (Gangloff) vs Châteauneuf-du-Pape (Clos des Papes), both 2011
