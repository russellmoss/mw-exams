# Proposed annotation for 2018 Paper 1 Question 3

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 11-12 are made from the same single grape variety. a) Identify the grape variety (10) b) Identify the origin of each wine (2x8) c) Comment on the winemaking, quality and maturity of each wine (2x12)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W11: Kiedrich Gräfenberg Riesling Trocken GG, Robert Weil, 2016. Rheingau. (13%)
- W12: Eitelsbacher Karthäuserhofberg Riesling Auslese, Karthäuserhof, 2007. Mosel (Ruwer). (9%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of German Riesling across the full style spectrum — specifically, whether you understand that Riesling is not a single style. You have two wines from the same variety made in completely different ways at completely different quality levels and sweetness levels. With same variety and only two wines and marks on maturity/quality, the examiner is almost certainly pushing you toward a grape that can be made bone dry and very sweet, and that has serious aging potential. In Germany that's basically only Riesling.

The two wines set up the most classic contrast in German wine: GG Trocken (bone dry, 13% ABV, full power, 2016 vintage — still young) versus Auslese (9% ABV, sweet, honeyed, already 11 years old by exam date 2018 and fully evolved). Riesling is not hard to identify once you know it — the petrol/kerosene note developing with age on the Auslese is basically diagnostic. The challenge is identifying Rheingau versus Mosel. Rheingau tends to more body and power, Mosel/Ruwer tends to more delicacy and finesse. The 13% GG is noticeably fuller than what you'd expect from a Mosel, and the 9% Auslese is obviously from the lighter, more delicate Ruwer tributary. Sub-region precision here would be impressive but just getting Germany + Riesling is the floor for marks.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - Same single variety, 2 wines, heavy marks on maturity/quality (24 marks total)
  - One wine will likely be sweet or off-dry, one dry — otherwise why use only two wines?
- Universe I considered:
  - Riesling (Germany, Alsace, Austria, Australia), Chenin (for dry/sweet range), Semillon
- Rule-outs:
  - Chenin already used in Q2 (South Africa); wouldn't repeat
  - The ABV contrast (13% vs 9%) makes Riesling almost certain — only German Riesling regularly sits at 9% bone dry is unusual, 9% = sweet/off-dry
- Conclusion: Riesling, one bone-dry Rheingau GG, one Mosel Auslese
