# Proposed annotation for 2025 Paper 1 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 3-6 are from the same single grape variety.

With reference to all four wines:

a) Identify the grape variety. (16 marks)

For each wine:

b) Comment on the style, quality, and maturity. (4 x 13 marks)

c) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (4 x 8 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference -- DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W3: Chablis 1er Cru Cote de Lechet, Domaine Defaix, 2010. Burgundy, France. (13%)
- W4: Chardonnay, Old Stage, 2019. Monterey, California, USA. (14%)
- W5: Chardonnay, Iona, 2022. Elgin Highlands, South Africa. (12.5%)
- W6: Chardonnay, Moss Wood, 2023. Margaret River, Australia. (14%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of a single white grape variety.  In these questions, it's likely going to be one of the top 5 single white grape varieties made into single varietal wines globally.  So it would either be Chardonnay, Riesling, Sauvignon Blanc, Pinot Gris/Grigio, or MAYBE Muscat or Chenin Blanc.  Because the question asks about style, quality AND maturity across four wines, they probably want a grape that can express itself in radically different ways depending on climate and winemaking -- unoaked vs. oaked, lean vs. rich, young vs. aged.  That really points to Chardonnay or Riesling.  You can rule out Riesling pretty quickly because four Rieslings would likely all show high acidity and aromatic character, and it's harder to get the style diversity they're looking for with Riesling (though you could argue Alsace vs. Mosel vs. Australia).  Sauvignon Blanc could work but it doesn't age as well and the maturity angle is harder to test.  Chardonnay is the most likely answer because it's the most versatile white grape -- you can have unoaked Chablis sitting next to oaked Margaret River and they taste like completely different wines.  The maturity question also suggests at least one wine with real age on it, which again points to Chardonnay since it ages beautifully in both Burgundy and the New World.  In this case it was all Chardonnay -- spanning Chablis, California, South Africa and Australia, which gives you the full spectrum from cool-climate unoaked mineral to warm-climate oaked and rich.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference -- DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - "same single grape variety" across four wines -- points to a major international variety
  - "style, quality, and maturity" asked for each wine -- they want a grape that shows dramatically different styles and has aging capacity
  - 16 marks for variety ID is significant -- they really want you to get this right
  - Four wines is a large flight for one variety, suggesting a grape grown globally in many climates
  - Paper 1 = whites, so we're in the white grape universe

- Universe I considered:
  - Chardonnay -- most versatile, grown everywhere, ages well, shows oak/no-oak diversity
  - Riesling -- aromatic, ages well, but harder to show the range of winemaking styles
  - Sauvignon Blanc -- international but less versatile in style, ages less well
  - Pinot Gris/Grigio -- possible but rarely the subject of a 4-wine single-variety flight
  - Chenin Blanc -- interesting but less globally planted, mostly Loire and South Africa
  - Muscat -- too aromatic and distinctive, unlikely for a 4-wine flight

- Rule-outs:
  - Riesling: always shows its aromatic character regardless of origin, hard to get 4 truly different styles; also the maturity question works less well since aged Riesling develops petrol/kerosene which is pretty unmistakable
  - Sauvignon Blanc: doesn't age as gracefully across diverse origins, and 4 Sauvignons would be a lot of similar aromatic profiles
  - Pinot Gris: not enough global diversity at the quality level IMW would want to test
  - Chenin: only really two countries (France and South Africa) make it at volume, hard to fill 4 slots
  - Muscat: too obviously aromatic, you'd identify it immediately

- Conclusion: Chardonnay is the overwhelmingly likely answer. The flight is designed to test whether you can distinguish unoaked cool-climate (Chablis), moderate-climate with some oak (Monterey/Elgin), and full-bodied warm-climate oaked (Margaret River) expressions. The 2010 Chablis adds the maturity dimension -- can you tell the difference between a 15-year-old unoaked Chardonnay and a young oaked one?
