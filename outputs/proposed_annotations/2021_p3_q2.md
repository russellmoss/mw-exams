# Proposed annotation for 2021 Paper 3 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 4-8 are all made using different methods of production. a) Describe the method of production with reference to residual sugar and alcohol of each wine (5x10) b) Identify the origin and variety of each wine (5x10) c) Comment on the style and quality in context of origin (5x[remaining marks])

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W4: Amarone, Torre del Falasco, 2015. Valpolicella. (16%)
- W5: Brachetto d'Acqui, Contero, 2020. Piedmont. (5.5%)
- W6: Graham's Vintage Port, 2000. Douro. (20%)
- W7: Maury Grande Reserve, Pouderoux, NV. Roussillon. (15.5%)
- W8: Rutherglen Muscat Classic, Stanton & Killeen, NV. Victoria. (18.5%)

## Proposed annotation

This is a classic Paper 3 Q2 format: five wines, each made by a completely different production method, and the examiner wants you to identify the method first and variety/origin second. The "different methods" stem on a five-wine flight basically guarantees you're going to see a range of techniques for achieving sweetness and/or high alcohol — dried grape, arrested fermentation (port), vin doux naturel, and botrytis or some equivalent.

The five methods here are: appassimento/drying (Amarone — dry, no RS, but high alcohol from concentrated sugar), interrupted fermentation via CO2-sparging or carbonation (Brachetto d'Acqui — lightly sparkling, sweet, low alcohol), mistelle/mutage (Graham's Vintage Port — grape spirit added during fermentation to arrest it), mutage-style VDN (Maury — same principle but using Grenache Noir in Roussillon), and partial fermentation/fortification with very high RS (Rutherglen Muscat — fortified, heavily oxidative, syrup-like).

The Brachetto is the curveball — 5.5% alcohol, frizzante, sweet red. It's unmistakably aromatic, floral, and low-ABV. The Rutherglen Muscat at 18.5% is the extreme opposite. The Amarone at 16% is the highest-ABV dry wine in the group and should show dried cherry, tar, and the tell-tale pruney/figgy character of appassimento.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - "All different methods of production" + RS and alcohol specifically asked = each wine uses a distinct winemaking technique to achieve its alcohol/sweetness profile
  - Five wines = the examiner has enough to cover the full spectrum from dry/high-ABV to sweet/low-ABV
- Universe I considered:
  - Appassimento (Amarone/Recioto): STRONG SIGNAL
  - Port (fortified mid-fermentation): STRONG SIGNAL
  - VDN/Maury/Banyuls: PLAUSIBLE alongside Port
  - Rutherglen Muscat: classic Paper 3 inclusion, very distinctive
  - Low-alcohol sweet red (Brachetto, Lambrusco): PLAUSIBLE curveball
- Rule-outs:
  - Botrytis wines: not in this flight (Sauternes/TBA would typically appear in a sweet whites question)
  - Icewine: not represented here
- Conclusion: Five methods confirmed. The Brachetto is the hardest to place for most candidates — knowing it's carbonated/interrupted fermentation rather than mistelle is a specific technical point.
