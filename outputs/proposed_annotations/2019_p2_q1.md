# Proposed annotation for 2019 Paper 2 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-4 are made from Bordeaux varieties.

For each wine:

a) Identify the origin and grape variety(ies). (4 x 10 marks)

b) Comment on the quality in context of the origin, with reference to maturity and the potential for development. (4 x 10 marks)

c) Identify the key winemaking techniques used. (4 x 5 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W1: Château de la Grenière, 2012. Lussac-Saint-Émilion, Bordeaux, France. (13.5%)
- W2: Vigna d'Alceo, Castello dei Rampolla, 2012. Tuscany, Italy. (13.5%)
- W3: Saumur-Champigny Le Prince, Domaine de Rocheville, 2014. Loire, France. (13%)
- W4: "V", Vergelegen, 2012. Stellenbosch, South Africa. (14.5%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of Bordeaux grape varieties grown outside of Bordeaux, which is a well-established MW exam theme. The "Bordeaux varieties" clue means you know the varieties before you start — Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Cabernet Franc, Petit Verdot, Malbec — but you still have to sort out which is dominant in each wine and where each wine comes from. That's the real challenge here.

The universe is big: you could have Right Bank Bordeaux (Merlot), Left Bank (Cab Sauv), Loire Cab Franc, Super Tuscan Cab Sauv, South Africa Bordeaux blend, Napa, Margaret River, etc. The way to navigate this is by style: Merlot-dominant right-bank Bordeaux will be soft and plummy with brick edge at 7 years; a Super Tuscan Cab Sauv will be dense with fine-grained tannins and more structural backbone; Loire Cab Franc (Saumur-Champigny) will be lighter, higher acid, more herbaceous; and a Stellenbosch blend will be big, warm and 14.5% ABV.

The 14.5% on W4 is an immediate flag for New World / warm climate — probably South Africa or Napa. Part b) asks specifically about maturity and development potential, so you need to know your vintages and assess from the glass.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - "Bordeaux varieties" — narrows the variety list immediately
  - Origin and variety both in part a) at 10 marks — significant
  - Part b) explicitly cites maturity and development — vintage knowledge matters
  - Part c) winemaking is only 5 marks — brief identification is enough
- Universe I considered:
  - France: Bordeaux right bank (Merlot), Loire Cab Franc (Chinon, Saumur-Champigny, Bourgueil), Bordeaux left bank
  - Italy: Super Tuscan Cab Sauv or Cab Franc blend
  - South Africa: Cape Bordeaux blend (Stellenbosch)
  - New World: Napa Cab, Margaret River, Coonawarra
- Rule-outs:
  - With four wines and the need for variety across the flight, you probably get one light Loire Cab Franc (W3), one right-bank Bordeaux (W1, satellite level given modest quality signals), one Super Tuscan (W2, dense Cab Sauv-led), one New World big blend (W4 at 14.5% — South Africa fits perfectly)
  - Napa possible for W4 but Vergelegen "V" is an iconic Cape Bordeaux and an examiner favourite
- Conclusion: Lussac-Saint-Émilion Merlot (W1), Super Tuscan Cab Sauv (W2), Saumur-Champigny Cab Franc (W3), Stellenbosch Cab Sauv-led blend (W4)
