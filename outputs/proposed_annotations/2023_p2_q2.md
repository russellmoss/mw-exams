# Proposed annotation for 2023 Paper 2 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 5-8 are a mixed bag.

For each wine:

a) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (4 x 7 marks)

b) Comment on the method of production with specific reference to the use of oak. (4 x 8 marks)

c) Comment on quality and commercial position. (4 x 10 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W5: Shiraz, Yellow Tail, 2021. South Eastern Australia. (13.5%)
- W6: Rioja Gran Reserva Imperial, CVNE, 2016. Rioja, Spain. (14.0%)
- W7: Margaux, Château Rauzan-Ségla, 2005. Bordeaux, France. (13.5%)
- W8: Grenache Samuel's Collection Bush Vine, Yalumba, 2019. Barossa, Australia. (14.0%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your ability to navigate a deliberately wide range of quality and commercial positioning within red wine, with oak use as the key differentiating factor. The "mixed bag" stem is the examiner's way of saying "these wines have nothing in common except that they're red and they involve oak in different ways." The fact that oak is specifically called out in part b suggests they've chosen wines that represent very different oak philosophies — from no meaningful oak to extended American and French barrel ageing.

The quality spread here is the whole point. Yellow Tail is intentionally the commercial commodity reference at one end of the quality spectrum, while a Rauzan-Ségla Margaux 2005 is at the very top. The CVNE Imperial Gran Reserva is a traditional Spanish fine wine with integrated extended oak. The Yalumba Grenache is somewhere in the middle — premium for its category, polished, old-vine bush vines with older oak. You need to be able to say plainly that Yellow Tail has limited quality ceiling and is sold at massive volume through supermarkets globally, while Rauzan-Ségla is a classified growth with auction market relevance and investment potential. Origin identification is worth less (7 marks) than quality/commercial position (10 marks), which signals that the examiner is more interested in whether you understand what each wine represents commercially.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - "mixed bag" = deliberately heterogeneous flight, no connective tissue between wines
  - "specific reference to the use of oak" at 8 marks = oak is the core technical thread
  - Origin worth only 7 marks = commercial/quality discussion is primary

- Universe I considered:
  - A "mixed bag" of reds could be anything. With oak as the focus you'd expect: a heavily oaked classified growth, a traditional old-world wine with extended maturation, a new-world commercial wine, possibly something with no or minimal oak

- Rule-outs:
  - The question rules nothing out by definition — that's the point of "mixed bag"

- Conclusion: Yellow Tail (commercial/minimal meaningful oak), CVNE Imperial Gran Reserva (traditional Spanish oak maturation), Rauzan-Ségla Margaux 2005 (new French oak, fine wine), Yalumba Grenache (older oak, premium regional)
