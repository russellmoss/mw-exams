# Proposed annotation for 2017 Paper 2 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 5-8 are from the same country but from different regions. a) ID origin and variety (4x12) b) Quality in context of origin (4x7) c) Maturity (4x6)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W5: Montepulciano d'Abruzzo, La Piuma, 2015. Abruzzo. (13%)
- W6: Chianti Classico Riserva Rancia, Felsina, 2012. Tuscany. (14.5%)
- W7: Barolo, Pio Cesare, 2012. Piemonte. (14%)
- W8: Etna Rosso Archineri, Pietradolce, 2014. Sicily. (15%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of Italian red wine regions and their key grape varieties. Same country, four different regions — the country almost has to be Italy. France could work (Burgundy/Bordeaux/Rhône/Beaujolais) but Italy is the only country with four genuinely distinct regional reds that would each come from a different variety. Spain could theoretically work but the regions aren't as distinctive varietally as Italy's.

The four wines here represent the entire Italian red spectrum: a commercial, entry-level Montepulciano d'Abruzzo; a serious Chianti Classico Riserva from a benchmark Tuscany producer; a Barolo from one of Piemonte's established houses; and an Etna Rosso which is the curveball. The Etna Rosso (Nerello Mascalese) is the most challenging — pale colour, very high ABV (15%), high tannin and acid, which is a confusing combination. The candidate needs to know that Sicily's Mount Etna produces this paradoxical style. The other three should be more accessible to a well-prepared MW candidate.

Origin and variety together for 12 marks each means you really need both — getting "Italian red" but not the region/grape won't score well. Maturity for 6 marks means they want you to assess where each wine is in its drinking window, which tests vintage knowledge and the different aging trajectories of Montepulciano (drink young) vs. Barolo (needs more time) vs. the 2012s vs. 2014.
