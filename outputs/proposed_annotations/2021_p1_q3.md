# Proposed annotation for 2021 Paper 1 Question 3

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 8 and 9 are from the same producer, the same grape variety and the same region of origin. a) Identify the variety and region (30) b) Compare the quality, maturity, winemaking and market position of each wine (20)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W8: FX Pichler Riesling Burgstall Federspiel, 2018. Wachau. (12.5%)
- W9: FX Pichler Riesling Kellerberg Smaragd, 2018. Wachau. (13.5%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of the Wachau classification system and what it means in the glass. The "same producer, same variety, same region" stem is really specific — it's essentially telling you the two wines will differ only in site quality and ripeness tier. That combination points pretty directly at a region with an internal quality hierarchy based on natural alcohol, which in Paper 1 white wines means either Alsace (Grand Cru vs. village) or the Wachau (Federspiel / Steinfeder / Smaragd).

The Riesling ID should be fairly straightforward — FX Pichler makes very pure, technically precise Riesling, the Wachau style is dry, no botrytis, high acidity, mineral. What the examiner really wants here is the comparison: W8 is a Federspiel (12.5%, lighter, earlier drinking, named after a falcon kept by local hunters) vs. W9 which is Smaragd (13.5%, richer, site-driven from Kellerberg, built to age). Both are 2018, so vintage isn't the variable.

The market position piece matters too — FX Pichler Smaragd commands serious prices internationally, whereas the Federspiel is a more accessible entry. They want you to articulate why the same producer in the same region can occupy two very different commercial tiers.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - "Same producer, same variety, same region" = pure hierarchy question, style/quality within one producer's range
  - Two wines only, so the contrast must be really clear
  - Paper 1 (white still), no mention of blending
- Universe I considered:
  - Wachau Riesling: Federspiel vs Smaragd is the classic comparison
  - Alsace Grand Cru vs AOC: plausible, but less codified in terms of alcohol floors
  - Burgundy premier cru vs village: would likely involve Chardonnay, possible
  - Mosel GG/Kabinett: less likely as "same region" could work but Germany's hierarchy is more vintage-dependent
- Rule-outs:
  - Any blend-dominant region: stem says "same variety"
  - Any region without a visible internal ripeness/site hierarchy
- Conclusion: Wachau Riesling confirmed. The Federspiel/Smaragd comparison is an MW classic and FX Pichler is the benchmark producer.
