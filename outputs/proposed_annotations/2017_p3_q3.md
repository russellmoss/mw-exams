# Proposed annotation for 2017 Paper 3 Question 3

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 5-6 are labelled as different single grape varieties. a) ID variety (2x8) b) RS level g/l and alcohol % (2x4) c) Quality/commercial potential (2x13)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W5: Riesling, Giesen, 2015. NZ. (10%)
- W6: Gewürztraminer Late Harvest, Husch, 2014. Anderson Valley, California. (11%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of aromatic white varieties, and specifically your ability to assess residual sugar levels and sweetness by taste. The RS sub-question (4 marks each) is a strong signal that the wines sit at different sugar levels and the examiner wants you to quantify them — or at least place them in a band (dry, off-dry, medium-sweet, sweet). The low marks on RS/ABV suggest they want a numerical estimate or clear category, not an essay.

The variety identification (8 marks each) is very achievable here. Riesling and Gewürztraminer are two of the most recognizable varieties in the world. Riesling's lime/petrol/high acid profile and Gewürztraminer's lychee/rose/turkish delight character are about as distinct as white grapes get. The question is probably harder on the origin side — you'd need to know that a commercial, off-dry NZ Riesling at 10% exists (Giesen is one of NZ's biggest commercial Riesling producers) and that Anderson Valley in California's Mendocino makes cool-climate late harvest styles.

The quality and commercial potential (13 marks each) is the real focus. The Giesen Riesling is a commercial, off-dry entry-level New World Riesling — accessible, supermarket tier, moderate quality. The Husch Late Harvest Gewurztraminer is a sweet wine from a small California AVA — premium tier but very niche. These are very different commercial propositions: one is a volume wine for the everyday market, the other is a specialty product for wine enthusiasts and restaurant dessert lists.
