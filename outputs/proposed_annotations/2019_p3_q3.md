# Proposed annotation for 2019 Paper 3 Question 3

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 5-8 are from four different countries (two Old World and two New World), and are made from four different predominant varieties.

For each wine:

a) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (4 x 6 marks)

b) Comment on the method of production. (4 x 7 marks)

c) Comment on the style, quality, and commercial positioning. (4 x 12 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W5: Rosé, Château Miraval, 2018. Côtes de Provence, France. (13%)
- W6: Rosado, Cune, 2018. Rioja, Spain. (13.5%)
- W7: Rosé of Pinot Noir, Rodney Strong, 2018. Russian River Valley, California. (12.5%)
- W8: Rosé, Kim Crawford, 2018. New Zealand. (13%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of rosé wines from four different countries, and the ability to distinguish styles within a category that can look superficially similar in the glass. The "two Old World, two New World" clue and "four different predominant varieties" tells you each wine is a different grape variety, so you're differentiating by colour shade, aromatics, body and ABV as well as by production method.

The two Old World wines are Provence (Grenache/Cinsault/Syrah blend, pale salmon, dry, restrained herb-mineral character, classic direct press) and Rioja Rosado (100% Tempranillo, deeper pink, more body and fruit weight, slightly earthy/rustic, also direct press with short skin contact). The two New World wines are California Pinot Noir rosé (Russian River, cool-climate, pale, delicate red fruit, 12.5%) and New Zealand rosé (Marlborough/multi-regional, commercial, fruit-forward, clean 13%). 

The commercial positioning question is worth 12 marks per wine — the highest per-wine allocation — which suggests the examiner wants genuine market analysis, not just "dry and sold in restaurants." Miraval is high-profile celebrity-backed premium Provence rosé; Cune is value-for-money Spanish rosado for the export market; Rodney Strong is quality mid-tier US rosé for the domestic on-trade; Kim Crawford is commercial volume New Zealand rosé, mostly US off-trade.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - "Four different predominant varieties" in four different countries — no two wines share a grape
  - Two OW, two NW — structure helps you organize the flight immediately
  - Commercial positioning = 12 marks per wine — the biggest mark allocation per wine per task
  - Origin = only 6 marks — being roughly right is enough (country + region)
- Universe I considered:
  - OW rosé: Provence (Grenache-Cinsault blend), Rioja Rosado (Tempranillo), Tavel (Grenache), Navarra, Bardolino Chiaretto
  - NW rosé: California (Pinot Noir, Zinfandel, Syrah), New Zealand, South Africa, Argentina
- Rule-outs:
  - Tavel would be fuller and often has more structure than standard Provence — probably not used alongside Miraval
  - South Africa rosé is possible but less typical at this level in the MW corpus
  - Four different varieties rules out two Grenache-based OW wines being used together
- Conclusion: Provence rosé blend (W5), Rioja Tempranillo Rosado (W6), Russian River Pinot Noir rosé (W7), NZ commercial rosé (W8)
