# Proposed annotation for 2016 Paper 3 Question 4

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 11 and 12 come from the same region and producer.

With reference to both wines:

a) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (20 marks)

b) Discuss the key winemaking techniques used to arrive at these styles. (20 marks)

c) Discuss how the styles of these wines determine their commercial appeal. (10 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W11: Valpolicella Ripasso Superiore, Campi Magri, Corte Sant'Alda, 2012. Veneto, Italy. (13.5%)
- W12: Recioto della Valpolicella, Corte Sant'Alda, 2013. Veneto, Italy. (14.5%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of the dried-grape wine tradition in Valpolicella and specifically the appassimento method. "Same region and producer" with two wines that clearly differ in style is a beautifully structured question — the examiner is asking you to figure out the origin once (worth 20 marks, reflecting the difficulty) and then contrast the winemaking approaches to produce a dry wine (Ripasso) and a sweet wine (Recioto) from the same producer and region.

The universe for "same region, same producer, different styles" on Paper 3 could theoretically be: Valpolicella (Ripasso vs Amarone, or Ripasso vs Recioto), Alsace (same producer Vendange Tardive vs Sélection de Grains Nobles), or Douro (dry table wine vs Port). In this case, with a dry and a sweet red, Valpolicella is the most distinctive fit, because the Ripasso technique — refermentation on dried Amarone/Recioto pomace — is unique to this region and gives a very specific tasting profile.

The winemaking question at 20 marks is the heart of this: Ripasso = standard Valpolicella (Corvina-led) refermented on the lees/pomace of Recioto or Amarone, picking up extra body, tannin, and dried-grape richness while remaining dry; Recioto = full appassimento of grapes on bamboo racks (arele) for 3–4 months, then fermentation stopped with residual sugar retained, resulting in a sweet, intensely concentrated red with bitter-cherry and chocolate. Both dry and sweet emerge from the same appassimento tradition but diverge completely in sweetness level.

Commercial appeal: Ripasso is the gateway product — richer than basic Valpolicella but more approachable and affordable than Amarone, very commercially successful. Recioto is a niche sweet red for dessert and cheese pairings, less commercially prominent, but loved by collectors.
