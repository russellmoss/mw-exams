# Proposed annotation for 2023 Paper 2 Question 3

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 9-12 are each made from a single grape variety. Two different grape varieties are represented. Wines 9 and 10 are from one country; wines 11 and 12 are from another country.

For each wine:

a) Identify the grape variety. (4 x 8 marks)

b) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (4 x 7 marks)

c) Comment on style, quality, and commercial position. (4 x 10 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W9: Nuits-Saint-Georges Vieilles Vignes, Robert Chevillon, 2019. Burgundy, France. (13.5%)
- W10: Cahors "Probus", Clos Triguedina, 2018. Cahors, France. (15.0%)
- W11: Pinot Noir, Humberto Canale, 2021. Rio Negro, Argentina. (13.5%)
- W12: Malbec, Catena Alta, 2018. Mendoza, Argentina. (14.0%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your ability to identify two grape varieties — Pinot Noir and Malbec — across two countries, France and Argentina. The stem is very helpfully structured: two grapes, two countries, two wines each. Once you figure out that it's Pinot Noir and Malbec, the pairing becomes obvious. Pinot Noir is the most likely candidate for the lighter, cooler-climate French wine (NSG Vieilles Vignes) and the lighter Patagonian wine. Malbec is the grape for both the Cahors and the Mendoza.

The interesting intellectual challenge here is recognizing Malbec in two very different expressions: Cahors Malbec (called Côt or Malbec, dark, tannic, quite rustic or old-school in style, 15% ABV in this case) versus Mendoza Malbec (polished, violet-scented, premium, high-altitude freshness). They taste quite different. Similarly, NSG Pinot Noir reads richer and more structured than the Humberto Canale Patagonian Pinot. The France-Argentina pairing is the examiner pointing to Malbec's historical home (Cahors) vs. its modern home (Argentina), which is a fantastic educational device. If you know that Malbec originated in southwest France and is the signature grape of both Cahors and Mendoza, you can get four wines in one go.

Quality/commercial spread here goes from entry-premium Humberto Canale Patagonian Pinot up to Catena Alta and Chevillon NSG — all serious, but very different price points and audiences.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - Two grapes, two countries = a paired structure
  - Single varieties only = no blends
  - Grape identification worth 8 marks each = getting the grape right is critical

- Universe I considered:
  - Two grapes that each appear in two countries: Pinot Noir (France + Argentina/NZ/US/Germany), Malbec (France/Cahors + Argentina), Syrah/Shiraz (France + Australia), Cab Sauv (France + New World)
  - The pairing of France and Argentina points strongly at Pinot/Malbec

- Rule-outs:
  - Cab Sauv from France (Bordeaux) + Argentina is possible but stylistically the distinction would be harder to engineer
  - Syrah from France + Argentina is pretty unlikely (Argentina has little Syrah profile at the premium end)

- Conclusion: Pinot Noir (France/Burgundy + Argentina/Patagonia) and Malbec (France/Cahors + Argentina/Mendoza)
