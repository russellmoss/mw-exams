# Proposed annotation for 2022 Paper 2 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 5 and 6 are from the same region but different sub-regions. a) Identify the sub-region of each wine. (2x10) b) Comment on the key winemaking decisions for each wine. (2x5) c) Comment on the style and quality of each wine. (2x10)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W5: Chianti Classico Brolio, Barone Ricasoli, 2017. Tuscany, Italy. (13.5%)
- W6: Brunello di Montalcino Montosoli, Altesino, 2015. Montalcino, Tuscany, Italy. (14.5%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of Tuscany's two most important red wine sub-regions and the hierarchy between them. "Same region, different sub-regions" almost always means the examiner wants you to work within a famous wine region and distinguish the sub-appellations — in this case Chianti Classico vs Brunello di Montalcino, both built on Sangiovese.

The key identifier for wine 5 is the Sangiovese signature at a slightly lower intensity: bright red cherry, dried herbs, iris, medium tannin and Chianti's characteristic lifted acidity. Note that Brolio is 80% Sangiovese plus some Merlot and Cab Sauv — a typical modern Chianti Classico blend. Wine 6 should be more serious in every dimension: 100% Sangiovese, with a deeper, more concentrated expression, broader tannic architecture and more obvious longevity. The ABV difference (13.5% vs 14.5%) and the vintage (2017 vs 2015) both push wine 6 further into power and extraction.

The winemaking sub-question (b) is worth knowing: Chianti Classico at this level gets tonneaux or Slavonian cask at a shorter aging period; Brunello di Montalcino by DOCG law requires minimum 2 years in wood and lengthy total aging. Getting those regulatory details correct is easy marks. Style and quality (c) should center on the clear step-up in intensity, complexity and aging potential that distinguishes a Montalcino single-vineyard Brunello from a sound if polished Chianti Classico.
