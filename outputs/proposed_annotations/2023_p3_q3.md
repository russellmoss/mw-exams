# Proposed annotation for 2023 Paper 3 Question 3

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 8 and 9 are from the same region.

With reference to both wines:

a) Identify the origin and grape variety/ies. (14 marks)

b) Compare the production methods of the two wines, identifying the key factors that have influenced the style. (16 marks)

Then for each wine:

c) Comment on quality and commercial potential. (2 x 10 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W8: Furmint Tokaji, Dubogó, 2019. Örökya, Tokaj, Hungary. (13.5%)
- W9: Tokaji Aszú 5 Puttonyos, Disznokő, 2013. Tokaj, Hungary. (12.0%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of Tokaj and the ability to distinguish a dry Furmint still wine from a classic Tokaji Aszú sweet wine, both from the same region. The "same region" stem combined with Paper 3 (the special paper) strongly suggests a wine region that makes both a dry and a sweet or fortified style from the same grapes. The obvious candidates for this setup are: Tokaj (dry Furmint vs Aszú), Jerez (fino vs oloroso), Sauternes/Graves (dry white vs botrytised sweet), Alsace (dry vs Sélection de Grains Nobles), and to a lesser extent Germany (dry vs Prädikat sweet).

Tokaj is actually the best fit here because: the dry style (Furmint) has risen in prominence recently and is a legitimate fine wine in its own right, and the Aszú is one of the most famous sweet wines in the world. Having both in the glass from the same region is a great educational pairing. The comparison of production methods is where the marks really are — 16 marks is the biggest single chunk in this question. You need to explain how dry Furmint is made (normal dry white winemaking, maybe some skin contact or lees ageing) versus how Aszú is made (hand-selecting botrytised berries, the paste or berry-addition method, long ageing in Gönci barrels under partial oxidation). These are very different processes despite sharing the same grape and region.

Commercial potential is also interesting — dry Furmint is a growth category, increasingly on fine wine lists globally, while Aszú remains relatively niche outside specialist and sweet wine circles.
