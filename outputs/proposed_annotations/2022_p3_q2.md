# Proposed annotation for 2022 Paper 3 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 5-9 are all from different countries and different predominant grape varieties. a) Identify the grape variety and origin of each wine. (5x10) b) Comment on the key winemaking decisions with reference to the residual sugar level in each wine. (5x5 plus additional marks) c) [Additional sub-question on quality / commercial appeal]

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W5: Gewürztraminer Single Vineyard, Te Whare Ra, 2020. Marlborough, NZ. (14.0%)
- W6: Riesling Spätlese, Schloss Reinhartshausen, 2015. Rheingau, Germany. (7.0%)
- W7: T Noble Late Harvest Chenin Blanc, Ken Forrester, 2019. Stellenbosch, SA. (11.5%)
- W8: Tokaji Aszú 5 Puttonyos, Sauska, 2017. Tokaj, Hungary. (11.5%)
- W9: Château Coutet, 2016. Barsac, Bordeaux, France. (13.0%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of sweet and off-dry white wines across five different countries, and specifically how each wine achieves its residual sugar. That's actually the organizing principle of the sub-question on winemaking — the examiner wants you to articulate the different mechanisms: arrested fermentation for the Spätlese (yeast die off or fermentation stopped by chilling), botrytis concentration for the Sauternes and the South African Chenin, the Aszú dough maceration technique unique to Tokaj, and probably just a tiny RS from the Gewurztraminer's partial sugar balance.

The five varieties and five countries are: NZ (Gewurztraminer), Germany (Riesling Spätlese), South Africa (Chenin Blanc), Hungary (Tokaj/Furmint), France (Sauternes/Sémillon blend). These are genuinely five distinct styles ranging from slightly off-dry (the Gewurztraminer and arguably the Spätlese) to extremely sweet (the Tokaji and Sauternes).

The easiest to identify should be the Riesling Spätlese at 7% ABV — nothing in the world reads quite like that: low alcohol, high acidity, slate minerality, petrol notes beginning to develop in a 2015. The Gewurztraminer from NZ is unmistakably aromatic (lychee, rose, ginger) at 14%. The Tokaji has that distinctive dried apricot/quince concentration with very high acidity that distinguishes it from Sauternes. The Sauternes is the richest and most glycerol-laden. The South African Chenin NLH is the hardest to place — botrytis, honey, high RS, but the Chenin grape character and the new oak influence are the cues.
