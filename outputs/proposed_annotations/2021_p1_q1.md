# Proposed annotation for 2021 Paper 1 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-4 all come from the same country. a) Identify the origin and variety of each wine (4x10) b) Comment on the quality and maturity of each wine (4x10) c) Describe the method of production of each wine (4x5)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W1: Vin Jaune, Daniel Dugois, 2013. Arbois. (14.5%)
- W2: Corton Charlemagne, Antonin Guyon, 2017. Burgundy. (13.5%)
- W3: Inopia Blanc, Rotem & Mounir Saouma, 2018. CdR-Villages. (14%)
- W4: Pinot Gris, Cave de Hunawihr, 2018. Alsace. (13%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of French white wines across very different regions and styles. The "same country" clue here is doing a lot of work — France is the only country that can plausibly produce four distinct, well-known white wine styles at this quality level. You could theoretically argue for the US (California Chardonnay, Oregon Pinot Gris, NY Riesling…), but the styles would be too similar. Italy only really gives you two or three recognizable whites. So France is pretty much the answer from the stem alone.

From there you need to navigate Burgundy, the Jura, the Rhone, and Alsace in the same flight. Corton Charlemagne should be identifiable — rich, weighty Chardonnay with oak and serious extract. Pinot Gris from Alsace is very distinctive — oily texture, low acidity, phenolic bitterness. The Vin Jaune is the real curveball: oxidative, walnut, curry, high alcohol, very specific to the Jura, and nothing else really tastes like it. The Grenache-based CdR-Villages white (Inopia Blanc) is the hardest — it reads warm, herby, could fool you into thinking it's Viognier or a southern Rhone blend.

Winemaking is where the marks pile up. The Vin Jaune has its own règles — six years and three months in a 228L cask under voile, no topping up. The Corton Charlemagne will have been barrel-fermented with malo. The Pinot Gris likely minimal intervention. The Rhone white its own warm-climate handling.
