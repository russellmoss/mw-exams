# Proposed annotation for 2017 Paper 3 Question 4

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 7-8 are made predominately from the same grape variety. a) Name dominant variety (10) b) ID origin (20) c) Compare styles/market positioning (20)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W7: Cuvée Juveniles, Torbreck, 2014. Barossa. (14.5%)
- W8: CdP, Vieux Lazaret, 2013. Rhône. (14%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of Grenache — specifically the contrast between its New World (Barossa) and Old World (Châteauneuf-du-Pape) expressions in a GSM-style blend. The heavy mark allocation on origin (20) and style/market comparison (20) relative to just 10 for variety tells you that the variety itself is meant to be findable — the real exam is whether you can distinguish where each wine comes from and articulate what makes them different commercially and stylistically.

Grenache is probably the right answer and it's a good test case because it's dominant in both southern Rhône blends and Barossa GSM blends. You might also consider Syrah/Shiraz which is dominant in both regions, but Grenache-dominant blends (Châteauneuf-du-Pape typically 65–70% Grenache, Torbreck Juveniles similarly Grenache-led) are the more natural pairing. At 14.5% vs. 14%, the ABVs are very close, which removes alcohol as an easy differentiator.

The style comparison is where it gets interesting: Old World CdP has the classic garrigue (thyme, lavender, herbs), iron, leather, ripe dark fruit, and minerality from galets roulés soils. The Barossa version will be riper, more jammy, fuller in body, darker fruit, more direct and less savory. Both are full-bodied serious reds but one reads as Old World terroir-expressive and the other as New World warm-climate fruit-driven. Market positioning is also quite different: CdP has global restaurant and fine wine credibility; Barossa GSM from Torbreck sits in the premium New World segment targeting wine enthusiasts and high-end restaurants.
