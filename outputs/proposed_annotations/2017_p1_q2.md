# Proposed annotation for 2017 Paper 1 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 4-5 are made from the same grape variety. a) ID variety (16) b) ID origin (2x6) c) Quality/maturity in context (2x6) d) What...

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W4: Pinot Gris, Charles Schleret, 2007. Alsace. (14%)
- W5: Pinot Gris, Neudorf, 2015. Nelson, NZ. (14%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of Pinot Gris across different climates and origins. With two wines from the same variety, and Paper 1 being white still wines, the variety has to be something that is made as a single-varietal wine in multiple countries at a decent quality level. The top candidates are Chardonnay, Riesling, Pinot Gris/Grigio, Sauvignon Blanc, or maybe Chenin Blanc. Variety identification carries the most marks here (16), which tells you the variety is identifiable but requires some work.

The pairing of Alsace 2007 and New Zealand 2015 is a classic contrasting pair. The Alsace is nine years old at tasting — developed, spicy, honeyed, textural, higher alcohol (14%), and likely quite rich. The New Zealand is two years old — fresher, more primary fruit-forward, probably less extraction and aromatic development. Same grape, same ABV (14%), but completely different style profiles. The age difference between the two (2007 vs. 2015) makes maturity assessment a genuine test, not just a formality.

Pinot Gris is probably the most plausible answer over Chardonnay or Riesling because you could have two very distinct styles from these regions — Alsace Pinot Gris has that distinctive smoky, spiced, rich quality whereas New Zealand Pinot Gris trends cleaner and more delicate. Chardonnay would also work but you'd probably expect more oak in both. Riesling from Alsace tends to be higher acid and more linear.
