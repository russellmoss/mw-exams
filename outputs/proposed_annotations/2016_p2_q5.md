# Proposed annotation for 2016 Paper 2 Question 5

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 10-12 come from three different single grape varieties, each closely associated with their origin.

For each wine:

a) Identify the origin as closely as possible, with particular reference to the grape variety used. (3 x 12 marks)

b) Discuss the key winemaking techniques used to arrive at this style. (3 x 6 marks)

c) How would you sell this wine to a potential customer? (3 x 7 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W10: Malbec, Colomé, 2013. Salta, Argentina. (14.5%)
- W11: Blaufränkisch, Moric, 2011. Burgenland, Austria. (13.5%)
- W12: Tributo Carménère, Caliterra, 2013. Colchagua, Chile. (14%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of regional signature grape varieties — grapes that are so associated with one place that variety and origin are essentially one answer. The question even says "closely associated with their origin" which is a direct hint. The universe here is varieties that are effectively defined by their home country or region: Malbec/Argentina, Carménère/Chile, Blaufränkisch/Burgenland, Zinfandel/California, Pinotage/South Africa, Mencia/Galicia, Primitivo/Puglia. On Paper 2 at the end of the flight, three different countries, three distinctive varieties — this is the grab-bag.

The "how would you sell this wine" sub-question is uncommon phrasing but essentially asking for commercial appeal — who buys it, where, at what price tier, through what channel. This rewards knowing the market narrative for each: Argentine Malbec has an easy story (altitude, concentration, great value at entry tier, high-altitude Salta adds premium/boutique narrative); Blaufränkisch is an interesting sell — Austria's signature red, gaining collector interest, pairs well with food, but less consumer recognition requiring education; Carménère is Chile's identity grape with a Bordeaux-lost-variety backstory that's a great pitch to wine-curious consumers.

Moric Blaufränkisch is the hardest identification here — Blaufränkisch is not commonly encountered blind and could be confused with Pinot Noir (pale, acidic, fine tannin) or Dolcetto. The Colomé Salta Malbec at 14.5% is dense and intense, quite different from mid-altitude Mendoza Malbec — a more challenging call. Carménère should be identifiable by its herbaceous-dark-fruit Bordeaux-adjacent profile.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - "Closely associated with their origin" = regional signature varieties
  - Selling question = commercial positioning and market narrative
  - Three different countries

- Conclusion: Malbec/Salta Argentina (Colomé), Blaufränkisch/Burgenland Austria (Moric), Carménère/Colchagua Chile (Caliterra)
