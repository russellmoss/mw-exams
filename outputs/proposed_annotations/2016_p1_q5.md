# Proposed annotation for 2016 Paper 1 Question 5

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 9-12 are from four different countries and are made from four different grape varieties.

For each wine:

a) Identify the grape variety. (4 x 8 marks)

b) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (4 x 7 marks)

c) Comment on quality and state of maturity. (4 x 10 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W9: Crios de Susana Balbo Torrontés, Dominio del Plata, 2015. Argentina. (13.9%)
- W10: Gewürztraminer, Waimea, 2014. Nelson, New Zealand. (13.5%)
- W11: Vouvray, Domaine Marc Brédif, 2013. Loire Valley, France. (12.5%)
- W12: Riesling, Ried Schütt, Dürnsteiner, Emmerich Knoll, 2012. Wachau, Austria. (13.5%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of distinctive aromatic white varieties from four different countries. Four different countries, four different varieties is a classic MW grab-bag question — the examiner is giving you maximum variety signals and testing breadth. The four candidates here are Torrontés (Argentina), Gewürztraminer (New Zealand), Vouvray/Chenin Blanc (France) and Wachau Riesling (Austria).

The trick with this question is that two wines (Torrontés and Gewürztraminer) have overlapping aromatic profiles — both show rose petal, lychee, exotic florals. The key differentiation: Torrontés is bone dry despite the perfume, has higher alcohol (13.9%), and the origin is Argentina; Gewürztraminer is slightly off-dry, oilier texture, more Turkish delight and ginger. A candidate who knows both varieties should be able to split them.

The Vouvray Chenin Blanc is distinctive for its honey-quince-wool character, high acidity, and Loire signature. The Wachau Riesling Smaragd from Emmerich Knoll at 13.5% is the biggest and fullest of the Rieslings you'd encounter — fuller-bodied than Mosel or German counterparts, dry, mineral slate and petrol notes developing at 4 years. Quality and maturity sub-question: Torrontés at 1 year is at peak (it doesn't age), Gewürztraminer at 2 years is fresh, Vouvray at 3 years is drinking well but has more to give, and the Knoll Smaragd at 4 years is just entering its window.
