# Proposed annotation for 2018 Paper 2 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 6-8 are made from the same single grape variety. a) Identify the grape variety (15) b) Identify the origin of each wine (3x10) c) Comment on the quality of each wine with reference to the winemaking (3x10)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W6: Pinot Noir, DuMOL, 2015. Russian River Valley, California. (14.1%)
- W7: Pinot Noir, Felton Road, 2016. Central Otago, New Zealand. (13.5%)
- W8: Reserve Pinot Noir, Argyle, 2015. Willamette Valley, Oregon. (14.1%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of Pinot Noir across New World regions. With three wines from the same single variety, 15 marks on variety ID, and quality/winemaking at 30 marks total, the examiner wants you to really know this grape. For Paper 2 reds, same variety with three wines: the top candidates are Pinot Noir, Cabernet Sauvignon, Syrah, or Tempranillo. But the range of fruit profiles and structural weights you'd get from these three wines — with no particularly tannic or extracted wine in the set — is most consistent with Pinot Noir.

The three wines here cover the canonical New World Pinot triangle: California Russian River (ripe, plush, dark cherry, 14.1%), Central Otago (vivid red cherry, schist-mineral edge, tighter structure), and Willamette Valley (between the two — cooler than California, riper than Burgundy, cranberry and spice). The ABV spread is relatively tight (13.5-14.1%), which means you're reading the fruit and structure more than the alcohol to differentiate. Russian River will be the ripest and most opulent. Central Otago will be the most lifted and mineral. Willamette sits in the middle. The examiner probably chose this trio specifically to test whether you can articulate regional differences within New World Pinot rather than just naming the grape.
