# Proposed annotation for 2022 Paper 2 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-3 are from different countries, each from a different single grape variety. Wine 4 is a blend of all three of these varieties. a) Identify the grape variety and origin of wines 1-3. (3x15) b) Comment on the style of wine 4 and suggest the likely blend components and their proportions. Discuss why the varieties used in wine 4 might be blended together. (45)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W1: Chinon Cuvée Terroir, Charles Joguet, 2019. Chinon, Loire, France. (13.5%)
- W2: Cabernet Sauvignon Double Diamond, Schrader, 2018. Oakville, Napa. (14.5%)
- W3: Merlot, Vino de Eyzaguirre, 2019. Colchagua, Chile. (13.0%)
- W4: Château Larrivet Haut-Brion, 2018. Pessac-Léognan, Bordeaux. (14.0%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of the Bordeaux blend and its three primary red varieties. The question is almost perfectly designed to teach the concept: three single-variety wines from three different countries, then the blended expression. Once you identify wines 1-3 as Cabernet Franc, Cabernet Sauvignon and Merlot, the penny should drop that wine 4 is a Bordeaux blend.

The hardest of the three to identify is probably wine 1 (Cabernet Franc from Chinon) — Cab Franc can be confused with Cab Sauv, especially in a warm year like 2019 in the Loire. The tell is the pyrazines, the earthiness, and the relative freshness and lower body versus a Napa Cabernet. Wine 2 is the richest and most opulent at 14.5%, with the polish and density you'd expect from Oakville Cabernet — a candidate who's tasted Napa should land on Cabernet Sauvignon there. Wine 3 should be softer and plummier than both, with easier tannins — that's the Merlot register.

Part b is where the real marks are (45). You need to describe wine 4 (a Pessac-Léognan in a serious vintage) and then argue why these three varieties work together: Cabernet Sauvignon gives structure, tannin and cassis depth; Merlot adds softness, flesh and earlier approachability; Cabernet Franc contributes lift, aromatics and mid-palate freshness. That's the whole Bordeaux blending logic in one answer.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - Wines 1-3 are single-variety, different countries; wine 4 is a blend of all three
  - Paper 2 = reds — classic Bordeaux blending question
  - Wine 4 being a blend of all three almost certainly points to Bordeaux
- Universe I considered:
  - Three varieties that blend into Bordeaux: Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Cabernet Franc
  - Could potentially be Cabernet-dominated blends from elsewhere (Napa, Chile) but "blend of all three" strongly implies Bordeaux
- Rule-outs:
  - A Malbec or Petit Verdot set would not blend so cleanly into one well-known blend
  - Rioja blend (Tempranillo + Garnacha + Graciano) is possible but rarely described this way
- Conclusion: Cab Franc (Loire), Cab Sauv (Napa), Merlot (Chile) + Bordeaux blend (Pessac-Léognan)
