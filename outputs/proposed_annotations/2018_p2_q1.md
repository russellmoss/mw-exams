# Proposed annotation for 2018 Paper 2 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-5 all come from classic Western European origins. a) Identify the origin and grape variety or varieties of each wine (5x10) b) Comment on the quality of each wine in context (5x8) c) What has the winemaker done to maximise the quality of each wine (5x?)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W1: Brunello di Montalcino Riserva Lupi e Sirene, Podere Le Ripi, 2011. Tuscany. (14.5%)
- W2: Chambolle-Musigny, Domaine Roumier, 2014. Burgundy. (13%)
- W3: Côte Rôtie La Landonne, Delas Frères, 2012. Northern Rhône. (13.5%)
- W4: Clos de Sarpe, 2000. Saint-Émilion. (13.5%)
- W5: Baron de Ley Gran Reserva, 2011. Rioja. (14%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of the classic Western European red wine canon. The stem says "classic Western European origins" which immediately narrows you to France, Italy, and Spain as the most likely countries, possibly with Germany or Portugal at the margins. But the mark split — 10 marks for origin AND variety — tells you these are identifiable wines where the examiner expects you to name the grape. So no weird Southern Italian IGT blends here.

What's interesting about this flight is the spread of structural types: you have a 2000 Bordeaux (Merlot-dominant, fully mature, tertiary), a village-level Chambolle Pinot (relatively delicate and perfumed, 13%), a big Brunello Riserva (high tannin, high acid, Sangiovese, 14.5%), a Northern Rhône Syrah with the La Landonne smoke/meat/dark fruit character, and a mature Rioja Gran Reserva with American oak coconut-vanilla. Each of these is highly distinctive if you know the styles. The 2000 Bordeaux vintage adds maturity dimension — that wine is 18 years old by exam date, should show cedar/tobacco/dried fruit evolution. Gran Reserva Rioja at 14% with years of oak is also distinctly itself. The hardest differentiation is probably between the Côte Rôtie (pure Syrah) and the Brunello (pure Sangiovese) if you're unsure — but the tannin and acid structure plus the savory/iron quality of Brunello versus the smoked meat/violet of Syrah should be distinguishable.
