# Proposed annotation for 2017 Paper 1 Question 5

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 11-12 are made from the same grape variety. a) ID origin (2x10) b) ID vintage, capacity to improve (2x8) c) Oak (2x7)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W11: Meursault 1er Cru Les Charmes Dessus, Château de Meursault, 2013. Burgundy. (13.5%)
- W12: Chablis VV Sainte Claire, Brocard, 2014. Burgundy. (12.5%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of Chardonnay across different Burgundian origins, and specifically your ability to distinguish the oaked, richer Côte de Beaune style from the unoaked, linear Chablis style. The sub-questions are very revealing: oak is worth 7 marks per wine, which means the examiner specifically wants you to discuss oak treatment. That's a massive clue that one wine has obvious oak and the other does not. Chablis and Meursault is the classic contrast here — Meursault Premier Cru will have barrel fermentation and French oak ageing, while a quality Chablis at this level should be stainless steel or neutral.

The variety isn't asked directly — but it's clearly Chardonnay, the only grape used in both appellations. Origin is the first and heaviest sub-question (10 marks each), meaning you need to nail not just "France" or "Burgundy" but the specific sub-appellation. The vintage identification sub-question (8 marks each) is interesting because the two wines are actually from different vintages — 2013 Meursault and 2014 Chablis. Both are relatively recent but 2013 Burgundy was a challenging cooler year while 2014 was excellent in Chablis particularly. The capacity to improve aspect tests whether you know that quality Meursault 1er Cru has real aging potential while village-level Chablis is typically consumed earlier.

This question is really about knowing Burgundy's internal geography and winemaking differentiation, not just the grape.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - Same variety, two wines — classic contrast pair
  - Oak sub-question (7 marks each) — one oaked, one unoaked almost certainly
  - Capacity to improve — implies at least one is age-worthy
  - Origin is heaviest sub-question — need sub-appellation specificity
- Universe I considered:
  - Chardonnay (Burgundy — Chablis vs. Côte de Beaune) — STRONG SIGNAL
  - Chardonnay (Burgundy vs. somewhere else, e.g. Macon vs. Meursault) — possible
  - Chardonnay (Old World vs. New World) — possible but oak contrast is then very obvious
  - Riesling (two styles) — possible but oak question less natural for Riesling
- Rule-outs:
  - Sauvignon Blanc rarely oaked at this level
  - Pinot Gris possible but Burgundy's intra-regional contrast is the most exam-like scenario
- Conclusion: Chardonnay — oaked Meursault 1er Cru (Côte de Beaune) vs. unoaked Chablis VV
