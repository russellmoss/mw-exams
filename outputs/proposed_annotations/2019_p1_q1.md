# Proposed annotation for 2019 Paper 1 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-4 are from two different countries. They may be blends or single varieties, but one variety is common to all.

With reference to all four wines:

a) Identify the common grape variety. (20 marks)

For each wine:

b) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (4 x 10 marks)

c) Comment on quality and style with reference to winemaking. (4 x 10 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W1: Sancerre Les Boucauds, Claude Riffault, 2017. Loire, France. (13%)
- W2: Clifford Bay Reserve Sauvignon Blanc, Villa Maria, 2018. Awatere Valley, Marlborough, NZ. (13%)
- W3: Château La Louvière Blanc, 2013. Pessac-Léognan, Bordeaux, France. (12.5%)
- W4: Sauvignon Blanc, The Doctors', 2017. Marlborough, NZ. (9.5%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of Sauvignon Blanc across different climates, countries and winemaking styles. The "two countries" clue combined with "one variety common to all" really narrows this down — you need a variety that is made meaningfully in at least two different countries and also exists as both pure varietal and as part of a blend. That points almost immediately to Sauvignon Blanc (Loire vs Marlborough, with Bordeaux blends also in play) or maybe Chardonnay, but Chardonnay doesn't really make a pale, piercing blanc de Bordeaux so you can rule that out fairly fast.

The curveball here is W3 — Pessac-Léognan Blanc is a Sauvignon Blanc/Sémillon blend, and you need to spot the Sauvignon Blanc thread running through a wine that also has oak, texture and Sémillon breadth. That's the whole point: can you identify the shared variety even when one expression is a lean, stony Loire example and another is a structured, barrel-shaped Bordeaux blanc?

W4 is also interesting because 9.5% ABV should immediately signal something odd — arrested fermentation or deliberate low-alcohol production. That tests whether you can separate grape identity from winemaking decisions, which is exactly what part c) is asking for.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - "Two different countries" — not a single region sweep
  - "May be blends or single varieties, but one variety is common to all" — classic Sauvignon Blanc/Bordeaux blend setup
  - Part a) is worth 20 marks for just naming the variety — that's a generous allocation, suggesting the examiner wants real discussion not just a one-word answer
- Universe I considered:
  - Sauvignon Blanc (Loire, Marlborough, white Bordeaux), Chardonnay, Riesling, Chenin Blanc
- Rule-outs:
  - Riesling: not used in blends the same way; two Riesling countries would more likely be Germany/Alsace
  - Chenin: possible but La Louvière is not Chenin; Pessac-Léognan is definitively Sauvignon Blanc/Sémillon
  - Chardonnay: no Bordeaux blanc connection
- Conclusion: Sauvignon Blanc — Sancerre (W1), Awatere/Marlborough (W2), Pessac-Léognan blend (W3), low-ABV Marlborough (W4)
