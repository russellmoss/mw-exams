# Proposed annotation for 2018 Paper 1 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-4 are all made from the same single grape variety. a) Identify the grape variety (20) b) Identify the origin of each wine (4x10) c) Comment on the quality of each wine with reference to the winemaking (4x10)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W1: Chardonnay [yellow tail], Casella Family Brands, 2017. South East Australia. (13%)
- W2: Chablis Grand Cru Les Preuses, Domaine William Fèvre, 2012. Burgundy. (12.5%)
- W3: Red Shoulder Ranch Chardonnay, Shafer Vineyards, 2015. Napa Valley. (14.9%)
- W4: Hunting Hill Chardonnay, Kumeu River, 2015. Auckland, New Zealand. (14%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of Chardonnay at vastly different quality and style levels. Because the stem says same single variety, you're working through the top single-variety whites: Chardonnay, Riesling, Pinot Gris, Sauvignon Blanc. But the 20-mark variety ID combined with origin and winemaking questions makes this very likely Chardonnay — it's the only white grape grown at serious quality levels in all four corners of the New World and Old World simultaneously.

Once you've landed on Chardonnay, the real exercise is quality discrimination. You have a commercial mass-market SE Australia wine (low acid, soft, no oak signal, 13%), a grand cru Chablis (low ABV, laser acidity, flinty mineral, bone dry, zero oak impression), a big-extraction Napa (14.9% is a dead giveaway — full malo, tropical fruit, toasty oak), and a Burgundian-aspirational Kumeu River (14%, lees complexity, but southern hemisphere fruit). The ABV spread from 12.5% to 14.9% is the most obvious structural range across any four-wine Chardonnay group you're likely to see. The quality ordering is also pretty clear: Chablis GC and Kumeu at the top, Napa premium but very different in style, [yellow tail] as the commercial anchor.
