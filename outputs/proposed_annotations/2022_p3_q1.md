# Proposed annotation for 2022 Paper 3 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-4 are all sparkling rosé wines. Wines 1-2 are from the same country and wines 3-4 are from another country. a) Identify the origin and variety of each wine. (4x10) b) Comment on the key winemaking decisions for each wine. (4x10) c) [Additional sub-question on commercial prospects / market]

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W1: Prosecco Rosé Brut, La Gioiosa, 2020. Veneto, Italy. (11.0%)
- W2: Spumante Rosato Cadelvento, Venturini Baldini, 2020. Lambrusco Reggiano, Emilia-Romagna, Italy. (11.5%)
- W3: Rosé Brut, Bollinger, NV. Champagne, France. (12.0%)
- W4: Brut Rosé, Simonnet-Febvre, NV. Crémant de Bourgogne, France. (12.0%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of sparkling rosé wines and specifically the distinction between tank-method (Charmat) and traditional-method sparkling. All four are rosé, which narrows the grape discussion significantly. The two-country split (wines 1-2 from one country, 3-4 from another) with four sparkling rosés most obviously points to Italy vs France.

The most critical winemaking distinction is Charmat method (wines 1 and 2, both Italian) vs traditional method (wines 3 and 4, both French). That should be legible in the glass: wines 1 and 2 should be fresh, fruity, simple in autolytic character; wines 3 and 4 should show more complexity, biscuit/brioche from lees contact.

Wine 1 is Prosecco Rosé DOC (new designation from 2020) — Glera-led with Pinot Nero for color. Wine 2 is a Lambrusco Spumante Rosato — the curveball here. Most candidates will know Prosecco but Lambrusco as a quality dry sparkling rosé from Emilia-Romagna is less expected. The key tell is that wine 2 should read darker pink with red-berry freshness and high acidity, where wine 1 is paler salmon and more delicate.

On the French side, wine 3 (Bollinger Rosé) should have obvious Pinot Noir richness, vinous depth and the autolytic complexity you expect from a prestige Champagne house. Wine 4 (Crémant de Bourgogne) should be lighter, fresher and less complex than the Champagne, but still clearly traditional-method — a very good value-quality ratio wine that the examiner wants you to assess commercially.
