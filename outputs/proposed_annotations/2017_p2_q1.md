# Proposed annotation for 2017 Paper 2 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-4 are from different countries. They all share a common dominant grape variety. a) ID dominant variety (16) b) ID origin (4x8) c) Key winemaking (4x5)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W1: Château Montrose, 1996. St Estèphe, Bordeaux. (12.5%)
- W2: Oxford Landing Cab/Shiraz, 2014. South Australia. (13.5%)
- W3: Silver Oak Cab Sauv, 2012. Alexander Valley, California. (14.2%)
- W4: Rubicon, Meerlust, 2013. Stellenbosch, SA. (14%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of Cabernet Sauvignon across different countries and quality levels. The stem says same dominant variety, four different countries — in Paper 2 (reds), this is almost certainly one of Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Syrah/Shiraz, Pinot Noir, or Tempranillo. The fact that it's from four different countries makes it most likely Cabernet Sauvignon, which is the only variety genuinely made at volume in France, Australia, USA, and South Africa simultaneously. Syrah could work but is less dominant in Bordeaux. Merlot could work but is less prominent in Australia. Pinot Noir doesn't really work — it's not a dominant variety in South Africa or Australia in the way Cabernet is.

The quality spread here is enormous: a 21-year-old 1996 Classified Growth Bordeaux, an entry-level Australian commercial blend, an American oak-aged premium Californian, and a flagship South African Bordeaux-blend. The variety identification (16 marks) is accessible, but origin at 8 marks each requires getting to country level at minimum. The winemaking at 5 marks each is quite low — so they want a brief, focused answer there, probably just oak regime and fermentation basics.

The 1996 Montrose at 12.5% vs. modern New World examples at 14–14.2% is a deliberate contrast in alcohol and style — the mature Bordeaux structure vs. primary New World fruit is the key discriminator.

- Stem signals I picked up on:

  - Variety worth 16 marks — identifiable but central

- Universe I considered:

  - Pinot Noir — unlikely for four-country spread at this scale
- Rule-outs:
  - Tempranillo: limited to Spain primarily
  - Grenache: less prominent in these four specific countries
- Conclusion: Cabernet Sauvignon — Bordeaux/Australia/California/South Africa
