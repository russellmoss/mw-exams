# Proposed annotation for 2022 Paper 1 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 3-5 are from the same country. a) Identify the origin and grape variety of each wine. (3x10) b) Comment on the quality and maturity of each wine. (3x10) c) Comment on the winemaking of each wine. (3x5)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W3: Riesling Springvale, Grosset, 2021. Clare Valley, Australia. (12.9%)
- W4: Chardonnay, Pierro, 2019. Margaret River, Australia. (13.5%)
- W5: Marsanne Museum Release, Tahbilk, 2014. Nagambie Lakes, Victoria, Australia. (13.0%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of premium Australian white wines. The "same country" stem combined with three different varieties asks you to know which countries make multiple, meaningfully different white varietals at a serious level. That rules out Spain pretty quickly (just Albariño and maybe Verdejo), makes Italy harder unless all three are Italian, and steers you toward Australia, France, or maybe New Zealand. The dead giveaway when tasting should be that two of the three are very distinctly non-French in character.

The plausible countries with 3 different serious whites are France (Chardonnay/Riesling/Chenin, Chardonnay/Viognier/Marsanne, etc.), Australia (Riesling/Chardonnay/Marsanne or Semillon), and maybe the US. What makes this distinctly Australian is the Tahbilk Marsanne museum release, which is a really distinctive wine — very waxy, beeswax, toasty, no oak, aged. That's one of the more recognizable curveballs in Australian wine and should get you to Australia once you've identified it.

The winemaking sub-question (c) is interesting: you've got unoaked Riesling in stainless, barrel-fermented and malo'd Chardonnay from Margaret River, and a museum-released neutral white with bottle development. That's a clever range. Quality and maturity will see the Marsanne as the most developed/aged, the Grosset Riesling as most primary, and the Pierro somewhere in between.
