# Proposed annotation for 2021 Paper 1 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 5-7 are from the same single grape variety. a) Compare the quality and maturity of each wine (36) b) Identify the variety (15) c) Identify the origin of each wine (3x8)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W5: Wally Chenin Blanc, Les Caves de Loire, 2019. Anjou. (12.5%)
- W6: Clos du Papillon, Baumard, 2007. Savennières. (14.5%)
- W7: Botanica Chenin Blanc, 2018. Citrusdal Mountains, SA. (13%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of Chenin Blanc across different terroirs and quality levels. When you see "same single variety" on Paper 1 with the quality/maturity question weighted most heavily (36 marks vs 15 for variety ID), they want you to demonstrate range — not just name the grape. The likely candidates for a question like this are Chardonnay or Riesling because they age and vary hugely by origin. But Chenin is the right answer here, which is less obvious but entirely consistent with the examiner's habit of using Chenin when they want to show dramatic quality and style divergence.

The range is obvious once you taste it: W5 is a basic Anjou at 12.5% — clean, simple, decent fruit, not built for age. W6 is Savennières — serious, dry, mineral, potentially still youthful at 14 years old given the terroir and the 14.5% alcohol. Baumard's Clos du Papillon is one of Savennières' benchmark labels, so even without knowing the exact wine the seriousness should be apparent in the glass. W7 is South African Chenin from Citrusdal Mountains — riper, more tropical, different oak approach, but should still show that distinctive waxy/quince/lanolin Chenin character.

The variety ID is worth only 15 marks, which confirms the examiner cares more about whether you can articulate the quality gap than whether you correctly say "Chenin." Though you absolutely should — it's very identifiable.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - "Same single variety" + quality/maturity marks heavily weighted (36) = grape with wide quality and style range
  - Origin asked separately (3x8) = three different origins, probably at least two countries
- Universe I considered:
  - Chardonnay: most likely candidate
  - Riesling: second most likely, fits the aging/style diversity
  - Chenin Blanc: fits Loire + SA narrative, used before in MW papers (see 2015 P1 Q2)
  - Pinot Gris: less likely for this kind of age diversity
- Rule-outs:
  - Sauvignon Blanc: doesn't age dramatically, less interesting for quality/maturity question
  - Muscat: doesn't produce this kind of tasting range
- Conclusion: Chenin confirmed by actual wines. 2015 P1 Q2 precedent directly relevant here — same Loire-to-SA structure.
