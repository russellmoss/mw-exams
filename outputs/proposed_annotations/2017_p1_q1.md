# Proposed annotation for 2017 Paper 1 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-3 are all from the same country and region. a) ID origin, grape variety/ies (3x11) b) Winemaking (3x8) c) Commercial potential (3x6)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W1: Muscadet Sèvre et Maine, Chéreau, 2015. Loire. (12%)
- W2: Savennières, Château d'Epiré, 2014. Loire. (13.5%)
- W3: Pouilly Fumé, Les Griottes, Bailly, 2015. Loire. (12.5%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of the Loire Valley, specifically that France's Loire produces a range of quite distinct still white wines from different grape varieties across different sub-regions. The stem tells you same country and region — so you need three distinct whites from one country/region. That really narrows it down. France is the most likely country because the Loire is the only wine region in the world that produces three genuinely famous, distinct, single-varietal dry whites: Muscadet (Melon de Bourgogne), Chenin Blanc (Savennières, Vouvray etc.), and Sauvignon Blanc (Pouilly-Fumé, Sancerre). You could potentially get three distinct German whites from different grapes but that's unlikely as they'd probably just be different Rieslings. You can't really do this with Spain, Italy, or the New World.

The winemaking sub-question is interesting here — Muscadet is all about sur-lie ageing (lees contact for texture), Savennières is typically barrel-fermented or oak-aged with quite serious texture and age-worthiness from Chenin, and Pouilly-Fumé is almost always stainless steel with no oak, showcasing the pure varietal aromatics. So the winemaking contrast between the three is genuinely meaningful.

Commercial potential also tests very different stories: Muscadet is the cheapest and most commodity-oriented, Pouilly-Fumé sits in the mid-to-upper tier globally, and Savennières is one of the world's great dry Chenin expressions but commercially niche.

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - Same country AND same region (not just country) — this is key, narrows to a single wine region
  - Paper 1: all still whites

- Universe I considered:
  - Loire (Muscadet/Chenin/Sauvignon Blanc) — STRONG SIGNAL

  - Alsace (Riesling/Pinot Gris/Gewurz/Muscat) — possible but Alsace is sub-regional not regional
  - New Zealand — unlikely, no three distinct major grapes from one NZ region
- Rule-outs:
  - Spain: too few distinct famous regional whites in one region
  - Italy: hard to find three well-known distinct whites from one region
  - Australia: possible (Barossa/Clare Riesling vs. Semillon etc.) but very unlikely
- Conclusion: Loire France — Muscadet, Chenin, Sauvignon Blanc
