# Proposed annotation for 2019 Paper 2 Question 3

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 9-12 come from Europe, but not from France, Italy or Spain.

For each wine:

a) Comment on the quality and commercial potential. (4 x 10 marks)

b) Assess the current state of maturity and the potential for development. (4 x 8 marks)

c) Comment on the possible origin and grape variety(ies). (4 x 7 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W9: Pinot Noir, Maximin Grünhaus, 2015. Mosel, Germany. (13.5%)
- W10: Blaufränkisch, Weingut Heinrich, 2015. Leithaberg, Austria. (13%)
- W11: Quinta da Leda, 2015. Douro, Portugal. (13.5%)
- W12: Villányi Franc, Vylyan, 2015. Villány, Hungary. (13.5%)

## Proposed annotation

This question is less about correctly identifying the exact variety and origin (only 7 marks for that) and more about showing that you understand European reds outside the big three countries. Quality and maturity are the headline tasks. The mark split is deliberately inverted — quality/commercial first, maturity second, origin last — which tells you the examiner expects these wines to be hard to place precisely.

The "not France, Italy or Spain" clue reduces your universe to: Germany, Austria, Portugal, Greece, Hungary, Romania, Georgia, England (reds are possible but unlikely), Switzerland, and the Balkans. In practice, for a well-constructed Paper 2 question, you're probably looking at Germany (Spätburgunder), Austria (Blaufränkisch or Zweigelt), Portugal (Douro or Alentejo), and one curveball from Hungary, Greece or the Balkans. 

The fact that all four wines are 2015 vintage is interesting — same year, which could mean the examiner is testing maturity comparison across four different terroirs and varieties rather than age comparison. Germany's 2015 was warm; Austria's was warm; Douro's 2015 was excellent; Hungary's was fine. All would be approaching a nice drinking window in 2019 but with different trajectories based on grape and structure.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - "Not France, Italy or Spain" — unusual and explicit elimination
  - Quality + commercial = 10 marks, maturity = 8 marks, origin + variety = only 7 marks — origin is least important
  - All four wines are from 2015 — consistent vintage, unusual; may emphasize style/terroir differences over age
- Universe I considered:
  - Germany: Spätburgunder (Pinot Noir) — light body, high acid, slate mineral
  - Austria: Blaufränkisch (peppery, firm tannin, medium body), Zweigelt (lighter, cherry-driven)
  - Portugal: Douro red (Touriga Nacional blend, schist mineral, robust tannins)
  - Hungary: Cab Franc from Villány, Egri Bikavér blend
  - Greece: Xinomavro, Agiorgitiko — possible but less typical exam choice
- Rule-outs:
  - Switzerland: possible but very rarely used in MW exam
  - Balkans: less common at this quality tier
  - Germany Pinot Noir signals: pale, high acid, mineral, low-extraction — not like a traditional big red
  - Douro signals: dark, schist mineral, firm robust structure, indigenous variety complexity
- Conclusion: German Spätburgunder/Pinot Noir (W9), Austrian Blaufränkisch (W10), Douro/Portugal (W11), Hungarian Cabernet Franc from Villány (W12)
