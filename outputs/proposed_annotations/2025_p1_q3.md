# Proposed annotation for 2025 Paper 1 Question 3

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 7-9 are from the same country and different single grape varieties.

For each wine:

a) Identify the origin as closely as possible, making reference to the grape variety. (3 x 10 marks)

b) Comment on the method of production. (3 x 5 marks)

c) Comment on the style, quality, and maturity. (3 x 10 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference -- DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W7: Muscadet Sevre-et-Maine Monnieres Saint-Fiacre l'Ancestrale, Vignobles Gunther-Chereau, 2018. Loire, France. (12%)
- W8: Riesling Silberberg de Rorschwihr, Rolly Gassmann, 2021. Alsace, France. (13.5%)
- W9: Vouvray Clos du Bourg Demi-Sec, Domaine Huet, 2022. Loire, France. (13%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of varietal white wines from the same country.  This narrows it down because there have to be 3 different single white varietal wines that are well known globally from one country.  The most likely candidate is France because France has Chardonnay from Burgundy, Riesling from Alsace, Sauvignon Blanc from Loire, Chenin Blanc from Loire, Pinot Gris from Alsace, and Melon de Bourgogne from Muscadet -- that's more credible options than any other country.  It could be Australia with Riesling, Semillon and Chardonnay.  It could MAYBE be the US with Chardonnay from CA, Sauvignon Blanc from CA and Pinot Gris from Oregon or Riesling from Finger Lakes.  It is unlikely going to be Spain as that would really only be Albarino, white Rioja and maybe sherry.  It is unlikely going to be Italy as it's hard to come up with 3 distinct well-known single varietal whites from there (maybe Pinot Grigio, Verdicchio and...Fiano?).  New Zealand could work with Sauvignon Blanc, Chardonnay and Riesling but the third one is always a stretch.  South Africa could maybe do Chenin Blanc, Sauvignon Blanc and Chardonnay but pretty unlikely.

The question also wants you to comment on method of production which tells you there is probably something interesting about how at least one of these wines is made -- like extended lees ageing, off-dry residual sugar, or something unusual.  Origin is worth the most marks (10 each) so they really want you to nail the region and grape.  In this case it is French: Muscadet from Loire (Melon de Bourgogne with extended lees ageing on a cru communal), Riesling from Alsace (off-dry style typical of Rolly Gassmann) and Chenin Blanc demi-sec from Vouvray.  The Muscadet is the curveball here because a cru communal with 4+ years on lees will taste much richer and more complex than typical Muscadet and could fool you into thinking Chardonnay.
