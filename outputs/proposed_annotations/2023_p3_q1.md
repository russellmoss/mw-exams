# Proposed annotation for 2023 Paper 3 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-4 are traditional method sparkling wines from four different countries. None is from Champagne.

For each wine:

a) Identify the origin and grape variety/ies as closely as possible. (4 x 13 marks)

b) Comment on quality in the context of origin. (4 x 10 marks)

c) State the residual sugar. (4 x 2 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W1: Cava Brut Nature Reserva de la Familia, Juvé y Camps, 2017. Penedès, Spain. (12.0%)
- W2: Brut, Clément Klur, NV. Crémant d'Alsace, France. (12.5%)
- W3: Brut Blanc de Blancs, Nyetimber, 2014. West Sussex, England. (12.0%)
- W4: Riesling Extra Brut, Schloss Reinhartshausen, NV. Marcobrunn, Rheingau, Germany. (13.0%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of traditional method sparkling wines outside Champagne. The "none is from Champagne" instruction is a key stem signal — it means the examiner is specifically testing whether you know what other countries make bottle-fermented sparkling wines and what makes each distinct.

The universe of traditional method sparkling wines outside Champagne is fairly well-defined: Cava (Spain), Crémant (various French regions), English sparkling, Sekt (Germany/Austria), Franciacorta (Italy), Cap Classique (South Africa), sparkling Burgundy, Prosecco (tank method so unlikely here), Trento DOC (Italy), and others. With four wines from four different countries, and the stem pointing to traditional method, you're essentially looking at the most prominent non-Champagne sparkling nations.

The residual sugar question at only 2 marks is a small but specific test — you need to know that Brut Nature is 0-3 g/L, Brut is under 12 g/L, Extra Brut is 0-6 g/L, etc. Getting those categories right matters. Each wine here sits in the dry to very dry zone, which is the modern trend, but they differ in grape variety in interesting ways — the Cava is made from Xarel·lo/Macabeo/Parellada, the Crémant from Alsace varieties (likely Pinot Blanc or Auxerrois), the Nyetimber is Chardonnay-based Blanc de Blancs, and the Sekt is Riesling, which is the most distinctive aromatically and probably easiest to identify. Riesling Sekt should stick out clearly because of the variety's aromatics even under mousse.

- Conclusion: Cava (Spain), Crémant d'Alsace (France), English sparkling (England), German Riesling Sekt — four European countries, one distinctively aromatic variety
