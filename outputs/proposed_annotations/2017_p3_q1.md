# Proposed annotation for 2017 Paper 3 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-3 are rosés from three different countries. a) ID origin re style (3x7) b) Key winemaking (3x8) c) Commercial appeal/positioning (3x10)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W1: White Zinfandel, Sutter Home, NV. California. (9.5%)
- W2: Billette Rosé, Pradel, 2015. Provence. (13%)
- W3: Pelorus Rosé, Cloudy Bay, NV. Marlborough. (12.5%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of the full spectrum of rosé wine styles — from commercial sweet still to dry traditional-method sparkling. Three rosés from three different countries sounds simple but the styles here are radically different: a sweet, low-alcohol Californian White Zinfandel; a dry, bone-dry Provence still rosé; and a traditional-method sparkling rosé from New Zealand.

The commercial appeal sub-question carries the most marks (10 each), which makes sense because these three wines represent completely different commercial universes. White Zinfandel is mass-market, sweet, sold in US supermarkets to entry-level consumers — it's a category unto itself. Provence dry rosé is the current global premium rosé darling, driven by lifestyle marketing and on-trade placement. And sparkling NZ rosé from Cloudy Bay (LVMH-owned) sits in the premium on-trade and gifting segment, positioned more like entry Champagne than commodity pink wine.

The winemaking sub-question (8 marks each) is interesting because all three use completely different techniques: White Zinfandel is made by brief maceration and arrested fermentation; Provence rosé uses direct press; and Cloudy Bay Pelorus is traditional method with secondary fermentation in bottle and lees ageing. That's a very broad winemaking knowledge test packed into one question.

Origin relative to style (7 marks) rewards candidates who can link each wine's palate character to its provenance.
