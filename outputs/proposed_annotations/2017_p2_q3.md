# Proposed annotation for 2017 Paper 2 Question 3

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 9-12 are made from different grape varieties. a) ID variety (4x8) b) ID origin (4x8) c) Quality/commercial potential (4x9)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W9: Chinon, Domaine de la Semellerie, 2014. Loire. (12.5%)
- W10: Spätburgunder, Martin Wassmer, 2014. Baden. (13.5%)
- W11: Pinotage, Kaapzicht, 2013. Stellenbosch. (14.5%)
- W12: Lagrein, Tiefenbrunner, 2014. Alto Adige. (13%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of less-mainstream red grape varieties and your ability to assess their commercial positioning. Four different varieties, and the commercial potential question worth 9 marks each makes this less about identification and more about what happens once you've identified the wine. That said, variety and origin are both worth 8 marks each, so you still need to get those right.

Looking at this group: Cabernet Franc from the Loire (Chinon) is a distinctive, lower-alcohol, herbaceous red that's relatively identifiable once you know it. Spätburgunder from Baden is essentially Pinot Noir — but German Pinot, which is fuller-bodied than Burgundy and has that distinct German precision; the taster needs to decide whether they're calling this Pinot Noir generically or getting to Germany specifically. Pinotage from Stellenbosch is one of the most identifiable reds in the world due to its rubber/acetone note, so the variety identification should be doable if you've ever tasted it. Lagrein from Alto Adige is probably the trickiest — deep inky colour, firm tannins, but very fresh acidity; it could be confused with other dark-fruited Italian reds if you're not familiar with it.

The commercial potential question is interesting because all four of these varieties have quite different global footprints: Cab Franc from Loire has strong Somm-community buzz but limited mainstream reach; German Spätburgunder is increasingly prestigious but still niche; Pinotage is almost exclusively a South African conversation; and Lagrein barely exists outside Alto Adige.
