# Proposed annotation for 2016 Paper 1 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 3 and 4 are from the same country and the same grape variety.

With reference to both wines:

a) Identify the grape variety and origin(s) as closely as possible. (16 marks)

b) Discuss winemaking with particular reference to maturation post fermentation. (14 marks)

c) Comment on quality within the context of the region of origin. (20 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W3: Chardonnay, Clos du Bois, 2014. California, USA. (13.5%)
- W4: Chardonnay, Kistler McCrea Vineyard, 2013. Sonoma Mountain, California, USA. (14.1%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of California Chardonnay at two different quality tiers. The "same country, same variety" stem combined with Paper 1 immediately narrows the universe to the top single-varietal whites made globally at different quality levels. Most likely candidates are Chardonnay (it's made everywhere in the US at wildly different qualities), Riesling (possible but both from same country is tricky), or MAYBE Pinot Gris. Chardonnay is the obvious answer because it's the one variety where you can find a commercial commodity version and a premium terroir version from the same state.

The maturation question is the real focus — it's worth 14 marks and is specifically asking about post-fermentation maturation. That's a clear signal that the examiner wants you to talk about new oak vs old oak, barrel fermentation, malolactic, bâtonnage, lees aging — the California Chardonnay winemaking toolkit. The quality question at 20 marks is the biggest section, so they want you to compare these two wines meaningfully: Clos du Bois is a large-volume commodity California Chardonnay under Constellation, whereas Kistler McCrea is a single-vineyard, allocated, Burgundy-inspired premium bottling. The examiner wants you to articulate why one is better than the other and how that maps to commercial positioning and price.

In blind tasting, both should scream California Chardonnay — full malo, oak-shaped, tropical to stone-fruit driven — but Kistler has much more intensity, concentration, precision, and a higher ABV (14.1%) that signals the elevated Sonoma Mountain site.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - "Same country + same variety" = one variety, two different quality levels within one country
  - "Maturation post fermentation" = oak regime is the main winemaking topic
  - Quality = 20 marks = major mark proportion, meaning quality differentiation is key
- Universe I considered:
  - California Chardonnay (commodity vs premium)
  - German Riesling (Spätlese vs GG — possible but winemaking less distinct)
  - White Burgundy (village vs Grand Cru — possible but "same country" less useful as a limiting clue from France)
- Rule-outs:
  - Riesling: post-fermentation maturation less of a quality differentiator
  - Burgundy: possible but the maturation/quality differentiation question fits California better
- Conclusion: California Chardonnay — commercial (Clos du Bois) vs premium single-vineyard (Kistler McCrea)
