# Proposed annotation for 2019 Paper 3 Question 5

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 11-12 come from two different countries.

For both wines:

a) Compare and contrast the method of production. (14 marks)

For each wine:

b) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (2 x 7 marks)

c) Comment on quality and maturity. (2 x 7 marks)

d) State the level of residual sugar (g/l). (2 x 2 marks)

e) State the level of alcohol (%). (2 x 2 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W11: Ben Ryé Passito di Pantelleria, Donnafugata, 2016. Pantelleria, Sicily, Italy. (14.5%)
- W12: Muscat, Campbells, NV. Rutherglen, Victoria, Australia. (17%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of sweet wine production methods — specifically appassimento (dried grape) production versus fortified solera-style production. The question structure is very precise: they ask you to state RS and alcohol levels to the decimal, which means they want specific knowledge of these wine styles, not vague gestures at "high sugar" and "elevated alcohol."

The two wines are clearly both sweet and both Muscat-based (Zibibbo/Muscat of Alexandria for Pantelleria; Brown Muscat/Muscat Blanc à Petits Grains for Rutherglen), which is an elegant pairing — same aromatic family, totally different production approach and origin. The appassimento method for Pantelleria means drying grapes in the sun on the island, then macerating dried grapes with fresh Zibibbo, and allowing fermentation to arrest naturally — no fortification. The Rutherglen method means late harvest or semi-dried grapes with fortification by grape spirit to arrest fermentation, then long fractional blending in old oak casks.

Part a) comparing the two production methods is 14 marks for both wines combined — the biggest single allocation — so you need to go deep on the contrast between unfortified arrested fermentation vs fortification + solera ageing.

For RS and ABV: Pantelleria is roughly 130+ g/L RS and 14.5%; Rutherglen Muscat is much higher RS (200+ g/L at even base tier) and 17%. The visual and palate differences should be dramatic if you're lucky enough to have both in front of you.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - RS and ABV asked explicitly (unusual) — examiner wants specific numbers, suggesting well-defined wine styles
  - "Compare and contrast production" for 14 marks — the main task
  - "Two different countries" — Italy + Australia would be the pair here
- Universe I considered:
  - Sweet wines from appassimento: Pantelleria (Zibibbo), Amarone-style, Vin Santo
  - Fortified sweet wines: Rutherglen Muscat (Australia), Vintage Port, Madeira, PX Sherry, Muscat de Beaumes-de-Venise
  - Two wines both being Muscat-family is a deliberate choice to highlight production method differences
- Rule-outs:
  - If both are Muscat-based with floral aromatics but different colour and ABV, it narrows immediately to Pantelleria vs Rutherglen
  - Muscat de Beaumes-de-Venise is fortified + French; possible but Rutherglen has higher concentration and tawny colour
  - Vin Santo is Trebbiano/Malvasia not Muscat — different aromatics
- Conclusion: Pantelleria Passito (appassimento, Italy) vs Rutherglen Muscat (fortified solera, Australia)
