# Proposed annotation for 2018 Paper 1 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 5-10 are all made from different single varieties and are from different countries. a) Identify the grape variety of each wine (6x7) b) Identify the origin of each wine (6x8) c) Comment on the quality of each wine with reference to the winemaking (6x10)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W5: Semillon Lovedale, Mount Pleasant, 2011. Hunter Valley, Australia. (10%)
- W6: Viña Gravonia Blanco Crianza, Lopez de Heredia, 2007. Rioja, Spain. (12.5%)
- W7: Ried Lamm Grüner Veltliner 1 STK, Schloss Gobelsburg, 2016. Kamptal, Austria. (13.5%)
- W8: La Rocca Soave Classico, Pieropan, 2015. Veneto, Italy. (13%)
- W9: Old Vine Chenin Blanc, Raats, 2017. Stellenbosch, South Africa. (13.5%)
- W10: Torrontés, Susanna Balbo, 2016. Valle de Uco, Argentina. (13%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of six different single-variety whites from six different countries, which means it's deliberately going beyond the top three or four international varieties. You're going to need to identify some that are genuinely distinctive in the glass. The 42-mark variety/origin block is the bulk of the marks, so getting the variety right is critical even for the lesser-known ones.

The easier identifications are Grüner Veltliner (white pepper, bone dry, Austria — the diagnostic combo) and Torrontés (explosively floral, lychee and rose, Argentina — nothing else smells quite like it). Chenin from South Africa is recognizable if you know the tropical-fruit-heavier Cape style versus the Loire — 13.5% is a big clue that this is not Vouvray. Hunter Valley Semillon at 10% ABV is basically its own category: bone-dry but honeyed and toasty with age, no oak, insanely high acid. Pieropan Soave should show bitter almond and volcanic mineral. The genuinely tricky one is the Lopez de Heredia Gravonia — it's oxidative, amber-coloured, and made from Viura, which barely anyone knows. That one is testing whether you can identify extended wood aging in a Spanish white and make the leap to Rioja Blanco. Pretty unlikely most candidates get that one correct without specific knowledge of the Lopez de Heredia house style.
