# Proposed annotation for 2016 Paper 1 Question 3

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 5 and 6 are made from the same grape variety.

With reference to both wines:

a) Identify the grape variety. (20 marks)

b) Identify the region of origin as closely as possible. (2 x 8 marks)

c) Discuss style and commercial appeal. (2 x 7 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W5: Clos Windsbuhl Pinot Gris, Domaine Zind Humbrecht, 2013. Alsace, France. (14.5%)
- W6: Pinot Gris, Ponzi, 2014. Willamette Valley, Oregon, USA. (13.1%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of Pinot Gris across two different continents. The "same grape variety" stem from two wines on Paper 1 is really asking which single white variety could show up in dramatically different styles. The top candidates for this type of Old World vs New World same-variety question are Chardonnay, Riesling, Pinot Gris/Grigio, Sauvignon Blanc, and MAYBE Chenin Blanc. Pinot Gris is actually a great choice for this because the Alsatian version (rich, full, off-dry, smoky, high ABV) looks almost nothing like the Oregon or Northern Italian version (light, crisp, dry, aromatic but restrained).

The 20-mark variety question front-loaded is a strong signal that the variety itself is identifiable but perhaps tricky — Pinot Gris is not as obvious as Sauvignon Blanc or Riesling, and the examiner wants a decisive call. Getting the variety wrong costs the most marks here.

The style and commercial appeal sub-question is where you compare the two: Zind-Humbrecht Clos Windsbuhl is a premium, biodynamic, monopole-vineyard Alsatian wine at 14.5% — rich, complex, off-dry, long-lived, premium price tier. Ponzi Oregon is medium-bodied, dry, fresh, approachable, good everyday value. The commercial appeal discussion rewards knowing how each style is positioned in market and what consumer each targets.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - Single variety — both wines same grape, different origins
  - Variety worth 20 marks = high-value identification, probably not the most famous variety
  - Style + commercial question = comparing quality positions and market fit
- Universe I considered:
  - Pinot Gris: Alsace vs Oregon or Northern Italy — clear stylistic contrast
  - Riesling: Alsace vs Germany or Austria — possible
  - Chardonnay: would likely specify "same country" too
  - Sauvignon Blanc: Loire vs NZ — possible but Sauv Blanc is very obvious to ID
- Rule-outs:
  - Chardonnay: covered in Q2 of this same paper
  - Gewürztraminer: grown in fewer places, less likely to span two countries dramatically
- Conclusion: Pinot Gris — Alsace (Zind-Humbrecht) vs Oregon (Ponzi Willamette Valley)
