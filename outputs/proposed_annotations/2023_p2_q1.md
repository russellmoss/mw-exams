# Proposed annotation for 2023 Paper 2 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-4 are all from the same country.

For each wine:

a) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (4 x 10 marks)

b) Comment on quality, maturity and capacity to age. (4 x 10 marks)

c) Comment on the method of production. (4 x 5 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W1: Barolo Ravera, Elvio Cogno, 2015. Piedmont, Italy. (14.5%)
- W2: Valpolicella, Allegrini, 2022. Veneto, Italy. (13.0%)
- W3: Taurasi Riserva "Piano di Montevergine", Feudi di San Gregorio, 2015. Campania, Italy. (14.0%)
- W4: Etna Rosso, Pietradolce, 2020. Sicily, Italy. (13.5%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of Italian red wine regions and how radically different wines can all come from the same country. Italy is basically the only red wine country where you could get four wines this structurally different — all still Italian — and it makes sense as a question. The clue is that they're all from the same country but the question asks you to get as specific as possible on origin, which really rewards candidates who know their regions.

The universe you're working in is: Piedmont (Barolo/Barbaresco/Barbera), Veneto (Valpolicella/Amarone/Soave), Tuscany (Brunello/Chianti/Bolgheri), Campania (Taurasi), Sicily (Etna), Sardinia (Cannonau). Pretty much all of Italian red wine.

The key discrimination here is structural: Barolo and Taurasi are both high-tannin, high-acid, long-lived wines but from very different grapes (Nebbiolo vs Aglianico) and different regions; the Valpolicella is much lighter and fresher and is not made for long ageing in the same way; and the Etna Rosso reads almost Burgundian in its paleness and lift. If you can place Nebbiolo from the pale color and stern tannin, Aglianico from the volcanic power and balsamic edge, and Nerello Mascalese from the high altitude transparency, you're doing well. The Valpolicella is the accessible reference point in the flight — Corvina's sour-cherry brightness is hard to miss. The question is specifically asking about method of production, which rewards knowing things like large Slavonian cask ageing in Barolo vs French oak in Taurasi.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - "same country" + Paper 2 (reds) = Italy is the primary candidate — it's the only country with this breadth of distinct red appellations
  - "identify origin as closely as possible" = they want region, appellation, ideally sub-zone
  - Method of production asked at only 5 marks each = secondary to identification and quality/maturity

- Universe I considered:
  - Italy: Piedmont, Veneto, Tuscany, Campania, Sicily all represented
  - Spain could work (Rioja, Ribera, Priorat, Toro) but that's a more homogeneous Tempranillo-dominant country
  - France could work (Burgundy, Bordeaux, Rhône, SW) but they usually separate France out rather than use it here

- Rule-outs:
  - Spain unlikely because the 4 red wines would be too similar
  - France unlikely — the examiner would say France if so
  - The 4 wines span 4 very different Italian appellations confirming Italy

- Conclusion: Italy, with Barolo (Nebbiolo/Piedmont), Valpolicella (Corvina/Veneto), Taurasi (Aglianico/Campania), Etna Rosso (Nerello Mascalese/Sicily)
