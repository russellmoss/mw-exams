# Proposed annotation for 2016 Paper 2 Question 3

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 6 and 7 come from the same region and the same vintage.

For each wine:

a) Identify the specific origin as closely as possible, with particular reference to the dominant grape variety. (2 x 9 marks)

nWith reference to both wines:

b) Discuss the relative quality of both wines within the context of their specific region. (18 marks)

c) Identify the vintage. (14 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W6: Château Léoville Barton, 2me Cru Classé, 2005. St Julien, Bordeaux, France. (13%)
- W7: Château Berliquet, Grand Cru, 2005. St Emilion, Bordeaux, France. (13.5%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of Bordeaux and specifically your ability to distinguish left-bank from right-bank expressions, and then discuss relative quality hierarchy. "Same region and same vintage" on Paper 2 reds pointing to two wines with different dominant grape varieties is a massive Bordeaux signal. The only region that routinely features two wines from clearly different sub-appellations using different dominant varieties but technically the same "region" is Bordeaux — left bank (Cabernet-dominant) vs right bank (Merlot-dominant). You could theoretically get something like northern Rhône vs southern Rhône, but that's a stretch given the very different varieties and the hint about "specific origin" being worth marks.

The relative quality discussion at 18 marks is interesting — Léoville Barton is a 2ème cru classé St Julien, whereas Berliquet is Grand Cru (not Grand Cru Classé at this time). So Léoville Barton is a higher classification and a stronger commercial position, but the examiner wants you to discuss both in their specific regional context — left-bank Médoc hierarchy vs St Emilion's own system.

The vintage identification at 14 marks is unusual and very high-value — they want you to name 2005. That's one of the great Bordeaux vintages and should be identifiable by the structure, ripeness, and concentration at 11 years of age. Both wines at 11 years would be deep, structured, complex, and still not fully open.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - "Same region, same vintage" = Bordeaux almost certainly (left bank + right bank = same "region")
  - "Dominant grape variety" = expecting Cab Sauv vs Merlot discussion
  - Vintage identification = high-mark, expects knowledge of benchmark years
  - Quality comparison = hierarchy discussion, classification system
- Universe I considered:
  - Bordeaux: left bank vs right bank — ideal for this question structure
  - Northern vs Southern Rhône: possible but varieties differ more dramatically and both are from one AOC each
- Rule-outs:
  - Burgundy: single appellation would be same village, same variety
  - Italy/Spain: no classic two-sub-region same-vintage comparison tradition at MW level
- Conclusion: Bordeaux 2005 — Léoville Barton St Julien (Cabernet-dominant left bank) vs Berliquet St Emilion (Merlot-dominant right bank)
