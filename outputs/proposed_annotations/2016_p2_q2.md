# Proposed annotation for 2016 Paper 2 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 3-5 are not from France and are all made from the same single grape variety.

With reference to all three wines:

a) Identify the grape variety as closely as possible. (15 marks)

nFor each wine:

b) Identify the origin as closely as possible, with particular reference to the climate. (3 x 10 marks)

c) Comment on quality and consider key selling points. (3 x 10 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W3: Pinot Noir, Dog Point, 2012. Marlborough, New Zealand. (14%)
- W4: Spätburgunder Blauschiefer, Meyer-Näkel, 2014. Ahr, Germany. (13.5%)
- W5: Pinot Noir, Tyrrell's Old Winery, 2014. Australia. (12.5%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of Pinot Noir outside France. "Not from France" is the key signal — the examiner is directly removing Burgundy from the picture to see if candidates can identify Pinot in non-Burgundian contexts. This combined with "single grape variety" and Paper 2 reds means the variety is almost certainly Pinot Noir. It's the one variety where three different non-French countries can each produce a recognisably different expression while the variety character remains central. You could potentially get Syrah/Shiraz (Germany, Australia, NZ all make it) but the climate question points more toward Pinot, which is so climate-sensitive that three different climates produce dramatically different outcomes.

The three wines here span a really useful spectrum: Dog Point Marlborough is the herbal, savoury, taut cool-climate New World expression; Meyer-Näkel Ahr is the delicate, pale, acid-driven, minerally cool Old World expression; and Tyrrell's Old Winery Australia is the commercial, approachable, softer, broader-climate version. Climate sub-question directly targets these differences — cool Ahr maritime, cool-maritime Marlborough with its savoury herbal edge, vs broader South Eastern Australia. Selling points question rewards commercial awareness: Ahr Spätburgunder has the "German red wine is improving" story, Marlborough Pinot has the NZ quality narrative, and the Tyrrell's is the value-accessible easy-drinker story.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - "Not from France" = explicit clue ruling out Burgundy
  - "Single variety" + climate emphasis = variety that's highly climate-sensitive
  - Three countries with different climates = Pinot Noir fits perfectly
- Universe I considered:
  - Pinot Noir: NZ, Germany (Ahr), Australia — ideal spread
  - Syrah/Shiraz: possible but climate difference less dramatic across three examples
  - Cabernet Sauvignon: not typically made in Germany/Ahr at this level
- Rule-outs:
  - Grenache: not strongly represented in NZ or Germany
  - Merlot: Germany doesn't make notable Merlot
- Conclusion: Pinot Noir — Dog Point Marlborough (NZ), Meyer-Näkel Ahr (Germany), Tyrrell's Old Winery (Australia)
