# Proposed annotation for 2025 Paper 2 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 4-7 are from the same country and region.

For each wine:

a) Identify the origin and the grape variety/ies as closely as possible. (4 x 13 marks)

b) Comment on quality within the context of origin. (4 x 7 marks)

c) Comment on the wine's maturity. (4 x 5 marks)

## Wines actually used (for the user's reference — DO NOT include this in the eventual annotation)
- W4: Gigondas, Château de Saint Cosme, 2022. Rhône Valley, France. (14.5%)
- W5: Crozes-Hermitage "Papillon", Gilles Robin, 2023. Rhône Valley, France. (13%)
- W6: Cornas Granit 30, Vincent Paris, 2022. Rhône Valley, France. (13%)
- W7: Côtes du Rhône Réserve, Famille Perrin, 2022. Rhône Valley, France. (14%)

## Proposed annotation

This is testing your knowledge of the Rhône Valley appellation hierarchy within French red wine, and specifically your ability to navigate within a region rather than across regions. "Same country and region" combined with Paper 2 (reds) is a fantastic stem — it's essentially saying "we're going to stay inside France, inside a single region, and you need to know the hierarchy well enough to identify four different appellation levels."

The Rhône is probably the most likely candidate for this type of question because it contains such distinct appellations within a single region: Northern Rhône (Cornas, Crozes-Hermitage, Hermitage, Côte-Rôtie, St-Joseph) which is almost all 100% Syrah, and Southern Rhône (Gigondas, Châteauneuf-du-Pape, Côtes du Rhône) which is predominantly Grenache-based GSM blends. That North/South distinction within a single "Rhône Valley" region is exactly what the examiner is testing.

The blind-tasting challenge is real. Cornas and Crozes-Hermitage are both 100% Syrah, but they taste different: Cornas is from granite slopes, more structured and mineral; Crozes is from flatter plateau, more approachable and fruit-forward. Gigondas is Grenache-dominant from the Southern Rhône, riper and rounder, with garrigue notes. The Côtes du Rhône is the widest category, blended and most accessible. The fact that they're asking about maturity (at 5 marks each) is interesting given these are all recent vintages — the 2022s and 2023 are all pretty young and primary, which is a fair observation to make.

## Reasoning trace (for the user's reference — DO NOT include in final annotation)

- Stem signals I picked up on:
  - "Same country and region" + Paper 2 (reds) = a major French or European red wine region
  - "Identify origin as closely as possible" = they want appellation-level precision, not just "France"
  - Maturity asked at 5 marks = relatively young wines, or wines with contrasting maturity profiles

- Universe I considered:
  - Rhône Valley (multiple appellations, North/South distinction), Burgundy (Côte de Nuits/Côte de Beaune), Bordeaux (multiple communes), Tuscany (Chianti/Brunello/Bolgheri), Rioja (multiple sub-zones)
  - The Rhône is the strongest candidate because it has the clearest North/South split within a single named region

- Rule-outs:
  - Burgundy: all Pinot Noir, harder to differentiate appellations at this level without precise tasting
  - Bordeaux: would likely be blends, harder to get 4 wines that are "from the same region" at appellation level
  - Italy: usually identified by country first in these questions

- Conclusion: Rhône Valley, France — Gigondas (Southern, GSM), Crozes-Hermitage (Northern, Syrah), Cornas (Northern, Syrah), Côtes du Rhône (Southern/regional blend)
