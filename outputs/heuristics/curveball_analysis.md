- **90 wines (17.9%)** are medium curveball
- **383 wines (75.9%)** are low curveball (standard/expected)

## The Furmint/Tokaji Question (2023 P3 Q3)

The definitive curveball-as-pedagogical-tool example:

**Stem**: "Wines 8 and 9 are from the same region."
- a) Identify origin and grape varieties (14 marks)
- b) Compare production methods (16 marks — the main mark-driver)
- c) Comment on quality and maturity (2 × 10 marks)

**Wines**:
- Wine 8: **Furmint Tokaji, Dubogo, 2019** (13.5%) — DRY Furmint. The curveball.
- Wine 9: **Tokaji Aszú 5 Puttonyos, Disznokó, 2013** (12%) — Sweet Aszú. The anchor.

**Why it works**: The dry Furmint is the curveball (most candidates associate Tokaj only with sweet wine). The Aszú is the anchor (recognizable benchmark sweet wine). The 16-mark production-method comparison is the heart — candidates must contrast reductive dry Furmint winemaking vs. botrytis/passerillage/oxidative Aszú aging. Even if a candidate can't name "Furmint," they can earn marks by describing the style contrast and inferring the region from Wine 9.

**Key pattern**: curveball is downweighted on identification marks and upweighted on style/method/quality discussion marks.

## Frequency Statistics

### Per paper average (high curveball only)
- Paper 1: **0.8** per paper
- Paper 2: **0.4** per paper
- Paper 3: **1.1** per paper

### Per question
- High curveball: 0.20 per question
- High + medium: 0.79 per question
- **18.3%** of questions contain at least one high curveball
- **44.4%** contain at least one high or medium curveball

### Ratio within questions
- 2-wine question: 1 curveball out of 2 (50%) — always paired with a clear anchor
- 3-wine question: 1 curveball out of 3 (33%)
- 4-wine question: 1 curveball out of 4 (25%) — **modal format**
- 5–6 wine question: 1–2 curveballs out of 5–6 (17–33%)

### By question type
- **Breadth/mixed-bag questions**: #1 host for curveballs
- **Same-country questions**: commonly include 1 curveball to test depth
- **Same-variety questions**: RARELY include curveballs (variety is stated)

### Price tier
- Commercial ($10–20): ~20% — Torrontes, Brachetto, Moschofilero, Lambrusco
- Specialist/premium ($20–50): ~65% — the bulk of curveballs
- Fine wine ($50+): ~15% — Franciacorta Bellavista, Colheita Madeira
- Almost no curveballs at luxury tier

## Examiner Deployment Patterns

1. **"1 in 4" rule**: In a multi-wine question, typically exactly 1 wine is significantly harder to identify. The others are anchors.

2. **Mark redistribution**: When a curveball is present, identification marks are often downweighted. The question upweights winemaking, quality, and commercial discussion — rewarding candidates who can describe what they taste even without naming the wine.

3. **Paper 3 concentration**: P3's mandate (sparkling, fortified, sweet, rosé, oxidative) inherently hosts more niche styles. Curveballs are most at home here.

4. **Never random**: Every curveball serves a pedagogical purpose within its question. The Tannat is in an Americas flight to test depth beyond Malbec/Carmenere. The dry Furmint is paired with Aszú to test understanding of Tokaj's modern evolution.

5. **Increasing frequency 2023–2025**: More deliberate curveball deployment (Moschofilero, Xinomavro, dry Furmint, Cab Franc IGT, Szamorodni) suggests the exam is expanding its varietal and stylistic scope.

6. **Curveball + anchor pairing**: In 2-wine curveball questions, the anchor wine (recognizable benchmark) provides the candidate a foothold. The curveball tests the edge of knowledge while the anchor tests the core. Example: Manzanilla (anchor) + Jura sous voile (curveball).
