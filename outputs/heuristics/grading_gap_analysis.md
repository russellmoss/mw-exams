# Gap Analysis: MW Practical Grading Prompts vs. 2017–2025 Examiner Reports

> Generated by the `examiner-grading-mining` workflow (13 reports — Practical 2017, 2018, 2019,
> 2021–2025 + Chief 2021–2025; 291 raw principles extracted) and gap-analyzed against the app's
> current grading prompts: `answer-evaluation-prompt.ts`, `funnelling.ts`, `evaluate-full/route.ts`,
> `pre-glass-prompt.ts`. Every recommendation traces to a verbatim examiner quote.

## 1. Already covered — principles the current grader already encodes

- **Reasoned wrong answer > unreasoned right answer** — Cardinal Rule 1 (per-answer grader) + `FUNNELLING_PRINCIPLE` (all files). Strongly covered.
- **Specificity within identification (commune/sub-region) earns more** — Cardinal Rule 2 (per-answer grader); funnel point 3 ("narrow to the specific call").
- **Quality must be contextualized (not bare "good")** — Cardinal Rule 3 (per-answer) / Rule 2 (full-debrief).
- **Winemaking must connect to the glass with specific parameters** — Cardinal Rule 4 (per-answer); "% new oak / toast" example given.
- **Commercial: price, channel, market, drinking window** — Cardinal Rule 5 (per-answer); Rule 6 (full-debrief: "channel, geography, price, competitive set").
- **Marks available for EVERY sub-question; answer each separately** — Cardinal Rule 6 (per-answer) / Rule 4 (full-debrief).
- **Depth must scale to mark allocation** — Cardinal Rule 8 (per-answer), well-developed.
- **Maturity = current age / drink now / improve how long / hold how long** — Rule 5 (full-debrief). (Note: missing from the per-answer grader.)
- **Funnel reads HARD STRUCTURAL EVIDENCE first (alcohol/sugar)** — funnel point 1 (shared constant).
- **No shoehorning; structure drives the call** — funnel anti-patterns + Rule 3 (full-debrief). Strongly covered.
- **Commit to a decisive final call; unfinished < wrong** — funnel point 4 (shared constant).
- **Lead with conclusion / give credit for sound reasoning to a different conclusion** — present across all four files.

## 2. Missing or under-weighted — prioritised additions

### P1 — Internal consistency between stated structure and stated identity (the "invented attributes" / cascade error)

This is the highest-value missing rule. The grader rewards funnelling and flags shoehorning *as a reasoning style*, but it has no explicit instruction to **detect that a candidate's own stated alcohol/sugar/tannin numbers contradict the wine they named**, nor to **penalize the downstream cascade** (quality/commercial answers written off a wrong ID rather than off the glass).

> "candidates identified the wine incorrectly and then put in figures of alcohol and sugar to match, rather than assess the evidence in the glass" (2022); "having guessed the identity incorrectly, a lot of candidates then dropped marks by writing an answer for what they had guessed it was, rather than referring to the wine itself" (2021); "statements such as 'A VDN with an alcohol of 20%' cannot earn points and is illogical" (2022).

Why it matters: examiners treat internal contradiction as a *zero* for the conclusion mark and a contaminating error across dependent sub-questions. An automated grader that scores each sub-question in isolation will over-reward a self-consistent-but-fabricated answer and under-detect the single most penalized failure mode of 2021–2025.

Paste-ready snippet (add to the per-answer grader's evaluation approach, and mirror in full-debrief):
```
## Internal-consistency check (apply to every answer)
Before scoring, cross-check the candidate's OWN stated structural figures (alcohol, residual sugar, acidity, tannin) against the wine they identified. If the numbers contradict the named wine — e.g. "Champagne at 14% alcohol", a "VDN at 20%", a dry-wine alcohol attached to a botrytis sweet wine — this is a logical impossibility: award NO conclusion mark for that sub-question even if a figure is individually plausible, and flag it as a theory error. Equally, watch for the CASCADE error: a candidate who misidentifies a wine and then writes quality / style / commercial answers describing what they GUESSED rather than what is in the glass. When style/commercial content reads as boilerplate for the named wine rather than derived from the candidate's own tasting notes, mark those sub-questions down for being disconnected from the glass — "assess the evidence in the glass and come to a logical outcome" (2022). Reward the opposite: a candidate who reports the glass faithfully even when their ID is wrong.
```

### P1 — Theory howlers sink borderline papers (cross-section credibility penalty)

The grader has no concept of a **credibility-destroying factual error** that should pull down the *whole script*, especially at the pass boundary. This is one of the most consistent themes across every year.

> "This totally undermines the confidence the examiner might have had in the candidate" (2017, on "Douro, Spain" + 15% Vosne-Romanée); "It is hard to feel confident about a borderline candidate when they make obvious theory mistakes" (2024); "Students who claim that Tawny Port is aged in a solera system, or that Amontillado Sherry has 14.5% alcohol, should do some more reading" (2025); "the sorts of statements that are likely to ensure a borderline paper would fail to gain the final few marks" (2018).

Why it matters: a borderline (BORDERLINE/55–64%) verdict should be tipped to FAIL by a clear theory howler, not averaged away. The grader currently has no mechanism to let a single fatal error override an otherwise passing aggregate.

Paste-ready snippet (per-answer grader, as a hard rule; reference in full-debrief):
```
## Theory howlers and the borderline (a hard rule)
The Practical is "a theory exam with a tasting" — production-method, appellation, and legal facts are marked, not decorative. Treat as a HOWLER any factually impossible or grossly wrong claim: wrong country/region pairing ("Douro, Spain"), an impossible parameter (15% alcohol for a Burgundian Pinot, 20% for a VDN), a wrong production method for a classic style (Tawny aged in solera, Amontillado at 14.5%, red fermented in barrel as the norm, Tawny Port described as Vintage). One howler caps the affected sub-question's conclusion mark at zero AND should reduce confidence in adjacent claims. Decisively: if your aggregate lands the candidate at BORDERLINE and the script contains a clear howler, resolve to FAIL and name the howler — examiners "do not feel confident about a borderline candidate who makes obvious theory mistakes" (2024).
```

### P1 — Quality overcalling / mis-calibration as a distinct penalty

The grader covers "contextualize quality" but not the inverse and more heavily penalized error: **inflating** a lesser wine to a prestige tier.

> "Calling a Côtes du Rhône a Châteauneuf-du-Pape, or describing the Ruby Port… as a vintage Port, undermines the examiner's confidence" (2025); "please do not assume that old world wines will always be the superior examples" (2017); "Far too many candidates mistook its maturity for quality" (2019).

Why it matters: quality is the single largest scoring domain (20%+ in 2025) and overcalling is both a quality error and a confidence error.

Paste-ready snippet (per-answer grader, augment Cardinal Rule 3; coaching in full-debrief):
```
Quality mis-calibration cuts both ways. Penalize OVERCALLING — assigning a prestige appellation/tier to a demonstrably lesser wine (a Côtes du Rhône called Châteauneuf, a Ruby called Vintage Port) — as a distinct error that also dents examiner confidence (2025). Do not let "Old World = superior" stand unargued (2017). And check that the candidate has not mistaken MATURITY for QUALITY — a developed wine is not thereby a great one (2019). Where a legal quality tier exists and is relevant, expect it to be named even if the question did not explicitly ask: "we do expect an official quality level if there is one and it is relevant" (2025).
```

### P2 — Maturity rubric missing from the per-answer grader; tighten to the 4-part / no-vague-timeframe standard

The full-debrief has a maturity rule; the **per-answer grader does not**. And both should adopt the explicit four-component standard plus the ban on vague timeframes.

> "an assessment of the current age… whether it is ready to drink now or would benefit from further ageing and, if so, how long it might improve for, and finally how long it is likely to hold before it declines" (2023); "examiners expect some reasonable time frame… not just 'matured for many years'" (2023); drinking-window questions ARE maturity questions (2024).

Paste-ready snippet (add to per-answer grader as Cardinal Rule 9):
```
9. **Maturity / drinking-window answers need four parts.** Full marks require: (a) estimated current age/vintage, (b) ready-to-drink-now judgement, (c) how much longer it will improve, (d) how long it will hold before decline — with CONCRETE timeframes, not "matured for many years." A "drinking window" question is a maturity question even when not labelled as such (2024); if maturity reasoning is absent, that sub-part scores near zero regardless of a correct ID.
```

### P2 — Cut-and-paste / undifferentiated answers across wines

Cited as a serious negative every year from 2017 to 2024, and entirely absent from all four prompts.

> "no two answers should ever be completely the same" (2017); "an increasing number of answers… clearly using cut and paste… unlikely to impress the markers" (2023); "the overuse of copying and pasting… creates considerable doubt in the mind of the reader" (2024); "cold soak on virtually every wine" / "obsessed by whole bunch… in every wine" (2024).

Why it matters: examiners chose wines that differ; identical language signals the candidate stopped reading the glass. This is detectable in a multi-wine answer and currently goes unflagged.

Paste-ready snippet (per-answer grader, new Cardinal Rule 10; also note in full-debrief):
```
10. **No cut-and-paste across wines.** Examiners deliberately choose wines with different attributes, so near-identical wording, the same winemaking technique applied to every wine (e.g. "cold soak" or "whole-bunch" on all of them), or recycled commercial boilerplate is a credibility-reducing pattern — "creates considerable doubt in the mind of the reader" (2024). Flag repetition across wines in a flight and mark down for failure to differentiate, even if each individual statement is defensible.
```

### P2 — Answer the EXACT question asked (compare/contrast; opportunities AND challenges; quality-in-context-of-origin; restating the stem)

The grader says "answer each sub-question," but examiners repeatedly penalize answering an *adjacent* question or omitting an explicit half.

> "two of the questions… were about commercial opportunities and challenges and many neglected to answer the latter" (2024); "the vast majority of candidates did not seek to answer the question as it was asked, instead just offering some winemaking details" (2025); "when asked to assess the quality in the context of the origin, many made no reference to the origin" (2023); "Having been told all the first 4 wines were traditional method, a surprising number went on to unnecessarily describe how they deduced it" — restating the stem earns nothing (2023).

Paste-ready snippet (per-answer grader; strong fit for full-debrief per-sub-question step):
```
## Answer the precise question (not an adjacent one)
For each sub-question, check the answer matches the EXACT instruction, not a generic version of it:
- "Opportunities AND challenges" → both halves required; award zero for any omitted half (2024).
- "Compare/contrast the wines" → explicit cross-referencing required; describing each wine in isolation scores half at best (2023).
- "Quality in the context of origin" / "discuss climate with respect to quality" → the answer MUST tie quality/style back to origin or climate with causal reasoning; free-floating quality loses the marks (2023, 2024).
- A specific frame (e.g. "relative importance of human inputs vs nature", "quality GLOBALLY") → generic winemaking detail or regional-only benchmarking is off-brief and loses marks (2025).
Award NOTHING for content that merely restates information already given in the stem (2023).
```

### P3 — Spelling / professional language / vague jargon as a scorable dimension

Minor but recurrent, and trivially detectable in typed answers.

> "spelling of 'wine words' continues to be poor" (2017); "'stonking', 'icon', 'Goldilocks', 'hippies, beatniks and bobos' reflects poorly" (2018); "the incessant use of PREMIUM or LOWER PREMIUM" (2019); misspellings "can cost marks and do not encourage the examiners to have confidence" (2021).

Paste-ready snippet (per-answer grader, light touch in "Important"):
```
- Flag misspelled appellation/variety/producer names and unprofessional or jargon language (colloquialisms like "stonking"; catch-all labels like "PREMIUM" used without context) as minor credibility deductions — examiners say these "do not encourage confidence" (2018, 2019, 2021). Do not over-weight, but note them.
```

### P3 — Reward genuine engagement / second-order insight (the top-band differentiator)

Currently nothing distinguishes a *good* answer from an *outstanding* one.

> "convey the genuine pleasure they experience in tasting a 40-year-old Tawny… shared by those reading the paper" (2025); the Puttonyos-exceeding-minimum reasoning "instilled much confidence in the eyes of the examiners" (2025); "get under the skin of the wine, which would show mastery" (2022).

Paste-ready snippet (full-debrief, and the per-answer "For deeper study" line):
```
Top-band differentiator: reserve the highest marks for answers that get "under the skin of the wine" (2022) — engaged, specific sensory description and second-order insight (e.g. reasoning that an exceptional producer exceeds a classification's minimum requirements). Genuine enthusiasm conveyed in writing is rewarded by examiners (2025); flat, formulaic prose on a great wine is not.
```

The new P1 internal-consistency rule is the natural *complement* to the existing shoehorning warning — shoehorning is the behavior, the consistency check is how the grader *detects and scores* it — so place it in the graders, not the funnelling constant.

## 4. Cross-cutting calibration notes (scoring temperament)

These should shape the grader's overall disposition; several can be stated once near the top of the per-answer grader and the full-debrief system prompt.

- **Identification is a MINORITY of marks — stop over-rewarding a correct ID — but grade to the PRINTED per-question tariff, not a fixed average.** ID ≈ 39–46% *paper-wide* across years, but any single question varies enormously (e.g. 2023 P1 Q1: variety ID 20 of 100, winemaking 30, quality+ageing 50). The grader must weight to the marks the question actually prints, sub-part by sub-part; the average is only a temperament check ("a correct ID with thin higher-tariff answers still fails; a wrong-but-reasoned ID with strong higher-tariff answers can pass"). [Corrected after the original draft over-stated this as a flat ~40% per-question rule.]
- **More ID marks live in the ARGUMENT than the conclusion.** "The examiners awarded a much higher proportion of the marks available for identification for the argument rather than for the conclusion" (2022). The grader should allocate the larger share of each ID sub-question's marks to the reasoning/elimination, not the bare name.
- **Score sub-questions independently — do NOT cascade an ID error into a zero elsewhere** *unless* the ID error logically invalidates that sub-answer or the answer was fabricated to fit the ID. "students could have misidentified the varieties and still passed this question" (2023). This sits in tension with the P1 cascade rule, and the resolution is exact: cascade-penalize only when the downstream answer describes the *guessed* wine rather than the glass; otherwise score on its own merits.
- **Plausibility gradient on wrong IDs.** Adjacent/plausible wrong answers get partial credit; implausible ones get little. "USA → Australia still received some credit, however Italy… few marks" (2021). The grader should grade wrong IDs on a sliding scale of geographic/stylistic plausibility, not binary.
- **Mark-allocation drives depth (already in Rule 8) — keep, and add the planning corollary:** a 15%-of-paper part-question mis-framed costs disproportionately (2025); depth and the penalty for getting it wrong both scale with the tariff.
- **Borderline = flag, don't hard-cut.** Moderation gives benefit of the doubt and unlocks many passes (in 2025, 11 of 16 passes came only after moderation; 2018/2019 describe two-stage moderation). The BORDERLINE verdict is correct behavior — but per the P1 howler rule, a clear theory howler is exactly what moderation withholds the benefit of the doubt for. So: BORDERLINE + coherent reasoning → lean toward the pass language; BORDERLINE + howler → FAIL.
- **Fixed absolute standard.** Pass is an absolute 65% per paper, not a curve; sub-45% essentially never recovers (2018, 2021). The grader's PASS/BORDERLINE/FAIL bands should anchor to F<45, D 50–54, C 55–59, B 65–69 (2021 boundaries) rather than relative impressions.
- **Mastery across four dimensions for a pass** (2024): tasting/structural reading, communication, theory accuracy, quality judgement. A spike in one cannot rescue a hole in another — which is the deeper justification for both the howler rule and the "ID isn't enough" rule.

The two new P1 hard rules (internal-consistency/cascade and theory-howler→borderline) and the ID-weighting calibration note are the changes most likely to move the grader's verdicts toward examiner-faithful outcomes; everything else refines an already-solid prompt set.
