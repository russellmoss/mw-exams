# Examiner Report Synthesis — MW Practical Exam 2017–2025

Synthesized from 8 practical examiner reports and 5 chief examiner reports across 2017–2025.

## 1. Mark Allocation (Trending Over Time)

The exam is shifting away from identification and toward analytical competencies:

| Year | Identification | Quality/Maturity | Winemaking | Commercial |
|------|---------------|-----------------|------------|------------|
| 2021 | 43% | 22% | 20% | 15% |
| 2022 | 46% | 24% | 21% | 9% |
| 2023 | 39% | 37% | ~16% | ~8% |
| 2024 | 39% | — | — | — |
| 2025 | — | 20%+ (quality alone) | — | — |

**Key quote (2024)**: "The MW practical exam is a theory exam with a tasting. This is truer today than ever before."

**Implication for mock exams**: Questions must allocate marks consistently with this trend. Identification should be ~35–45% of marks per question. Quality/maturity/winemaking/commercial should collectively be 55–65%.

## 2. The Seven Cardinal Rules (Recurring Across All Reports)

### Rule 1: Reasoning > Identification
Every single report (2017–2025) emphasizes this. Selected quotes:
- "More marks can be given when the conclusion is wrong, as we can then see and reward intelligent thinking." (2019)
- "Over half did not get a single origin exactly right, however many still managed 5-6 marks out of 8 if their reasoning was sound." (2025)
- "The argument is as important as the conclusion and is the key to passing identification questions." (2022)
- "A wrong answer yields more marks than an answer that is unfinished, so whatever you do: Make a choice." (2021)

### Rule 2: Quality Must Be Contextualized
Every report flags "good" or "very good" without context as earning zero or near-zero marks.
- Must use official classification levels where they exist (Grand Cru Classé, Cru Bourgeois, DOCG, Pradikat, VORS, etc.)
- Must use price points to corroborate quality position
- Must position wine on a quality ladder relative to its origin/peers
- "Even if we don't ask for it specifically, we do expect an official quality level if there is one and it is relevant." (2025)
- "The key is to try to communicate from an MW to an MW where on a quality scale this wine would be placed." (2023)
- One student "used the phrase 'good quality wine' for all twelve wines, yielding a lot of zero marks." (2021)

### Rule 3: No Shoehorning
Named explicitly in 2023, 2024, 2025; described in every earlier report.
- Candidates decide identity first, then force tasting notes to fit
- "There was a lot of shoehorning on this year's paper two exam, and ultimately, it led to the failure of many candidates." (2025)
- The antidote is honest structural assessment: read alcohol, acidity, tannin, RS accurately FIRST, then deduce identity

### Rule 4: Answer the Question as Asked
Flagged in every single report. Candidates routinely:
- Answer the question they prepared for, not the one in front of them
- Miss sub-parts (e.g., "opportunities AND challenges" — many answer only opportunities)
- Waste time on aspects not asked for (e.g., describing traditional method deduction when told all wines are traditional method)
- Confuse "compare and contrast" with "describe each separately"

### Rule 5: Maturity Has Four Required Elements
Explicitly defined (2023): "For maturity there needs to be an assessment of (1) the current age of the wine, (2) whether it is ready to drink now or would benefit from further ageing, (3) how long it might improve for, and (4) how long it is likely to hold before it declines."
- Vague statements like "matured for many years" earn minimal marks
- Specific timeframes expected
- "Best answers described both positive and negative evolution trajectories." (2023)

### Rule 6: Commercial Must Be Specific and Global
- Channels: on-trade vs off-trade, specialist vs supermarket, by-the-glass vs list
- Geography: domestic market, export markets, global perspective
- Price point: realistic and evidence-based
- Competitive set: what other wines compete for this shelf/list position
- "We expect students to tell us where we can find this wine. On or off trade? Specialist or supermarket? Michelin Star restaurant or the local pub?" (2025)
- "Too many talked about steakhouses as the answer to commercial appeal." (2022, 2023, 2024)
- "Rote responses" are explicitly penalized
- Food pairings "rarely rewarded with any marks" (2022)

### Rule 7: Structural Evidence Is the Foundation
- Accurate alcohol, acidity, tannin, RS assessment is the non-negotiable starting point
- "Hard evidence like alcohol and sugar are often more reliable than the flavour profile." (2025)
- "One potential pitfall in paper two is candidates relying too much on their noses." (2025)
- Inaccurate structural calls cascade through the entire answer

## 3. What Examiners Reward (Positive Patterns)

### Funnelling technique
Endorsed **by name** in the 2017 practical report — "the funnelling technique is by far the best
method to construct a compelling argument for geographical origin" — and the *principle* is
reinforced in every report since (2024, 2025 endorse it heavily but do not use the word). The marks
live in the argument, not the bare answer. Structure:
1. Read the HARD STRUCTURAL EVIDENCE FIRST (alcohol, acidity, tannin, RS) and let it drive the deduction — these are "more reliable than the flavour profile" (2025).
2. Put 2–3 genuinely plausible options on the table with evidence FOR and AGAINST each — "intelligent consideration to what it might have been, but was not" (2017); examiners "expect to see plausible options ... rather than an assumption that it is whatever the candidate has in mind" (2025).
3. Commit to the broad anchor (variety + major region) clearly and EARLY, then narrow to the specific call — "went straight to Bordeaux ... then argued for a specific origin from there" (2017); "zoomed in on Sauternes" from Bordeaux (2024). Do NOT bury the conclusion to the end.
4. Land a DECISIVE final call — "make a choice" (2021); a committed wrong-but-plausible call outscores a hedge or an unfinished answer.

**Two anti-patterns** (both lose marks): the **snap-call** (naming one wine outright, no alternatives weighed — fragile, under-scores even when right) and **shoehorning** (the inverse of funnelling — deciding identity first, then forcing structure to fit; "led to the failure of many candidates", 2025). Evidence drives the call, never the reverse.

### Enthusiasm and conviction
- "When candidates can convey the genuine pleasure they experience in tasting a 40-year-old Tawny, that enthusiasm is shared by those reading the paper." (2025)
- "It's essential that enthusiasm and a genuine love of great wines comes through in your answers." (2018)
- "Confidence and authority" are rewarded; hedging and vagueness are not

### Cross-referencing wines within flights
- Using one wine to unlock another within the same question
- "Where you have more than one wine to help you, one of them will show enough character to open the door." (2017)
- "The best candidates remembered to draw on all four wines in their arguments." (2025)

### Honest engagement with difficult wines
- Describing what is actually in the glass, even when the wine is unidentifiable
- "Even if candidates don't know the wine, they should trust their palates." (2021)
- "A lot of points could have been gained from simply describing what was in the glass even if the region was unknown." (2024)

### Linking winemaking to sensory evidence
- Not just listing techniques, but connecting them to what can be tasted
- "The best candidates were thorough in their winemaking observations and tied these back to what they could taste in the glass." (2018)
- Chronological approach to winemaking endorsed (reception → fermentation → maturation → bottling)

## 4. What Examiners Penalize (Error Patterns)

### Theory errors that undermine confidence
- "Tawny Port aged in a solera system" (2025)
- "Amontillado Sherry has 14.5% alcohol" (2025)
- "Malolactic before fermentation, oak ageing before crushing" (2021)
- "Sauternes fermented at 16°C in stainless steel" (2024)
- "Meursault, Grand Cru" (2017)
- "Pouilly Fuissé in the Loire" (2017)
- These "howlers" sink borderline papers

### Copy-paste / repetitive answers
- "An increasing number of answers appear repetitive and are clearly using cut and paste. It is unlikely examiners have chosen two wines with the same attributes." (2023)
- "Creates considerable doubt in the mind of the reader." (2024)

### Over-reliance on aromatics for reds
- "One potential pitfall in paper two is candidates relying too much on their noses." (2025)
- Structural elements (tannin, body, alcohol, acidity) are often more diagnostic than aromatic profiles

### Phantom oak detection
- "Many more answers found oak when it wasn't there than vice versa." (2018)
- Conversely, "it was noticeable how many candidates missed the obvious new oak use." (2023)

### Failure to recognize quality extremes
- Both overrating simple wines and underrating great wines
- "Calling a Côtes du Rhône a Châteauneuf-du-Pape, or describing the Ruby Port as a vintage Port, undermines the examiner's confidence." (2025)
- "Hardly anyone was sufficiently effusive about the quality of wine 12" — a Chilean Cabernet whose quality was downplayed due to origin bias (2018)

### Unprofessional language
- Terms like "stonking," "icon," "Goldilocks" are explicitly criticized (2018)
- Bullet points "rarely make for a strong argument" (2019)

## 5. Paper-Specific Insights

### Paper 1 (Whites)
- Acidity/RS interplay is a persistent weak spot: "A number seem to struggle with reading acidity, especially when there is some residual sugar involved." (2025)
- MLF absence is as important as MLF presence: "Their absence is as much of a winemaking choice as their presence." (2022)
- Oak detection on whites requires precision (lees, MLF, barrel size, new vs old)

### Paper 2 (Reds)
- Over-reliance on nose is the biggest trap
- Classic regions must be known cold: Bordeaux (commune-level), Burgundy (village+cru), Barolo/Barbaresco, Rioja, Northern/Southern Rhône
- Candidates default to famous appellations (CdP, Hermitage) even when they're not present
- "Where in the northern Rhone is going to produce a wine with the high alcohol and breadth of wine 8?" (2017)

### Paper 3 (Sparkling/Fortified/Sweet/Rosé/Oxidative)
- Consistently the weakest paper and the deciding factor for borderline candidates
- Theory knowledge is non-negotiable: "It is very hard, if not impossible, to pass the MW exam without knowing how Madeira or Sherry is made." (2025)
- Production method questions require explaining HOW (e.g., how fermentation was stopped), not just WHAT
- Official quality levels must be cited: Beerenauslese, VORS, Reserva, etc.
- Accurate alcohol and sugar estimation is critical — candidates who get these wrong cascade into wrong identifications

## 6. Exam Design Principles (For Mock Exam Writer)

### Questions are designed to allow marks even when identification fails
- "The questions were structured in a way that prevented misidentification of origin from affecting candidates' performances too adversely." (2019)
- "Most of the marks were for discussing quality, maturity and commercial potential, rather than precise identification." (2019, P2 Q3)
- On curveball wines: "No marks were given for attempts on identification." (2019, P1 Q3)

### Mark allocation signals required depth
- "Students need to be very mindful of the number of marks allocated to each element of the question." (2017, 2018)
- High mark values signal the need for detailed treatment; low mark values signal concise answers

### New question types appear regularly
- 2024: "Comment on the quality within the context of wine globally" — never asked before
- 2025: "Discuss the relative importance of human inputs vs nature" — never asked before
- Template answers will not suffice; the exam evolves

### Curveball wine marks are redistributed
- When a curveball is present, identification marks are downweighted
- Style, winemaking, quality, and commercial discussion absorb the marks
- This allows candidates to pass even with misidentification

### Wine selection balances classic and challenging
- "For every challenging question, there will be a correspondingly straightforward question." (2017)
- Paper 2 tends to have the most classic wines and the highest pass rate
- Paper 3 has the most stylistic diversity and tests breadth

## 7. Key Quotes for Mock Answer Writer Calibration

### On the purpose of the exam:
> "The ability to determine a wine's quality and value is arguably more important than being able to identify a wine blind." (2019)

### On what MWs must be:
> "Masters of Wine need to be generalists; it is not good enough to rely on expertise from a limited number of regions." (2018)

### On the exam's evolution:
> "As many wine styles around the world are more alike than different, the practical exam has evolved over the years and today it relies less on 'getting the wine right' and focuses more on theoretical aspects such as commercial appeal, winemaking, maturity and vintage." (2024)

### On the correct approach:
> "Taste like a detective; argue like a lawyer." (2019)

### On moderation criteria:
> "Reviewing all three papers allows one to clearly see strengths and weaknesses, whether the candidate shows convincing logic even if wrong, whether they recognize and clearly depict quality, style, and commercial aspects, and whether they can relate evidence from the glass to correctly distinguish winemaking techniques." (2023)
