# Examiner Heuristics -- Cross-Corpus Pattern Analysis

This document extracts patterns from the full 112-question, 10-year MW practical exam corpus. All analysis is stem-only -- no wine lists were consulted during pattern extraction. Every heuristic cites at least two source questions as evidence.

## Category 1: Stem phrasing --> variety/region signals

### 1.1 "Same single grape variety" in Paper 1 (whites)
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

This phrasing appears in P1 in 7 of 10 years: 2025 P1 Q2, 2024 P1 Q1, 2021 P1 Q2, 2021 P1 Q3, 2018 P1 Q1, 2018 P1 Q3, 2017 P1 Q2, 2017 P1 Q5, 2016 P1 Q2, 2016 P1 Q3, 2015 P1 Q2.

When the IMW groups white wines as "same single grape variety," the top candidates are:
- **Chardonnay** -- the most frequently tested. Appears as the "same variety" in 2018 P1 Q1 (4 wines), 2024 P1 Q1 (pair), 2016 P1 Q2 (pair). Chardonnay's extraordinary quality range (Yellowtail to Corton-Charlemagne) makes it ideal for compare-and-contrast structures.
- **Riesling** -- second most common. 2024 P1 Q1 (pair), 2018 P1 Q3 (pair), 2025 P3 Q1. The IMW loves Riesling for its stylistic breadth (Kabinett to GG, sweet to dry, young to aged).
- **Sauvignon Blanc** -- 2024 P1 Q1 (pair), 2019 P1 Q1 (common variety in all 4).
- **Chenin Blanc** -- 2021 P1 Q2 (3 wines), 2015 P1 Q2 (pair). Often used to test Loire vs South Africa contrast.
- **Pinot Gris** -- 2017 P1 Q2 (pair), 2016 P1 Q3 (pair). Alsace vs New World.

**Actionable rule**: When P1 says "same single grape variety" with 3-4+ wines, expect Chardonnay or Riesling. With 2 wines (a pair), Chenin Blanc, Pinot Gris, or Sauvignon Blanc are also strong candidates.

### 1.2 "Same single grape variety" in Paper 2 (reds)
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

Appears in P2: 2024 P2 Q1, 2024 P2 Q3, 2018 P2 Q2, 2018 P2 Q3, 2016 P2 Q1, 2016 P2 Q2, 2022 P2 Q4, 2022 P2 Q5.

Top red candidates for "same single grape variety":
- **Syrah/Shiraz** -- 2024 P2 Q1 (3 wines, Chile/Rhone/Barossa), 2018 P2 Q3 (pair, Hawke's Bay NZ). The Syrah/Shiraz duality is the IMW's favourite red variety question.
- **Pinot Noir** -- 2024 P2 Q3 (5 wines, 5 countries), 2016 P2 Q2 (3 wines non-France), 2018 P2 Q2 (3 wines). Global Pinot Noir comparison is an examiner staple.
- **Malbec** -- 2022 P2 Q4 (pair, same region Mendoza).
- **Cabernet Sauvignon** -- 2017 P2 Q1 (common dominant variety across 4 countries).
- **Gamay/Beaujolais** -- 2016 P2 Q1 (pair, same region Beaujolais).
- **Zinfandel** -- 2022 P2 Q5 (pair, same region).

**Actionable rule**: "Same single variety" + 3+ reds from multiple countries = almost certainly Syrah/Shiraz or Pinot Noir. "Same variety + same region" pairs in P2 test intra-regional quality discrimination (Beaujolais, Mendoza, etc.).

### 1.3 "Same country" constraint
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

Appears 19 times across all papers. This is the single most common constraint type.

In P1 (whites): 2025 P1 Q3 (France), 2024 P1 Q3 (France), 2023 P1 Q3 (South Africa), 2022 P1 Q2 (Australia), 2022 P1 Q3 (Italy), 2021 P1 Q1 (France), 2021 P1 Q4 (USA), 2017 P1 Q1 (France/Loire), 2016 P1 Q1 (France), 2016 P1 Q2 (USA), 2016 P1 Q4 (Spain).

In P2 (reds): 2025 P2 Q2 (France/Rhone), 2023 P2 Q1 (Italy), 2017 P2 Q2 (Italy), 2015 P2 Q3 (Italy).

**Key signal**: When the stem says "same country" without naming the country, the candidate must identify it from the glass. France is the most common answer (appears in ~50% of "same country" questions), followed by Italy, Australia, USA, and South Africa. When the question says "same country" and asks to "identify the country" for significant marks (e.g., 2023 P1 Q3 with 40 marks for country + varieties), expect a country with 3+ recognizably different regions or varieties.

**Evidence**: 2023 P1 Q3 (South Africa -- Chardonnay, Chenin Blanc, Sauvignon Blanc from different regions), 2022 P1 Q2 (Australia -- Riesling, Chardonnay, Marsanne from Clare Valley/Margaret River/Nagambie), 2021 P1 Q1 (France -- Jura, Burgundy, Rhone, Alsace), 2023 P2 Q1 (Italy -- Barolo, Valpolicella, Taurasi, Etna).

### 1.4 "Different countries, different varieties" -- the mixed-bag identifier
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

This phrasing appears 11+ times: 2022 P1 Q4, 2016 P1 Q5, 2018 P1 Q2, 2015 P1 Q3, 2017 P2 Q3, 2022 P3 Q2, 2023 P1 Q2, 2021 P2 Q2, 2019 P3 Q3, 2025 P2 Q3, 2016 P2 Q5.

This is the hardest question type -- each wine must be identified independently with no linking constraint. The IMW typically uses this structure to test breadth of knowledge.

When this appears in P1, expect a mix of aromatic (Gewurztraminer, Torrontes, Viognier, Muscat) and neutral (Albarino, Gruner Veltliner, Chenin) varieties. Evidence: 2022 P1 Q4 (Torrontes, Albarino, Gruner Veltliner, Roussanne), 2018 P1 Q2 (Semillon, Viura, Gruner Veltliner, Garganega, Chenin Blanc, Torrontes), 2016 P1 Q5 (Torrontes, Gewurztraminer, Chenin Blanc, Riesling).

When this appears in P2, expect at least one "curveball" variety alongside more recognizable ones. Evidence: 2017 P2 Q3 (Cabernet Franc, Spatburgunder, Pinotage, Lagrein), 2016 P2 Q5 (Malbec, Blaufrankisch, Carmenere).

### 1.5 "Varieties closely associated with their origin"
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

This specific phrasing appears 3 times: 2025 P2 Q3, 2018 P3 Q3, 2016 P2 Q5.

It is a strong signal that the wines are indigenous/signature varieties from specific regions, NOT international varieties. The IMW uses this to test knowledge of "local hero" grapes.

Evidence: 2025 P2 Q3 (Touriga Nacional/Douro, Zweigelt/Austria, Barbera/Piedmont, Gamay/Beaujolais, Xinomavro/Greece), 2016 P2 Q5 (Malbec/Argentina, Blaufrankisch/Austria, Carmenere/Chile), 2018 P3 Q3 (Cinsault/California, Garnacha/Spain, Mourvèdre/Bandol).

**Actionable rule**: "Closely associated with origin" = expect indigenous varieties from their home terroir, not international varieties planted globally.

### 1.6 "Classic European origins" / "Classic Western European"
**Confidence: PLAUSIBLE**

Appears twice: 2024 P2 Q2, 2018 P2 Q1.

Signals wines from the traditional European fine wine regions. Expect: Bordeaux, Burgundy, Rhone, Piedmont, Tuscany, Rioja, Mosel/Rheingau, Douro.

Evidence: 2024 P2 Q2 (Barolo, Brunello, Saint-Emilion, Rioja Gran Reserva), 2018 P2 Q1 (Brunello Riserva, Chambolle-Musigny, Cote-Rotie, Saint-Emilion, Rioja Gran Reserva).

**Actionable rule**: "Classic European" in P2 = the major appellations of France, Italy, and Spain. Quality level will be mid-to-high (village/cru level, not entry-level).

### 1.7 "From the Americas"
**Confidence: PLAUSIBLE**

Appears 3 times: 2021 P2 Q3, 2019 P2 Q2, 2018 P2 Q4.

Signals red wines from USA, Argentina, Chile, Uruguay, or (rarely) Canada/Brazil. The IMW uses this to test New World red variety breadth.

Evidence: 2019 P2 Q2 (USA Pinot Noir, Argentina Malbec, Chile Carmenere, Uruguay Tannat), 2018 P2 Q4 (USA Zinfandel, Chile Cabernet Sauvignon), 2021 P2 Q3 (USA Petite Sirah, Chile Carmenere).

**Actionable rule**: "Americas" = expect signature varieties from each country, not international varieties. Malbec (Argentina), Carmenere (Chile), Zinfandel or Petite Sirah (USA), Tannat (Uruguay).

### 1.8 "Europe, but not France, Italy or Spain"
**Confidence: CURVEBALL**

Appears twice: 2019 P2 Q3. Also implicit in 2025 P2 Q3 (Europe with indigenous varieties).

This is the explicit curveball signal. Expect: Germany (Spatburgunder), Austria (Blaufrankisch, Zweigelt), Portugal (Douro reds, Touriga Nacional), Hungary (Villányi Franc, Kékfrankos), Greece (Xinomavro, Agiorgitiko).

Evidence: 2019 P2 Q3 (German Pinot Noir, Austrian Blaufrankisch, Portuguese Douro red, Hungarian Franc).

### 1.9 "Blends" in Paper 1
**Confidence: PLAUSIBLE**

Explicit "blend" in P1 stems: 2025 P1 Q1, 2024 P1 Q3, 2019 P1 Q1, 2016 P1 Q1.

White blends in the MW exam almost always involve Bordeaux-style blends (Sauvignon Blanc/Semillon), Rhone whites (Marsanne/Roussanne/Viognier), or southern French blends. The 2025 P1 Q1 was Sauvignon-Semillon (Pessac-Leognan + Margaret River). The 2024 P1 Q3 was French blends (Jurancon, Pessac-Leognan, Chateauneuf-du-Pape Blanc).

**Actionable rule**: "White blends" = think Bordeaux Blanc (SB/Sem), Rhone Blanc (Marsanne/Roussanne), or CdP Blanc first.

### 1.10 "Do not spend time thinking about the wine's specific origin"
**Confidence: CURVEBALL**

Appears once explicitly: 2019 P1 Q3. But this phrasing (or equivalent "consider wine to be of unknown origin" in 2017 P3 Q2) signals a wine that is deliberately hard to place geographically -- likely an unusual style, orange wine, natural wine, or a variety grown far from its traditional home.

Evidence: 2019 P1 Q3 (Georgian Chinuri qvevri wine + Sicilian Marsala-style wine), 2017 P3 Q2 (Margaret River "amber" wine by Cullen).

**Actionable rule**: When the IMW tells you NOT to identify origin, the wine is almost certainly a stylistic outlier. Focus on winemaking technique and commercial analysis.

### 1.11 "Predominant grape variety" vs "single grape variety"
**Confidence: PLAUSIBLE**

"Single grape variety" is used when the wine is 100% varietal. "Predominant" hedges toward blends where one variety dominates (e.g., Bordeaux blends, Rioja, CdP). Compare: 2025 P2 Q1 ("same single grape variety or predominant grape variety") vs 2024 P2 Q1 ("same single grape variety").

When "predominant" appears, expect blended wines from classic regions: Bordeaux, Rioja, Rhone, Chianti. Evidence: 2022 P2 Q5 (Zinfandel/Zinfandel blend, Dry Creek), 2017 P2 Q1 (Cabernet Sauvignon dominant -- Bordeaux, Australian blend, Napa Cab, Stellenbosch blend).

## Category 2: Mark distribution --> difficulty signals

### 2.1 Variety identification marks scale with difficulty
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

When variety ID is worth **10-15 marks** for a single call (one variety for all wines), the variety is mainstream and should be identifiable with confidence. Evidence: 2018 P1 Q3 (10 marks, Riesling pair), 2024 P2 Q1 (15 marks, Syrah), 2021 P1 Q2 (15 marks, Chenin Blanc), 2015 P1 Q2 (10 marks, Chenin Blanc).

When variety ID is worth **16-25 marks**, the variety is important but may be less obvious or there is a deliberate difficulty factor. Evidence: 2025 P1 Q2 (16 marks, Chardonnay from 4 very different origins), 2024 P2 Q3 (25 marks, Pinot Noir from 5 different countries), 2023 P3 Q4 (18 marks, Grenache from 3 different countries/styles).

When variety ID is worth **4-8 marks per wine** (independent identification of each wine), the wines are diverse and each one is a separate identification challenge. Evidence: 2023 P1 Q2 (4 x 5 = 20 marks, 4 different varieties including Riesling Kabinett, Viognier, Chardonnay, Muscat), 2018 P1 Q2 (6 x 7 = 42 marks, 6 different varieties from 6 countries).

**Actionable rule**: High marks per variety ID call = easier variety but deeper analysis expected. Low marks per variety = harder identification but less depth.

### 2.2 Origin marks inversely signal examiner emphasis
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

When origin is worth **5-6 marks per wine**, the IMW cares more about style/quality/winemaking than pinpoint geography. The candidate should spend proportionally more time on the higher-marked components. Evidence: 2024 P2 Q3 (5 x 5 = 25 marks origin vs 5 x 15 = 75 marks climate/winemaking), 2015 P1 Q3 (6 x 5 = 30 marks origin vs 6 x 10 = 60 marks quality/style).

When origin is worth **10-15 marks per wine**, the IMW expects precise geography. Evidence: 2024 P1 Q1 (6 x 10 = 60 marks origin), 2022 P1 Q4 (4 x 15 marks combined variety+origin), 2018 P2 Q1 (5 x 10 = 50 marks origin).

**Actionable rule**: If origin is worth less than 8 marks per wine, the wines probably come from well-known regions and origin is a "gimme" -- invest your time in the higher-marked components.

### 2.3 "Compare and contrast" commands heavy mark allocation
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

When the stem uses "compare and contrast," the mark allocation is always heavy (20-36 marks for the comparison block). This signals the IMW wants structured analytical comparison, not just two independent tasting notes.

Evidence: 2024 P2 Q1 (30 marks compare/contrast styles and consumer appeal), 2024 P1 Q1 (3 x 20 = 60 marks compare/contrast quality/maturity/ageing), 2022 P1 Q1 (30 marks compare), 2021 P1 Q2 (36 marks compare/contrast quality and maturity), 2016 P2 Q1 (18 marks compare winemaking, 14 marks compare quality).

**Actionable rule**: "Compare and contrast" = structure your answer as direct comparison (wine A does X while wine B does Y), not as two separate tasting notes. This is where 60% of the marks live.

### 2.4 "Commercial position/appeal" marks indicate market-knowledge testing
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

When "commercial" or "market" appears in sub-questions, expect 5-15 marks per wine. These marks reward knowledge of price tiers, distribution channels, consumer segments, and regional market dynamics.

Evidence: 2024 P3 Q1 (20 marks commercial opportunities/challenges for sparkling), 2019 P1 Q3 (15 marks per wine on commercial potential -- curveball wines), 2023 P2 Q2 (10 marks per wine commercial position), 2017 P1 Q1 (3 x 6 = 18 marks commercial potential for Loire whites).

**Actionable rule**: High commercial marks + unusual wines = the IMW is testing whether you understand WHERE and HOW these wines sell, not just what they taste like.

### 2.5 Total marks per question correlate with wine count
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

The standard allocation is approximately 25 marks per wine. Deviations signal examiner emphasis:

- 2-wine questions: typically 50 marks (25/wine). Evidence: 2022 P1 Q1, 2024 P3 Q1, 2019 P3 Q1.
- 3-wine questions: typically 75 marks (25/wine). Evidence: 2025 P1 Q1, 2024 P1 Q2, 2025 P2 Q1.
- 4-wine questions: typically 100 marks (25/wine). Evidence: 2023 P1 Q1, 2023 P2 Q1, 2022 P1 Q4.
- 5-wine questions: typically 125 marks (25/wine). Evidence: 2024 P2 Q3, 2022 P3 Q2, 2025 P2 Q3.
- 6-wine questions: typically 150 marks (25/wine). Evidence: 2024 P1 Q1, 2018 P1 Q2, 2015 P1 Q3.

Exceptions occur when a question front-loads marks on variety ID (e.g., 2023 P1 Q3: 40 marks for country + varieties, pushing the total to 100 for 4 wines).

### 2.6 Vintage identification = high-difficulty signal
**Confidence: PLAUSIBLE**

Explicit "identify the vintage" appears in only 4 questions across 10 years: 2015 P1 Q1 (12 marks), 2016 P2 Q3 (14 marks), 2017 P1 Q5 (2 x 8 marks), 2021 P2 Q1 (3 x 20 marks), 2018 P3 Q1 (3 x 10 marks).

This is always paired with same-producer or same-region constraints that give the candidate contextual reference points. Vintage ID is never asked in isolation.

Evidence: 2021 P2 Q1 (three Ducru-Beaucaillou from different vintages, 3 x 20 marks), 2015 P1 Q1 (four Burgundy Chardonnays, same vintage = 2012, worth 12 marks), 2018 P3 Q1 (three pairs -- Champagne, Sauternes, Port -- each from different vintages).

**Actionable rule**: Vintage ID questions always have high contextual scaffolding (same producer, same region, same variety). Use the wine-to-wine comparison to triangulate vintage character.

## Category 3: Question structure --> answer style

### 3.1 "Pairs" structure -- frequency and implications
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

Explicit pair structures appear in 6 questions: 2024 P1 Q1, 2023 P1 Q1, 2021 P2 Q4, 2021 P3 Q3, 2019 P1 Q2, 2018 P3 Q1. Many more questions present implicit pairs (2-wine flights from same variety/region).

Pairs always test intra-variety or intra-region quality discrimination. One wine in the pair is typically premium/benchmark and the other is commercial/entry-level. Evidence: 2024 P1 Q1 (Kabinett vs GG Riesling; Corton-Charlemagne vs Yellowtail Chardonnay; commodity vs premium Marlborough SB).

**Actionable rule**: In a pair, identify which wine is the "benchmark" and which is the "commercial" example first. This determines the entire quality/maturity/ageability narrative.

### 3.2 "Mixed bag" -- the most open-ended structure
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

Explicit "mixed bag" appears only twice (2025 P3 Q3, 2023 P2 Q2), but the concept -- wines with no linking constraint except paper type -- appears more often when each wine is independently identified.

"Mixed bag" in P2 (reds): 2023 P2 Q2 (Yellow Tail Shiraz, Rioja Gran Reserva, Rauzan-Segla Margaux, Yalumba Grenache -- extreme diversity). Mixed bag questions always include at least one commodity wine and one benchmark, testing range.

"Mixed bag" in P3: 2025 P3 Q3 (6 wines, likely spanning rosé, fortified, sweet, unusual -- the widest possible stylistic range).

**Actionable rule**: Mixed bag = prepare for maximum variety. No linking thread means each wine must be assessed independently. Budget time carefully (6 wines at 25 marks each with ~8 minutes per wine is brutal).

### 3.3 "Same country" with multiple varieties -- the country tour
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

This structure appears in 12+ questions and tests regional breadth within a single country. The key challenge is identifying the country from diverse wines, then placing each within the country's wine geography.

Most frequently used countries:
- **France**: 2021 P1 Q1 (Jura, Burgundy, Rhone, Alsace), 2024 P1 Q3 (Jurancon, Pessac-Leognan, CdP Blanc), 2025 P1 Q3 (Muscadet, Alsace, Vouvray), 2017 P1 Q1 (Loire: Muscadet, Savennieres, Pouilly-Fume), 2025 P2 Q2 (Rhone: Gigondas, Crozes, Cornas, CdR), 2018 P3 Q2 (Pessac-Leognan, CdP Blanc, Burgundy).
- **Italy**: 2023 P2 Q1 (Barolo, Valpolicella, Taurasi, Etna Rosso), 2022 P1 Q3 (Pinot Grigio, Gewurztraminer Alto Adige, Etna Bianco), 2017 P2 Q2 (Montepulciano, Chianti, Barolo, Etna), 2015 P2 Q3 (Chianti, Brunello, Nebbiolo, Barolo).
- **Australia**: 2022 P1 Q2 (Riesling, Chardonnay, Marsanne).
- **South Africa**: 2023 P1 Q3 (Chardonnay, Chenin Blanc x2, Sauvignon Blanc).
- **USA**: 2021 P1 Q4 (Moscato, Chardonnay, Pinot Gris).
- **Spain**: 2016 P1 Q4 (Rioja Blanco, Albarino), 2025 P3 Q2 (Cava, Garnacha, Oloroso).

**Actionable rule**: When 3-4 wines share a country, the IMW picks wines that showcase that country's diversity -- different regions, different varieties, different quality levels. Prepare mental maps of 3-4 signature varieties per major country.

### 3.4 "Same region" with compare/contrast -- terroir deep-dive
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

This structure (17 occurrences total) tests the candidate's ability to discriminate within a single region. It appears frequently in P2 and P3.

Key examples: 2022 P2 Q2 and Q3 (Tuscany sub-regions -- Chianti Classico vs Brunello; Burgundy sub-regions -- Hautes-Cotes vs Clos Vougeot), 2016 P2 Q1 (Beaujolais -- Morgon vs Moulin-a-Vent), 2019 P3 Q1 (Champagne -- NV vs vintage), 2019 P3 Q2 (Prosecco -- Superiore vs basic), 2024 P3 Q2 (Jura -- fresh vs oxidative), 2024 P3 Q3 (Sauternes -- dry vs sweet vs aged).

**Actionable rule**: "Same region" questions reward sub-regional knowledge and producer-level differentiation. The candidate who can distinguish Chianti Classico from Brunello, or NV Champagne from vintage, earns the marks.

### 3.5 Six-wine "identify each" -- the stamina test
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

Questions covering 6 wines (the maximum in a single question) appear in 7 of the 112 questions: 2024 P1 Q1, 2019 P1 Q2, 2018 P1 Q2, 2018 P3 Q1, 2015 P1 Q3, 2016 P3 Q1, 2025 P3 Q3.

These are always the highest-mark questions (150 marks) and test stamina and breadth. When 6 wines are individually identified (2018 P1 Q2: 6 different varieties from 6 countries; 2015 P1 Q3: 6 different varieties), the candidate must work fast -- approximately 2 minutes of writing per wine.

When 6 wines are structured as 3 pairs (2024 P1 Q1, 2019 P1 Q2, 2018 P3 Q1), the pairs provide internal comparison points that actually make identification easier.

**Actionable rule**: 6-wine questions with individual identification = prioritize variety ID and origin over discursive analysis. 6-wine questions structured as pairs = use the pairs as diagnostic tools (the contrast between wines in a pair reveals more than either wine alone).

## Category 4: Cross-paper trends

### 4.1 P3 Q1 is usually sparkling
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

In 7 of 10 years, P3 opens with sparkling wine or a category that includes sparkling:
- 2024 P3 Q1: sparkling (2 wines, not Champagne)
- 2023 P3 Q1: traditional method sparkling (4 wines, not Champagne)
- 2022 P3 Q1: sparkling rosé (4 wines)
- 2021 P3 Q1: sparkling (3 wines, includes Champagne)
- 2019 P3 Q1: same region (Champagne -- 2 sparkling wines)
- 2018 P3 Q1: pairs by producer (includes Champagne, Sauternes, Port)
- 2016 P3 Q1: same single grape variety (Chardonnay -- sparkling + still)
- 2015 P3 Q1: Europe (includes Champagne, Sherry, Savagnin)

The only clear exceptions where P3 Q1 has no sparkling component: 2025 P3 Q1 (same single grape variety -- Riesling across sparkling/still/sweet, but this does include Riesling Sekt), 2017 P3 Q1 (rosés from three countries).

In the 4 most recent years (2021-2024), P3 Q1 has been explicitly sparkling every time. The trend is strengthening.

**Actionable rule**: Prepare for sparkling as the opening flight of P3. In recent years, the IMW increasingly specifies "not Champagne" -- study Cava, Cremant, English sparkling, Franciacorta, California sparkling, German Sekt, and Prosecco Superiore thoroughly.

### 4.2 P3 ending slots (wines 10-12) trend toward fortified and sweet
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

Across all 10 years, P3 wines 10-12 are overwhelmingly fortified or sweet:

- 2025: Madeira, Ruby Port, 40yr Tawny Port
- 2024: Vin Santo, Muscat de Beaumes de Venise, Vouvray Demi-Sec
- 2023: Maury VDN, Garnacha still, Grenache still (exception -- last 2 are dry)
- 2022: Recioto, 10yr Tawny Port, Australian Tawny
- 2021: Sherry Oloroso, Sherry Oloroso Dulce, Madeira x2
- 2019: Manzanilla, Jura oxidative, Passito, Rutherglen Muscat
- 2018: Cinsault, Garnacha, Bandol (exception -- dry reds for P3 Q3)
- 2017: Palo Cortado, Maury VDN
- 2016: Tokaji, Sauternes, Valpolicella Ripasso, Recioto
- 2015: Riesling Spatlese, Icewine, Tokaji, Vin Santo

In 8 of 10 years, wines 11-12 are fortified or sweet. The exceptions (2018, 2023) placed Rhone-associated reds or Grenache in those slots.

**Actionable rule**: When you reach P3 wines 10-12, assume fortified or sweet unless the stem explicitly indicates otherwise. The most common categories in these slots: Port (Tawny/Vintage), Sherry (Oloroso, Palo Cortado), Madeira, Tokaji, Sauternes/Barsac, Vin Santo, and VDN (Maury, Banyuls, Muscat de Beaumes de Venise, Rutherglen).

### 4.3 P1 always includes Chardonnay
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

In every year of the corpus, at least one P1 white wine is Chardonnay. The variety appears in P1 in all 10 years, sometimes as a named variety question, sometimes embedded within a "same country" or "mixed" flight.

Evidence (selected): 2025 P1 (Chablis, California, South Africa, Margaret River Chardonnay in Q2), 2024 P1 (Corton-Charlemagne + Yellowtail Chardonnay in Q1), 2023 P1 (La Crema Chardonnay in Q2, Hamilton Russell in Q3), 2022 P1 (Meursault + Corton-Charlemagne in Q1, Pierro Chardonnay in Q2), 2021 P1 (Corton-Charlemagne in Q1, Hyde Vineyard Chardonnay in Q4), 2018 P1 Q1 (4 Chardonnays), 2017 P1 Q5 (Meursault + Chablis), 2016 P1 Q2 (2 USA Chardonnays), 2015 P1 Q1 (4 Burgundy Chardonnays).

**Actionable rule**: You will taste Chardonnay in Paper 1. It is the only variety that appears in P1 every single year. Prepare for the full spectrum: unoaked Chablis to full malo/oak California, village to Grand Cru.

### 4.4 Riesling appears in P1 in 8 of 10 years
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

Riesling appears in P1 in: 2025 (Q3), 2024 (Q1), 2023 (Q2), 2022 (Q2), 2021 (Q3), 2018 (Q3), 2016 (Q5), 2015 (Q3). Possibly absent in 2019 and 2017 -- though both years featured aromatic whites that could test similar skills.

Evidence: 2024 P1 Q1 (Mosel Kabinett + Franken GG), 2023 P1 Q2 (Kabinett Mosel), 2022 P1 Q2 (Clare Valley Riesling), 2018 P1 Q3 (Rheingau GG + Mosel Auslese), 2015 P1 Q3 (Alsace Riesling).

**Actionable rule**: Riesling is the second most reliable P1 variety. Study the full stylistic spectrum: Mosel Kabinett (light, sweet), Alsace (rich, dry), Clare Valley (lime, dry), GG (powerful, dry).

### 4.5 P2 typically includes a Bordeaux or Bordeaux-variety question
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

In 8 of 10 years, P2 includes wines from Bordeaux or explicitly "Bordeaux varieties":
- 2025 P2 Q2 (Rhone -- exception)
- 2024 P2 Q2 (Barolo, Brunello, Saint-Emilion, Rioja -- includes Bordeaux)
- 2023 P2 Q2 (Rauzan-Segla Margaux)
- 2022 P2 Q1 (Chinon/Cab Franc, Napa Cab Sauv, Chile Merlot + Bordeaux blend)
- 2021 P2 Q1 (Ducru-Beaucaillou x3)
- 2019 P2 Q1 (explicitly "Bordeaux varieties")
- 2018 P2 Q1 (Saint-Emilion)
- 2017 P2 Q1 (Montrose + Cab Sauv from 4 countries)
- 2016 P2 Q3 (Leoville-Barton + Berliquet)
- 2015 P2 Q3 (Italy), Q4 (Napa Merlot + Franciscan blend)

**Actionable rule**: Expect at least one Bordeaux wine or Bordeaux-variety flight in P2. This could be a classic Left Bank, a Right Bank, or a global Cabernet Sauvignon/Merlot comparison.

### 4.6 P3 includes residual sugar questions regularly
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

Questions explicitly mentioning residual sugar or requiring candidates to state RS levels appear in P3 in 7 of 10 years: 2024 P3 Q4, 2023 P3 Q1, 2022 P3 Q2, 2021 P3 Q2, 2019 P3 Q5, 2017 P3 Q3, 2015 P3 Q3.

The IMW tests whether candidates can assess sweetness level accurately and correlate it with winemaking method and origin. Common RS levels tested: Brut (0-12 g/l), Demi-Sec (32-50 g/l), Late Harvest (50-100+ g/l), Beerenauslese/Icewine (150-250+ g/l), Vin Santo/Port (variable).

Evidence: 2024 P3 Q4 (5 wines with RS: Icewine, BBA, Vin Santo, VDN Muscat, Vouvray Demi-Sec), 2015 P3 Q3 (Spatlese, Icewine, Tokaji, Vin Santo), 2019 P3 Q5 (Passito + Rutherglen Muscat).

**Actionable rule**: Memorize RS thresholds for major sweet wine categories. Practice estimating RS from perceived sweetness in the glass.

Distribution across 30 papers:
- P1: 3 questions (5x), 4 questions (3x), 5 questions (2x). Average: 3.7
- P2: 3 questions (5x), 4 questions (3x), 5 questions (2x). Average: 3.7
- P3: 3 questions (5x), 4 questions (3x), 5 questions (1x), 6 questions (1x). Average: 3.8

The trend is toward fewer, larger questions. In 2023-2025, only once has a paper exceeded 4 questions (2024 P3 had 4). The 2017 and 2016 exams had more questions (up to 5-6 per paper), suggesting the IMW has moved toward deeper analysis of fewer flights.

**Actionable rule**: Expect 3-4 questions per paper. Budget time as ~30 minutes per question for 3-question papers, ~22 minutes for 4-question papers.

### 4.8 The "curveball slot" -- unusual wines appear in specific positions
**Confidence: PLAUSIBLE**

Unusual or challenging wines tend to cluster in specific positions:
- **P1 last question** (wines 10-12): This is where the IMW places unusual white varieties or unexpected origins. Evidence: 2022 P1 Q4 (Torrontes, Albarino, Gruner Veltliner, Roussanne -- 4 curveball varieties), 2019 P1 Q3 (Georgian Chinuri + Vecchio Samperi -- two extreme curveballs), 2025 P1 Q4 (Mosel Riesling, Rioja Blanco, Vin Santo -- unusual category mix).
- **P2 last question** (wines 9-12): The IMW places non-mainstream reds here. Evidence: 2019 P2 Q3 (German PN, Austrian Blaufrankisch, Douro, Hungarian Franc), 2017 P2 Q3 (Chinon CF, German PN, Pinotage, Lagrein), 2016 P2 Q5 (Malbec, Blaufrankisch, Carmenere).
- **P3 middle questions**: The stylistic outliers (orange wine, oxidative wine, unusual rosé). Evidence: 2017 P3 Q2 (Cullen "Amber" -- orange wine, deliberately no origin), 2019 P3 Q4 (Manzanilla + Jura oxidative).

**Actionable rule**: Curveball wines concentrate in the final question of P1 and P2, and in the middle questions of P3. Prepare mentally: when you reach the last P1 or P2 question, expect at least one wine you cannot immediately place.

### 4.9 Year-over-year trend: increasing diversity and specificity
**Confidence: PLAUSIBLE**

Comparing 2015-2017 papers with 2023-2025 papers reveals shifts:
1. **More indigenous/non-international varieties tested**: 2025 P2 Q3 (Touriga Nacional, Zweigelt, Barbera, Gamay, Xinomavro), 2023 P3 Q4 (Grenache from Maury, Montsant, Napa). Earlier years relied more heavily on the "big 6" international varieties.
2. **More explicit "not Champagne" sparkling**: 2023 and 2024 P3 both specify non-Champagne sparkling. Earlier years (2019, 2018, 2016, 2015) included Champagne itself in P3 Q1.
3. **More commercial/market questions**: "Commercial position," "consumer appeal," and "market potential" appear with increasing frequency in recent years, reflecting the IMW's emphasis on trade knowledge.
4. **Fewer 5-6 question papers**: 2017 P3 had 6 questions; recent years max at 4, allowing deeper analysis per flight.

Evidence: Compare 2017 P3 (6 questions, 12 wines, many short-answer) with 2025 P3 (3 questions, 12 wines, deeper analysis per flight).

### 4.10 Sherry and Madeira appear in P3 at least every other year
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

Sherry appears in P3: 2021 (Oloroso x2), 2019 (Manzanilla), 2017 (Palo Cortado), 2016 (Palo Cortado), 2015 (Fino, Matusalem). Absent in 2022, 2023. Present in 2025 (Oloroso VORS).

Madeira appears in P3: 2025 (Sercial), 2021 (Henriques & Henriques x2), 2016 (Henriques & Henriques Sercial), 2015 (Colheita). Absent in 2019, 2022, 2023.

Port appears in P3: 2025 (Ruby + Tawny), 2022 (Tawny x2), 2021 (Vintage Port), 2018 (Taylor's Vintage x2), 2016 (Graham's Tawny), 2015 (Warre Vintage).

**Actionable rule**: At least one fortified wine from Iberia/Madeira will appear in P3 in any given year. Prepare all major styles: Fino/Manzanilla, Amontillado, Oloroso, Palo Cortado (Sherry); Vintage/Tawny/Ruby/LBV/Colheita (Port); Sercial/Verdelho/Bual/Malmsey (Madeira).

### 4.11 Tokaji appears in P3 with remarkable frequency
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

Tokaji appears in P3 in 5 of 10 years: 2025 (Edes Szamorodni), 2024 (not Tokaji but similar sweet), 2023 (Aszú 5 Puttonyos in Q3, dry Furmint in Q3), 2022 (Aszú 5 Puttonyos), 2016 (Aszú 6 Puttonyos), 2015 (Aszú 5 Puttonyos).

The IMW clearly considers Tokaji a core competency for P3. Both botrytized Aszú and dry Furmint have appeared.

**Actionable rule**: Study Tokaji thoroughly -- Aszú puttonyos levels, Szamorodni (édes and száraz), and dry Furmint. This is the most frequently tested sweet wine origin after Sauternes.

### 4.12 P2 Q1 often provides an "anchor" variety or region
**Confidence: STRONG SIGNAL**

In 7 of 10 years, P2 Q1 gives a strong constraining signal that helps the candidate start confidently:
- 2025 P2 Q1: "same single grape variety or predominant" (Cabernet Franc)
- 2024 P2 Q1: "same single grape variety" (Syrah)
- 2023 P2 Q1: "same country" (Italy)
- 2022 P2 Q1: elaborately structured Bordeaux blend question
- 2021 P2 Q1: "same region and producer, different vintages" (Bordeaux)
- 2019 P2 Q1: "Bordeaux varieties" (explicit)
- 2018 P2 Q1: "classic Western European origins"
- 2017 P2 Q1: "common dominant grape variety" (Cab Sauv)
- 2016 P2 Q1: "same region, same variety" (Beaujolais/Gamay)
- 2015 P2 Q1: "Pinot Noir" (explicit)

In 3 of 10 years the variety is given explicitly (2015, 2019). In most other years, the constraint (same variety, same country, or classic origins) limits the universe enough that an experienced candidate can identify with confidence.

**Actionable rule**: P2 Q1 is designed to let you build confidence before harder flights. Use the stem constraint aggressively to narrow candidates before tasting.
