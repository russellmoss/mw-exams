### Rules Derived: Sweet Wine Flights

**RULE S1: Mechanism diversity is paramount.**
In any flight of 4+ sweet wines where mechanism is a question focus, the IMW uses at least 3 distinct mechanisms. In 5-wine flights, they aim for 4-5 distinct mechanisms. The 2024 flight achieves 5 for 5.

**RULE S2: If a mechanism repeats, every other dimension must differ.**
When botrytis appears twice (2013, 2022), the two botrytis wines are from different countries, different varieties, and different stylistic traditions. The IMW never places two wines with the same mechanism + same variety + similar price in the same mechanism-testing flight.

**RULE S3: Same-variety repetition is permitted only in explicitly same-variety questions.**
The 2012 Muscat flight (Q4) and 2012 Riesling pair (Q3) both repeat varieties because the question stem says "same grape variety." In mechanism-breadth questions, all varieties should differ.

**RULE S4: No extreme price tier gaps without bridging.**
The IMW does not pair a ~$2000 TBA with a ~$60 wine as the only two botrytis examples. When luxury wines appear, the flight includes wines across multiple price tiers to create a continuum.

**RULE S5: The sweetness spectrum should be represented.**
The best flights (2013, 2022, 2024) include wines from off-dry through medium-sweet to fully sweet to lusciously sweet. This tests the candidate's ability to calibrate RS levels.

**RULE S6: Fortification sub-traditions count as distinct methods.**
Port, Sherry, VDN (Muscat or Grenache), Madeira, and Rutherglen Muscat are treated as different production methods even though all involve spirit addition.

## Part 2: All Question Types — General Cross-Tabulation

### Flight size by paper and question family

| Paper | Typical flight size | Range | Notes |
|-------|-------------------|-------|-------|
| P1 | 2-6 wines | 2-6 | Most common: 2-4 |
| P2 | 2-6 wines | 2-6 | Most common: 3-4 |
| P3 | 2-6 wines | 1-6 | Most common: 2-4; single wine only in 2017 Q2 (orange wine) |

### Variety repetition by question type

**Same-variety questions (F1)**: Variety repetition is the entire point. All wines share one variety. This accounts for ~35% of all questions.

**Same-origin questions (F2)**: Variety repetition is rare but not prohibited. When the origin constraint is tight (same region), different varieties are expected. Approximately 5% of F2 questions repeat a variety.

**Mixed-identification questions (F4)**: Variety repetition is almost never seen. The stem typically says "different grape varieties." Less than 3% of F4 questions repeat a variety.

**Method/production questions (F5/F6)**: Variety repetition is acceptable when the production method, not the variety, is the testing point. ~15% of method questions use the same variety twice but in different production styles.

### Country distribution patterns

**Papers 1 and 2**: The IMW routinely concentrates wines in 1-2 countries for same-origin questions. For cross-origin questions, 3-6 countries is standard.

**Paper 3**: Geographic diversity is a hallmark. Even when wines share a style (all sparkling, all fortified), the IMW typically draws from 3+ countries. The only exception is same-producer flights (e.g., Dom Pérignon 1999/2009 in 2018; Madeira flights).

### Price tier distribution by question type

| Question Type | Value % | Mainstream % | Premium % | Super-premium % | Luxury % |
|--------------|---------|-------------|-----------|----------------|----------|
| F1 (same variety) | 10-15% | 25-30% | 35-40% | 15-20% | 0-5% |
| F2 (same origin) | 5-10% | 20-30% | 40-50% | 15-20% | 0-5% |
| F4 (mixed ID) | 10-20% | 30-40% | 30-40% | 5-15% | 0-2% |
| F5 (method) | 10-15% | 25-35% | 30-40% | 15-20% | 0-5% |
| F7 (hierarchy) | 5-10% | 15-25% | 30-40% | 20-30% | 5-10% |
| Sweet flights | 0-5% | 15-25% | 35-45% | 20-30% | 0-5% |

**Key finding**: The IMW rarely includes more than one luxury-tier wine per question. Hierarchy questions (F7) are the most likely to include a luxury wine, and even then it typically appears alongside a value or mainstream wine for contrast.

### Benchmark vs non-benchmark distribution

Across all papers:
- ~55-65% of wines are from benchmark regions/varieties (the top 20 that appear most frequently in the corpus)
- ~25-35% are from established but less common origins
- ~5-15% are genuine curveballs (unusual variety, unusual origin, or unusual style for the paper)

**Per question**: The IMW typically includes 0-1 curveball wines per question. A question with 2+ genuine curveballs is extremely rare (occurs ~5% of the time, almost exclusively in mixed-bag Q5-style questions).

## Part 3: Guardrails for Mock Exam Generation

These rules should be enforced by the mock-exam-writer agent when constructing any question.

### General rules (all papers, all question types)

**G1: Variety repetition within a question must be intentional.**
- In same-variety questions (F1): repetition is mandatory (that's the question)
- In method/style questions (F5/F6): allow at most 1 repeated variety, and only if the two wines differ sharply in style or mechanism
- In all other questions: no variety repetition unless the stem explicitly permits it

**G2: Price tier balance.**
- No more than 2 wines from the same price tier in a 4+ wine flight, unless the question is specifically a hierarchy/quality question
- Do not pair a luxury wine (>$200) with only value wines (<$15) in the same flight. Include at least one bridging wine in between
- The maximum price ratio in a sweet wine flight should not exceed ~20:1 (e.g., a $500 TBA is fine alongside a $25 Spätlese, but a $3000 TBA alongside a $15 Moscato is not)

**G3: Country diversity targets.**
- 2-wine flights: 2 countries (unless same-origin question)
- 3-wine flights: 2-3 countries
- 4-wine flights: 3-4 countries (unless same-origin)
- 5-6 wine flights: 4-6 countries (unless same-origin)
- Exception: same-origin questions can have all wines from one country

**G4: Curveball budget.**
- Maximum 1 curveball wine per question
- Maximum 2 curveball wines per paper
- A curveball should not appear in a same-variety question (it breaks the ID logic)

### Sweet wine flight specific rules

**S1: Mechanism diversity requirement.**
For any sweet wine flight of 4+ wines where "method of production" or "mechanism of sweetness" appears in the question:
- 4-wine flight: minimum 3 distinct mechanisms
- 5-wine flight: minimum 4 distinct mechanisms (target 5)
- 6-wine flight: minimum 5 distinct mechanisms

**S2: No same-mechanism + same-variety pairing.**
Never place two wines in the same flight that share BOTH the same sweetness mechanism AND the same grape variety, unless the question explicitly tests comparison within one tradition.

**S3: Mechanism taxonomy for counting.**
Count these as distinct mechanisms:
1. **Ice wine / cryoextraction** (natural freeze or cryo)
2. **Botrytis / noble rot** (Sauternes, BA, TBA, Tokaji Aszú, Alsace SGN)
3. **Passerillage / dried grape** (Vin Santo, Passito, Recioto, Amarone-adjacent, Jurançon sec-appassimento)
4. **Fortification — Port tradition** (Vintage Port, Ruby, Tawny, LBV)
5. **Fortification — VDN/Muscat tradition** (Beaumes de Venise, Rivesaltes, Rutherglen Muscat, Banyuls)
6. **Fortification — Sherry tradition** (Oloroso dulce, Cream, PX)
7. **Fortification — Madeira tradition** (estufagem/canteiro)
8. **Natural RS / stopped fermentation** (German Prädikat, off-dry Riesling, Moscato d'Asti, demi-sec Vouvray, Brachetto)
9. **Late harvest** (Spätlese, Vendanges Tardives, Late Harvest without botrytis)
10. **Chaptalisation-adjacent** (theoretical — extremely rare in the corpus)

For the purpose of the "distinct mechanism" count:
- Items 4-7 are all "fortification" at the broadest level but the IMW treats them as distinct methods
- Items 2 and 9 both involve overripe grapes, but botrytis is a distinct fungal process vs simple late harvest
- Items 1 and 2 both concentrate sugars naturally but by radically different processes

**S4: Price tier spread in sweet flights.**
Sweet wine prices vary enormously, but the IMW has a hard practical ceiling. The exam needs ~25 bottles per wine; the most expensive wine ever poured was approximately $300-400/bottle (Batard-Montrachet Grand Cru, Dom Perignon 1999). The most expensive sweet wine ever used was Kracher BA at ~$80-120. No Egon Muller TBA ($2000+), no Yquem ($300+), no Donnhoff Oberhäuser Brücke ($200+).
- Hard cap: no wine above ~$400 retail in any mock exam
- For sweet flights specifically: max ~$120 per wine (historical sweet wine ceiling)
- The most expensive wine should not be more than 20x the price of the cheapest wine
- If including a super-premium sweet wine (~$80-120), include at least 2 wines in the $20-60 range
- The IMW typically uses mid-range premium sweet wines as the backbone ($30-80)

**S5: Sweetness level spectrum.**
A good sweet wine flight should span a range of residual sugar levels:
- Off-dry / light sweet: 10-30 g/l (e.g., Spätlese, off-dry Gewürz, demi-sec)
- Medium sweet: 30-80 g/l (e.g., Auslese, Late Harvest)
- Sweet: 80-150 g/l (e.g., Sauternes, Aszú 5 Putt)
- Lusciously sweet: 150+ g/l (e.g., TBA, Eiswein, Aszú 6 Putt)
- Fortified sweet: variable RS but with fortification context

Include at least 3 different sweetness levels in a 5-wine flight.

### Fortified wine flight specific rules

**F1: Fortified flights should span traditions.**
The IMW never puts 3 wines from the same fortified category in one flight unless the question is specifically about that category (e.g., 2014 P3 Q2 — four fortified wines from four different traditions: Sherry, Madeira, Port, Banyuls).

**F2: Within fortified flights, vary the style axis.**
If using multiple fortified wines, vary along: dry vs sweet, oxidative vs reductive, young vs aged, grape-driven vs process-driven.

### Sparkling wine flight specific rules

**SP1: Method diversity in sparkling flights.**
Non-Champagne sparkling questions should include at least 2 different sparkling methods (Traditional, Charmat, Transfer, Ancestral).

**SP2: Quality spread in sparkling flights.**
Include at least one value/commercial and one premium/artisan sparkling to test calibration.

## Part 4: V6 Mock Exam Evaluation — P3 Q3

### The v6 selection

The v6 mock P3 Q3 presents 5 wines "from five different countries... with the presence of residual sugar" and asks about "the mechanism by which sweetness has been achieved":

| Wine | Mechanism | Variety | Country | Approx Price |
|------|-----------|---------|---------|-------------|
| W8: Muscat de Beaumes-de-Venise, Durban 2023 | Fortification (VDN) | **Muscat** | France | ~$20-30 |
| W9: Muscat Liqueur, Chambers Rosewood NV Rutherglen | Fortification (Liqueur Muscat) | **Muscat** | Australia | ~$25-40 |
| W10: Riesling TBA, Egon Müller Scharzhof 2016 | Botrytis (TBA) | Riesling | Germany | **~$2,000-5,000** |
| W11: Tokaji Aszú 6 Puttonyos, Royal Tokaji 2017 | Botrytis (Aszú) | Furmint | Hungary | ~$50-80 |
| W12: Riesling Icewine, Inniskillin 2022 | Ice wine | Riesling | Canada | ~$50-80 |

### Violations identified

**Violation 1: Same variety repeated — Muscat x2 (SEVERE)**
W8 and W9 are both Muscat. In a 5-wine mechanism question, the IMW has NEVER repeated a variety in the historical corpus (2013, 2015, 2022, 2024 — all 4+ wine sweet flights have all-distinct varieties). Rule G1 violated.

**Violation 2: Same mechanism repeated — Fortification x2 with same grape (SEVERE)**
W8 (VDN Muscat) and W9 (Rutherglen Muscat) are both fortified Muscat wines. While the IMW does sometimes consider VDN and Rutherglen as "different methods" (2021 P3 Q2), that 2021 question explicitly said "different methods of production" and used different grapes for the two fortified-Muscat entries plus there were 3 other fortified wines alongside. Here, 2 of 5 wines share both mechanism (fortification) and variety (Muscat). No historical precedent for this in a mechanism-testing question.

**Violation 3: Same mechanism repeated — Botrytis x2 (MODERATE)**
W10 (TBA) and W11 (Tokaji Aszú) are both botrytis wines. This is not unprecedented — 2013 and 2022 both have botrytis appearing twice. However, in those cases, the flight had 5-6 wines with 4-5 total mechanisms. Here, with only 3 distinct mechanisms out of 5 wines (fortification, botrytis, ice), the mechanism diversity is below the historical floor.

**Violation 4: Only 3 distinct mechanisms in a 5-wine mechanism question (SEVERE)**
Mechanisms present: fortification (x2), botrytis (x2), ice wine (x1). That's 3 distinct mechanisms for 5 wines. Historical comparison:
- 2013 (6 wines): 5 mechanisms
- 2015 (4 wines): 4 mechanisms (all different)
- 2022 (5 wines): 3-4 mechanisms
- 2024 (5 wines): 5 mechanisms (all different)
The v6 mock is at the absolute floor and below the typical standard. Missing: passerillage/dried grape, natural RS/late harvest, stopped fermentation.

**Violation 5: Extreme price gap (SEVERE)**
Egon Müller TBA ($2,000-5,000) alongside wines in the $20-80 range. The price ratio is 50:1 to 250:1, far exceeding the historical maximum of ~20:1. The IMW has NEVER included a wine in the $2000+ range in a mechanism-testing flight. In the 2013 flight (arguably the closest to a luxury sweet wine with Kracher BA at ~$80-120), no wine exceeds ~$120.

**Violation 6: Missing key mechanisms (MODERATE)**
The flight has no representation of:
- Passerillage/dried grape (Vin Santo, Passito, Recioto) — appears in 4 of 5 historical sweet flights
- Natural RS / stopped fermentation (Spätlese, Moscato, demi-sec) — appears in 3 of 5 historical sweet flights
- Late harvest without botrytis — appears in 2 of 5 historical sweet flights

These are among the most commonly tested mechanisms.

### How the IMW would actually structure this question

Based on the historical patterns, a 5-wine sweet mechanism flight should look like:

**Example reconstruction (mechanism-diverse)**:
1. Riesling Spätlese, [German producer] — **Natural RS** (Germany, ~$20-30)
2. Tokaji Aszú 5 Puttonyos, [Hungarian producer] — **Botrytis** (Hungary, ~$40-60)
3. Vidal Icewine, [Canadian producer] — **Ice wine** (Canada, ~$50-80)
4. Vin Santo del Chianti, [Italian producer] — **Passerillage** (Italy, ~$30-50)
5. Muscat de Beaumes de Venise, [French producer] — **Fortification/VDN** (France, ~$15-25)

Result: 5 mechanisms, 5 varieties, 5 countries, price range $15-80 (ratio ~5:1). This mirrors the 2024 actual exam flight almost exactly.

### Severity summary

| Violation | Severity | Historical precedent for violation |
|-----------|----------|------------------------------------|
| Muscat x2 (same variety) | HIGH | Never in mechanism questions |
| Fortification x2 + same grape | HIGH | Never |
| Botrytis x2 | MODERATE | Seen 2x but with compensating diversity |
| Only 3 mechanisms in 5 wines | HIGH | Below historical floor |
| $2000+ TBA in mechanism flight | HIGH | Never |
| Missing passerillage/natural RS | MODERATE | Present in 4/5 historical flights |

**Overall assessment**: The v6 P3 Q3 has 3 high-severity and 2 moderate-severity violations against historical patterns. It should be rebuilt from scratch using the guardrails above.
