## F7 tasting branch order

1. Confirm region on the most typical wine first — Bordeaux: cassis / plum / cedar / graphite family on all three
2. Lock the region — do not flip after the first wine
3. Order the three wines by concentration and development: lightest/most evolved = not necessarily oldest — separate primary-vs-tertiary from concentration
4. Translate each position into a vintage tier: lean/green hints = difficult year; generous/ripe = good year; concentrated/complex/evolved = great vintage
5. Name the vintage — anchor with sensory evidence: "the concentration and tertiary development are consistent with a wine of 15+ years suggesting the early 2000s; the polished tannins and depth suggest a great year, most likely 2005"
6. Quality in vintage context (20 marks): describe the growing season → sensory consequence → market impact at release and now

## Quality calibration notes (F7)
The 20-mark vintage discussion demands more than naming a year. The IMW expects:
- Growing season narrative: "2005 saw exceptional summer drought conditions, low yields, producing wines of remarkable concentration and ripeness"
- Sensory consequence: "these conditions manifest in the glass as deep colour, polished tannins without harsh edges, and concentrated cassis/blackcurrant fruit that has now developed into leather and cigar box with 15+ years of bottle age"
- Market consequence: "the 2005 vintage was received with exceptional critical acclaim (Parker awarded 100 points to several Left Bank growths) and commands a significant premium on the secondary market"

For the difficult vintage:
- "The cooler, wetter 2013 conditions produced a wine of lighter concentration, higher visible acidity, and less extracted tannins — honest and elegant rather than landmark, offering better near-term drinking at lower price points"

## What to focus on in the glass

- Order the three wines by concentration: lightest = most difficult vintage; deepest = legendary vintage
- Colour: the legendary vintage (oldest of the three) should show brick/garnet at the rim from age; the excellent vintage shows deep ruby with purple hints; the difficult vintage shows medium ruby
- Tannin: the legendary vintage tannins should be the most resolved and silky; the difficult vintage tannins slightly firmer or leaner for the concentration
- Tertiary character: the legendary vintage shows most leather/tobacco/cedar/truffle development; the excellent vintage is moving from primary to secondary; the difficult vintage is younger or lighter in tertiary development
- ABV: all three wines are the same producer, so ABV differences reflect ripeness — the warm legendary year may be 0.5% higher than the difficult year
- Avoid confusing a cool-year Bordeaux with New World lightness: the Bordeaux acid structure and graphite character should confirm the region before vintage dating begins

*Lesson:*
Bordeaux classified growth verticals are the gold standard for F7d vintage-knowledge testing in P2. The three-vintage structure of difficult (2013) + excellent (2015) + legendary (2005) creates a perfectly differentiated flight. ABV sameness at the same property means concentration and secondary/tertiary development carry the entire vintage-ID argument — the candidate cannot use a shortcut metric. The F7 tasting branch order (region lock → ladder placement → vintage naming → growing season narrative) is the correct answer structure for 20-mark vintage questions.
