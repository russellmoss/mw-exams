### Oak spectrum (the structural lens for this question)

The 8-mark oak question creates a deliberate spectrum across the four wines. The IMW selects wines at four distinct points:

1. **Commodity / cheap oak** — obvious vanilla/coconut, possible chip-stave, simple fruit; Australian commercial Shiraz is the archetype
2. **American oak, extended** — dill, vanilla, cedar, sweet spice, well integrated into Tempranillo structure; Rioja Reserva / Gran Reserva is the canonical example
3. **French barrique, mature** — cedar, tobacco, graphite, fully integrated; requires at least 10–15 years for Bordeaux classified growth
4. **Neutral / minimal new oak** — old vine fruit purity, no oak intrusion; Grenache bush vine in large old vessels

## IMW's likely intent
Testing the candidate's ability to taste and articulate oak influence across its full spectrum. The IMW wants nuanced commentary — not "this has oak" but type, age, vessel, integration, and impact on structure and commercial positioning. Commercial position spans from mass-market commodity ($8) to collectible fine wine ($100+), and candidates must speak in trade language: supermarket volume driver, on-premise by-the-glass, fine wine merchant, auction-grade.

## What to focus on in the glass

- Oak character first: vanilla/coconut/dill (American oak) vs. cedar/smoke/tobacco (French oak) vs. clean/pure fruit (neutral old vessels) vs. artificial/chippy (cheap stave oak)
- Tannin quality: silky and fine (French barrique integrated) vs. firm and grainy (American oak structured) vs. soft and fruity (no new oak)
- Fruit concentration: does the quality of fruit justify the oak treatment?
- Age markers: browning rim and tertiary aromatics (leather, dried fruit, cedar) = mature classified wine; deep purple and primary fruit = young commercial wine
- Alcohol: both Australian wines likely 13.5–14.5%; Bordeaux and Rioja likely 13–14%
