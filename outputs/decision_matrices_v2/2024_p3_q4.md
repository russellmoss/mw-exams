### Candidate sweet wine types (confidence tiers)

- Canadian Icewine (Vidal or Riesling, Ontario — Inniskillin): **STRONG SIGNAL** — extreme RS (180–320 g/L), very low alcohol (9–11%), freeze-concentration method; always appears in five-wine sweet mechanism lineups; the clearest single-mechanism marker in the flight.
- Austrian/German Beerenauslese or TBA (Burgenland Kracher / Mosel TBA): **STRONG SIGNAL** — botrytis-affected, medium-high RS (100–250 g/L), medium alcohol (10–12%); exam staple for the botrytis corner; Kracher (Burgenland, Welschriesling or Chardonnay-based blends) is the canonical producer.
- Vin Santo (Tuscany, Italy — Isole e Olena, Badia a Coltibuono): **STRONG SIGNAL** — passito/drying method; oxidative ageing in caratelli; higher alcohol without spirit heat (14–16%); amber colour; raisin/caramel/walnut profile.
- Muscat de Beaumes-de-Venise (Rhône, France — Domaine de Durban, Jaboulet): **STRONG SIGNAL** — VDN fortified via mutage; grapey/floral/peachy; ~15% alcohol; moderate RS (~100–120 g/L); fits the fortified corner without duplicating Sauternes (botrytis, already in Q3).
- Vouvray demi-sec (Loire, France — Domaine Huet, Clos du Bourg): **PLAUSIBLE** — lightest anchor wine in the flight; arrested fermentation or late-harvest Chenin Blanc; RS 20–50 g/L, moderate alcohol 11–13%; Domaine Huet is the recurring canonical producer in this question type across multiple years.
- Port as standalone: **CURVEBALL** — possible in the fortified slot; but Muscat de Beaumes-de-Venise is more distinctive and provides a different mechanism narrative (mutage of white grape vs. spirit-stopped red fermentation); IMW may save Port for a dedicated fortified question.
- Sauternes: **RULE-OUT** — already used in P3 Q3 of this paper; IMW does not repeat regions within the same paper.

### Rule-outs

- Sauternes: already used in P3 Q3; IMW does not repeat regions within the same paper.
- Dry wines: explicitly excluded by stem ("all have residual sugar").
- Sparkling sweet (Moscato d'Asti): the explicit RS/alcohol mark structure favours wines where RS and alcohol are diagnostically spread across a wide range; Moscato d'Asti at 5–6% ABV and ~120 g/L RS is possible but an unusual examiner choice when Vouvray demi-sec covers the "lightest sweet" corner more elegantly.
- Multiple fortified wines: the five-mechanism structure requires each slot to represent a different route to sweetness; two fortified wines would collapse two slots into one mechanism.

### IMW's likely intent

Testing the five mechanisms of sweetness and whether candidates can connect mechanism → RS level → alcohol level → region → grape variety. This is the definitive P3 "sweet wine knowledge" question. The RS and alcohol mark blocks are diagnostic tools: 200+ g/L RS and 9.5% ABV = frozen grapes; 100 g/L RS and 15% with spirit warmth = VDN fortified; 80 g/L RS and 15.5% without spirit burn = passito concentration. Candidates who understand the mechanistic logic can work backwards from the RS × alcohol coordinates to the mechanism, then to the origin.

### What to focus on in the glass

1. Estimate alcohol from warmth and weight per wine — this is step 1 of the F6 tasting sequence and the most important discriminator. Build a rough ranking across all five wines before writing any answers. Sub-12% = botrytis or freeze-concentration; 14–16% without spirit = passito; 15–16% with spirit warmth = VDN fortified; 11–13% moderate = arrested-fermentation late harvest.
2. Estimate RS from sweetness × acid balance per wine. Piercing electric acid = Icewine (acid preserved under cryoextraction); structured acid beneath sweetness = Tokaji or Austrian BA; rich but lower acid = passito; moderate sweetness with fresh acid = Vouvray demi-sec.
3. Plot all five wines on the RS × alcohol matrix before committing to any candidate assignment.
4. Choose the mechanism for each plotted position (step 4 of F6 sequence): freeze-concentration / botrytis / drying-passito / mutage-fortification / natural arrest.
5. Confirm via aromatics: petrol/lime (Riesling Icewine) or tropical (Vidal Icewine); honey/apricot/saffron (botrytis BA); raisin/fig/walnut/oxidative amber (Vin Santo); grapey/floral/peachy (Muscat VDN); quince/wax/lanolin (Chenin Blanc demi-sec).
6. Colour gradient as a cross-check: pale gold (Icewine), gold-amber (Austrian BA), deep amber (Vin Santo), gold-copper (Muscat de Beaumes-de-Venise), pale gold (Vouvray).
7. For the winemaking comment (10 marks per wine): the mechanism drives the essay — name the critical decision point (temperature of freeze, botrytis incidence, duration of drying, timing of spirit addition, moment of fermentation arrest) and explain how it translates to the wine in the glass.
