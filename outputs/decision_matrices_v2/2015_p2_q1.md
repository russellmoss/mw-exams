Wines 1-3 are made from Pinot Noir.

a) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (3 x 7 marks)

d) Comment on the commercial appeal. (3 x 5 marks)

### Comparative axis

Quality axis: village/regional versus premier/grand cru or equivalent. Commercial axis: collector/fine dining versus accessible restaurant versus broad retail.

## Family tree application (F1b)

### Branch 1.1 — Cross-country single variety
Triggered by: three wines, variety given, no origin restriction in stem.

Working prior from family pack: Pinot Noir is the most common F1b variety in P2. When marks heavily favour quality/maturity and commercial appeal (as here — 27 + 15 = 42 marks), Burgundy must anchor the flight as the classification reference point.

### Tasting branch order (F1)
1. Confirm Pinot Noir family on the most typical wine first (translucent ruby, red fruit, high acid, fine tannin).
2. Lock the grape — do not flip later.
3. Sub-tree on comparative axis: red-fruit weight → earth/forest vs cherry → oak handling → maturity.
4. Origin narrowed: Burgundy if restrained and earthy; Germany/Sonoma/Oregon/NZ on sliding ripeness scale.

### Branch-specific candidate narrowing
- Translucent ruby + red fruit + earth + high acid + fine tannin → Pinot Noir confirmed.
- Mushroom/forest floor + restraint → promote Burgundy. Eliminate Sonoma/Central Otago.
- Riper cherry/plum + sweeter oak + broader palate → survive NZ. Eliminate Burgundy village.
- Commercial appeal framing: Burgundy Premier Cru = premium on-trade/collector; NZ Pinot = accessible restaurant by-glass; California = broad retail/chain.
- Ability to improve: Burgundy Premier/Grand Cru most age-worthy; NZ moderate; commercial California/Australia drink sooner.

## Rule-outs
- All three New World: the commercial appeal and ability-to-improve discussion would lack a classification reference point.
- Champagne Pinot: Paper 2 is reds, not sparkling.
- Very obscure Pinot origins (Romania, England): commercial appeal question requires recognized market positions with established trade channels.
- Germany alone without Burgundy: German Pinot is increasingly tested but unlikely as the sole Old World anchor.

## IMW's likely intent
Origin discrimination within Pinot Noir — the hardest blind tasting skill, because Pinot's transparency means terroir shows through clearly. The variety is given so the candidate cannot hide behind varietal description. They must articulate what makes Côte de Nuits different from Marlborough, and why one will improve for 20 years while the other is at peak. The commercial appeal sub-question tests the trade side of MW knowledge: who buys Gevrey-Chambertin versus NZ Pinot, at what price points, for what occasions.

## What to focus on in the glass (family-pack guided)
- Colour translucency: lighter ruby-garnet (Burgundy) versus slightly deeper and more vibrant (NZ/Oregon).
- Nose: sous-bois/earth/mushroom = Burgundy development; red cherry/floral purity = NZ; riper red/black fruit = warmer climate.
- Tannin grain: Burgundy Premier Cru shows fine, granular, silky tannins; NZ tends toward clean and supple.
- Acidity: all Pinot has high acid; the shape (linear/angular = Burgundy; rounder = New World) is the key discriminator.
- Oak: subtle French oak integration (Burgundy); sometimes more obvious on NZ or Sonoma.
- Alcohol: below 13% (Burgundy/Germany); 13–14% (NZ/Oregon); above 14% (California/warmer sites).
