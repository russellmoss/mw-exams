# Decision matrix v2 — 2018 P1 Q3

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 11-12 are made from the same single grape variety.

With reference to both wines:
a) Identify the grape variety. (10 marks)

For each wine:
b) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (2 x 8 marks)

c) Comment on winemaking, quality and state of maturity. (2 x 12 marks)

**Variety lock:** single unnamed white grape, identified from both wines together

**Comparative axis:** style (dry vs. sweet), winemaking method, maturity / development stage

**Sub-form:** F1b — nominally cross-country, but in practice the corpus answer is intra-Germany two-style pair (Rheingau vs. Mosel)

**Candidate map (by slot):**

- Slot W11: STRONG SIGNAL — dry GG-tier Riesling, Old World, Germany. Most plausible origin: Rheingau or Franken.
- Slot W12: STRONG SIGNAL — aged sweet Auslese Riesling, Mosel. Vintage likely 2007 or older given maturity emphasis.

**F1b pre-taste decision logic:**

Context rule for P1 Q3 in 2018: Q1 used Chardonnay; Q2 eliminated Semillon, oxidative Rioja, Gruner, Soave, Chenin, Torrontés. The only major ageworthy white variety with dramatic maturity and production contrast not yet deployed is Riesling. Riesling is the only STRONG SIGNAL candidate remaining once context elimination is applied.

The tree's F1b cross-country candidate universe (Riesling: Mosel / Alsace / Clare / Eden / Washington / NY) maps here. The two-wine pair with a winemaking and maturity emphasis is the Rheingau Trocken GG vs. Mosel Auslese structure — exactly the contrast the IMW uses to test the full Riesling stylistic spectrum.

**Anti-collapse rule:** do not predict the same Riesling style (dry-dry or sweet-sweet) in both slots. The "state of maturity" instruction requires developmental difference; the winemaking question requires stylistic difference. Two bone-dry GGs from the same vintage do not generate 24 marks of differentiated winemaking/quality/maturity commentary.

**Single rule-out worth naming:** Chenin Blanc — ruled out by Q2 context; also less likely for a two-wine intra-Germany pair.

## Anti-collapse note

The two wines share Riesling as the bound variety, but every other dimension diverges: alcohol (13% vs. ~9%), residual sugar (bone dry vs. Auslese sweetness), origin (Rheingau/Franken vs. Mosel), winemaking philosophy (GG = dry terroir expression vs. Auslese = arrested fermentation + botrytis), and maturity stage (primary–secondary vs. fully secondary–tertiary). The candidate must articulate all five axes of contrast in part (c). Simply saying "one is dry, one is sweet" earns low partial marks. The winemaking paragraph must name arrested fermentation, botrytis, and the role of low alcohol in Mosel Auslese longevity.

## Reality check

*Actual wines:*
- W11: Kiedrich Gräfenberg, Riesling Trocken GG, Robert Weil, 2016. Rheingau, Germany. (13%)
- W12: Eitelsbacher Karthäuserhof Riesling Auslese, Karthäuserhofberg, 2007. Mosel, Germany. (9%)

**F1b family prediction: EXACT.** Riesling confirmed as bound variety. W11 = Rheingau GG (13%, bone dry, young-primary) as predicted; W12 = Mosel Auslese (9%, sweet, 2007 = 11 years old at 2018 exam, fully secondary-tertiary petrol/honey development) as predicted.

**Comparative axis accuracy:** dry vs. sweet, GG vs. Auslese, structured vs. delicate, primary vs. tertiary — all four predicted axes confirmed.

**F1b template performance:** the variety-lock-first then comparative-axis structure worked cleanly. The anti-collapse rule (no same-style prediction) was essential — without it a candidate might have listed two dry Rieslings.

**Lesson:** When P1 final-pair question asks "state of maturity" explicitly, the IMW almost always structures the pair as a dramatic developmental contrast, not a subtle one. For Riesling this means GG-vs-Auslese or dry-vs-sweet, not two wines from the same style category aged by three years. The 9% alcohol on W12 is the immediate glass signal that enforces this interpretation.
