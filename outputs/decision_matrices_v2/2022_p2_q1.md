# Decision matrix v2 — 2022 P2 Q1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-3 are from different countries and are each made from a different, single grape variety. Wine 4 is a blend of all three of these varieties.

For each wine 1-3:

a) Identify the grape variety and the origin as closely as possible. (3 x 15 marks)

b) Comment on the style, considering possible reasons for not blending the variety used for this wine. (3 x 10 marks)

For wine 4:

c) Comment on the purpose of blending these varieties with reference to balance and quality. (15 marks)

d) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (10 marks)

## Pre-taste matrix

### Universal header
- **Paper:** P2
- **Family:** F3d Blend / Composition Logic Set — *blend-vs-varietal-expression contrast* (three single varieties + a blend of those three)
- **Stem summary:** Three single-variety reds from three different countries + a fourth wine that is a blend of all three; rare structural pattern forces explicit varietal-role articulation.
- **Mark map:** singles a+b 15+10 each (75 total) · blend c+d 15+10 (25 total) = 100 total. Variety + origin per single = 15 (the single highest variety-and-origin mark band in the corpus).
- **Constraint:** three varieties must be commonly blended together; three different countries (each variety from its strongest single-varietal expression); the blend (W4) from the *origin where these three are habitually blended*.

### F3 pre-taste matrix
- **Blend family lock:** Bordeaux-variety family (Cabernet Sauvignon + Merlot + Cabernet Franc). The only three-variety canonical blend that simultaneously (a) has three globally famous single-varietal homes outside its origin, (b) has a clear "home blend" region (Bordeaux), and (c) is a benchmark MW exam pattern. GSM (Grenache + Syrah + Mourvèdre) is the only structural alternative but lacks Mourvèdre's single-variety prestige outside Bandol.
- **Sub-form:** F3d — blend-vs-varietal contrast.
- **Composition prior:** W4 will show the three Bordeaux grapes integrated; the marks reward articulation of what each variety contributes (structure from Cab Sauv; flesh and approachability from Merlot; aromatic lift and acid from Cab Franc).

- **Candidate map (by slot, independent):**
  - Slot W1: France — Loire (Cabernet Franc; Chinon / Saumur-Champigny; the global single-variety Cab Franc benchmark) → **STRONG SIGNAL**
  - Slot W2: USA — Napa Valley (Cabernet Sauvignon; Oakville-tier; the global single-variety Cab Sauv benchmark) → **STRONG SIGNAL**
  - Slot W3: Chile — Colchagua / Rapel (Merlot; the strongest single-variety Merlot identity outside Bordeaux right bank) → **STRONG SIGNAL**
  - Slot W4: France — Bordeaux (Pessac-Léognan or Médoc cru classé; the home blend) → **STRONG SIGNAL**
  - PLAUSIBLE swap-ins: Coonawarra Cab Sauv (W2 alternative); Hawke's Bay Merlot or NZ Cab Franc (W3 alternative); Italy Bolgheri (W4 alternative — but Bordeaux is the canonical home blend).
  - CURVEBALL: South African Cab Franc (Stellenbosch new wave) for W1; Argentinian Cab Sauv for W2.

- **Anti-collapse rule:** Each of the three singles must come from a different country (stem); none of the three should be from Bordeaux (the home-blend is reserved for W4); the four-wine assignment is: Cab Franc = Loire / USA = Cab Sauv / Chile = Merlot / Bordeaux = blend, in any order.

- **Default rule-outs:** Pinot Noir, Syrah, Tempranillo, Nebbiolo, Sangiovese (none are Bordeaux varieties); New World Meritage blend for W4 (the home blend is Bordeaux); GSM (only realistic alternative but fails the single-variety-prestige test for Mourvèdre).

- **Main traps:** Pattern-matching W4 to a New World Bordeaux blend; predicting Petit Verdot or Malbec single-varietals (off-template — the three varieties are the three classic Bordeaux backbones); under-using the *reason for not blending* prompt (Loire Cab Franc, Napa Cab Sauv, Chilean Merlot each have *terroir-specific reasons* not to blend — articulate each).

## Anti-overlink note
The blend lock is the single sanctioned link. The four wines test exactly the *opposite* of overlink discipline: the candidate must *explicitly link* the three single varieties to the W4 blend, articulating each variety's contribution. The matrix's pedagogy is "what does each grape contribute" — not "what is each grape independently."

Lesson:
A four-wine P2 Q1 stem of "three different countries / three different single varieties + one blend of those three" is the corpus signature for the F3d blend-vs-varietal-contrast question. The three varieties are *always* the Bordeaux backbones (Cab Sauv + Merlot + Cab Franc); the blend is *always* from Bordeaux itself; the three singles come from the three best non-Bordeaux single-varietal homes (Loire / Napa / Chile). This question is a near-fixed template — the matrix should focus on writing the *reason for not blending* (per single) and *reason for blending* (per W4) with varietal-role precision.
