# Decision matrix v2 — 2024 P1 Q1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-2, 3-4 and 5-6 are pairs. Each pair is made from the same single grape variety.

For each pair:

a) Identify the grape variety with reference to both wines. (3 x 10 marks)

b) Compare and contrast the quality, maturity, and capacity to age. (3 x 20 marks)

For each wine:

c) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (6 x 10 marks)

## Anti-collapse note

Three pairs, three independent variety identifications. The pair structure is designed to test whether the candidate can hold variety constant and then discriminate purely on quality, origin, and ageing arguments. The three main traps: (1) guessing Chardonnay for both pair 1 and pair 2 because oak/lees vocabulary overlaps with a structured Riesling GG; (2) guessing Loire Sauvignon Blanc for pair 3 when both wines are tropically Marlborough (the quality split is intra-region, not cross-country); (3) reading the Mosel Kabinett's low alcohol as an aromatic variety (Muscat, Pinot Gris) rather than Riesling. The 8% ABV + petrol + lime combination locks Riesling with high confidence.

- W1: Riesling Ürziger Würzgarten Kabinett, Dr. Loosen, 2022. Mosel, Germany. (8%)

- W6: Sauvignon Blanc Section 94, Dog Point, 2018. Marlborough, New Zealand. (14.5%)

**F1b family prediction: EXACT.** All three variety pairs (Riesling, Chardonnay, Sauvignon Blanc) correctly identified. All six origin predictions confirmed: Mosel Kabinett (W1), Franken GG (W2), Corton-Charlemagne Burgundy (W3), South Eastern Australia commercial (W4), Marlborough entry-level (W5), Marlborough premium Dog Point barrel (W6).

**Comparative axis accuracy:** alcohol split (8% vs. 13%) confirmed as Riesling pair diagnostic; Grand Cru vs. Yellow Tail confirmed as Chardonnay pair structure; Marlborough intra-region quality split confirmed over Loire-vs-NZ hypothesis.

**F1b template performance:** the variety-lock-first then comparative-axis structure worked perfectly for all three pairs. The Sauvignon Blanc intra-region quality split (Marlborough-vs-Marlborough) is now an explicitly named F1b sub-pattern; the template's anti-collapse rule prevented the common error of predicting Loire for W6 simply because both wines needed different origins.

**Lesson:** intra-region quality contrast is a structurally valid F1b variant. When both wines in a pair share obvious Marlborough tropical/capsicum fingerprints, resist forcing a cross-country explanation. The IMW's quality/maturity question works perfectly within one appellation when the quality gap is as wide as Cowrie Bay vs. Dog Point Section 94.
