# Decision matrix v2 — 2017 P3 Q1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-3 are rosés from three different countries.

For each wine:

a) Identify the origin as closely as possible with reference to the style. (3 x 7 marks)

b) Highlight the key winemaking techniques used. (3 x 8 marks)

c) Discuss the commercial appeal and market positioning. (3 x 10 marks)

## Pre-taste matrix

### Universal header

- **Stem summary:** Three rosé wines from three different countries; commercial positioning is the dominant mark band.
- **Mark map:** origin with style 7 each (21 total) · winemaking 8 each (24 total) · **commercial appeal + market positioning 10 each (30 total — dominant)** = 75 total.
- **Constraint:** category locked (rosé); three different countries; production methods almost certainly span three different rosé pathways.

### F4 pre-taste matrix
- **Pure breadth (F4a) / partly linked (F4b) / hidden theme (F4c):** F4a but with a category-bound theme (rosé). The cross-method nature still produces breadth-style independence between slots.
- **Theme (if any):** rosé production-spectrum + commercial-spectrum sweep. The flight is designed to span the full commercial range: mass-market sweet → benchmark dry premium → growth-narrative third country.

- **Slot ballots (independent, no cross-inference):**
  - Slot W1: USA — White Zinfandel (California, Sutter Home–style) → **STRONG SIGNAL**. Mass-market off-dry, arrested fermentation, low ABV.
  - Slot W2: France — Provence dry rosé (Côtes de Provence or premium tier) → **STRONG SIGNAL**. Direct-press benchmark; the global premium dry-rosé reference.
  - Slot W3: New Zealand — sparkling rosé (Cloudy Bay Pelorus or analogous) → **PLAUSIBLE**. The corpus-recurring "one wine may be sparkling even if the stem says rosé" twist.
  - PLAUSIBLE swap-ins for W3: Spanish rosado (Navarra Garnacha-led), Italian rosato (Cerasuolo d'Abruzzo).
  - CURVEBALL: South African Pinotage rosé, Argentine Malbec rosé.

- **Hard rule:** "Do not infer wine N from wine N−1." The three rosés may be still / still / sparkling, dry / off-dry / dry-sparkling, or any other combination; each slot is assessed independently.

- **Default rule-outs:** All-European dry rosés (loses the commercial-spectrum contrast); fortified or sweet non-rosé categories (stem says rosé).

- **Main traps:** Assuming all three are still (one is often sparkling); under-rating the White Zinfandel commercial story (the IMW deliberately includes the world's biggest commodity rosé brand because the 10-mark commercial band rewards knowledge of mass-market positioning); writing the commercial band in generic adjectives instead of channel-and-demographic language.

- **Style / acid / body / sugar / alcohol:** Still rosé · med acid · light body · off-dry → medium · ~9–10%.
- **Method family in one phrase:** White Zinfandel — arrested fermentation rosé from Zinfandel grape.
- **Best-guess origin:** USA — California (Central Valley / Lodi).
- **Best-guess variety:** Zinfandel (run as a white-style rosé).
- **Branch-B alternative:** Other US commercial pink (Mateus-style if Portuguese signatures appear) — unlikely given stem says "rosés from three different countries."

Opportunity: huge volume; challenge: declining mass-market relevance as drinkers trade up to Provence-style dry rosés.

### W2
- **Snapshot:** Pale salmon / onion-skin, dry, red-currant / strawberry / Mediterranean herb / garrigue, medium body.
- **Style / acid / body / sugar / alcohol:** Still rosé · med acid · medium body · dry · ~13%.
- **Method family in one phrase:** Provence direct-press dry rosé.
- **Best-guess origin:** France — Provence — Côtes de Provence (or Coteaux d'Aix / Bandol Rosé at premium tier).
- **Best-guess variety:** Grenache + Cinsault + Mourvèdre + Syrah + Vermentino (Rolle) blend.
- **Branch-B alternative:** Tavel (deeper colour, fuller body) if structurally heavier; Cru Classé Provence if premium polish appears.
- **Method comment:** Whole-bunch direct press of red grapes — very short juice-skin contact yields the pale salmon colour; cool fermentation; reductive handling preserves primary fruit + herb.
- **Quality tier:** Standard to premium Côtes de Provence.
- **Commercial position:** Global benchmark dry rosé; explosive growth ("Whispering Angel effect"); on-trade and off-trade premium tier; lifestyle / aspirational drinker, especially younger urban; channel = wine-shop and on-premise; ~$15–25. Opportunity: category-leading premium rosé brand prestige; challenge: price-tier creep risking commoditisation at the top end.

### W3
- **Snapshot:** Pale-mid pink, **fine persistent mousse**, brioche / red-fruit / autolytic, dry, medium body.
- **Style / acid / body / sugar / alcohol:** **Sparkling rosé** · high acid · medium body · Brut · ~12.5%.
- **Method family in one phrase:** Traditional-method sparkling rosé — NZ Champagne-method.
- **Best-guess origin:** New Zealand — Marlborough (Cloudy Bay Pelorus or similar).
- **Best-guess variety:** Pinot Noir + Chardonnay (Champagne varieties).
- **Branch-B alternative:** Crémant rosé from France if French signature appears; English sparkling rosé as a less-likely alternative.
- **Method comment:** Saignée or short skin maceration for pink colour; second fermentation in bottle on lees; extended autolysis; dosage to Brut.
- **Quality tier:** Premium NZ sparkling — Cloudy Bay's prestige range.
- **Commercial position:** Mid-premium global on-trade gift / brunch / celebration; ~$30; aspirational drinker; channel = independent retail and on-premise. Opportunity: NZ premium-sparkling growth story riding the Marlborough brand halo; challenge: distance from Champagne pricing power.

## Anti-overlink note
The three-way commercial spectrum is the engine of this question. The matrix's 10-mark commercial band must articulate three different *channels and demographics* — not just three different price points.

Actual wines (from data/exams.json):

- W2: Billette Rosé, Pradel, 2015. Provence, France. (13%)

Pre-taste accuracy:

- White Zinfandel + Provence + NZ sparkling rosé — exact match.
- The "one wine may be sparkling" twist played (W3 = Pelorus sparkling rosé).

In-taste accuracy:
- W1 Sutter Home White Zinfandel 9.5%: confirms low-ABV arrested-fermentation mass-market.
- W2 Pradel Provence Rosé 13%: confirms direct-press dry premium.
- W3 Cloudy Bay Pelorus Rosé 12.5%: confirms traditional-method sparkling rosé.

Lesson:
A three-rosé cross-country stem with heavy commercial marks is the corpus signature for the commercial-spectrum sweep. The default working prior is mass-market USA / benchmark France / sparkling NZ-or-other. The matrix should name channels and demographics in the commercial band — generic "good commercial appeal" wording loses marks. Always allow one slot for a sparkling rosé even when the stem says "rosés" without "still."
