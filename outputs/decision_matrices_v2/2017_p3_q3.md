### Structural axis

This is a compressed F6a question: two wines placed at two positions on the RS × alcohol axis, both above the dry threshold. The structural spread is less dramatic than a five-wine survey but the axis logic still applies — the two wines should sit at meaningfully different points (otherwise the RS sub-question would be trivial).

| Position | RS band | Alcohol band | Expected variety / style |
|---|---|---|---|
| Off-dry / medium-sweet | 20–80 g/L | 9–11% | Riesling Spätlese or Kabinett, or New World off-dry Riesling |
| Medium-sweet / sweet | 40–120 g/L | 9–13% | Gewürztraminer Late Harvest; or Muscat |

Anti-collapse rule: two aromatic whites with RS quantified implies two varieties whose RS and alcohol vary meaningfully. Riesling and Gewürztraminer are the canonical pair because their RS and alcohol ranges diverge enough to fill two distinct mark-bank answers.

### Candidate varieties (confidence tiers)

- Riesling (off-dry or lightly sweet): **STRONG SIGNAL** — RS and alcohol quantification is the defining Riesling examination skill; acid structure masks sweetness making sensory estimation challenging and thus mark-worthy; commercial market for off-dry Riesling (New World off-dry, Mosel Kabinett/Spätlese) is a legitimate discussion topic.
- Gewürztraminer (Late Harvest or off-dry): **STRONG SIGNAL** — pairs naturally with Riesling as the second classic aromatic off-dry variety; lychee/rose/oriental spice aromatics are distinctive enough for an 8-mark variety ID; lower acid than Riesling means the wine feels richer, providing palate contrast.
- Muscat/Moscato: **PLAUSIBLE** — Moscato d'Asti is low alcohol and sweet; but both varieties in the flight would be within the Muscat family, which would collapse the "different varieties" requirement into one aromatic type.
- Chenin Blanc (demi-sec Vouvray): **PLAUSIBLE** — possible P3 off-dry candidate; but less commonly paired with Riesling in a two-wine structure, and commercial potential of demi-sec Vouvray is a narrower topic than off-dry Riesling.
- Pinot Gris (Alsace): **CURVEBALL** — off-dry or late harvest expression is plausible; but RS × alcohol testing is less canonically associated with Pinot Gris than with Riesling or Gewürztraminer.

### Candidate origins per slot

- Wine 5 (Riesling axis): Germany (Mosel Spätlese, ~10% ABV, ~40–70 g/L RS — Mosel-Saar-Ruwer) or New Zealand (Giesen or similar off-dry, ~10% ABV, commercial off-dry style); the NZ option is consistent with the tree's note that "New World origins are common for this structure."
- Wine 6 (Gewürztraminer axis): Alsace (Gewürztraminer Late Harvest, ~12% ABV, ~80–120 g/L RS) or California cool-climate (Anderson Valley, Navarro or Husch, ~11% ABV, ~60–100 g/L RS); California Late Harvest Gewürztraminer is a recognised commercial category with clear market positioning.

### Rule-outs

- Fully dry wines: the explicit RS g/L sub-question would be trivial (0–2 g/L) and unworthy of 4 marks per wine; the question is designed to test sensory estimation of sweetness levels.
- Fortified wines: spirit heat would render the alcohol question obvious rather than diagnostic; covered in a separate P3 question.
- Chardonnay or Sauvignon Blanc: not associated with RS quantification as a primary examination skill; commercial potential essay would be generic rather than RS-mechanism-specific.
- Two wines from the same aromatic family (e.g. two Muscats): the stem explicitly states "different single grape varieties."

### IMW's likely intent

Testing the practical skill of estimating residual sugar and alcohol from the palate — a core MW tasting competency that the IMW considers diagnostically important for aromatic off-dry whites. The two varieties are chosen to produce a contrast in sweetness-acid balance: the Riesling will show high acid moderating its perceived sweetness; the Gewürztraminer will have lower acid and higher aromatic weight, making the sweetness feel more prominent at a comparable RS level. The commercial question (13 marks each) asks about the market for off-dry and sweet aromatic whites — a sector with significant commercial volume but often misunderstood positioning.

Per `p3_special_tree.md` Layer B, sort each wine by appearance into a production family before working the per-wine deductions below.

| Wine | What you see | Gate tripped | Family routed to |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| W5 | Pale green-gold (young), no bubbles, not pink, thin-to-normal tears | Gate 3 hue — pale lemon/straw white | Sweet-wine (off-dry white lane) |
| W6 | Deeper gold, no bubbles, not pink, slightly fuller tears | Gate 3 hue — deeper gold, white spectrum | Sweet-wine (late-harvest white lane) |

**Confirmation cue (Step 2):** neither is sparkling (Gate 1) or pink (Gate 2); both sit in the white spectrum at Gate 3. The discriminator here is RS-vs-acid and weight, not colour — high acid compressing perceived sweetness at low ABV = Riesling (W5); lower acid, riper aromatic weight, deeper gold and higher RS = Late Harvest Gewürztraminer (W6). No spirit heat on either rules out fortified.

1. Estimate alcohol from warmth and weight: sub-11% (likely off-dry, low-intervention) vs. 11–13% (late harvest, more concentrated).
2. Estimate RS from sweetness-to-acid balance: Riesling acid actively compresses perceived sweetness; Gewürztraminer's lower acid lets sweetness project more openly.
3. Plot each wine on the axis: Riesling = lower-RS / lower-alcohol corner; Gewürztraminer = higher-RS / higher-alcohol corner.
4. Variety confirmation via aromatics: petrol/lime/slate = Riesling; lychee/rose/oriental spice/Turkish delight = Gewürztraminer.
5. Colour: pale green-gold (Riesling, especially young); deeper gold (Gewürztraminer late harvest, especially Alsace).
6. For the commercial potential essay: think category (off-dry aromatic), price point, food-pairing positioning, and whether the style is fashionable or niche in current trade.

Lesson from F6a re-map: On a two-wine F6a question, the structural axis still applies but the spread is narrower than a five-wine survey. Both wines sit in the off-dry-to-medium-sweet zone rather than spanning the full matrix from Kabinett to TBA. The commercial potential mark band (13/25 marks per wine) signals that the IMW wants market-aware writing: what does off-dry Riesling or Late Harvest Gewürztraminer mean commercially in the current on-trade and retail landscape? The RS and alcohol quantification (4 marks) is the technical anchor; the commercial essay is the payoff.
