## F4 tasting branch order (per wine)

1. Colour and intensity — pale garnet vs. inky deep purple immediately separates Pinot from Malbec
2. Aroma family (red/black fruit weight, floral, spice, earth)
3. Structure (tannin grain, acid, alcohol)
4. Climate signal — cool restraint (France) vs. warm fruit weight (Argentina)
5. Best-match grape for colour + aroma + structure
6. Best-match origin for that grape × climate reading
7. Quality tier and commercial positioning
8. Reset before next wine

### F4 branch-specific narrowing cues

- Translucent ruby + red fruit + earth + high acid + fine tannin → Pinot Noir. France if more restraint and earth; Argentina if riper cherry and wider body.
- Deep purple / inky + plush dark fruit + violet + moderate-high tannin → Malbec. France (Cahors) if firmer, darker, more rustic, higher alcohol (15%+); Argentina (Mendoza) if floral, polished, sunny.

## IMW's likely intent
Testing whether the candidate can identify two key varieties and recognize that a variety can originate in one country while being more commercially famous in another. The Cahors/Cot trap is intentional — candidates who know Malbec only as Argentine may struggle with the French expression. Commercial position spans from rustic Cahors (niche, value, challenging) to polished premium Mendoza Malbec (internationally distributed, award-winning).

## What to focus on in the glass

- Establish colour first: pale / translucent garnet = Pinot Noir; inky deep purple = Malbec
- Within each variety: the French expression will be more restrained, mineral, or rustic vs. the New World expression
- Cahors tells: very dark, firm tannins, iron/earthy character, very different from Argentine Malbec (which is floral and plush)
- Patagonian Pinot: lighter style than Burgundy but with some ripe fruit; cooler-climate but not as austere; confirm via structure not just alcohol
- Alcohol: Cahors can be 15%+ in warm years — highest ABV in the set is a Cahors tell
