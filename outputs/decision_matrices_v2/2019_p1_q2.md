# Decision matrix v2 — 2019 P1 Q2

For each wine:

a) Identify the origin and grape variety(ies) as closely as possible. (6 x 12 marks)

c) Comment on the quality. (6 x 6 marks)

### F4 pre-taste matrix

**Classification: Partly linked (F4b)** — each country contributes two wines. Within each pair, the country is the anchor; variety, region, and winemaking are the discrimination targets. Across pairs, do not let the first country identification pull the whole flight toward a single geographic direction.

**Theme filter:** The "paired by country" structure rewards candidates who know two distinct white wine styles from each of three countries — a pair where both wines are closely matched in style would be a poor exam question. Expect maximum within-pair contrast (oxidative vs. fresh; high alcohol vs. low; aromatic vs. neutral; oaked vs. unoaked).

**Context elimination (prior questions):**

## Anti-collapse note

F4b anti-overlink discipline: This is a partly-linked flight — three country pairs, not six independent wines. The country anchor within each pair is the permitted inference. No cross-pair inference is permitted: identifying Spain (W5-6) does not provide information about Italy (W7-8) or Australia (W9-10). The most dangerous trap in F4b is letting the first identified country "seed" a geographic narrative for the rest of the flight. The within-pair contrast is deliberate and maximal in each case (oxidative Rioja vs. fresh Albarino; neutral Sicilian dry vs. sweet Piedmontese frizzante; lean Riesling vs. creamy Chardonnay). The Moscato d'Asti in W8 is the sharpest curveball: a sweet, low-alcohol wine appearing in P1 (nominally dry whites). Do not re-classify it as a dry wine under exam pressure — the 5.5% ABV is the unmistakable diagnostic.
