## F4 tasting branch order (per wine)

1. Colour and intensity — pale garnet (Xinomavro, Nebbiolo) vs. medium ruby (Gamay, Zweigelt) vs. deep purple (Touriga Nacional, Barbera)
2. Aroma family (black fruit/violet = Touriga; crunchy red fruit = Gamay; cherry/almond = Barbera; tomato/olive/savory = Xinomavro; blue-black + alpine = Zweigelt)
3. Structure (tannin grain, acid, alcohol)
4. Climate signal (cool/moderate Europe — no warm-climate New World applies)
5. Best-match grape for colour + aroma + structure combination
6. Best-match origin for grape × regional signature
7. Quality in global context (can this grape compete at benchmark level?)
8. Commercial appeal (who buys this, through what channel, trend direction)
9. Reset before next wine

### F4 branch-specific narrowing cues (F4c indigenous European form)

- Deep purple + black fruit / violet + massive tannin + high alcohol (14%+) → Touriga Nacional. Douro if old-vine concentration.
- Blue-black fruit + alpine freshness + medium body + lower tannin than Touriga → Zweigelt. Austria if structured but juicy.
- Deep colour + very high acid + low tannin for colour depth + cherry / almond → Barbera. Piedmont if warm cherry + some oak.
- Translucent ruby + crunchy red fruit + low tannin + juicy acid + possible carbonic hint → Gamay. Beaujolais if primary fruit purity; cru Beaujolais if more structured.
- Pale to medium garnet + tomato / olive / savory red fruit + firm acid + firm tannin → Xinomavro. Greece if high acid and austere structure match Naoussa or Amyndeon profile.

## Rule-outs

- Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Pinot Noir, Syrah: too "international" — not "closely associated with one origin" in the narrow sense the stem intends
- New World countries: explicitly excluded
- Two varieties from the same country: IMW typically spreads five varieties across five countries for maximum breadth; one country can contribute two if the varieties are sufficiently different (e.g. Barbera + Nebbiolo both from Italy), but this is less likely than a five-country circuit
- Tempranillo, Sangiovese, Nebbiolo: technically qualify as "closely associated with origin," but the stem's five-country logic more naturally points to one per country; Tempranillo (Spain) and Sangiovese (Italy) are covered well by the other Italian/Spanish choices

## IMW's likely intent
Testing whether the candidate knows European indigenous red grapes beyond the Big 5. The "quality globally" sub-question forces candidates to articulate whether a Xinomavro or Zweigelt can compete with a top Bordeaux or Burgundy — this is a critical market awareness question. Commercial appeal is the primary mark-earner on the back half: who buys these wines, through what channels, and what are the growth or headwind stories? Gamay (Beaujolais market recovery), Xinomavro (niche but growing premium positioning), Touriga Nacional (establishing international table wine identity beyond Port), Zweigelt (Austrian domestic strength, limited export presence), Barbera (reliable mid-market with quality upside).

## What to focus on in the glass

- Tannin and acid together: very high acid + high tannin → Nebbiolo or Xinomavro; very high acid + low tannin → Barbera or Gamay; high tannin + violet fruit → Touriga Nacional
- Colour depth: pale garnet (Xinomavro or Nebbiolo) vs. deep purple (Touriga Nacional, Barbera)
- Aromatic markers: tar/roses (Nebbiolo), tomato/olive/savory (Xinomavro), black fruit/violet (Touriga Nacional), crunchy red fruit/banana (Gamay), cherry/almond/high acid (Barbera)
- Alcohol: Gamay (12–13%) as the low anchor; Touriga Nacional (14%+) as the high anchor; Xinomavro (14%) as structurally austere despite high alcohol
- Carbonic maceration: banana/bubblegum notes confirm Beaujolais Gamay (whole-bunch/carbonic); more structured and mineral suggests cru Beaujolais
- Freshness and alpine quality: confirms Austrian Zweigelt over Barbera if the wine has an elegant cool lift
