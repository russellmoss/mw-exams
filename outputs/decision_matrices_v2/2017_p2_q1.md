### Branch: F4a with shared dominant variety
This question nominally resembles F1b (same variety, cross-country) but carries F4a characteristics: the individual wines are from independent contexts and should not be inferred from each other. The shared anchor (dominant variety) is the prize, and the diversity axis (four countries) is the examination of breadth.

**Critical modifier — blending language:** "Dominant" rather than "single" plus the explicit winemaking sub-question asking about "other grape varieties" signals that blending partners exist. Among the STRONG SIGNAL P2 varieties:
- Pinot Noir is almost never blended — argued against.
- Syrah can be blended (Côte-Rôtie adds Viognier; Barossa Shiraz/Cab exists).
- Cabernet Sauvignon is the pre-eminent global blending variety (Bordeaux/Merlot+Cab Franc; Australia/Shiraz; South Africa/Cape Bordeaux blend).

This elevates Cabernet Sauvignon from PLAUSIBLE to the primary call.

### Rule-outs
- Pinot Noir: rarely blended; "dominant" + blending language argues strongly against
- Syrah/Shiraz: four-country cross-country distribution with Shiraz as the dominant is less natural than for Cabernet; Shiraz works better as a blend partner in Australia
- Merlot: rarely dominant across four countries except Right Bank Bordeaux
- Grenache: blending culture exists but four-country dominant-variety framing is harder to justify

### IMW's likely intent
Global Cabernet Sauvignon literacy across four distinct climatic and cultural contexts. The blending-partner and oak sub-questions test whether candidates know: Bordeaux uses French oak + Merlot/Cab Franc assemblage; Australian Cab/Shiraz uses American oak; Napa often deploys high proportions of new French oak; Stellenbosch Cape blends echo Bordeaux in form but with warmer-climate weight. The quality range from commercial Australian to Cru Classé Bordeaux is an enormous span — the maturity/potential sub-question tests whether candidates can read a 20-year-old Bordeaux alongside a 3-year-old Australian commercial.
