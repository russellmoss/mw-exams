# Decision matrix v2 — 2015 P3 Q3 (F6a re-map)

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 9-12 are from four different countries.

For each wine:

a) State alcohol level (%). (4 x 2 marks)

b) Place a tick in the appropriate box for the residual sugar. (4 x 2 marks)

c) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (4 x 5 marks)

d) From what you can detect in the glass, describe the method of production from vineyard to bottle. (4 x 10 marks)

e) Comment on quality and maturity. (4 x 6 marks)

### Structural axis

F6a operates on the RS × alcohol matrix. Four wines from four countries spread across the axis:

| Position | RS band | Alcohol band | Expected mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|
| Low RS / low alc | ~50–80 g/L | 7–9% | Natural arrest or late-harvest (Mosel Spätlese) |
| Very high RS / low alc | 200–350 g/L | 8–10% | Freeze-concentration (Icewine/Eiswein) |
| High RS / mid alc | 100–180 g/L | 10–13% | Noble rot/botrytis (Tokaji Aszú) |
| Med-high RS / high alc | 80–150 g/L | 13–17% | Drying/passito (Vin Santo) |

Anti-collapse rule: spread predictions across at least three distinct positions on the axis. Do not put two wines in the botrytis corner.

### Candidate categories (confidence tiers)

- German Riesling Spätlese or Auslese (Mosel/Rheingau): **STRONG SIGNAL** — botrytis or physiological late harvest; very low alcohol (7–9%); textbook RS contrast anchor; citrus/petrol/slate aromatic profile.
- Canadian Riesling or Vidal Icewine (Ontario): **STRONG SIGNAL** — freeze-concentration; low alcohol (8–10%); extreme RS (200–350 g/L); piercing acid; tropical or citrus-lime intensity.
- Tokaji Aszú 4–6 puttonyos (Hungary): **STRONG SIGNAL** — noble rot; medium alcohol (10–13%); saffron/orange peel/ginger; structured acid beneath thick sweetness.
- Vin Santo (Tuscany, Italy): **STRONG SIGNAL** — passito drying on racks; higher alcohol without spirit heat (13–17%); oxidative amber character; raisin/dried fig/walnut.
- Sauternes (France, botrytis): **PLAUSIBLE** — overlap with Tokaji in mechanism; less likely given Tokaji already occupies the botrytis slot and four-country spread prefers mechanism diversity.
- Austrian BA/TBA (Burgenland): **PLAUSIBLE** — overlaps with German slot; less likely in a four-country structure if Germany is already assigned.

### Candidate origins per slot

- Wine 10 — Canada, Ontario: Riesling or Vidal Icewine (Inniskillin); ~9–10% ABV; ~200–350 g/L RS; tropical or lime-citrus; electric acid; pale gold.
- Wine 11 — Hungary, Tokaj: Aszú 5 puttonyos (Royal Tokaji, Disznókő); ~11–12% ABV; ~120–180 g/L RS; saffron, orange peel, ginger, dried apricot; structured acid.

### Rule-outs

- Fortified sweet wines (Port, VDN Muscat, Rutherglen): alcohol would push 17–20%+; the explicit RS tick-box and the question's four-country spread do not fit a fortified-dominant flight — and a candidate who assigns two wines to "spirit heat" range cannot fill four distinct country positions.
- Sparkling sweet wines: mousse would dominate the production description question; P3 Q1 typically handles sparkling.
- Sauternes alongside Tokaji: two botrytis wines would collapse the mechanism spread and repeat the same vineyard-to-bottle production narrative — the examiner builds a question to test four different essays.

### IMW's likely intent

Testing whether the candidate can infer the sweetness production route from the wine in the glass rather than from a label. The vineyard-to-bottle production description (10 marks × 4 = 40 total) rewards articulation of: harvest decisions and conditions (botrytis selection, freezing temperatures, drying racks), fermentation management (natural arrest by sugar weight, cold, minimal intervention), ageing (oxidative for Vin Santo; stainless for Icewine), and how each route produces its distinctive sweet-acid balance. The explicit alcohol and RS tick-boxes force the candidate to be precise before attempting the longer production narrative.

### What to focus on in the glass

1. Estimate alcohol from warmth and weight (step 1 of the F6 tasting branch order): very low (7–9%) = Mosel axis; low (9–11%) = Icewine or Tokaji; higher (13–17%) = Vin Santo.
2. Estimate RS from sweetness-to-acid balance (step 2): piercing acid masking sweetness = Riesling or Icewine; structured acid beneath sweetness = Tokaji; rich but lower acid = Vin Santo.
3. Plot each wine on the RS × alcohol matrix (step 3) before assigning any candidate.
4. Choose the mechanism that fits the plotted point (step 4): freeze-concentration, botrytis, drying, late-harvest.
5. Colour as confirmation: pale gold (German Riesling); golden amber (Icewine, Tokaji); deep amber-orange (Vin Santo from oxidative ageing).
6. Aromatic profile as confirmation: petrol/citrus slate (Riesling); tropical/stone-fruit intensity (Icewine); saffron/marmalade/ginger (Tokaji); raisin/caramel/walnut (Vin Santo).

F6a axis accuracy: all four wines land exactly at the predicted positions on the RS × alcohol matrix. The four-mechanism structure (late-harvest Riesling / freeze-concentration Icewine / botrytis Tokaji / passito Vin Santo) was the correct template.

Lesson from F6a re-map: This question is the cleanest single example of the sweetness/RS axis in the corpus. Four wines, four countries, four distinct positions on the matrix. When the stem explicitly asks for alcohol and RS as separate mark sub-questions on a four-country P3 flight, the F6a template (spread-axis, one mechanism per position) is correct. The production essay (40/100 marks) is the primary payoff — a candidate who can name the mechanism from the RS × alcohol position will write four distinct 10-mark narratives rather than recycling botrytis language.
