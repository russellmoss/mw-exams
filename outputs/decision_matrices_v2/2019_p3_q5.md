# Decision matrix v2 — 2019 P3 Q5 (F6a re-map)

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 11-12 come from two different countries.

For both wines:

a) Compare and contrast the method of production. (14 marks)

For each wine:

b) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (2 x 7 marks)

c) Comment on quality and maturity. (2 x 7 marks)

d) State the level of residual sugar (g/l). (2 x 2 marks)

e) State the level of alcohol (%). (2 x 2 marks)

### Structural axis

The "compare and contrast" framing is the diagnostic signal: the examiner needs two wines at opposite ends of the RS × alcohol axis so the contrast essay has content. A passito (unfortified, drying method, ~14–15% ABV, ~200+ g/L RS) vs. a spirit-fortified sweet (17–19% ABV, very high RS) produces the maximum diagnostic contrast — different RS, meaningfully different alcohol, and completely different production narratives.

| Position | RS band | Alcohol band | Mechanism | Typical origin |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Very high RS / mid-high alc (no spirit) | 200–300 g/L | 14–15% | Drying/passito, unfortified | Italy (Sicily/Pantelleria, Tuscany, Veneto) |
| Very high RS / high alc (spirit) | 400–550 g/L | 17–19% | Spirit fortification + fractional ageing | Australia (Rutherglen), France (VDN) |

Anti-collapse rule: both wines are sweet, but they must not share a mechanism. Do not collapse both into botrytis; do not collapse both into fortification. The contrast essay only works if the mechanisms are categorically different.

### Candidate styles (confidence tiers)

- Italian passito (Sicily/Pantelleria — Zibibbo/Muscat of Alexandria; or Tuscany — Vin Santo; or Veneto — Recioto di Soave) + Australian fortified Muscat (Rutherglen — Campbells, Morris, Stanton & Killeen): **STRONG SIGNAL** — the passito (dried grape, no fortification, ~14–15% ABV, ~200+ g/L RS) vs. fortified Muscat (spirit addition + fractional blending = 17–19% ABV, very high RS) provides the ideal production contrast; two countries, fundamentally different mechanisms, significantly different alcohol levels; the combined 14-mark production comparison essay has maximal content.
- Italian Vin Santo + French VDN (Muscat de Beaumes-de-Venise or Banyuls): **PLAUSIBLE** — similar contrast logic but slightly less cleanly differentiated commercially; Vin Santo is passito-adjacent; French VDN is fortified but less extreme in concentration than Rutherglen.
- German TBA (botrytis, very low alcohol ~8%) + Port (fortified, ~19–20%): **CURVEBALL** — the alcohol contrast would be extreme (8% vs 20%); possible but the botrytis vs. fortification pairing is less commonly used in this format; origin discussion for German TBA and Port would be straightforward and not require much comparative production writing.

### Candidate origins per slot

- Wine 12 — Australia (Rutherglen, Victoria): Rutherglen Muscat; Campbells, Morris, or Stanton & Killeen NV; Muscat Blanc à Petits Grains; spirit fortification + fractional blending through Solera-adjacent system; ~17–18% ABV; ~400–550 g/L RS; dark tawny-amber; sticky raisined fruit, coffee, caramel, rancio, dried orange peel.

### Rule-outs

- Dry fortified wines (Fino, Amontillado, dry Madeira): explicit RS sub-question rules these out — a bone-dry wine would score 0 g/L and waste the sub-question marks.
- Sparkling sweet wines: Q1 and Q2 in this paper already covered sparkling (Champagne and Prosecco respectively); mousse would dominate the production narrative.
- Two botrytis wines from different countries: the production comparison would yield near-identical essays (selective harvest of botrytised grapes, low temperature fermentation, natural arrest); the contrast at 14 marks demands mechanistically distinct processes.
- Two fortified wines: the production comparison would similarly converge (spirit added to arrest fermentation); the contrast only generates full marks if the two mechanisms are categorically different.

### IMW's likely intent

Testing the candidate's ability to distinguish naturally concentrated sweet wine from spirit-fortified sweetness, then tie that structural difference to alcohol level, texture, and commercial use. The joint 14-mark production comparison sub-question is the payoff: the examiner wants a clear explanation of desiccation/drying (sun, wind, or hanging) with natural fermentation arrested by sugar weight vs. spirit addition that terminates fermentation by alcohol toxicity, followed by extended fractional ageing in small casks. The RS and alcohol numbers are asked separately because they are diagnostic: a candidate who estimates ~250 g/L RS and ~14.5% ABV for the passito, and ~500 g/L RS and ~17.5% ABV for the Rutherglen, demonstrates mechanical understanding even before writing the comparison essay.

### What to focus on in the glass

1. Estimate alcohol from warmth and weight — this is step 1 of the F6 tasting sequence and the most important discriminator: sub-16% (likely passito/unfortified) vs. 17–19% (fortified); the spirit warmth on the back palate is unmistakable.
2. Estimate RS from viscosity, sweetness perception, and acid balance; both wines are very sweet but Rutherglen Muscat typically shows more extreme concentration.
3. Plot both wines on the RS × alcohol matrix: passito = very high RS / mid-high alc; Rutherglen = very high RS / high alc with spirit.
4. Confirm the mechanism: passito = "fresh-dried" dried apricot, fig, honey, citrus peel; Rutherglen = sticky raisin, coffee, caramel, rancio, hot marmalade with oxidative ageing profile.
5. Colour as confirmation: both amber-gold; Rutherglen may be darker tawny-brown from fractional blending and long cask ageing.
6. For the production comparison essay: the passito process (drying of grapes → concentration of sugars and flavours → slow unfortified fermentation arrested naturally) contrasts point-for-point with the Rutherglen process (fortification timing → spirit addition → fractional cask blending → extreme concentration of RS, alcohol, and extract over decades).

The candidate-specific producers (Donnafugata / Campbells) were predicted as top-confidence examples.

Lesson from F6a re-map: The "compare and contrast" joint sub-question is the clearest F6a trigger in the corpus. When the examiner writes a single 14-mark production comparison question spanning two wines, those two wines will sit at opposite ends of the RS × alcohol matrix — one unfortified, one fortified — so the comparison generates categorical contrast rather than degrees-of-difference. The RS and ABV quantification sub-questions (2 marks each) are diagnostic anchors: if a candidate plots 14.5% vs 17% correctly, the production comparison essay almost writes itself.
