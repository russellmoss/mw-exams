### Variety lock
F1b sub-form: Branch 1.1 — Cross-country single (or predominant) variety. The "or predominant" wording is the decisive branch signal — it elevates Cabernet Franc above the default PLAUSIBLE tier.

The tree note explains why: Chinon and Bourgueil are legally defined as Cabernet Franc-dominant but may incorporate up to 10% Cabernet Sauvignon or Côt alongside the dominant grape, making "predominant" the accurate legal description. No other variety in the P2 F1b STRONG SIGNAL set requires this hedge:
- Pinot Noir is virtually always 100% (the hedge is unnecessary)
- Syrah/Shiraz is 100% in Northern Rhône and Barossa (the two key exam origins)
- Cabernet Sauvignon is 100% in Napa and most exam-quality New World wines

- **Cabernet Franc: STRONG SIGNAL** (elevated from PLAUSIBLE in the Phase 5A matrix) — uniquely spans cool Loire (12.5%, herbal/pyrazine, crunchy red fruit), moderate Argentina (13.5%, riper fruit, less herbaceous), warm Tuscany (14.5%, darkest fruit, most extraction). The climate arc is clean and teachable. The "or predominant" wording is exact legal language for Chinon. IMW uses this variety increasingly for climate-based questions. 2025 P2 Q1 explicitly cited in the tree's evidence base.
- **Syrah/Shiraz: PLAUSIBLE** — used in 2024 P2 Q1 in the prior year's paper; unlikely to repeat in consecutive years. The "or predominant" wording is less applicable to Syrah.
- **Cabernet Sauvignon: PLAUSIBLE** — global but the cool/warm climate discussion is less dramatic (warm everywhere outside Bordeaux); "predominant" wording less expected.
- **Pinot Noir: CURVEBALL** — used frequently; "or predominant" wording is essentially inapplicable to Pinot.

### Comparative axis
Cross-country cool-to-warm climate gradient: Loire Valley (cool, pyrazine-driven) → Mendoza (moderate, riper fruit) → Tuscany (warm, extracted). Alcohol gradient of approximately 12.5% → 13.5% → 14.5% is the glass-level confirmation of the axis.

## IMW's likely intent
Testing the climate-winemaking nexus for a variety with high climate sensitivity. The heavy 45-mark weighting on climate/winemaking means the candidate must explain: (1) how cool Loire climate produces herbaceous, crunchy, lighter-bodied Cab Franc vs warm Tuscany's riper, extracted expression; (2) how winemaking adapts to climate (extraction length, oak percentage, whole-bunch inclusion in Loire); (3) what makes the same grape taste so different in three distinct climate zones. The "or predominant" wording also tests legal/labelling awareness — specifically that Chinon legally permits Cab Franc as the predominant variety in a legally permitted multi-grape blend.

## What to focus on in the glass
- Pyrazine/herbaceous signature: green bell pepper / tomato leaf / pencil shavings = cool-climate Cab Franc; absent or minimal = warmer climate expression.
- Fruit profile: bright red cherry/raspberry (cool Loire) → dark cherry/blackcurrant (warm Tuscany).
- Tannin fineness: delicate/crunchy (Loire) to firmer/extracted (Tuscany).
- Acid level: high and fresh (cool Loire) to softer and broader (warm Tuscany).
- Alcohol: 12–12.5% (cool Loire) → 13.5% (moderate Mendoza) → 14.5%+ (warm Tuscany) — the clearest glass-level confirmation of the climate gradient.
- Oak influence: Loire CF typically minimal new oak; New World and Tuscany CF more oak-forward.
- The "or predominant" wording: if one wine shows slightly more tannin grip or weight than pure Cab Franc typically delivers in its region, the minor blending component is the explanation.
