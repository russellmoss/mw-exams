- Sauvignon Blanc: reductive is the default; very limited oak tradition outside barrel-aged Fume Blanc

## IMW's likely intent
Testing oak as a winemaking variable rather than a simple quality indicator. The candidate must demonstrate: (1) in prestige sparkling Chardonnay, oak appears in reserve-wine handling or base-wine fermentation, not in the final style; (2) in Grand Cru Burgundy, oak is about texture and spice integration rather than flavour dominance; (3) in commercial Macon, the absence of oak is itself a deliberate and commercially rational choice; (4) in New World premium, new oak percentage and vessel size (barrique vs puncheon) vary by producer philosophy. The commercial appeal dimension rewards candidates who can differentiate the market for a Selosse Champagne from a NZ Chardonnay from a Macon.

## What to focus on in the glass (F1d branch guidance)
- Confirm Chardonnay anchor on the cleanest, driest still wine first (apple/citrus/lees, no heavy oak)
- Lock the grape family; then for each remaining wine, only diagnose what production did to it
- For sparkling wines: autolytic (brioche/toast/nuts) signals lees age; barrel-fermented reserve character is more oxidative and creamy vs clean
- For still wines: toast/vanilla/cedar = new French oak; butter/cream = malo and lees; tense high acid without these = no oak, no malo (Chablis/tank)
- MLF: buttery/creamy texture vs tense high acid (no MLF, e.g. most Champagne base wine and many commercial Macons)
- Alcohol bands: Champagne ~12%; Chablis ~12.5%; Burgundy Grand Cru ~13%; NZ ~13.5%; California ~14%+
- Finish length: primary quality indicator across all five; used to separate Grand Cru (very long) from commercial (short)
