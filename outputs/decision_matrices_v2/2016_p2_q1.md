# Decision matrix v2 — 2016 P2 Q1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1 and 2 come from the same region and are made from the same single grape variety.

With reference to both wines:

a) Identify the grape variety and origin as closely as possible. (18 marks)

b) Compare the winemaking styles. (18 marks)

c) Compare quality, with particular reference to both wines' potential evolution. (14 marks)

### Variety lock
Variety not stated. Must be identified from the glass. The identification carries 18 marks combined with origin — the IMW expects a confident named call.

### Comparative axis
F1c: same region, intra-regional stylistic split driven by winemaking philosophy. The axis is traditional versus modern production (or conventional versus minimal-intervention/natural). Evolution potential is explicitly tested: one wine must be more age-worthy than the other through the winemaking lens.

### Candidate map (by slot)

The family tree pack identifies F1c questions as dominated by regions where two clearly divergent production philosophies for the same grape coexist in the same appellation. The definitive example in the corpus is Beaujolais — carbonic maceration versus Burgundian vinification.

Alternative (if Beaujolais ruled out by sensory evidence):

- Rioja — Tempranillo, traditional American-oak extended ageing vs. modern French-oak fruit-forward.

### Anti-collapse rule
Do not assign both wines to the same winemaking style. The 18-mark comparison mark allocation requires two clearly divergent expressions. If both wines read as conventionally vinified, the identification is wrong.

### Single rule-out worth naming
Burgundy Pinot Noir: winemaking differences within Burgundy exist but are insufficiently dramatic to generate 18 marks of explicit winemaking contrast discussion.

The family pack cites this question (2016 P2 Q1) as the exemplar for F1c Beaujolais/Gamay — the branch was built on this exam's evidence.

### Tasting branch order (F1, adjusted for F1c)
1. Confirm grape family on the more typical wine (higher structure, dark red fruit): Gamay or Nebbiolo or Tempranillo.
2. Lock the grape and region simultaneously — do not treat them as independent.
3. Sub-tree on winemaking axis: carbonic markers (banana, kirsch, bubblegum) versus traditional markers (tannin, oak spice, dark cherry, length).
4. Evolution assessment: structured wine shows length and persistence; carbonic expression is fruit-immediate and drink-early.

### Branch-specific candidate narrowing (F1c Beaujolais path)
- Purple fruit + low tannin + juicy acid + carbonic hints → survive Gamay/Beaujolais carbonic expression.
- Within Beaujolais: more structure/mineral grip → promote Morgon/Moulin-à-Vent over Beaujolais-Villages.
- Carbonic maceration markers: banana, bubblegum, kirsch, very light colour, fruit-forward, tannin-light.
- Burgundian vinification markers: deeper colour, more tannin structure, complexity, longer finish, dark cherry/plum over simple red fruit.
- If no carbonic markers detected: rotate to Barolo or Rioja on the traditional-vs-modern axis.

## Rule-outs
- Pinot Noir/Burgundy: winemaking differences exist but are not dramatic enough for 18-mark comparison.
- Cabernet Sauvignon: "compare winemaking styles" within one Cab region is possible but less cleanly structured for exam purposes, and cross-country Cab is more naturally F1b.
- Sangiovese/Chianti: traditional vs modern Chianti exists but the divide is less starkly encoded in sensory signatures.
- New World regions generally: "compare winemaking styles" within one New World region is less standard IMW exam territory.

## IMW's likely intent
Testing understanding of how the same grape within the same region produces radically different wines through winemaking philosophy. The carbonic maceration versus Burgundian vinification debate is the richest possible winemaking discussion for a single grape/region pair. The evolution sub-question rewards candidates who know that cru Beaujolais in the Burgundian style can age 10–15 years while natural/carbonic examples are drink-early. The equal mark weighting between ID and winemaking signals that neither is optional.

## What to focus on in the glass (family-pack guided)
- Carbonic maceration markers: banana, bubblegum, kirsch, Smarties, fresh raspberry; very low tannin; lower alcohol.
- Traditional/Burgundian markers: darker red fruit (cherry, plum), more tannin structure, oak spice, complexity and length.
- Colour: both should be medium ruby with Gamay's characteristic purple tinge; the natural/carbonic wine tends lighter.
- Acidity: both high — Gamay is always high-acid; this does not discriminate.
- Finish: the measure of potential evolution — the structured cru wine has length and persistence that the carbonic expression lacks.
- Overall freshness vs complexity: carbonic = pure and immediate; Burgundian = more complex and structured.
