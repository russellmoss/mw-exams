## F4 tasting branch order (per wine)

1. Colour and intensity — pale garnet / brick (Nebbiolo, aged Rioja) vs. deep ruby (Brunello, younger Bordeaux)
2. Aroma family (rose/tar vs. sour cherry vs. cassis/cedar vs. vanilla/dill)
3. Structure (tannin: savage/drying = Nebbiolo; fine-grained = Sangiovese; firm/structured = Left Bank Cab; soft/sweet = Right Bank Merlot; dusty/integrated = aged Rioja)
4. Oak character: American oak vanilla/coconut (Rioja) vs. French oak cedar/tobacco (Bordeaux) vs. subtle/integrated (modern Barolo) vs. large-vessel/neutral (Brunello)
5. Tertiary development: leather, truffle, dried herbs, cigar box → maturity signal → vintage range
6. Climate signal: consistently cool to moderate (all classic European)
7. Quality calibration in classification language
8. Vintage narrowing: combine colour evolution (browning rim = older) + aromatic development + structural integration
9. Reset before next wine

### F4 branch-specific narrowing cues

- Pale garnet + browning rim + rose + tar + fierce acid and tannin → Nebbiolo (Barolo / Barbaresco)
- Sour cherry + dried herbs + savoury + high acid + medium body → Sangiovese (Brunello if aged and concentrated)
- Cassis + cedar + graphite + firmer fine tannin → Bordeaux Cabernet-dominant Left Bank
- Plum + chocolate + softer rounder tannins → Bordeaux Merlot-dominant Right Bank (Saint-Émilion / Pomerol)
- American oak vanilla/coconut + dusty integrated tannin + browning garnet → Rioja Gran Reserva Tempranillo

## IMW's likely intent
Benchmark recognition and vintage literacy. The examiner wants to know if candidates drink the great European reds and follow vintage reports. The vintage ID + drinking window sub-question (7 marks) is the differentiator — candidates who can articulate "this 2018 Barolo needs 10 more years; this 2010 Rioja Gran Reserva is at its peak now" demonstrate practical wine trade knowledge that a textbook-only candidate will miss. "Quality in the context of the origin" (8 marks) rewards calibrated language: "for Barolo this represents a competent but not exceptional producer — lacks the length and granular tannin of a top MGA vineyard."

## What to focus on in the glass

- Tannin structure first: this separates the four candidates more reliably than fruit alone
  - Grippy / angular / drying (Nebbiolo)
  - Fine-grained, savoury-dry (Sangiovese Brunello)
  - Firm / structured, cassis-dominant (Bordeaux Left Bank)
  - Soft / sweet, plum-dominant (Right Bank Merlot)
  - Dusty / integrated, sweet-spice (aged Rioja Tempranillo)
- Colour evolution: browning / garnet rim = older wine (Rioja 2010, aged Barolo); deep purple-red = younger (2018 Bordeaux or Brunello)
- Oak character: American oak vanilla/coconut/dill vs. French oak cedar/tobacco vs. subtle/integrated (modern Barolo) vs. neutral large vessel (Brunello)
- Tertiary character: leather, truffle, dried herbs, forest floor → maturity → narrow vintage range
- Alcohol: Barolo/Brunello often 14–14.5%; Bordeaux 13.5–14%; Rioja Gran Reserva varies 13.5–14%
