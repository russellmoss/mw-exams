# Decision matrix v2 — 2017 P1 Q1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-3 are all from the same country and region.

For each wine:

a) Identify the origin as closely as possible, with reference to the grape variety(ies) used. (3 x 11 marks)

b) Discuss the winemaking techniques used to arrive at this style. (3 x 8 marks)

c) Discuss the commercial potential. (3 x 6 marks)

## Pre-taste matrix

```
Paper:        P1
Family:       F2a (same country AND same region, different varieties)
Stem summary: Three wines from the same country and region; each has a different variety and winemaking tradition; commercial potential also tested.
Mark map:     Origin + variety 11 each = 33 marks dominant; winemaking 8 each = 24 marks substantial; commercial potential 6 each = 18 marks secondary.
Constraint:   Same country AND region — a single region must contain at least three distinct, recognizable grape varieties with meaningfully different production approaches.
```

**Origin lock:** Same country and region (stricter than F2a typical — locks to sub-national region). The region must have genuine multi-varietal identity and contrasting winemaking traditions.

**Internal divergence axis:** Variety + winemaking philosophy within one region; commercial potential spans different market tiers within the same geography.

**Sub-form:** F2a — same country, different varieties; tighter than usual because region is also fixed.

**Region candidates within F2 universe:**

The F2 tree's Branch 2.1 (three-grape French triangle) is the canonical answer here. France is Question 1 of the paper — the opening question — and the IMW consistently opens P1 with France. The Loire Valley is the only French region (or world region at this scale) that uniquely fields three entirely different grape varieties with three entirely different winemaking traditions, three different appellation identities, and three clearly distinct commercial profiles within the same administrative region:

- Muscadet (Melon de Bourgogne): neutral, sur lie, low alcohol, very low price, high volume
- Savennières (Chenin Blanc): oxidative-adjacent or barrel-fermented, higher alcohol, niche premium
- Pouilly-Fumé / Sancerre (Sauvignon Blanc): reductive, aromatic, high export recognition, premium

No other region matches this breadth at the variety × winemaking × commercial axis simultaneously.

**Candidate map:**
```
Slot W1: Muscadet Sèvre-et-Maine — Loire Valley, France — STRONG SIGNAL
         Melon de Bourgogne; sur lie; low alcohol (12%); entry-level/volume commercial
Slot W2: Savennières — Loire Valley, France — STRONG SIGNAL
         Chenin Blanc; barrel-touched or oxidative-adjacent; higher alcohol (13–14%); niche premium
Slot W3: Pouilly-Fumé or Sancerre — Loire Valley, France — STRONG SIGNAL
         Sauvignon Blanc; reductive stainless; aromatic; premium export recognition (12.5%)
```

**Anti-collapse rule:** Do not predict the same variety twice. Do not collapse all three into Sauvignon Blanc or Chenin Blanc because they are the most famous Loire varieties. The region's identity depends on all three occupying genuinely different positions.

**Single rule-out worth naming:** Alsace — has variety diversity but winemaking differences are less dramatized, and commercial contrast across Riesling / Pinot Gris / Gewurztraminer is narrower. Burgundy — single-variety (Chardonnay) so cannot fill three distinct variety slots.

**Confirm bound origin (Loire Valley, France):** Low alcohol + yeasty mineral + saline + phenolic grip on finish = Muscadet sur lie signature. The bitterness is the variety diagnostic (Melon de Bourgogne finishes bitter in youth).

**Place on divergence axis:** Low commercial price tier, high volume; entry point for Loire whites; winemaking story = sur lie lees contact.

**Sub-region:** Muscadet Sèvre-et-Maine, Loire Valley. Variety: Melon de Bourgogne.

**Classification / tier:** If extended lees contact and single parcel: Cru Communal (Clisson, Gorges, Goulaine, etc.) — worth naming. Standard sur lie at appellation level is the default.

**Quality in regional context:** Commercial benchmark quality — shows how sur lie transforms what would otherwise be neutral white wine into a textured, food-friendly style. Commercial potential: high-volume on-trade entry (brasserie, seafood restaurant); exports strongly to UK, USA, Belgium.

**Branch-B alternative:** Aligoté (Burgundy) if the low-alcohol, saline, high-acid, neutral profile is present without the sur lie breadth; but Muscadet's bitterness and sea-spray are distinct.

**Branch-B alternative:** Vouvray sec (Chenin, more reductive, less weight); Montlouis sec.

**Sub-region:** Pouilly-Fumé AOP or Sancerre AOP, Loire Valley. Variety: Sauvignon Blanc (100%).

**Classification / tier:** Appellation level. Jean-Pierre Bailly (Les Griottes) is a known Pouilly-Fumé producer — expect benchmark appellation quality.

**Quality in regional context:** Export-quality, internationally benchmarked. Commercial potential: premium restaurants, wine bars, supermarket premium tier internationally. The most globally recognized Loire white appellation.

**Branch-B alternative:** Reuilly or Menetou-Salon (SB, same appellation cluster, lesser known) if the smoke/flint is present but concentration is lower.

## Anti-collapse note

The most common error on this question would be assigning all three wines to the same variety (Sauvignon Blanc, or Chenin Blanc) because they are the most famous Loire grapes. The stem says same country AND region — Loire is unique in sustaining three entirely different grape variety identities. Melon de Bourgogne must be kept in the ballot even though it is less famous than the other two. The commercial potential sub-question (6 marks) signals that the three wines sit at genuinely different price and market-channel positions — if all three were the same variety, the commercial differentiation would collapse.

## Reality check (post-prediction)

**Actual wines:**

- W2: Savennières, Château d'Épiré, 2014. Loire, France. (13.5%)

**F2a prediction accuracy:**
- Country + region lock: Loire Valley, France — CORRECT. Branch 2.1 (three-grape French triangle) fired exactly.
- W1 variety + sub-appellation: Melon de Bourgogne / Muscadet Sèvre-et-Maine — CORRECT.
- W2 variety + sub-appellation: Chenin Blanc / Savennières — CORRECT. Château d'Épiré is exactly the quality producer predicted.
- W3 variety + sub-appellation: Sauvignon Blanc / Pouilly-Fumé — CORRECT. Jean-Pierre Bailly Les Griottes = benchmark appellation quality.
- All three confidence tiers: STRONG SIGNAL for all three.

**Lesson:** When the stem says "same country AND region" in P1 Q1, Loire Valley is the primary answer. It is the only wine region that can fill three markedly different variety × winemaking × commercial slots simultaneously. Muscadet must be included; omitting it in favour of a third Burgundy-style wine is the common error. The winemaking story for Muscadet (sur lie) and Savennières (barrel/semi-oxidative Chenin) are each worth 8 marks of rich discussion.
