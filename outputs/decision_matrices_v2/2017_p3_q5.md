## IMW's likely intent

Maturity judgment under subtle conditions. The candidate must connect developmental stage to structural reserves and appellation level — not just estimate an age. A strong answer observes that the higher-quality wine may appear less evolved despite being the same age or older, because its structural reserves have preserved it. The improvement-potential question should reference acid line, tannin integration, concentration, and the hierarchical implications of site classification.

The vintage sub-question (7 marks) tests whether the candidate can read developmental cues (brick rim, fruit evolution from primary to tertiary, tannin integration) and translate them into a plausible year or age range. The quality and improvement sub-question (10 marks) tests whether the candidate can connect those developmental cues to classification language.

## What to focus on in the glass

- Brick/garnet rim ratio: estimate relative age; more brick = more evolved; compare rim width across the two wines
- Fruit profile: primary sour cherry vs. sous-bois/mushroom/earthy spice indicates developmental stage
- Tannin resolution: well-integrated (approaching plateau) vs. still slightly grippy (more runway remaining)
- Acidity: preserved freshness on the structured wine argues for further improvement
- Length and concentration: directly correlates with quality tier and ageability — the premier cru should show a measurably longer finish
- Colour depth: the better site often shows slightly deeper colour despite similar or older age
