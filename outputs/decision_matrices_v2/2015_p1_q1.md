## Universe (F7d reasoning)
Four still whites from one region at different quality levels within the same vintage. The 40-mark quality discussion anchored to "the region of production" demands familiarity with regional benchmark vocabulary: Chablis steel-and-chalk minerality, Côte de Beaune richness and oak integration, Grand Cru concentration and longevity. The vintage ID (12 marks) confirms the IMW wants candidates to name the vintage — not just describe development — which points to a region with a well-documented vintage calendar.

### Candidate varieties (confidence tiers)
- Chardonnay: **STRONG SIGNAL** — Burgundy is the only white region with established hierarchy deep enough to generate four wines at 12 marks each for specific origin; no other single white grape region has this depth
- Riesling: **PLAUSIBLE** — Mosel or Alsace could work, but four different Riesling sub-appellations within one region is harder to test; Burgundy's geographic range (Chablis + Côte de Beaune) provides more internal contrast
- Pinot Gris: **CURVEBALL** — Alsace has grand crus, but limited sub-appellation diversity for a four-wine same-region set

### Candidate origins per wine
Assuming Burgundy (most likely):
- Wine 1: Village-level Chablis or Mâcon-Villages / Bourgogne Blanc (entry point); lean, steely, minimal oak; lowest concentration
- Wine 2: Chablis Premier Cru or Côte de Beaune village (Meursault village or Puligny-Montrachet village); step up in complexity and body
- Wine 3: Chablis Grand Cru (one of the seven — Vaudésir, Valmur, Les Clos, Grenouilles, Preuses, Bougros, Blanchots) or Puligny-Montrachet Premier Cru; material increase in concentration and length
- Wine 4: Grand Cru — Bâtard-Montrachet or Bienvenues-Bâtard-Montrachet (richest, most powerful) or Corton-Charlemagne (more mineral, elegant)

The spread from village Chablis to Grand Cru covers the hierarchy from entry Burgundy to the summit, testing whether the candidate can calibrate quality across appellations within one vintage.

Note on Burgundy's internal geography: Chablis is geographically and stylistically distinct from the Côte de Beaune, yet both sit within the Burgundy appellation family. A question testing sub-appellation origin at 12 marks each can legitimately include both Chablis and Côte de Beaune as "same region" sub-appellations — this is precisely the kind of regional nuance that the question rewards.

### Rule-outs
- New World regions: "same region" with 12-mark specific origin implies a deeply structured appellation system; no New World white region qualifies for this depth
- German regional breadth at sub-region level: German regions rarely field four identifiable sub-origin wines in one region; Pradikat levels vary but are not sub-regional in the same way
- Alsace: four wines would likely span different varieties (Riesling, Pinot Gris, Gewürztraminer, Pinot Blanc), not four sub-appellations of one region; less natural fit for a same-variety quality hierarchy

### IMW's likely intent
Deep regional quality hierarchy knowledge for a single white benchmark region. The candidate must: (1) identify the appellation at sub-appellation level for each wine; (2) build a 40-mark quality argument using regional classification vocabulary (not just adjectives); (3) name the vintage from tasting evidence — colour development, secondary aromas (hazelnut, honey), texture, and overall cohort character. The 40-mark collective quality discussion is the most demanding task on this paper; it rewards candidates who have tasted across the Burgundy hierarchy and can articulate what distinguishes each level.
