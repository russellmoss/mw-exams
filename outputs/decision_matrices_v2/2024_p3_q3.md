## F2 pre-taste analysis

**Origin lock:** Sauternes/Bordeaux. The 30-mark commercial sub-question tied to region identification is the key diagnostic. P3 regions generating this level of commercial discussion for three different styles from the same geography: Sauternes is the dominant answer because (1) the commercial headwinds facing sweet wine are the most acute and globally discussed case study in the wine trade, (2) the dry/second-wine/grand-vin structure from one estate provides the clearest stylistic spread in three wines, and (3) Jura has already been used in P3 Q2 of this same paper.

**Internal divergence axis:** Sweetness and botrytis development level:
1. Dry estate white (W5): SB/Sémillon blend, dry-fermented, no botrytis character, made as commercial survival/portfolio diversification play; 12–12.5% alcohol; "R de Rieussec," "G de Guiraud," "Y d'Yquem" are the best-known examples
2. Second wine or accessible vintage (W6): some botrytis character but less concentrated than grand vin; younger vintage, less oak aging, more forward and approachable; designed to generate cash flow earlier; 13–14% alcohol
3. Grand vin aged Sauternes (W7): full botrytis (multiple tries through vineyard over weeks), maximum concentration, significant barrel aging (18+ months in new French oak), cellared for years before release; 14%+ alcohol; deepest amber-gold colour; 15–25+ year ageing potential

**Sub-form: F2d (Branch 2.3 — same origin, different sweetness/age):** Working priors for Sauternes F2d: the dry estate white as the pivot wine (lowest commercial price but guarantees cash flow), the second wine or younger vintage as the accessible entry point into sweet Sauternes, and the grand vin as the full expression of the appellation. The commercial sub-question (30 marks) is built around this three-tier structure.

**Mark distribution analysis:** 30 marks undivided on region + commercial is unprecedented in the P3 question corpus — it signals that the examiner wants a deep commercial essay tied to the regional identity. The IMW expects the candidate to address: declining global demand for sweet wine; climate change threatening botrytis reliability; high production costs (multiple vineyard passes, low yields, hand-harvesting); the dry white diversification as commercial survival strategy; the age-worthy grand vin market (gifts, collectors, high-end restaurants); the second wine / accessible vintage as consumer entry points.

## IMW's likely intent

Commercial analysis disguised as a tasting question. The 30-mark undivided sub-question on region and commercial opportunities/challenges is the largest single mark block in P3 Q3. The IMW expects the candidate to address the following as minimum content:
- Why estates make dry whites: declining sweet wine demand, vintage risk, cash flow, brand diversification
- The economics of botrytis production: multiple passes through the vineyard (tries), 50% or more yield loss, hand-harvesting, vintage dependency (botrytis unreliable in some years), high labor cost per bottle
- The global market for sweet wine: declining volume demand in most markets, health consciousness, changing dining habits, reduced wine consumption with food
- The age-worthy premium positioning of grand vin Sauternes: collector market, sommelier prestige, long-term investment argument, small-production narrative
- Maturity argument across the three wines: dry white is drinking now; second wine/accessible sweet is mid-development; grand vin is at or approaching peak and demonstrates why patience is rewarded
