**Candidate varieties across the set**

- Riesling (Mosel Kabinett): **STRONG SIGNAL** — archetype of high acid/low alcohol whites with extraordinary ageing potential; the obvious anchor for the acid extreme
- Chardonnay (oaked, moderate climate): **STRONG SIGNAL** — versatile texture (lean to creamy); oak and lees discussion available; reliable ageing discussion
- Viognier: **PLAUSIBLE** — distinctive low-acid oily texture; stark structural contrast to Riesling; shorter ageing potential
- Gewurztraminer or Muscat: **PLAUSIBLE** — aromatic, phenolic texture, moderate-to-lower acid; interesting ageing narrative
- Chenin Blanc: **PLAUSIBLE** — high acid + waxy texture + brilliant ageing; defensible as the high-acid representative
- Pinot Gris (Alsace, full body): **PLAUSIBLE** — textural richness without obvious aromatics

**Hard rule:** Do not infer wine N from wine N−1. Identify each wine's structure independently.

**Default rule-outs**
- Sauvignon Blanc alone: limited texture discussion; ageing discussion is thin
- Obscure indigenous grapes: unlikely at only 5 marks for variety ID
- Any blend: explicitly excluded by stem

**Main traps**
- Trap 1: With variety ID worth only 5 marks, do not spend exam time over-arguing variety — commit to a call and pivot immediately to structure
- Trap 2: Viognier and Gewurztraminer can both seem "low acid and aromatic" — discriminate by texture (Viognier = oily/apricot; Gewurztraminer = broader/lychee/rose)
- Trap 3: Muscat and Gewurztraminer can look similar on the nose — Muscat is more purely grapey/floral; Gewurztraminer has the rose-petal/lychee/spice specificity and broader palate
