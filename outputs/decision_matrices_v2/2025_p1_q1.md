### Blend family lock
Sub-form F3a: same blend family expressed in different origins. "Same two grape varieties" is the most restrictive possible blend constraint — it eliminates all three-or-more variety blends (Rhône Blanc, CdP Blanc, Jurançon). The universe reduces to classic **two-variety** white blend recipes:
- **Sauvignon Blanc + Sémillon**: the most globally replicated two-variety white blend; canonical in Bordeaux (Pessac-Léognan, Graves) and New World (Margaret River, Hunter Valley, Chile, South Africa)
- **Marsanne + Roussanne**: classic Rhône pair; possible but origin discrimination with only two wines is narrower (both likely Northern Rhône or Northern Rhône + Victoria)

Sauvignon Blanc + Sémillon is overwhelmingly the STRONG SIGNAL because it is the only two-variety white blend tested across Old World and New World with sufficient geographic spread for a two-wine cross-origin comparison.

### Composition prior
Sauvignon Blanc + Sémillon: ratio varies significantly — Bordeaux typically SB-dominant (70–80% SB / 20–30% Sém); Margaret River varies (some Sém-dominant producers; some SB-dominant). Composition reading from the glass is achievable: SB dominance shows aromatic freshness and acidity; Sémillon dominance shows wax, lanolin, lower aromatics, broader texture.

### Anti-collapse rule
Do not predict both wines from the same country. The same two-variety recipe expressed in two different countries is the defining F3a structure. Old World (Bordeaux) vs New World (Margaret River) is the canonical cross-origin contrast for SB + Sémillon.

### One rule-out worth naming
Riesling + anything: Riesling is almost never blended with a named second variety in classic still-wine winemaking.

## Universe (F3a reasoning)
The "same two grape varieties" constraint eliminates Rhône Blanc, CdP Blanc, Jurançon, Rioja Blanco, and all multi-variety blends. The remaining universe is two-variety recipes only. Sauvignon Blanc + Sémillon is the dominant candidate because it produces both the benchmark Old World expression (Pessac-Léognan) and the most famous New World expression (Margaret River) — an Old World / New World pair provides maximum style contrast for the 12-mark style/winemaking/quality discussion.

### Candidate varieties (confidence tiers)
- Sauvignon Blanc + Sémillon: **STRONG SIGNAL** — the most globally replicated two-variety white blend; Bordeaux Blanc and Margaret River are canonical; blend ratio varies providing built-in contrast material for style/winemaking discussion
- Marsanne + Roussanne: **PLAUSIBLE** — classic Rhône pair; testable but narrower geographic spread for two-wine comparison

### Rule-outs
- Pinot Gris + anything: no classic two-variety template

- Viognier + anything as a stable two-variety blend: not a canonical recipe in the way SB/Sém is

### IMW's likely intent
Testing: (1) identifying specific blend components from the glass rather than guessing "white blend"; (2) discriminating between Old World and New World expressions of the same two-variety recipe; (3) articulating winemaking differences — barrel fermentation, lees stirring, malo, SO2 management — that create different styles from identical varieties. The 24-mark style/winemaking/quality block (12 per wine) rewards candidates who can explain WHY the wines taste different despite sharing a grape recipe.
