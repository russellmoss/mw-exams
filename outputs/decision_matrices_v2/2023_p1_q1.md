# Decision matrix v2 — 2023 P1 Q1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-4 are from four different countries and two different grape varieties. 1 & 2 are a pair and 3 & 4 are a pair. Each pair is from the same single grape variety.

For each pair:

a) Identify the grape variety. (2 x 10 marks)

b) Compare and contrast the major winemaking influences. (2 x 15 marks)

c) Comment on quality and ageing potential. (2 x 25 marks)

**Variety lock (per pair):** two separate unnamed white grapes, each identified from its pair of wines

**Comparative axis (within each pair):** winemaking philosophy (reductive vs. oxidative, oak vs. no oak, lees contact, climatic intervention), quality tier, ageing trajectory

**Sub-form:** F1b cross-country — pair 1 covers two countries, pair 2 covers two different countries; four countries total across the question

**Candidate map (by slot):**

Pair 1 (wines 1–2):
- Slot W1: STRONG SIGNAL — Old World Sauvignon Blanc; France (Loire). Restrained, smoke/flint, linear acid, herbal/citrus profile.
- Slot W2: STRONG SIGNAL — New World Sauvignon Blanc; USA (Napa Valley) or NZ (Marlborough). Riper, rounder, tropical-fruit dominant, higher alcohol.

Pair 2 (wines 3–4):
- Slot W3: STRONG SIGNAL — Sémillon, Australia (Hunter Valley). 10–11% alcohol, waxy/lemon-curd texture, extreme linearity, very low alcohol is the defining tell.
- Slot W4: PLAUSIBLE — Sémillon, Chile (Maule Valley) or Bordeaux-adjacent. Higher alcohol (~13.5%), more tropical-ripe expression, less austere linearity.

**F1b pre-taste decision logic:**

The four-country / two-variety structure maps directly to F1b Branch 1.1. The tree's top three STRONG SIGNAL varieties are Chardonnay, Riesling, and Sauvignon Blanc. Sauvignon Blanc is the obvious pair-1 candidate because Loire-vs.-New-World is the archetype winemaking contrast for SB: no oak + high acid + herbaceous vs. more tropical + barrel-fermented (in Napa) or pungent tropical-fruit-dominant (Marlborough). The 15-mark winemaking question is perfectly served by the Loire-Napa contrast.

For pair 2, the 25-mark ageing question and four-different-countries constraint argue against a second mainstream STRONG SIGNAL variety (Riesling or Chardonnay appearing in both pairs would be unusual). Sémillon is the PLAUSIBLE candidate: Hunter Valley Sémillon is one of the world's most distinctive ageing trajectories (waxy/toasty at 10+ years, very low alcohol, virtually no oak) and the Chile alternative provides a meaningful winemaking and climatic contrast with the same grape. The extreme low-alcohol tell (10.5% in Hunter Tyrrell's style) is the in-glass diagnostic that locks the Hunter Valley call.

**Anti-collapse rule:** never put the same variety in both pairs. The stem says "two different grape varieties." Do not predict Chardonnay for both pairs or SB for both.

**Single rule-out worth naming:** Chardonnay for pair 2 — plausible structurally, but with Chardonnay held back (or used in Q2/Q3), and with Sémillon providing a more distinctive ageing answer, Chardonnay in pair 2 is less distinctive as an exam construct.

## Anti-collapse note

Pairs 1 and 2 are fully independent identification problems. Do not let the identification of Sauvignon Blanc in pair 1 influence pair 2 in any direction — the varieties are stated as different. The critical discipline for pair 2 is resisting the pull toward Chardonnay (the most obvious white variety) when the structural evidence (especially very low alcohol on W3) points unmistakably toward Hunter Valley Sémillon. The 25-mark quality/ageing section per pair rewards candidates who can articulate specific ageing mechanisms: for SB, the acid-structure of Loire vs. tropical exuberance of New World; for Sémillon, the Hunter Valley paradox of maximum ageing potential from minimum intervention and very low alcohol.

- W4: Semillon Granito, Julio Bouchon, 2019. Maule Valley, Chile. (13.5%)

**F1b family prediction: EXACT.** Both varieties (Sauvignon Blanc, Sémillon) correctly identified. All four origins (Loire, Napa, Hunter Valley, Maule Valley) correctly predicted as primary or first-alternative candidates.

**Comparative axis accuracy:** Loire restraint vs. Napa richness (pair 1); Hunter Valley 10.5% austerity vs. Chilean 13.5% ripeness (pair 2) — all predicted axes confirmed.

**F1b template performance:** the anti-collapse rule (different varieties per pair, four different countries) shaped the slot ballot correctly. Hunter Valley Sémillon's extreme low-alcohol tell (10.5%) is the most reliable single glass marker in the F1b corpus — it should be a named diagnostic in any Sémillon-containing F1b matrix.

**Lesson from Chile miss in Phase 5A:** the tree's Sémillon candidates (South Africa, Hunter Valley, Bordeaux) did not explicitly include Chile. Maule Valley Sémillon is a legitimate PLAUSIBLE candidate that should be added to the F1b Sémillon regional map — Chile grows significant quantities of old-vine Sémillon in the Maule and Colchagua valleys.
