**F1b pre-taste decision logic:**

F1b Branch 1.1 (cross-country). Chardonnay is the only white variety that simultaneously occupies four geographically diverse exam-quality expressions (Burgundy, California, South Africa, Margaret River) and spans a quality and maturity range sufficient to generate 52 marks of style/quality/maturity commentary per wine. No other white grape achieves this scope.

The maturity sub-question is decisive routing logic: when an F1b question asks "comment on maturity" across four wines, one wine is almost always significantly older. The aged Burgundy (2010 Chablis Premier Cru at 15 years in 2025) is the maturity anchor. Without it, the "maturity" question would be marginally differentiated (all young primary); with it, the question tests genuine understanding of Chardonnay's tertiary development (honey, lanolin, toast, golden colour, preserved acidity).

**Anti-collapse rule:** all four slots must be assigned different countries. Do not predict two Burgundies or two Californias. The stem says "same single grape variety," not "same country" — geographic spread across four distinct national expressions is the required structure.

**Single rule-out worth naming:** Riesling — geographic spread is insufficient for four exam-quality origins; also already used prominently in 2024 P1 Q1, making a repeat in back-to-back years less likely.

## Anti-collapse note

All four wines are Chardonnay, but the comparative axis spans three structural dimensions: climate (cool Burgundy/Elgin vs. warm California/Margaret River), oak philosophy (unoaked Chablis vs. barrel-fermented California/Margaret River), and maturity stage (15-year Chablis Premier Cru vs. 1–3-year young New World wines). The candidate must articulate all three dimensions in the 52-mark style/quality/maturity section. Collapsing to "this one has more oak, this one is fresher" does not earn the marks. The aged Chablis requires specifically named tertiary descriptors (honey, beeswax, toast, lanolin overlay on citrus core) and an explicit statement that its maturity makes it the most developed but not necessarily the most immediately appealing wine in the flight.
