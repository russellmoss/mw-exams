a) Identify the grape variety with reference to both wines. (3 x 10 marks)

## IMW's likely intent

This question tests three core competencies simultaneously:

1. **Variety identification across mainstream whites** -- can the candidate name three major white grapes from sensory cues alone?
2. **Quality discrimination within a variety** -- can the candidate articulate what separates a benchmark/premium example from a commercial example of the same grape? This is the 60-mark heart of the question.
3. **Origin specificity** -- can the candidate go beyond "France" to "Mosel" or "Burgundy, Cote de Beaune"? The 10 marks per wine reward specificity.

The pair structure is pedagogically designed: by holding variety constant within each pair, the IMW isolates quality/maturity/ageability as the variable. The candidate who merely identifies the variety but cannot distinguish quality tiers will lose 60 marks.

## What to focus on in the glass

- **Primary aromatics** for variety ID: petrol/lime/slate for Riesling, apple/citrus/hazelnut for Chardonnay, green capsicum/passion fruit/gooseberry for Sauvignon Blanc
- **Colour intensity and hue**: golden tones suggest age or oak; pale green suggests youth
- **Texture and weight**: lees-derived richness (malo, batonnage) signals premium Chardonnay; racy acidity with low body signals young Riesling or commercial Sauvignon Blanc
- **Oak markers**: vanilla, toast, smoke point toward barrel-fermented Chardonnay or barrel-fermented Sauvignon Blanc (Fume Blanc style)
- **Residual sugar**: any hint of sweetness combined with high acidity = German Riesling (Kabinett/Spatlese style)
- **Acidity profile**: searing, linear acidity = Riesling or cool-climate Sauvignon Blanc; rounder, integrated acidity = Chardonnay with malo
- **Finish length and complexity**: the premium wine in each pair should show longer finish, more layers, better integration; the commercial wine will be simple, fruit-forward, short
