# Decision matrix -- 2017 Paper 1 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 4-5 are made from the same grape variety.

With reference to both wines:

a) Identify the grape variety. (16 marks)

For each wine:

b) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (2 x 6 marks)

c) Discuss the wine's quality and maturity within the context of its origin. (2 x 6 marks)

d) What are the key winemaking techniques used in the wine's production? (2 x 5 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 1 = white wines only. Two whites from the same single grape variety, tested across origins.

## Stem signals
- **Same grape variety** -- single variety, tested as a pair
- **16 marks for variety ID** -- very high weight; this is a well-known variety the IMW expects candidates to identify confidently
- **Origin only 6 marks each** -- less emphasis on pinpointing exact origin; variety is the star
- **Quality and maturity within context of origin** -- the candidate must judge each wine against its regional benchmark
- **Winemaking techniques** at 5 marks each -- suggests some production diversity (oak vs no oak, lees work, etc.)

## Universe
A single white grape variety grown in at least two countries/regions with enough stylistic range to justify comparing quality, maturity, and winemaking. The 16 marks for variety ID suggests a globally important variety that should be recognizable.

## Candidate varieties (confidence tiers)
- **Chardonnay**: **STRONG SIGNAL** -- the most globally planted white, made in hugely different styles worldwide, always a prime candidate for pair comparisons
- **Riesling**: **STRONG SIGNAL** -- highly recognizable, made in diverse styles (dry GG vs sweet Kabinett, or Alsace vs Clare Valley)
- **Pinot Gris/Grigio**: **PLAUSIBLE** -- grown in Alsace, Oregon, NZ, Italy; diverse styles from light crisp to rich and weighty
- **Sauvignon Blanc**: **PLAUSIBLE** -- Loire vs Marlborough is classic, but was used in Q1 already for Loire
- **Chenin Blanc**: **PLAUSIBLE** -- Loire vs South Africa, but less globally ubiquitous

## Candidate origins per slot
- If Pinot Gris: Wine 4 from Alsace (richer, higher alcohol, possibly off-dry), Wine 5 from New Zealand or Oregon (lighter, drier, more fruit-forward)
- If Chardonnay: one Old World (Burgundy), one New World
- If Riesling: one German, one Australian or Alsatian

## Rule-outs
- Unlikely Sauvignon Blanc -- already featured in Q1 with the Pouilly-Fume; IMW avoids repeating same variety within a paper
- Unlikely a very obscure variety given 16 marks for ID -- they want candidates to get this right
- Muscadet/Melon de Bourgogne already used in Q1 -- ruled out

## IMW's likely intent
Testing variety recognition at high stakes (16 marks) combined with the ability to contextualize quality within different origins. The winemaking component at 5 marks suggests one wine may be more winemaker-influenced (oak, malolactic, lees) while the other is more terroir-driven. The maturity question implies wines at different stages of development.

## What to focus on in the glass
- Aromatic markers: stone fruit and richness (Pinot Gris), citrus and petrol (Riesling), apple and butter (Chardonnay)
- Residual sugar or off-dry character -- key for Pinot Gris or Riesling
- Weight and texture -- rich and oily (Alsace PG) vs crisp and lean (Oregon PG)
- Oak influence or lack thereof
- Alcohol level differences as climate indicator
