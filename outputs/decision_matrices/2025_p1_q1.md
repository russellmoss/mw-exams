# Decision matrix — 2025 Paper 1 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1 and 2 are blends of the same two grape varieties.

For both wines:

a) Identify the grape varieties. (10 marks)

For each wine:

b) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (2 x 8 marks)

c) Comment on the style, winemaking and quality. (2 x 12 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 1 = white wines only. Two wines, both blends of the same two grape varieties. This is a focused pairing exercise.

- Only 2 wines — depth over breadth; expect detailed winemaking and quality commentary

## Universe
White blends where two varieties are consistently blended together and are recognizable as a classic combination. The universe is surprisingly narrow:
- **Sauvignon Blanc + Semillon** (white Bordeaux, Margaret River, occasionally South Africa)
- **Marsanne + Roussanne** (Northern Rhone, Languedoc, occasionally Australia or California)
- **Chardonnay + Pinot Blanc** — uncommon as a named blend pair
- **Grenache Blanc + Roussanne** or other Southern Rhone white blends — typically more than two varieties
- **Riesling + anything** — Riesling is rarely blended in serious wines

## Candidate varieties (confidence tiers)
- **Sauvignon Blanc + Semillon**: **STRONG SIGNAL** — the most globally replicated two-variety white blend. White Bordeaux (Pessac-Leognan, Graves, Entre-Deux-Mers) and Margaret River are the two classic sources. Blend ratios vary, giving natural compare/contrast material.
- **Marsanne + Roussanne**: **PLAUSIBLE** — classic Rhone pair, but these are typically from a narrow geographic range (Northern Rhone, with occasional appearances in Victoria, Australia). Less likely because origin discrimination is harder with only two wines from essentially one template.
- **Chenin Blanc + Chardonnay**: **CURVEBALL** — South African producers sometimes blend these, but it is not an established classic pair.

## Candidate origins per wine
- Wine 1: **STRONG SIGNAL** Pessac-Leognan or Graves (white Bordeaux) — the Old World benchmark for SB/Sem blends. Alternatively Northern Rhone for Marsanne/Roussanne.
- Wine 2: **STRONG SIGNAL** Margaret River, Australia — the New World counterpart for SB/Sem. Alternatively Languedoc or Victoria for Marsanne/Roussanne.
- The pairing structure strongly suggests Old World vs New World, or two quality tiers of the same blend.

## Rule-outs
- Unlikely Riesling blend — Riesling is almost never blended with another named variety at this level
- Unlikely Pinot Gris + anything — no classic two-variety template exists
- Unlikely Gewurztraminer blend — almost always bottled solo
- Unlikely any non-European/Australasian origin for this type of blend — South America and South Africa rarely produce benchmark two-variety white blends

## IMW's likely intent
Testing whether candidates can: (1) identify the specific blend components from the glass rather than just saying "white blend," (2) discriminate between Old World and New World expressions of the same recipe, and (3) articulate winemaking differences (barrel fermentation, lees stirring, skin contact, SO2 management) that create different styles from identical varieties.

## What to focus on in the glass
- **Aromatic profile**: grassy/herbaceous (SB) vs waxy/lanolin (Sem) vs stone fruit/almond (Marsanne/Roussanne)
- **Oak regime**: white Bordeaux often sees new oak; Margaret River varies; Rhone whites are often partly oaked
- **Texture and weight**: Semillon adds body; Roussanne adds oiliness
- **Acidity structure**: crisp and citric (Bordeaux) vs broader and lower (Margaret River)
- **Color**: pale green-gold (young SB/Sem) vs deeper gold (Marsanne/Roussanne or aged)
