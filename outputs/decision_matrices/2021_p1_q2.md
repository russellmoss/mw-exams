# Decision matrix -- 2021 Paper 1 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 5-7 are from the same single grape variety.

With reference to all three wines:

a) Compare and contrast quality and maturity. (36 marks)

b) Identify the variety. (15 marks)

For each wine:

c) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (3 x 8 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 1 = white wines only. Three whites from the same single grape variety.

## Stem signals
- Three wines, **same single grape variety**
- Quality and maturity comparison is the dominant scoring area (36 marks) -- this is the priority skill being tested
- Variety identification worth 15 marks -- significant but secondary to quality/maturity
- Origin worth only 8 marks per wine (24 total) -- least important
- The heavy weighting on quality/maturity comparison suggests wines at different quality tiers and/or different stages of development
- "Compare and contrast" wording means wines should show clear differences in quality and maturity

## Universe
Single white grape variety grown in at least three meaningfully different origins, with quality tiers ranging from entry-level to premium, and maturity differences. The variety must be capable of aging to test maturity assessment.

## Candidate varieties (confidence tiers)
- Chardonnay: **STRONG SIGNAL** -- the ultimate "grown everywhere at every quality level" white grape; from Chablis Grand Cru to Yellow Tail, with enormous maturity range
- Riesling: **STRONG SIGNAL** -- grown in Germany, Alsace, Australia, Austria; massive quality range; ages superbly; maturity differences are obvious and testable
- Chenin Blanc: **PLAUSIBLE** -- Loire (Savennieres, Vouvray dry), South Africa, possibly US; good quality range; ages well; less commonly tested in triplets
- Sauvignon Blanc: **PLAUSIBLE** -- Loire, Marlborough, California; but ages less dramatically, making maturity comparison less interesting
- Semillon: **CURVEBALL** -- Hunter Valley, Bordeaux; ages brilliantly but narrow origin range for three wines
- Pinot Gris: **CURVEBALL** -- Alsace, Italy, Oregon; less maturity variation typically

## Candidate origins per wine
If Chardonnay: Burgundy (premium), New World (mid-tier), entry-level commercial
If Riesling: Mosel (Kabinett/Spatlese), Alsace, Clare/Eden Valley Australia
If Chenin Blanc: Savennieres or Vouvray (aged/premium), Anjou (entry), South Africa (mid)

## Rule-outs
- Gewurztraminer: too narrow in origin for three wines
- Muscat: quality range too narrow for meaningful maturity comparison
- Viognier: limited aging potential makes the 36-mark maturity question less logical
- Albarino: single-region variety essentially
- Gruner Veltliner: primarily Austrian, hard to source three distinct origins

## IMW's likely intent
The 36 marks for quality/maturity comparison is enormous -- this question is fundamentally about demonstrating the ability to articulate *how* a white wine evolves and how quality manifests across different examples of the same grape. The variety should be one with dramatic quality and maturity variation. Chenin Blanc fits well here: it ranges from cheap Anjou to age-worthy Savennieres, and old Chenin develops extraordinary complexity. Chardonnay and Riesling are also strong candidates for the same reason.

## What to focus on in the glass
- Primary vs secondary vs tertiary aromas: young fruit vs honey/lanolin vs oxidative/nutty
- Acidity preservation: high residual acidity in aged wine = quality marker
- Color progression: pale lemon (young) vs gold (developing) vs deep gold (mature)
- Texture and weight: lean young wine vs rich developed wine
- Oak influence vs varietal purity: oaked Chardonnay vs unoaked Riesling/Chenin
- Length and complexity: simple fruit-driven vs multilayered
- Specific varietal markers: petrol (Riesling), quince/wool-wax (Chenin), citrus/butter (Chardonnay)
