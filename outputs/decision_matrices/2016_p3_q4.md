# Decision matrix -- 2016 Paper 3 Question 4

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 11 and 12 come from the same region and producer.

With reference to both wines:

a) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (20 marks)

b) Discuss the key winemaking techniques used to arrive at these styles. (20 marks)

c) Discuss how the styles of these wines determine their commercial appeal. (10 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 3 same-producer pairs usually test whether the candidate can map one producer's toolkit onto two distinct special styles.

## Stem signals
- Same **region and producer**
- Production is heavily weighted, equal to origin
- Commercial appeal differs by **style**, implying two recognizably different end points
- No separate varietal mark, so the regional family is more important than exact grapes

## Universe
The clearest exam fit is a region where one producer can make two related but different special-style wines from the same raw material base. Strong candidates:
- Valpolicella region: Ripasso and Recioto, or Amarone and Recioto
- Madeira producer with dry and sweet age tiers
- Sherry bodega with two styles, though "same producer and region" would be less interesting commercially

Valpolicella is especially persuasive because it allows a direct production comparison between dry and sweet or dry and appassimento-derived styles, plus clear commercial differentiation.

## Candidate origins (confidence tiers)
- Valpolicella, Veneto: **STRONG SIGNAL**
- Madeira: **PLAUSIBLE**
- Jerez: **CURVEBALL**

## Candidate style pair
- One wine likely a **dry appassimento-derived style** such as Ripasso or Amarone
- One likely a **sweet style** such as Recioto

## Rule-outs
- Unlikely two near-identical table wines, because the production/commercial split would be too weak
- Unlikely sparkling pair, because same producer and region would not naturally create a broad commercial-style contrast
- Unlikely New World region; this feels very classical and appellation-driven

## IMW's likely intent
The examiner wants the candidate to explain how one producer can manipulate drying, refermentation, residual sugar, extraction, and élevage to produce two distinct wines from one regional tradition. Commercial appeal then follows naturally: one style may be more gastronomic and versatile, the other more niche, indulgent, or dessert-led.

## What to focus on in the glass
- Dryness split first: are both dry, or is one sweet?
- Dried-fruit signatures suggest appassimento
- Alcohol, glycerol, and tannin can help separate Ripasso, Amarone, and Recioto families
- Commercial positioning should identify who buys the richer, sweeter, or more intense style and on what occasion
