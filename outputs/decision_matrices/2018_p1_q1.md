# Decision matrix — 2018 Paper 1 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-4 are all made from the same single grape variety.

With reference to all four wines:

a) Identify the grape variety. (20 marks)

For each wine:

b) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (4 x 10 marks)

c) Discuss quality with reference to winemaking techniques used. (4 x 10 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 1 = white wines. All four wines share a single grape variety, making variety ID the pivotal call.

## Stem signals
- **Same single grape variety** across four wines — must be a globally planted white grape with enough stylistic range to justify four examples
- **20 marks for variety ID** — enormous weighting, the IMW considers this the anchor of the question
- **4 x 10 marks for origin** — moderately weighted, expects sub-region specificity
- **Quality with reference to winemaking** — the IMW wants candidates to connect winemaking decisions (oak, MLF, lees, reductive/oxidative) to quality outcomes
- Four wines = likely a spread from entry-level to premium, from Old World to New World

## Universe
Given P1 (whites) + single variety + four wines from potentially global origins + winemaking-quality nexus, the universe narrows to varieties grown internationally at vastly different quality levels:
- Chardonnay, Riesling, Sauvignon Blanc, possibly Chenin Blanc or Pinot Gris

## Candidate varieties (confidence tiers)
- **Chardonnay**: **STRONG SIGNAL** — the quintessential variety for this structure. Four wines spanning basic to Grand Cru, New World to Old World, oaked to unoaked. The winemaking emphasis (20 marks on quality + winemaking) screams Chardonnay because oak, MLF, lees contact, and reductive vs. oxidative handling are all distinguishing markers.
- **Riesling**: **PLAUSIBLE** — can span quality tiers (Kabinett to GG to Auslese), but winemaking variation is narrower (minimal oak, less MLF). The "winemaking techniques" emphasis makes Riesling less likely for four wines.
- **Sauvignon Blanc**: **PLAUSIBLE** — Loire vs Marlborough vs Bordeaux could provide range, but winemaking variation is more limited than Chardonnay.
- **Pinot Gris/Grigio**: **CURVEBALL** — could span Alsace rich to Italian crisp, but four wines from different origins is a stretch.

## Candidate origins per wine
Assuming Chardonnay (STRONG SIGNAL):
- Wine 1: entry-level New World (Australia commodity, Chile, or California bulk) — to establish the floor
- Wine 2: classic Burgundy (Chablis or Cote de Beaune) — benchmark Old World
- Wine 3: premium New World (Napa, Sonoma, or Margaret River) — oaked, rich style
- Wine 4: secondary Old World or quality New World (New Zealand, South Africa, or second Burgundy expression)

## Rule-outs
- **Gewurztraminer** — too regionally concentrated (Alsace dominates); four diverse origins implausible
- **Muscat** — rarely made as a serious dry single-varietal white in four origins
- **Semillon** — too few global expressions to sustain four wines
- **Viognier** — similar problem; Condrieu dominates, limited New World range for four

## IMW's likely intent
Testing the candidate's ability to: (1) identify the world's most important white grape variety, (2) distinguish quality tiers from commodity to Grand Cru, (3) articulate how specific winemaking choices (oak regime, MLF, lees stirring, fermentation vessel) drive style and quality differences within the same variety, (4) pinpoint origin from palate cues across climate zones.

## What to focus on in the glass
- **Oak markers**: vanilla, toast, butterscotch = new oak; subtle integration = quality oak; absence = stainless/neutral
- **MLF indicators**: buttery, creamy texture vs. bright malic acidity
- **Fruit spectrum**: green apple/citrus (cool climate) vs. tropical/stone fruit (warm)
- **Alcohol level**: correlates with climate and ripeness philosophy
- **Colour**: pale/green-tinged (young/cool) vs. gold (warm/aged/oak)
- **Texture**: lean and mineral vs. rich and glycerol-laden
