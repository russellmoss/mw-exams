a) Comment on the style of the wine with reference to the relative importance of human inputs versus natural factors. (3 x 15 marks)

b) Identify the grape variety and origin as closely as possible. (3 x 10 marks)

## Stem signals

- **"Human inputs versus natural factors"** — this is a highly specific framing. The IMW wants discussion of terroir (natural: climate, soil, aspect) vs winemaker decisions (human: fermentation choices, oak, lees, oxidative/reductive handling, arrested fermentation, drying of grapes)
- **Style commentary is primary** (3 x 15 = 45 marks) — identification is secondary (3 x 10 = 30 marks). The IMW cares more about analytical thinking than naming.

## Universe
European white wines where the human vs. natural debate is particularly relevant:
- **Oxidative-style whites**: Vin Jaune (Jura, France), white Rioja (Spain), some Italian whites — heavy human input via oxidative aging

- **Cool-climate wines where climate dominates**: German Riesling (Mosel) — natural factors (slate, steep slopes, cool climate) arguably shape the wine more than winemaking
- **Oak-aged whites**: White Rioja, oaked Burgundy — barrel choice is a key human input
- **Dried-grape wines**: Vin Santo — the appassimento decision is entirely human

## IMW's likely intent
This is a philosophical question dressed as a tasting exercise. The IMW wants to see: (1) the candidate can taste a wine and articulate what comes from nature (climate-driven acidity, mineral expression, vintage character) vs what comes from the winemaker (oak, oxidation, lees, arrested fermentation, grape drying), (2) understanding of traditional European winemaking where these two forces interact in distinctive ways, and (3) comfort with wines that may be unusual or unfamiliar — the low ID marks suggest difficulty wines.

## What to focus on in the glass
- **Oxidation level**: golden/amber color, nutty/bruised apple aromas = significant human-driven oxidative aging
- **Sweetness level**: any RS points to human decision (arrest fermentation or select botrytized fruit)
- **Oak influence**: vanilla, toast, spice = barrel aging (human choice)
- **Purity of fruit**: if primary fruit is dominant, natural factors are leading
- **Alcohol level**: low alcohol (Germany) vs moderate (Spain) vs variable (Vin Santo)
- **Texture markers**: dried-fruit character, viscosity (Vin Santo); waxy, oxidative richness (white Rioja); steely precision (German Riesling)
