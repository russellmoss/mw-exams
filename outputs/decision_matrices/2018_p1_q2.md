For each wine:

a) Identify the grape variety. (6 x 7 marks)

b) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (6 x 8 marks)

c) Discuss quality with reference to winemaking techniques used. (6 x 10 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 1 = white wines. Six wines, each a different single variety, each a different country. This is the broadest possible variety-ID test on a P1 paper.

## Stem signals
- **Six different single varieties** — the IMW wants breadth of varietal knowledge, not depth on one grape
- **Six different countries** — global breadth; at least two or three Old World and two or three New World
- **6 x 7 marks for variety** — moderate but cumulative (42 marks total on variety alone); getting even one wrong is costly
- **Winemaking + quality** emphasis (60 marks total) — the IMW wants candidates to connect winemaking to style
- Six wines in one question means time pressure is extreme; candidates must be decisive

## IMW's likely intent

This is the "grab bag" question that separates candidates with narrow knowledge (Chard/Sauv Blanc/Riesling) from those who know Gruner Veltliner, Torrontes, Garganega, Semillon, etc. The winemaking component tests whether candidates can articulate technique differences across very different styles.

## What to focus on in the glass
- **Aromatic profile**: floral (Torrontes, Gewurz, Muscat), herbal (Gruner Veltliner, Sauvignon), neutral (Semillon, Garganega), petrol/lime (Riesling)
- **Body/texture**: lean and crisp (Riesling, Albarino) vs. waxy (Semillon) vs. oily (Gewurz, Pinot Gris)
- **Acidity structure**: high/racy (Riesling, Gruner) vs. moderate (Chenin, Soave) vs. low (Viognier)
- **Oak presence**: most of these varieties are vinified without oak, so any oak = important clue (white Rioja, oaked Semillon, Chenin)
- **Alcohol**: low (Riesling Germany) vs. high (Viognier, Torrontes)
- **Colour**: water-white (young Riesling) vs. golden (aged Semillon, oaked Rioja)
