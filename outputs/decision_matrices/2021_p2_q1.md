# Decision matrix -- 2021 Paper 2 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-3 are all from the same region and producer. Each wine is from a different vintage.

For all three wines:

a) Identify the region as closely as possible, referencing each wine. (15 marks)

For each wine:

b) Identify the vintage. Discuss quality in the context of the region and the vintage. (3 x 20 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 2 = red wines only. Three reds from the same region and producer, different vintages.

## Stem signals
- Three wines, same **region**, same **producer**, different **vintages**
- Region identification worth 15 marks -- must be as specific as possible
- Vintage identification + vintage quality discussion worth 20 marks EACH (60 total) -- this is the dominant test
- No variety identification requested -- the IMW assumes the candidate will know what's grown in the region
- The "discuss quality in the context of the region and the vintage" language means candidates must know vintage variation for the specific region
- This is a vertical tasting exercise: same producer, same region, three vintages

## Universe
Which red wine regions and producers are famous enough that the IMW would test vintage knowledge across three different years? The candidate must know vintage character (hot/cold, wet/dry, great/difficult years). This demands a classic region with well-documented vintage variation and iconic producers.

## Candidate regions (confidence tiers)
- **Bordeaux (Left Bank or Right Bank)**: **STRONG SIGNAL** -- the ultimate region for vintage-driven discussion; classified growths provide the famous-producer anchor; vintages like 2005, 2009, 2010, 2015, 2016 vs. weaker years like 2011, 2013 are well documented
- **Burgundy (Cote de Nuits or Beaune)**: **STRONG SIGNAL** -- vintage variation is extreme; top domaines are studied obsessively
- **Barolo/Barbaresco**: **PLAUSIBLE** -- strong vintage narrative; producers like Giacomo Conterno, Gaja, Bruno Giacosa
- **Rioja**: **PLAUSIBLE** -- Reserva/Gran Reserva system is vintage-dependent; CVNE, Lopez de Heredia, Muga
- **Northern Rhone (Cote Rotie, Hermitage)**: **PLAUSIBLE** -- Guigal, Jaboulet, Chapoutier
- **Napa Valley**: **CURVEBALL** -- vintage variation exists but less dramatic than Old World

## Candidate producers per region
If Bordeaux: Ducru-Beaucaillou, Leoville-Las Cases, Pichon-Baron, Palmer, Pontet-Canet (all classified growths with consistent style across vintages)
If Burgundy: DRC, Rousseau, Mugnier, Dujac (famous domaines)
If Barolo: Giacomo Conterno, Bartolo Mascarello, Giuseppe Rinaldi

## Rule-outs
- Australia: vintage variation too subtle for a 60-mark vintage-discussion question
- New World generally: the vintage knowledge requirement heavily favors Old World classics
- Southern Rhone: too much vintage consistency to generate 60 marks of discussion
- Tuscany (Chianti/Brunello): possible but less iconic for vintage vertical than Bordeaux/Burgundy

## IMW's likely intent
This is the most intellectually demanding question format: identify the region from the wine style, then demonstrate deep vintage knowledge. The 20 marks per wine for "quality in context of vintage" means the IMW wants candidates to say things like "this wine shows the concentration and ripe tannins typical of the 2009 vintage" or "the lighter body and higher acidity suggest the cooler 2013 vintage." Bordeaux is the strongest candidate because vintage reports are the most extensively published and debated, and because classified growths provide a fixed quality benchmark across vintages.

## What to focus on in the glass
- Tannin structure and ripeness: ripe, sweet tannins = warm vintage; green, gripping = cool vintage
- Alcohol level: higher alcohol = warmer year
- Color depth: deep/opaque = ripe/concentrated vintage; lighter = cooler year
- Fruit character: cassis/blackberry = ripe; red currant/cranberry = cooler
- Development: the oldest wine should show the most tertiary character (leather, tobacco, earth)
- Relative concentration across three wines: rank from lightest to richest
- Oak integration: well-integrated = good vintage with fruit to support it
- Producer consistency: house style should be recognizable across all three
