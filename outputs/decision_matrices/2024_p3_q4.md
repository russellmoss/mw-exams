## IMW's likely intent
This is a "sweet wine mechanisms" question. The IMW wants to test whether candidates understand the FIVE main ways wine can be made sweet: (1) botrytis/noble rot, (2) frozen grapes/ice wine, (3) dried grapes/passito, (4) fortification (spirit addition arrests fermentation), (5) natural arrested fermentation (high sugar stops yeast). The RS and alcohol statements are diagnostic tools — a wine with 200 g/l RS and 9.5% alcohol is clearly ice wine; a wine with 100 g/l RS and 15% is clearly fortified. The candidate must connect the dots: method of sweetening → RS level → alcohol level → region → grape variety.

## What to focus on in the glass
- **Colour intensity**: pale gold (off-dry Riesling/Vouvray), deep gold (botrytis), amber (Vin Santo, old Tokaji), dark amber/brown (fortified Muscat)
- **Viscosity**: ice wine = very viscous (extreme sugar), Vin Santo = syrupy, lighter sweet wines = less viscous
- **Sweetness vs. acidity balance**: essential for quality assessment and style differentiation
- **Botrytis markers**: honey, dried apricot, marmalade, ginger, saffron
- **Passito/dried fruit markers**: fig, raisin, caramel, nutty oxidation
- **Fortification indicators**: spirit warmth, higher alcohol heat on palate, grapey (Muscat) or raisiny (PX)
- **Freshness**: ice wines and German sweet wines tend toward bright, fresh acid; Vin Santo and fortified tend toward richer, oxidative character
- **Alcohol burn**: key diagnostic — below 12% suggests non-fortified, above 15% suggests fortified or passito
