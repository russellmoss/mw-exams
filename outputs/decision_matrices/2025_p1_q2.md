# Decision matrix — 2025 Paper 1 Question 2

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 3-6 are from the same single grape variety.

With reference to all four wines:

a) Identify the grape variety. (16 marks)

For each wine:

b) Comment on the style, quality, and maturity. (4 x 13 marks)

c) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (4 x 8 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 1 = white wines only. Four wines, all the same single variety. This is the classic "variety ID across multiple origins" format — the exam's bread and butter.

## Stem signals
- **Same single grape variety** across all four wines — high marks (16) for variety ID signals a mainstream, identifiable variety
- **Four wines** — enough to show geographic range, climate variation, and quality spread
- **Style, quality, maturity** (4 x 13 = 52 marks) is the dominant scoring area — the IMW wants nuanced discrimination across expressions
- **Origin** (4 x 8 = 32 marks) is secondary but substantial — these wines should be placeable by region
- No mention of "ageing potential" in the stem, but "maturity" implies some wines may show evolution

## Universe
A single white grape variety grown in at least four distinct origins that produce recognizably different styles. This is a short list:
- **Chardonnay** — the most globally planted quality white, grown everywhere from Burgundy to California to South Africa to Australia
- **Riesling** — grown in Germany, Alsace, Australia, Austria, New Zealand
- **Sauvignon Blanc** — Loire, Marlborough, California, South Africa
- **Pinot Gris/Grigio** — Alsace, Italy, Oregon, New Zealand (but rarely four distinct origins at exam level)
- **Chenin Blanc** — Loire and South Africa primarily; harder to find four distinct origins

## Candidate varieties (confidence tiers)
- **Chardonnay**: **STRONG SIGNAL** — the most versatile white variety for showing quality range (Grand Cru Burgundy to commercial entry-level), maturity spectrum (young unoaked to aged and complex), and global reach. Four different Chardonnays from four origins is the classic P1 question.
- **Riesling**: **PLAUSIBLE** — can show enormous style range (bone-dry GG to sweet Kabinett), but maturity commentary is less varied since Riesling ages similarly across origins. Also already used heavily in 2024 P1Q1.
- **Sauvignon Blanc**: **PLAUSIBLE** — good geographic range but narrower style variation; four SBs risk repetition. Also less likely because SB is harder to discuss at length for maturity.
- **Chenin Blanc**: **CURVEBALL** — only two major production countries (France, South Africa), making four distinct origins difficult.

## Candidate origins per wine
Assuming Chardonnay (most likely):
- Wine 3: **Burgundy, France** — the benchmark; likely a village or premier cru showing terroir and age
- Wine 4: **California or Australia** — commercial tier or warm-climate expression for contrast
- Wine 5: **South Africa, New Zealand, or cool-climate New World** — showing a modern, elegant style
- Wine 6: **Margaret River or another warm-climate region** — richer, possibly oaked

The IMW typically includes a quality spread: one benchmark/expensive, one commercial/entry-level, and two in between.

## Rule-outs
- Unlikely Gewurztraminer — too narrow a production base for four distinct origins
- Unlikely Viognier — same limitation; primarily Rhone and a few New World sites
- Unlikely Muscat — rarely shown as four still dry whites
- Unlikely Albarino, Gruner Veltliner, or other regionally specific varieties — insufficient geographic spread for four wines

## IMW's likely intent
Testing: (1) confident variety ID of a major white grape, (2) the ability to describe how the same variety expresses differently across climates and winemaking philosophies, (3) quality assessment within the variety — distinguishing a Grand Cru from a commercial wine of the same grape, and (4) maturity assessment — which wines are drinking now vs. which have evolved or need time.

## What to focus on in the glass
- **Oak vs. unoaked**: Chardonnay is the variety most transformed by oak contact; look for vanilla, toast, butterscotch
- **Malo vs. no malo**: creamy/buttery texture signals MLF; crisp/citric signals partial or no MLF
- **Alcohol and body**: warm-climate expressions (14%+) vs cool-climate (12-13%)
- **Color**: pale straw (young, cool) vs deeper gold (aged or warm or oaked)
- **Acidity type**: green apple/citrus (cool) vs tropical/stone fruit (warm)
- **Evolution markers**: honey, lanolin, toast development on aged wines
