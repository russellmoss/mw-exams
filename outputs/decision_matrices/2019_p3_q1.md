# Decision matrix — 2019 Paper 3 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-2 are both from the same region.

For both wines:

a) Identify the region. (10 marks)

b) Compare and contrast the method of production. (15 marks)

c) Compare and contrast the style, quality and commercial appeal of the two wines. (25 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 3 = mixed styles (sparkling, fortified, sweet, rose, oxidative, unusual). Two wines from the same region — the question does not specify style, so these could be any P3 category.

## Stem signals
- **"Same region"** — both wines come from one region, which is the anchor for 10 marks
- **"Compare and contrast the method of production" (15 marks)** — high weight on winemaking. This implies the two wines are made differently within the same region, which is the test: same place, different production methods
- **"Style, quality and commercial appeal" (25 marks)** — the bulk of marks. The IMW wants deep comparison of how different production methods yield different styles from the same terroir
- **Only 2 wines** — a focused, deep-dive pair rather than a breadth flight
- **No variety or origin-per-wine marks** — variety ID is implicit in region ID; the focus is entirely on production and style

## Universe
P3 regions that produce two clearly different styles via different production methods. The key constraint is that both wines are from the same region but made differently. Classic candidates:
- **Champagne**: NV vs. vintage, blanc de blancs vs. rose, different dosage levels — but this is a well-trodden comparison
- **Jerez (Sherry)**: Fino/Manzanilla vs. Oloroso, or biological vs. oxidative aging — radically different production methods, same region
- **Douro (Port)**: Ruby vs. Tawny, or Vintage Port vs. LBV — different aging regimes
- **Jura**: Vin Jaune (oxidative) vs. Ouille style (topped-up), or still vs. sparkling
- **Tokaj**: dry Furmint vs. Aszu — different residual sugar levels from same region
- **Sauternes**: dry vs. sweet from the same producer (R de Rieussec vs. Rieussec)

## Candidate styles (confidence tiers)
- **Champagne (two different quality/style levels)**: **STRONG SIGNAL** — NV vs. prestige cuvee, or brut vs. zero dosage; P3 frequently opens with sparkling
- **Sherry (two styles)**: **PLAUSIBLE** — Fino vs. Oloroso is a brilliant production-method comparison
- **Douro/Port (two aging styles)**: **PLAUSIBLE** — Ruby vs. Tawny tests oxidative vs. reductive aging

- **Prosecco (two quality levels)**: **CURVEBALL** — DOCG vs. DOC, Charmat method variations

## Rule-outs
- **Still table wines** — unlikely in P3 Q1 with this structure; the production-method focus points to sparkling or fortified
- **Sweet wines** — possible but less likely for a pair from the same region with production focus
- **Rosé** — unlikely as the sole focus for a same-region pair in P3

## IMW's likely intent
Testing understanding of how production methods shape wine style within a single region. The 15 marks for production and 25 marks for style/quality/commercial appeal mean the IMW wants detailed discussion of winemaking differences and their impact. This is a "depth over breadth" question.

## What to focus on in the glass
- **Bubbles**: if present, note mousse quality, bead size — distinguishes traditional method from Charmat
- **Oxidative character**: nuts, dried fruit, aldehydes = sherry or oxidative aging
- **Autolytic character**: bread, brioche, biscuit = extended lees aging (Champagne vintage)
- **Sweetness level**: residual sugar differences between the two wines
- **Color intensity**: deeper gold/amber in one = more oxidative aging or older
- **Alcohol level**: significant difference may indicate fortification
