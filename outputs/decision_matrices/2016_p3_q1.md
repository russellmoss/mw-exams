# Decision matrix -- 2016 Paper 3 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-5 are all made from the same single grape variety.

For each wine:

a) Discuss winemaking, with particular reference to oak. (5 x 10 marks)

b) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (5 x 8 marks)

c) Comment on quality and commercial appeal. (5 x 7 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 3 can still test one grape variety when the category spread is unusual enough to sit outside a normal white-paper structure.

## Stem signals
- All five wines are from the **same single grape variety**
- Winemaking, **particularly oak**, is the dominant clue
- Origin matters strongly, so the grape must be globally planted and stylistically flexible
- Quality and commercial appeal are also relevant, meaning the set probably spans prestige and more accessible examples

## Universe
The grape must be able to appear in multiple formats and interact meaningfully with oak. That immediately points to **Chardonnay**. Other possibilities such as Viognier or Chenin can see oak, but none are as globally exam-stable or as commercially broad.

The Paper 3 location suggests a twist: the examiner may include sparkling and still examples of the same grape rather than five ordinary still whites. Chardonnay is the cleanest variety for that move because it can be Blanc de Blancs, lean unoaked still wine, or oaked fine wine.

## Candidate varieties (confidence tiers)
- Chardonnay: **STRONG SIGNAL**
- Chenin Blanc: **PLAUSIBLE**
- Viognier: **CURVEBALL**

## Candidate origins per slot
- At least one wine likely from **Champagne** or another traditional-method sparkling region if the P3 twist is real
- One or more still Chardonnays likely from **Burgundy**
- One New World Chardonnay likely from **Australia, New Zealand, California, or South Africa**
- One lower-oak or lesser-oak commercial example likely to test commercial appeal versus prestige

## Rule-outs
- Unlikely Riesling, because oak would be a strange central theme
- Unlikely Sauvignon Blanc, same reason
- Unlikely all sparkling wines, because commercial appeal and origin would become too narrow across five slots

## IMW's likely intent
The examiner is testing whether the candidate understands oak as a stylistic variable rather than a simple marker of quality. Chardonnay allows discussion of fermentation in barrel, maturation in barrique, new versus used oak, malo, lees, and the near absence of overt oak in some sparkling expressions. The strongest answers will also notice that "commercial appeal" differs sharply between prestige autolytic Chardonnay and broad-market still Chardonnay.

## What to focus on in the glass
- First decide whether the grape can plausibly be Chardonnay across all five wines
- If there is sparkling wine, ask how oak may have been used in base-wine fermentation or reserve-wine handling
- In still wines, separate overt oak flavor from texture effects such as creaminess, weight, and spice
- Look for classic Chardonnay markers: apple/citrus/stone fruit, malo, lees breadth, oak-derived toast or vanilla
- Commercial appeal should distinguish prestige collectable wines from clean everyday examples
