# Decision matrix -- 2017 Paper 2 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-4 are from different countries. They all share a common dominant grape variety.

With reference to all four wines:

a) Identify the common dominant grape variety. (16 marks)

For each wine:

b) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (4 x 8 marks)

c) Identify the key winemaking techniques used, with reference to other grape varieties and any oak maturation. (4 x 6 marks)

d) Comment on quality and maturity. (4 x 7 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 2 = red wines only. Four reds from four different countries sharing one dominant variety. The word "dominant" implies some wines may be blends.

- **Quality and maturity at 7 marks** -- moderate weight; assessing development and quality tier

- **Cabernet Sauvignon**: **STRONG SIGNAL** -- the ultimate globally planted red, dominant in Bordeaux blends (with Merlot, Cab Franc), Australian blends (with Shiraz), South African blends, Napa blends. Four countries easy: France, USA, Australia, South Africa

## Rule-outs
- Unlikely Pinot Noir -- rarely blended, and question explicitly asks about blending partners
- Unlikely Nebbiolo or Sangiovese -- not grown in four distinct countries at significant levels
- Unlikely Malbec -- primarily Argentina, limited representation elsewhere

## IMW's likely intent
Testing global Cabernet Sauvignon literacy. The mention of blending partners is the key signal -- Bordeaux blends use Merlot and Cab Franc; Australian blends may use Shiraz; Napa uses the Bordeaux varieties; South Africa often blends Cape Bordeaux style. The question wants candidates to know how Cab Sauv expression changes across climates and how blending decisions differ by region. Oak maturation discussion will differentiate French oak (Bordeaux), American oak (some New World), and oak programs.

## What to focus on in the glass
- Cassis/blackcurrant core (Cab Sauv signature)
- Tannin structure: firm and angular (Bordeaux) vs plush and ripe (Napa/Barossa)
- Cedar/tobacco (aged Bordeaux) vs vanilla/coconut (American oak) vs dark fruit (warm climate)
- Alcohol levels as climate markers
- Blending evidence: plummy softness (Merlot component), herbal lift (Cab Franc), spice (Shiraz)
- Maturity indicators: brick rim, tertiary aromas, resolved tannins
