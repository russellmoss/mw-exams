# Decision matrix -- 2024 Paper 2 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-3 are from the same single grape variety.

With reference to all three wines:

a) Identify the grape variety. (15 marks)

b) Compare and contrast the styles and consumer appeal of the wines. (30 marks)

For each wine:

c) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (3 x 10 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 2 = red wines only. Three questions across 12 wines. Q1 is the opening question, typically covering wines 1-3 or 1-4, and the IMW often leads with a recognizable international variety to give candidates a confident start before moving to more challenging flights.

## Stem signals
- Three wines, all **same single grape variety** -- no blends, no "predominant variety" hedging
- **15 marks for variety ID** -- substantial, indicating this is a mainstream variety the candidate should be able to name with certainty
- **30 marks for styles and consumer appeal** -- this is the dominant mark allocation, suggesting the IMW wants deep comparative analysis of how one grape manifests differently depending on climate, winemaking, and price point
- **Origin only 10 marks each** -- relatively low, meaning exact origin is less critical than understanding the stylistic range
- The emphasis on "consumer appeal" signals wines at different quality/price tiers, likely spanning entry-level to premium

## Universe
Given P2 (reds only) + "same single grape variety" + emphasis on stylistic range and consumer appeal, the variety must be:
- Grown in multiple climates (cool and warm) to demonstrate style variation
- Made at vastly different quality levels with clear consumer differentiation
- Recognizable enough to justify 15 marks for a single identification call

This points to a top-tier international red variety. The user's annotation confirms: "a grape that can be made in vastly different styles, in cool and hot climates, and at a wide range of price points."

## Candidate varieties (confidence tiers)

- **Syrah/Shiraz: STRONG SIGNAL** -- the classic cool-vs-warm variety (Northern Rhone elegance vs Barossa power), enormous stylistic range from peppery and restrained to blockbuster and jammy, wide price spectrum from commodity to Cote-Rotie/Hermitage benchmark. The "consumer appeal" framing rewards candidates who can articulate why Shiraz sells differently than Syrah.
- **Pinot Noir: STRONG SIGNAL** -- huge stylistic range from Burgundy Grand Cru to New World fruit-bomb, vast quality pyramid, consumer appeal varies dramatically from fine-dining allocation lists to supermarket shelves. However, three Pinots without a pairing/compare structure is slightly less typical for IMW.
- **Cabernet Sauvignon: PLAUSIBLE** -- made globally at every price point, but stylistic variation is narrower than Syrah (warm-climate Cab dominates globally). The IMW might find less interesting contrasts in three Cabs.
- **Merlot: PLAUSIBLE** -- Right Bank Bordeaux vs. commodity Merlot is a legitimate pairing, grown globally, but three origins testing "consumer appeal" is slightly less common for Merlot.
- **Tempranillo: CURVEBALL** -- primarily associated with Spain (Rioja, Ribera del Duero), limited global plantings make three diverse origins harder to source. Unlikely given the stylistic breadth the question demands.
- **Grenache/Garnacha: CURVEBALL** -- increasingly global but still secondary as a single-variety bottling outside of specific contexts.

## Rule-outs
- **Nebbiolo**: too region-specific (Piedmont dominates) -- cannot source three convincingly different consumer profiles
- **Sangiovese**: primarily Italian; hard to get three diverse origins with meaningful consumer-appeal contrast
- **Malbec**: primarily Argentine; while grown elsewhere, three origins with "vastly different styles" is a stretch
- **Cabernet Franc**: the 2025 exam already used this for P2 Q1 with a climate-focused question, and Cab Franc is borderline "top 5" -- possible but less likely than Syrah or Pinot Noir
- Any obscure variety: 15 marks for variety ID + opening Q1 position means this is not a curveball

## IMW's likely intent
The examiners are testing whether the candidate can:
1. **Identify a major red variety** with confidence across three expressions
2. **Articulate how climate and winemaking drive style** -- the 30-mark compare/contrast demands the candidate explain WHY the wines taste different despite sharing a grape
3. **Understand consumer segmentation** -- who buys each wine, through what channel, at what price point, and why the style appeals to that buyer
4. Origin is secondary (10 marks each) -- the IMW cares less about pinpointing the exact subregion and more about the candidate demonstrating that variety + terroir + winemaking = style = market position

## What to focus on in the glass
- **Color depth and hue**: Syrah from warm climates will be inky and opaque; cool-climate Syrah/Pinot will show more transparency and ruby/garnet tones
- **Aromatic profile**: black pepper, olive, violet = Syrah cool-climate; dark chocolate, licorice, over-ripe blackberry = warm-climate Shiraz; red cherry, earth, mushroom = Pinot Noir
- **Tannin structure**: fine-grained vs. chunky -- this separates premium from commodity
- **Alcohol level**: if you perceive significant alcohol variation across the three wines, Syrah/Shiraz is the strongest candidate (8-14.5% range for cool vs warm)
- **Oak signature**: new French oak (spice, toast) vs. American oak (coconut, vanilla) vs. minimal oak (fruit purity) -- directly maps to consumer appeal and price point
- **Acidity**: high acid + moderate alcohol = Old World or cool New World; lower acid + high alcohol = warm New World
- **Finish length and complexity**: the premium wine will show persistence and secondary/tertiary development; the commodity wine will be simple and short
