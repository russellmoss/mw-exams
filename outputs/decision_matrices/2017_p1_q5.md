For each wine:

a) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (2 x 10 marks)

b) Identify the vintage and consider the capacity to improve in the bottle. (2 x 8 marks)

c) Discuss the method of production with particular reference to the use of oak. (2 x 7 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 1 = white wines. Final question: two whites from the same variety. The oak and ageing focus is prominent.

## Stem signals

- **Oak discussion at 7 marks** -- oak is central to the style; this is a variety where oak maturation is the norm

## Rule-outs
- Riesling unlikely due to oak emphasis
- Unlikely a non-European variety since the paper has been heavily European
- Unlikely Semillon -- while it ages well, the vintage identification question points more to Burgundy Chardonnay where vintage knowledge is essential

## IMW's likely intent
Testing Burgundy Chardonnay literacy at a deep level. The vintage identification question is the giveaway -- the IMW loves testing whether candidates can date Burgundy wines by their development, color, and aromatic evolution. The oak discussion tests whether the candidate can distinguish between new oak, old oak, barrel size, and fermentation in oak vs ageing in oak. This is fundamentally a "do you know white Burgundy?" question.

## What to focus on in the glass
- Color: golden tones indicate age; pale lemon suggests youth
- Oak character: toast, vanilla, butter (new oak) vs subtle integration (old oak/large format)
- Development aromas: hazelnut, honey, lanolin (mature Burgundy Chardonnay)
- Acidity structure and length -- key for predicting ageability
- Richness and viscosity -- Meursault is richer than Chablis
- Reduction vs oxidation -- barrel-fermented wines may show more controlled oxidation
