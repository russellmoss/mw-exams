# Decision matrix — 2019 Paper 1 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-4 are from two different countries. They may be blends or single varieties, but one variety is common to all.

With reference to all four wines:

a) Identify the common grape variety. (20 marks)

For each wine:

b) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (4 x 10 marks)

c) Comment on quality and style with reference to winemaking. (4 x 10 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 1 = white wines only. Four wines, two countries, one common variety across all — some may be blends.

## Stem signals
- **Two countries** — not a single-country flight; cross-country comparison is central
- **"May be blends or single varieties"** — at least one wine is likely a blend, but all share one grape
- **"One variety is common to all"** — the linking grape must be grown meaningfully in at least two countries and must be plausible as both a varietal wine and a blend component
- **20 marks for variety ID** — very high stakes on getting the common grape right
- **4 x 10 marks for origin** — origin discrimination matters, so the two countries should be distinguishable in the glass
- **Quality and style with reference to winemaking** — expects discussion of oak, lees, fermentation choices

## Universe
White grapes grown in at least two countries, used both as single varietal and in blends. The "blend" signal heavily points toward varieties that are classic blend components: Sauvignon Blanc (Bordeaux blanc blends + varietal Loire/NZ), Semillon (Bordeaux blanc + varietal Hunter Valley), Chardonnay (Burgundy blends are rare but exists globally), or Chenin Blanc (Loire + South Africa). The two-country constraint with blend possibility strongly favors **Sauvignon Blanc** (France varietal in Loire + blends in Bordeaux vs. New Zealand varietal) or **Semillon** (Australia varietal + Bordeaux blends).

## Candidate varieties (confidence tiers)
- **Sauvignon Blanc**: **STRONG SIGNAL** — classic France (Loire varietal + Bordeaux blanc blend) vs. New Zealand (varietal), and it is THE white grape most commonly seen both varietal and as a blend component in Bordeaux
- **Semillon**: **PLAUSIBLE** — Australia (Hunter Valley varietal) vs. France (Bordeaux blend), but less likely with 4 wines across only 2 countries
- **Chardonnay**: **CURVEBALL** — used in blends rarely (some Languedoc or sparkling base), less natural fit for "blend or single variety" phrasing
- **Chenin Blanc**: **CURVEBALL** — France/South Africa works for two countries, but Chenin is almost never a blend component in the classic sense

## Candidate origins per wine
Given Sauvignon Blanc as the most likely common variety across France and New Zealand:
- **Wine 1**: Loire Valley (Sancerre or Pouilly-Fumé) — pure varietal, mineral, high-acid
- **Wine 2**: Marlborough, New Zealand — varietal Sauvignon, pungent tropical/herbal
- **Wine 3**: Pessac-Léognan or Bordeaux Blanc — SB-dominant blend with Semillon, oak-aged
- **Wine 4**: Marlborough, New Zealand — possibly a lighter or different style (Awatere vs. Wairau)

## Rule-outs
- **Riesling** — almost never blended, so "may be blends" rules it out
- **Pinot Gris/Grigio** — rarely blended in the classic sense, and two-country comparison is weak
- **Gewurztraminer** — essentially never blended, too narrow geographically
- **Viognier** — sometimes blended (Rhone white) but two-country constraint is awkward

## IMW's likely intent
Testing ability to identify Sauvignon Blanc as a thread across different winemaking philosophies: unoaked varietal (Loire, NZ) vs. oak-aged blend (Bordeaux). The 20-mark variety ID weight means this is the anchor. The winemaking component tests whether candidates can distinguish reductive/unoaked (Loire/NZ style) from oxidative/barrel-fermented (Bordeaux blanc).

## What to focus on in the glass
- **Aromatic profile**: green capsicum, gooseberry, elderflower, passion fruit → Sauvignon Blanc confirmation
- **Oak presence**: vanilla, toast, broader texture → Bordeaux blanc blend vs. unoaked varietal
- **Acidity structure**: very high/nervy = Loire or cool-climate NZ; softer with weight = Bordeaux or warmer NZ
- **Alcohol level**: low (12.5-13%) suggests Loire/NZ; higher (13.5%+) could be Bordeaux with Semillon
- **Texture/weight**: lean and racy vs. creamy mid-palate separates varietal from blended styles
