# Decision matrix — 2018 Paper 1 Question 3

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 11-12 are made from the same single grape variety.

With reference to both wines:
a) Identify the grape variety. (10 marks)

For each wine:
b) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (2 x 8 marks)

c) Comment on winemaking, quality and state of maturity. (2 x 12 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 1 = white wines. Two wines sharing one variety. This is the closing question on P1 — typically a focused comparison of two expressions of a single grape, often at different quality/maturity levels.

## Stem signals
- **Same single grape variety** across two wines — the pair structure invites compare/contrast
- **10 marks for variety ID** — significant but not as dominant as Q1's 20 marks
- **"State of maturity"** explicitly called out — one wine is likely younger/fresher, the other older/more developed. This signals a grape that evolves meaningfully with age.
- **Winemaking** emphasis (combined with quality and maturity in 12 marks each) — the IMW wants discussion of how production choices interact with ageing
- Only two wines = more time per wine; the IMW expects deeper analysis

## Universe
Given P1 whites + same single variety + maturity emphasis + only two wines:
- Must be a variety that ages well and shows dramatically different profiles at different maturity stages
- Must not be Chardonnay (used in Q1) and should ideally not duplicate varieties from Q2

Varieties that age dramatically and reward maturity discussion: Riesling (dry GG vs. sweet Auslese at different ages), Chenin Blanc, Semillon. Riesling is the standout because its dry vs. sweet and young vs. aged expressions are MW examination staples.

## Candidate varieties (confidence tiers)
- **Riesling**: **STRONG SIGNAL** — the ultimate ageing white. One dry/trocken and one sweet/Auslese at different ages would perfectly test maturity assessment. Not used in Q1. May or may not have appeared in Q2 (six different varieties from six countries means Riesling could have been one, but it's a different question and the variety-repeat constraint is Q-internal).
- **Chenin Blanc**: **PLAUSIBLE** — ages well (Savennieres, Vouvray), but may overlap with Q2 if Chenin was already used there.
- **Semillon**: **PLAUSIBLE** — ages dramatically (Hunter Valley), but may overlap with Q2.
- **Sauvignon Blanc**: **CURVEBALL** — can age in Loire style, but maturity variation is less dramatic.

## Candidate origins per wine
Assuming Riesling:
- Wine 11: Germany dry (Rheingau GG or Pfalz trocken) — structured, mineral, moderate-high alcohol for Riesling
- Wine 12: Germany sweet (Mosel Auslese or Spatlese) — lower alcohol, higher RS, potentially aged
- Alternative: one from Germany, one from Alsace or Australia (Clare Valley aged Riesling)

## Rule-outs
- **Chardonnay** — used in Q1
- **Pinot Gris** — ages less dramatically; maturity discussion is thinner
- **Gewurztraminer** — ages but less commonly tested in pairs for maturity
- **Viognier** — does not age well; maturity emphasis argues against it

## IMW's likely intent
Testing the candidate's ability to: (1) identify a variety that can present in radically different styles (dry vs. sweet, young vs. aged), (2) articulate how maturity manifests in the glass (colour development, petrol/kerosene, honey, toast), (3) discuss winemaking differences that drive divergent styles from the same grape (arrested fermentation for sweetness, extended lees contact, reductive vs. oxidative handling).

## What to focus on in the glass
- **Petrol/kerosene** on the nose = aged Riesling hallmark
- **Residual sugar vs. dry**: tongue-coating sweetness vs. bone-dry finish
- **Alcohol level**: a dry GG may be 12-13%, an Auslese 7-9%
- **Acidity**: both should have high acidity, but the balance point differs (RS masks acid in sweet)
- **Colour**: pale green-gold (young dry) vs. deep gold (aged sweet)
- **Complexity markers**: honey, lanolin, dried apricot, lime marmalade = aged/sweet; slate, citrus, white flower = young/dry
