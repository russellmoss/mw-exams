a) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (3 x 10 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 3 = sparkling, fortified, sweet, rose, oxidative, and other special styles. "Same country" in P3 usually means the examiner wants breadth of styles from one major wine nation, not three near-duplicates.

## Stem signals
- Three wines from the **same country**
- Origin carries a full **10 marks per wine**, so the country is not enough; the candidate must likely name region or category accurately
- Winemaking is tested, but only at **5 marks per wine**, so the production methods should be clear and classically associated with the styles
- Quality and commercial position carry **10 marks per wine**, implying a deliberate spread of categories, price points, and target consumers
- No grape-variety question, which suggests category and place matter more than varietal exactness

## Universe
The likely country must support multiple distinct P3 categories at credible global scale. That immediately narrows the field to Spain, France, Italy, Portugal, and perhaps Germany. Of those, **Spain** is the cleanest fit because one country can plausibly supply:
- traditional method sparkling
- still table wine from an inland or mountain appellation
- fortified or oxidative wine with a very distinctive production method

France can also do that, but its sparkling/fortified/oxidative set is less compact under a single country clue. Italy is possible, but classic fortified styles are thinner. Spain is the strongest structural answer.

## Rule-outs
- Unlikely Germany: too narrow a special-styles universe for three sharply differentiated wines
- Unlikely Portugal: strong in fortified and table wine, weaker in globally recognized sparkling identity
- Unlikely three wines from the same narrow family, such as three fortifieds, because the commercial-position sub-question benefits from stylistic spread
- Unlikely a rose-led flight, because winemaking at 5 marks usually signals more technically distinct categories than just color extraction choices

## IMW's likely intent
This tests whether the candidate understands that a single country can occupy multiple market segments. The examiner likely wants one sparkling wine, one still wine, and one fortified or oxidative wine so the answer can move between autolysis, standard still-wine élevage, and biological or oxidative aging. Commercial position then becomes category-specific: celebratory sparkling, gastronomic still wine, and niche specialist fortified.

## What to focus on in the glass
- Presence or absence of bubbles immediately splits the set
- Oxidative aldehydic, walnut, or toffee notes would point toward a fortified or oxidative category
- Alcohol level is critical: a jump toward 15-20% strongly suggests Sherry or another fortified style
- For the still wine, look for whether it reads Atlantic, Mediterranean, continental, or mountain in profile
- Quality cues should be tied to category: lees complexity in sparkling, fruit purity and tannin management in still wine, concentration and aging character in fortified wine
