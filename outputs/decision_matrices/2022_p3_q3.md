## Paper context
Paper 3 three-wine sets usually hinge on category recognition first, then origin. "Three different countries" suggests a comparative flight within one broad family of wines.

## Stem signals
- Three wines from **three different countries**
- Origin is worth **10 marks per wine**, so the styles are likely distinctive enough to place fairly closely
- Winemaking gets only **5 marks**, suggesting the techniques are classic and well known rather than hyper-technical
- Style, quality, and commercial position get **10 marks**, which implies a meaningful market contrast
- No grape-variety question, so category and production style matter more than varietal exactness

## Universe
In P3, this structure often means the examiner is comparing wines from one broad style family made in multiple countries. The strongest candidates are:
- fortified wines
- sweet wines
- dried-grape or oxidative dessert styles

Because origin matters strongly and commercial position is tested, a likely pattern is one Old World benchmark, one fortified benchmark, and one New World analog. Australia often appears in these comparative special-style questions because it offers an exam-friendly counterpart to European sweet or fortified traditions.

## Candidate categories (confidence tiers)
- Tawny or oxidative fortified wines across countries: **STRONG SIGNAL**
- Naturally sweet wines from dried grapes or botrytis plus one fortified comparator: **PLAUSIBLE**
- Three red or amber sweet/fortified wines rather than fresh white sweet wines: **PLAUSIBLE**
- Sparkling wines: **CURVEBALL** because commercial position plus origin could work, but production would likely carry more than 5 marks

## Candidate origins per slot
- One wine likely from **Portugal**, especially Port, because it is the global benchmark for oxidative fortified style and easy to position commercially
- One wine likely from **Australia**, often as a New World fortified or tawny-style counterpoint
- One wine likely from **Italy**, Spain, or France, perhaps a dried-grape sweet wine or another oxidative category

## Rule-outs
- Unlikely three unrelated styles, because that would make origin identification too diffuse
- Unlikely all fresh-acid sweet whites, since the commercial-position question benefits from stronger stylistic contrast
- Unlikely all three are from Europe, because the "three different countries" construction in P3 often likes at least one New World comparator

## IMW's likely intent
The examiner likely wants candidates to compare countries that make wines with overlapping sensory signals but different production stories and market positions. This is classic trade-facing P3 design: the candidate should talk about whether the wine is niche, gift-driven, after-dinner, specialist independent retail, or volume classic. The production component is there to anchor the style, not to dominate the answer.

## What to focus on in the glass
- Alcohol level quickly separates fortified from unfortified sweet wines
- Oxidative nutty aromas suggest cask-aged fortified or dried-grape styles
- Residual sugar versus spirit lift helps distinguish Port-like wines from passito styles
- Color depth and viscosity matter: amber, tawny, garnet, or deep mahogany all point to different families
- Commercial position should reflect whether the wine is entry-level, age-indicated prestige, or a niche sommelier-category wine
