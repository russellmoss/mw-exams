# Decision matrix — 2019 Paper 1 Question 3

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 11-12 are from two different Old World countries.

With reference to each wine:

a) Comment on the winemaking. (2 x 10 marks)

b) Discuss the wine's style, quality and commercial potential. Do not spend time thinking about the wine's specific origin. (2 x 15 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 1 = white wines only. Only two wines. Crucially, the stem explicitly says **"Do not spend time thinking about the wine's specific origin"** — this is rare and significant.

## Stem signals
- **Two different Old World countries** — confirms European origin for both, but different nations
- **"Do not spend time thinking about the wine's specific origin"** — the IMW is deliberately de-emphasizing origin ID. This strongly suggests the wines are unusual, obscure, or hard to place. The examiners know candidates would struggle with origin and are redirecting toward winemaking and style analysis.
- **10 marks for winemaking** — high weight, suggesting the wines have distinctive or unusual production methods
- **15 marks for style, quality, and commercial potential** — the bulk of marks. The IMW wants candidates to describe what they taste and assess market viability, not guess a region.
- **No variety ID marks** — another signal these may be unusual or lesser-known varieties

## Universe
Old World white wines that are deliberately hard to place — likely from emerging or niche producers, unusual varieties, or unusual winemaking (orange wine, skin contact, amphora, oxidative). Think: Georgian qvevri whites, Greek indigenous varieties, Jura oxidative wines, Hungarian Furmint, Croatian/Slovenian whites, or Italian obscurities.

## Candidate varieties (confidence tiers)

- **Jura oxidative white (Savagnin/Vin Jaune style)**: **STRONG SIGNAL** — unusual winemaking (oxidative aging under flor or without), hard to place, commercial potential discussion is interesting

## Candidate origins per wine

- **Wine 12**: Italy (something unusual — oxidative Sicilian or Sardinian) or Jura France (but Jura may be too identifiable for an "ignore origin" instruction)

## Rule-outs
- **Mainstream French or Italian whites** — too identifiable; the stem explicitly discourages origin focus
- **German Riesling** — too distinctive and identifiable
- **Spanish whites** — already covered in Q2

## IMW's likely intent
This question tests **analytical tasting skills divorced from origin identification**. The IMW wants candidates who can describe winemaking (oxidative vs. reductive, skin contact, amphora, lees aging) and assess quality/commercial potential without relying on the crutch of knowing "where" a wine is from. This is testing pure sensory and analytical ability.

## What to focus on in the glass
- **Colour**: deep gold/amber suggests skin contact or oxidative aging
- **Oxidative vs. reductive markers**: aldehydes, bruised apple, dried fruit (oxidative) vs. fresh, bright, sulfur notes (reductive)
- **Texture**: tannin/grip on a white = skin contact/orange wine
- **Volatile acidity**: slight VA can indicate traditional/artisanal production
- **Unusual aromatics**: dried herbs, beeswax, honey, dried apricot — these point to non-standard winemaking
- **Commercial assessment**: is this a niche/artisan product or does it have broader market appeal?
