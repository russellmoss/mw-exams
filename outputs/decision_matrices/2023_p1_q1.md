# Decision matrix -- 2023 Paper 1 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1-4 are from four different countries and two different grape varieties. 1 & 2 are a pair and 3 & 4 are a pair. Each pair is from the same single grape variety.

For each pair:

a) Identify the grape variety. (2 x 10 marks)

b) Compare and contrast the major winemaking influences. (2 x 15 marks)

c) Comment on quality and ageing potential. (2 x 25 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 1 = white wines only. Three papers per year: P1 whites, P2 reds, P3 mixed (sparkling/fortified/sweet/rose/oxidative).

## Stem signals
- **Four wines, four different countries** -- geographic breadth is being tested
- **Two pairs**, each pair from **same single grape variety** -- two varieties total
- **Major winemaking influences** (15 marks per pair) -- the IMW wants discussion of oak, lees, malo, fermentation vessel, reductive vs. oxidative
- **Quality and ageing potential** at 25 marks per pair -- this is the highest-weighted section, meaning these are varieties that exhibit a meaningful range of quality tiers and ageing trajectories
- Variety ID at only 10 marks per pair -- these should be recognizable varieties, not curveballs

## Universe
P1 + two varieties across four countries + ageing potential emphasis narrows to internationally planted white varieties that show dramatically different profiles depending on country and winemaking. The IMW wants candidates to articulate how the same grape tastes different from two origins and why.

## Candidate varieties (confidence tiers)
- **Chardonnay**: STRONG SIGNAL -- the most globally planted white, made in radically different styles (oaked vs. unoaked, cool vs. warm climate), present in France, USA, Australia, South Africa, NZ, Chile, Argentina, Italy
- **Sauvignon Blanc**: STRONG SIGNAL -- Loire vs. Marlborough vs. Bordeaux vs. California is a classic comparison, highly testable
- **Riesling**: PLAUSIBLE -- strong ageing potential, but fewer countries produce benchmark examples (Germany, Australia, Alsace, Austria)
- **Semillon**: PLAUSIBLE -- Hunter Valley vs. Bordeaux is the classic pair, and it ages brilliantly, but only two major countries produce it
- **Chenin Blanc**: PLAUSIBLE -- Loire vs. South Africa is a testable pair, with real ageing potential
- **Pinot Gris**: CURVEBALL -- less variation in winemaking across countries

## Candidate origins per slot
- **Pair 1 (wines 1-2)**: Most likely Sauvignon Blanc or Chardonnay. If SB: France (Loire/Sancerre) + New Zealand (Marlborough) or USA (Napa/Sonoma). If Chardonnay: Burgundy + Australia or California.
- **Pair 2 (wines 3-4)**: If pair 1 is SB, then pair 2 is likely Chardonnay or Semillon. If pair 1 is Chardonnay, then pair 2 could be Riesling, Semillon, or Chenin. The four-country constraint means each wine is from a different country.

## Rule-outs
- Gewurztraminer: too narrow geographically (Alsace dominates), limited ageing discussion
- Muscat: not enough winemaking variation in still dry white form across countries
- Viognier: not enough countries produce benchmark examples to fill four slots
- Obscure varieties (Furmint, Assyrtiko, Albarino): unlikely when variety ID is only 10 marks -- the IMW wants recognizable grapes here

## IMW's likely intent
Testing variety identification of two mainstream white grapes, then the ability to articulate how winemaking (oak, lees, malo, fermentation temperature) and terroir differ across countries. The 25-mark quality/ageing section is the exam's center of gravity -- the candidate must demonstrate they can assess quality tier and predict ageing trajectory from what is in the glass.

## What to focus on in the glass
- **Primary fruit profile**: tropical vs. citrus vs. stone fruit to narrow variety
- **Oak markers**: vanilla, toast, smoke, cedar -- present vs. absent, and how integrated
- **Texture and weight**: lees-derived richness, malo vs. no malo
- **Acidity structure**: high acid + linear = cool climate / no malo; rounder = warmer / full malo
- **Colour**: pale green-gold (young/unoaked) vs. deeper gold (oak/age/warm climate)
- **Ageing indicators**: petrol (Riesling), lanolin (Semillon), honey/toast (aged Chardonnay), herbaceous fade (aged SB)
