# Decision matrix -- 2016 Paper 2 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1 and 2 come from the same region and are made from the same single grape variety.

With reference to both wines:

a) Identify the grape variety and origin as closely as possible. (18 marks)

b) Compare the winemaking styles. (18 marks)

c) Compare quality, with particular reference to both wines' potential evolution. (14 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 2 = red wines only. Two wines, same region, same single grape variety.

## Stem signals
- Same **region**, same **single grape variety** -- very tight constraint
- Variety + origin worth **18 marks** -- very high, suggests an identifiable but not trivially obvious grape/region
- Winemaking comparison worth **18 marks** -- equally high, the region must support radically different winemaking approaches for the same grape
- Quality + **potential evolution** worth 14 marks -- evolution/aging implies ageworthy wines
- "Compare winemaking styles" is the key phrase: the IMW wants traditional vs modern, or different extraction/oak philosophies

## Universe
A red grape variety grown in a single region where two dramatically different winemaking philosophies coexist. The 18-mark winemaking comparison demands a region known for a traditional vs modern debate.

Top candidates:
- **Beaujolais (Gamay)**: STRONG SIGNAL -- carbonic maceration (traditional Beaujolais) vs Burgundian vinification (cru Beaujolais). The traditional/natural vs structured debate is central to Beaujolais.
- **Barolo (Nebbiolo)**: STRONG SIGNAL -- traditional (long maceration, large Slavonian oak) vs modern (shorter maceration, barrique). Classic winemaking debate.
- **Burgundy (Pinot Noir)**: PLAUSIBLE -- whole-cluster vs destemmed, new oak percentages. But winemaking differences are subtler.
- **Rioja (Tempranillo)**: PLAUSIBLE -- traditional (American oak, long aging) vs modern (French oak, fruit-forward).

## Rule-outs
- Unlikely Cabernet Sauvignon -- winemaking doesn't vary as dramatically within a single region
- Unlikely Merlot -- same reasoning
- Unlikely Syrah/Shiraz -- Northern Rhone is fairly uniform in winemaking approach
- Unlikely New World regions -- the winemaking debate question fits Old World better

## IMW's likely intent
Testing the candidate's understanding of how the same grape in the same region can yield radically different wines through winemaking choices. This is a philosophical question about winemaker intervention, extraction, oak regimes, and how these choices affect aging potential. Beaujolais is the strongest candidate because carbonic maceration vs traditional vinification produces the most extreme stylistic contrast.

## What to focus on in the glass
- Carbonic maceration markers: banana, bubblegum, kirsch, very primary fruit
- Traditional vinification markers: more structured tannins, darker fruit, complexity
- Oak presence: new barrique (vanilla, toast) vs neutral/no oak
- Tannin structure and extraction levels
- Color intensity: carbonic = lighter, traditional = deeper
- Acidity and freshness
- Length and complexity as quality indicators
