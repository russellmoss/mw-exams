# Decision matrix — 2022 Paper 1 Question 1

## Question (verbatim)
Wines 1 and 2 are from the same producer and the same vintage.

For each wine:

a) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (2 x 10 marks)

With reference to both wines:

b) Compare the two wines discussing the quality, maturity, use of oak and market position. (30 marks)

## Paper context
Paper 1 = white wines only. All still whites.

## Stem signals
- Only **two wines** — a tight, focused comparison
- **Same producer, same vintage** — this is rare and highly specific. The IMW wants the candidate to isolate terroir and/or appellation differences while holding producer style and vintage constant
- **"Use of oak"** is explicitly called out (suggests oak is detectable and varies between the two wines)
- Origin worth 20 marks total, compare/contrast worth 30 marks — the comparison is more important than nailing the exact origin
- **"Market position"** asked — suggests wines at meaningfully different price points or prestige tiers

## Universe
Same producer + same vintage + two different white wines = a producer who makes multiple tiers or cuvees of white wine. Given P1 (whites) and the oak emphasis:
- Burgundy is the prime candidate — one producer making village-level AND premier/grand cru is the classic setup (e.g., Meursault vs Corton-Charlemagne, Puligny village vs Montrachet)
- Loire is less likely (fewer oak-driven comparisons within a single producer)
- Australia possible (e.g., a producer with both an unoaked and oaked Chardonnay)
- Alsace unlikely — oak is rarely a distinguishing feature

## Candidate varieties (confidence tiers)
- **Chardonnay**: **STRONG SIGNAL** — the only white variety where oak use is routinely the differentiating factor between tiers, especially in Burgundy
- **Semillon**: **CURVEBALL** — a Hunter Valley producer could conceivably show two tiers, but oak emphasis less common
- **Sauvignon Blanc/Semillon blend**: **CURVEBALL** — a Bordeaux Blanc producer could show two tiers but the question says nothing about blends

## Candidate origins per wine
- **Wine 1**: Burgundy village-level (Meursault, Puligny-Montrachet, or Chassagne-Montrachet) — likely the "lesser" or more accessible wine
- **Wine 2**: Burgundy premier or grand cru from the same producer — the prestige cuvee with more new oak, concentration, and ageability
- Alternative: Both from Margaret River or Sonoma, one reserve and one entry-level Chardonnay

## Rule-outs
- **Riesling** — oak is rarely used and would not be an explicit comparison point
- **Pinot Gris/Gewurztraminer** — same logic; oak is not a defining stylistic lever
- **New Zealand Sauvignon Blanc** — unoaked is the norm, two tiers from one producer with oak variation is unlikely
- **Italy** — very few Italian white producers have two tiers where oak is the distinguishing feature in a way the IMW would test

## IMW's likely intent
Testing the candidate's ability to: (1) recognize the same house style across two wines, (2) articulate how terroir hierarchy manifests within a single producer's range, (3) discuss oak regimes intelligently (new vs. old, percentage, fermentation vs. maturation), and (4) position wines at different market tiers. This is a quality-ladder question, not a variety-ID question.

## What to focus on in the glass
- **Colour**: deeper gold = more oak/lees contact or warmer site
- **Nose**: butter, toast, hazelnut, vanilla = new oak; mineral, citrus, green apple = less oak or older barrels
- **Palate texture**: creamy/broad vs. taut/linear — the grand cru should have more density and length
- **Acidity structure**: both should share a baseline (same vintage) but the better wine should integrate acidity more seamlessly
- **Finish length**: the premium wine should have significantly longer finish
