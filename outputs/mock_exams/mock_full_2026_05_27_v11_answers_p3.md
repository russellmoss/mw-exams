# Mock Answers -- Paper 3 (Special)

## Paper 3 Question 1

### Model answer

**Wine 1** - Manzanilla, Sanlucar de Barrameda, Spain; Palomino. The pale colour, bone-dry palate, 15% alcohol, saline edge, chamomile, bruised apple and fresh almond point first to biological ageing under flor. The lighter body and sea-salt delicacy make Manzanilla a better call than broader Fino. Fermentation was to dryness, then fortification only to around 15%, allowing flor to survive in partly filled old butts in solera. This gives acetaldehyde, flor-derived savouriness and a very dry finish. Very good quality, classic premium Manzanilla level, sold through specialists and restaurants at accessible premium pricing.

**Wine 2** - Dry Oloroso, Jerez, Spain; Palomino. The deep amber colour, walnut, orange peel, leather, dry finish and 20% alcohol indicate fortification above flor tolerance and long oxidative solera ageing. This is not Amontillado because there is no residual flor delicacy, and not Cream Sherry because the finish is dry. Very good quality, equivalent to a serious bodega Oloroso; commercial position is specialist retail, tapas/restaurants and by-the-glass education, with a small but loyal export audience.

**Wine 3** - 10-year-old Sercial Madeira, Portugal; Sercial. Volatile lift, citrus peel, walnut, smoke, saline length, piercing acidity and around 19-20% alcohol point to fortified Madeira. The dry, taut profile rules out Bual or Malmsey and favours Sercial. The key production evidence is fortification followed by long oxidative cask ageing, most likely canteiro rather than a simple heat-tank style. Very good quality, 10-year-old Madeira level; strong specialist and restaurant position because the acidity and age statement are easy to communicate.

**Wine 4** - Chateau-Chalon, Jura, France; Savagnin. The dry, unfortified palate at 14.5%, walnut, curry, bruised apple and no spirit heat point away from Sherry or Madeira and toward Vin Jaune-style oxidative ageing. Savagnin is fermented dry, then aged for over six years in ullaged cask under voile, producing flor-like aldehydic notes without fortification. Very good to excellent quality, Chateau-Chalon AOC level; commercial position is niche, high-value specialist retail and gastronomic restaurants, with limited volumes and a high education barrier.

## Proposed annotation

Tests whether the candidate starts with ageing environment and fortification level rather than variety. The four wines deliberately separate biological Sherry, oxidative Sherry, fortified oxidative Madeira, and unfortified Jura sous voile. Best answers should use alcohol, dryness, flor/voile notes, oxidation profile and spirit warmth to avoid collapsing all four into "Sherry-like".

## Reasoning trace

- Stem signals: dry wines; oxidative or biological ageing is the dominant mark driver.
- Universe: Sherry, Madeira, Jura Vin Jaune/Chateau-Chalon, possibly dry Marsala or oxidative white.
- Wine 1: 15% + pale + saline almond + dry = flor-aged Sherry; Sanlucar delicacy makes Manzanilla.
- Wine 2: 20% + dry walnut/caramel oxidative depth = dry Oloroso, not sweet Cream/PX.
- Wine 3: 19-20% + volatile lift + high acid + dry Sercial profile = Madeira, 10-year-old Sercial.
- Wine 4: 14.5% and no spirit heat = unfortified oxidative Jura; Chateau-Chalon implies Savagnin.
- Research used where matching/near-matching files exist: related Manzanilla, Oloroso, and H&H Sercial briefs. Chateau-Chalon detail is cautious category deduction from exam facts and standard AOC style.

## Paper 3 Question 2

### Model answer

All three are Champagne from France, from the same producer, based principally on Pinot Noir and Chardonnay. The persistent fine mousse, high acidity, autolytic bread dough, chalky finish and brut balance point to traditional-method Champagne rather than tank sparkling or warmer traditional-method origins.

**Quality order:** Wine 7 first, Wine 6 second, Wine 5 third. The basis is the formal Champagne quality ladder: NV Brut Reserve -> Vintage Brut -> Prestige Cuvee from a declared vintage. Wine 7 has the greatest concentration, length, most seamless mousse and deepest lees-aged complexity; Wine 6 has vintage depth and more development than NV; Wine 5 is high-quality non-vintage house Champagne but less concentrated and less age-worthy.

**Wine 5** - NV Brut Reserve. The fresh apple/citrus fruit, moderate autolysis, fine but less layered mousse and balanced dosage suggest reserve-wine blending for consistency rather than a single-vintage statement. Traditional method, second fermentation in bottle, lees ageing sufficient for biscuit notes, then brut dosage around the classic house style. Very good quality, NV Grande Marque level, ready now and for 2-3 years. Commercially this is premium retail, restaurants and gifting, competing with other major-house NV Champagnes.

**Wine 6** - Vintage Brut, 2015. Greater ripe fruit, broader mid-palate, more brioche and a longer chalky finish indicate a declared vintage wine with longer lees ageing. The 2015 warmth may show generosity rather than extreme austerity, but acidity and autolysis keep it balanced. Excellent quality, vintage Champagne level; drinking now with further improvement over 5-8 years and holding beyond that. Commercially specialist retail and restaurant lists, above NV but below prestige cuvee pricing.

**Wine 7** - Prestige Cuvee, 2015. The most complete wine: finest mousse, greatest intensity, long savoury autolysis, ripe citrus/stone fruit and a persistent mineral finish. It is traditional method with extended lees ageing and careful selection of top base wines. Outstanding quality, prestige cuvee Champagne level; still improving for 8-12 years and holding 15+. Commercially luxury gifting, fine dining, collectors and allocation-led export markets.

## Proposed annotation

The stem gives same country, same producer and three quality levels, so the answer should not waste time re-proving traditional method for each wine. The examiner wants Champagne hierarchy recognition: NV versus vintage versus prestige cuvee, evidenced by mousse finesse, lees depth, concentration, dosage integration, maturity and length. Producer identification is explicitly asked here, unlike normal blind answers.

## Reasoning trace

- Stem signals: sparkling, same country, same producer, three quality levels = classic Champagne house ladder.
- Universe: Champagne most likely; possible Cava/English/Franciacorta only if country not otherwise apparent, but same producer ladder strongly favours Champagne.
- Wine 5: NV Brut Reserve = entry Grande Marque quality with reserve-wine consistency and early drinkability.
- Wine 6: 2015 Vintage Brut = declared vintage, longer lees and more depth than NV.
- Wine 7: 2015 prestige cuvee = top selection, greatest concentration and prestige commercial role.
- Research used: related Pol Roger Vintage brief for house style, blend, lees-ageing logic and Champagne ladder. NV and prestige details are deduced from exam wine facts and category norms.

## Paper 3 Question 3

### Model answer

**Wine 8** - About 5-5.5% alcohol; around 100-130 g/L RS. Moscato d'Asti DOCG, Piedmont, Italy; Moscato Bianco. The light frothy mousse, grapey orange-blossom aromatics, very low alcohol and sweet but fresh palate point to fermentation stopped early in pressurised tank, retaining natural CO2 and sugar. Very good quality for Moscato d'Asti, DOCG/single-site level; youthful, drink now, commercial position is premium aromatic low-alcohol sweet sparkling.

**Wine 9** - About 9-10% alcohol; very high RS, around 180-230 g/L. Niagara Peninsula, Canada; Vidal Icewine. Pure tropical fruit, very high sweetness, piercing acidity and low alcohol point to grapes frozen on the vine, pressed frozen, then slowly fermented to low alcohol with high retained sugar. Very good to excellent quality, VQA Icewine level; mature enough to drink but can hold 10+ years as honeyed notes develop.

**Wine 10** - About 11-12% alcohol; around 120-150 g/L RS. Tokaji Aszu 5 Puttonyos, Hungary; Furmint-dominant with Harslevelu possible. Apricot, orange peel, honey, saffron, botrytis complexity and electric acidity point to botrytised aszu berries macerated with base wine/must, then aged before release. Excellent quality, 5 Puttonyos Tokaji level; developing but with long future, improving 8-15 years.

**Wine 11** - About 17% alcohol; very high RS, often 350+ g/L. Montilla-Moriles, Spain; Pedro Ximenez. Opaque brown colour, raisin, fig, molasses, coffee and spirit warmth indicate sun-dried PX grapes, fermentation arrested/fortified and long oxidative ageing. Outstanding concentration at Gran Reserva level; fully mature but stable, holding for decades. Commercially niche dessert, specialist and restaurant pour.

**Wine 12** - About 16% alcohol; around 80-110 g/L RS. Banyuls, Roussillon, France; Grenache. Sweet black cherry, cocoa, red-wine tannin and fortification warmth point to Vin Doux Naturel made by mutage during fermentation, preserving sugar and fruit. The Rimage style is less oxidative and more primary than old tawny-style VDN. Very good quality, Banyuls AOC premium level; youthful, drink now to 8 years.

## Proposed annotation

This is an RS/alcohol mechanism question. Best answers should plot each wine on alcohol and sweetness before naming origin: stopped tank fermentation for Moscato, cryo-concentration for Icewine, botrytis/aszu selection for Tokaji, dried-grape/fortified oxidative PX, and Grenache mutage for Banyuls. The examiner is looking for how sweetness is achieved, not just "sweet wine" labels.

## Reasoning trace

- Stem signals: all have RS, five countries, different production methods; alcohol and RS explicitly asked.
- Universe: Moscato d'Asti, Icewine/Eiswein, Tokaji/Sauternes/BA, PX/Muscat/Rutherglen, Port/VDN, passito.
- Wine 8: 5.5% + fizz + grapey Muscat = Moscato d'Asti; stopped tank fermentation.
- Wine 9: 9.5% + very high RS + high acid = Canadian Vidal Icewine.
- Wine 10: 11.5% + botrytis + acid + orange/ginger = Tokaji Aszu 5 Puttonyos.
- Wine 11: 17% + very high RS + raisined oxidative brown profile = PX Gran Reserva from Montilla-Moriles.
- Wine 12: 16% + sweet red fruit + tannin + spirit = Banyuls Rimage VDN.
- Research used where matching files exist: Sourgal Moscato d'Asti, Banyuls Rimage. Other wines are deduced cautiously from exam facts and standard category mechanisms.
