### Part c) Site, altitude, variety interaction (14 marks)

Alto Adige is defined by dramatic altitudinal variation — from valley floors at 200–250m (warmer, Mediterranean-influenced, better for full-bodied varieties) to high valley sites above 700m (cooler, continental, Alpine, better for aromatic varieties). Variety selection is altitude-driven as much as any other factor.

Wine 8 (Pinot Grigio, Magré area, ~350–450m): the southeast-facing slopes at moderate altitude capture warmth while the cool nights preserve freshness. Pinot Grigio's relatively early-ripening cycle suits this altitude band. The site adds mineral complexity to what is elsewhere a neutral variety.

Wine 9 (Gewurztraminer, Tramin village, ~500–600m): the deep alluvial soils and relatively warm microclimate of the Unterland (southern Adige valley) favour the aromatic development of Gewurztraminer. Higher altitude would preserve freshness but risk losing phenolic maturity. The Nussbaumer site is exceptional for capturing both aromatic intensity and enough acidity to prevent flatness.

Wine 10 (Kerner, Valle Isarco, ~700m): the highest and coolest of the three sites. Kerner, closely related to Riesling in aromatic profile, performs exceptionally well at altitude where the long cool growing season builds aromatic complexity without sacrificing acidity. The result is a wine of genuine finesse and character; the site interaction is visible in the wine's tension and length.

Together, these three wines illustrate how Alto Adige's unusual geography — mountains squeezing the vine into altitude bands — forces a variety-altitude matching that produces quite distinct wines from the same regional label. This is a pedagogically rich group precisely because the varieties differ dramatically in style but share a regional identity that ties them together.

### Part b) Maturity and winemaking style (2 x 10 marks)

**Wine 11 (Savennières, 2023):**
At 2–3 years of age, this wine is in its primary phase: fruit-driven, tightly wound, showing little tertiary development. The extreme acidity and mineral concentration mean this wine needs time; current drinking is possible but the wine is not giving its best. Winemaking: the Domaine aux Moines (Tessa Laroche since the 1980s) farms biodynamically and uses minimal intervention — natural yeast, no fining or filtration, ageing in old barrels or foudres. The result is a wine of remarkable tension and longevity. Natural factors dominate: the schist-based soils of Roche aux Moines, slope aspect, and the cold Loire river influence shape the wine's character more than human intervention. Human factors: biodynamic viticulture, low yields, careful cellar handling all play supporting roles.

**Wine 12 (Vouvray Demi-Sec, 2016):**
At 8–9 years of age, this wine is entering its first period of proper maturity. The waxy, honeyed, slightly oxidative character reflects the natural evolution of Chenin Blanc under demi-sec conditions. Winemaking: Foreau's Clos Naudin is a biodynamic estate; the wines are fermented with natural yeasts and see some ageing in old demi-muids. The demi-sec style (naturally occurring RS from arrested fermentation) is intentional and stylistically consistent across vintages. Natural factors: the tuffeau soils of Vouvray give a softer, more generous texture than the schist of Savennières. Human factors: the decision to leave residual sugar, timing of harvest at Botrytis-influenced ripeness, is a winemaking choice that dramatically shapes the wine's development trajectory.

### Part c) Style, quality, capacity to age (20 marks)

These two wines represent contrasting expressions of Chenin Blanc in its two most important dry and off-dry Loire manifestations, separated by 7 years and by a fundamental winemaking philosophy difference (bone-dry vs demi-sec), though both are made at outstanding quality.

**Style comparison:**
Wine 11 is angular, mineral, and austere at this age — all tension and latent power. Wine 12 is more giving and honeyed, showing the warmth and generosity that demi-sec Vouvray achieves with bottle age. Wine 11 prioritises purity and verticality; Wine 12 prioritises depth and complex secondary evolution.

**Quality:**
Both are of outstanding quality for their respective styles. Wine 11 (Roche aux Moines) from a top vintage would rank among the finest dry whites made anywhere in France. Wine 12 (Clos Naudin demi-sec) occupies a comparable position for demi-sec Vouvray — Foreau is one of the great artisan producers of the Loire. At 8–9 years, Wine 12 is arguably showing better pure drinking pleasure right now; Wine 11 has decades of potential ahead of it.

**Capacity to age:**

### Part a) Region identification (15 marks)

### Part b) Specific style and production method (4 x 8 marks)

**Wine 4 (La Guita Manzanilla, Sanlúcar de Barrameda):**
Pale gold, intensely saline and chamomile-scented, bone dry. The flor yeast layer (a layer of living yeast that forms naturally on the wine surface in barrels open to the humid coastal atmosphere of Sanlúcar) prevents oxidation and creates the characteristic saline, almond, and bruised-apple character. Manzanilla is specifically the flor-aged Fino style made in Sanlúcar de Barrameda (as opposed to Jerez de la Frontera, where the equivalent style is called Fino). The coastal humidity of Sanlúcar supports a thicker, more vigorous flor layer, producing a lighter-bodied, more saline expression. La Guita is the most widely recognised Manzanilla brand.

**Wine 5 (Lustau Amontillado Los Arcos):**
Deep amber, nutty and complex — dried fruits, caramel, walnuts, a hint of iodine and brine from its Fino/Manzanilla beginnings. Dry on the palate. Amontillado begins its life as a flor-aged Fino but loses its flor protection (naturally or by fortification to kill the flor) and then ages oxidatively in the solera system. The resulting wine combines the tertiary complexity of oxidative ageing with the structural framework developed under flor. Lustau's Los Arcos is a classic dry Amontillado Almacenista style.

**Wine 6 (Barbadillo Oloroso Emperatriz Eugenia):**
Deep mahogany, rich, nutty, and dry with remarkable complexity — dried figs, dark chocolate, leather, rancio. Oloroso is fortified to a higher degree (17–18%) immediately after fermentation to kill any potential flor, and then ages entirely oxidatively in the solera system. The absence of flor protection means full oxidative development from the start. The result is a dry, powerful wine of great complexity and length. Barbadillo's Emperatriz Eugenia is an aged, premium Oloroso of considerable reputation.

**Wine 7 (Romate Pedro Ximénez Celestino):**
Almost black in colour, intensely viscous. Nose: concentrated raisins, figs, dates, molasses, chocolate. Palate: extraordinarily sweet (RS 400+ g/l) and syrupy. Pedro Ximénez (PX) is made from dried PX grapes (passerillage/appassimento on mats in the sun) — the must is fortified immediately to arrest fermentation, preserving the concentrated natural sugars. The result is the sweetest wine in the world. PX is the extreme end of the Jerez sweet spectrum and a critical exam wine.

### Part d) Oxidation level comparison across the flight (16 marks)

The Jerez system is unique in offering a continuous spectrum of oxidative development within a single geographic and regulatory framework. These four wines represent four distinct positions on the oxidation axis:

**Wine 4 (Manzanilla): Zero oxidation (reductive/biological ageing).** The flor yeast layer between the wine and the barrel air surface prevents any oxygen contact. The wine ages reductively — under a living biological shield. The result is not truly "reductive" in the modern winemaking sense but is oxidation-free despite barrel ageing. The saline, chamomile, and brioche character comes from the flor's metabolic activity (aldehyde production, ester transformation), not from oxygen contact.

**Wine 5 (Amontillado): Sequential — first zero oxidation, then full oxidation.** The trajectory from Fino through to Amontillado involves an initial period under flor (zero oxidation) followed by deliberate or natural flor death and a subsequent period of full oxidative ageing. The wine therefore carries both the structural memory of flor contact and the tertiary complexity of oxidative development. The nutty/rancio quality sits alongside the delicate saline backbone.

**Wine 6 (Oloroso): Full oxidation throughout.** From the moment of fortification (to kill flor), the wine ages entirely oxidatively in the solera. There is no flor protection at any stage. The full development of rancio, dark fruit, chocolate, and leather comes purely from controlled oxygen contact over many years. The highest structural weight and most concentrated oxidative character in the flight.

**Wine 7 (PX): No ageing oxidation per se — concentrated by desiccation.** The sweetness and concentration of PX come not from oxidative ageing of the wine but from the desiccation of the grapes before pressing, followed by fortification of the must. The aged PX soleras develop further rancio and complexity, but the fundamental character is sugar concentration plus limited oxidation. It is the outlier in the flight in terms of the oxidation mechanism.

The pedagogical value of this flight is in demonstrating how the Jerez system uses oxidation as a deliberate winemaking tool — either protecting against it (flor), combining it sequentially (Amontillado), pursuing it fully (Oloroso), or avoiding it altogether in favour of concentration (PX). No other wine region makes this range.

### Part d) Sweetness mechanism and quality/maturity (5 x 8 marks)

**Wine 8 (Selbach-Oster Spätlese — Natural RS):**
Mechanism: the grapes are harvested at Spätlese (late harvest) ripeness levels; fermentation is arrested by temperature control and light filtration/centrifugation before all sugar is fermented out, leaving residual sugar naturally present in the unfermented must. This is the German Prädikat system's fundamental mechanism — using grape ripeness rather than any intervention to create sweetness. The low alcohol (8.5%) results from the partial fermentation. Quality: very good; the Zeltinger Sonnenuhr is a premier site. Maturity: at 2–3 years this wine is young; it will develop for 10–20 years as the petrol (TDN) character evolves.

**Wine 9 (Château Guiraud 2019 — Botrytis):**
Mechanism: Botrytis cinerea (noble rot) — the beneficial form of the fungus Botrytis — dehydrates the Sémillon grapes on the vine, concentrating sugars and adding complex metabolites (glycerol, glucuronic acid, sotolon) that create the characteristic honey/saffron/apricot character. Multiple tries (passages through the vineyard) are required to select only botrytis-affected berries. Quality: outstanding — Guiraud is an outstanding estate. The 2019 vintage is exceptional for Sauternes: a perfect botrytis attack and good acidity. Maturity: excellent for drinking now but will gain complexity for 20+ years.

**Wine 10 (Henry of Pelham Icewine — Cryoextraction):**
Mechanism: natural freezing of the grapes on the vine at temperatures below -8°C (Canadian standards require harvest at -8°C minimum). Only water freezes; the sugars, acids, and flavour compounds remain in a highly concentrated unfrozen juice (typically ~1 litre of juice per 6 litres of fresh pressed — a fraction of normal yield). The resulting must is fermented very slowly in cold conditions. Quality: good/very good — consistent commercial expression. Maturity: at ~3–4 years this wine is relatively young; Vidal Icewine can age but typically the freshness is the appeal. Drink within 5–10 years.

**Wine 11 (Ben Ryé Passito — Passerillage):**
Mechanism: the Zibibbo grapes are harvested and then laid out on mats or ropes in the island sun and wind of Pantelleria to dry (appassimento) for 4–6 weeks. The evaporation of water concentrates all components — sugar, acid, colour, aromatics. The dried grapes are then pressed and fermented; the high sugar concentration means fermentation stops naturally before completion, leaving significant RS. Donnafugata sometimes adds a small amount of grape spirit for stability. Quality: outstanding — Ben Ryé is among the world's great sweet wines. Maturity: the 2021 is approaching its peak. Will develop for a further 10–15 years.

**Wine 12 (Mas Amiel Maury Vintage — Fortification/Mutage):**
Mechanism: the freshly fermenting red must (Grenache-dominant) has grape spirit (minimum 96% ABV neutral spirit, as per VDN regulations) added during fermentation when approximately half the natural sugars have fermented out. This "mutage" (killing) arrests fermentation, preserves the remaining sugars, and raises the alcohol to 16–18%. The Vintage style is bottled within one year without deliberate oxidative ageing, preserving the fresh red-fruit character of the Grenache.
