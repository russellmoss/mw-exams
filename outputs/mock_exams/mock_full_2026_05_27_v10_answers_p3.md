### d) Discuss the mechanism by which sweetness has been achieved and comment on quality and maturity. (5 x 8 marks)

**Wine 1**
The sweetness in this Mosel Auslese is achieved through **natural residual sugar from arrested fermentation**. Ripe, fully mature Riesling grapes (possibly with some botrytis-affected berries at Auslese level) are harvested late in the season. Fermentation proceeds slowly at cool temperatures in stainless steel, and the high natural acidity and low temperature environment cause fermentation to arrest naturally before all sugar is consumed, leaving substantial RS balanced by the grape's inherent acidity. Quality is very good to outstanding for Mosel Auslese -- Bernkasteler Badstube is a respected Einzellage with steep slate slopes. The wine shows excellent balance between sweetness and acidity, with a precision and purity consistent with a top Mosel estate. Price band approximately EUR 25-40. The wine is youthful but already showing attractive complexity. **Wine 2**
Sweetness is achieved through **mutage (fortification)**. During fermentation of the Muscat must, neutral grape spirit at 96% ABV is added (at 5-10% of the must volume), killing the yeast and halting fermentation. This preserves the natural grape sugars while raising alcohol to the required minimum 15%. The early timing of mutage preserves the intensely aromatic, grapey character of Muscat. For Muscat de Rivesaltes, minimum must sugar before mutage is 252 g/l, resulting in wines with at least 100 g/l RS.

Quality is good to very good for the category -- a well-made commercial Muscat VDN showing varietal purity and balance. The wine is drinking at or near its peak. Unlike aged Rivesaltes (which develop rancio character over decades in barrel), Muscat de Rivesaltes is designed to be consumed young to preserve freshness. Drink now; unlikely to improve and may lose aromatic vibrancy within 2-3 years.

**Wine 3**
Sweetness is achieved through **appassimento (drying/passerillage)**. After harvest, the grape bunches are hung or laid on racks in a well-ventilated drying room (appassitoio) for 3-6 months. Water evaporates, concentrating sugars, acids, and flavours. The dried grapes are pressed in late winter, yielding a small quantity of intensely sweet must (approximately one-quarter of original volume). This must is transferred to small traditional casks (caratelli, 50-300 litres) where it undergoes a slow, intermittent fermentation over months or years, influenced by seasonal temperature fluctuations. The wine ages in these sealed caratelli for a minimum of 3 years (often 5+ for quality Vin Santo), developing the characteristic oxidative, nutty complexity.

Quality is good to very good -- a representative Vin Santo del Chianti showing the traditional profile of dried fruit, nuts, and honeyed oxidative character. The wine is mature and drinking well now, with the extended caratelli ageing having already integrated the components. It will hold for 5-10 years but is unlikely to improve further. Price band EUR 20-35 for a 375ml bottle.

**Wine 4**
Sweetness is achieved through **cryoextraction (natural freezing on the vine)**. Vidal grapes are left on the vine well into winter, and harvested by hand at temperatures of -8C to -13C, typically in December or January. The grapes are pressed while still frozen, so the water remains as ice crystals in the press, and only a tiny quantity of intensely concentrated juice is extracted -- one drop per grape. This juice has extremely high sugar concentration (typically 320-400 g/l at pressing). Fermentation is slow at cool temperatures and stops naturally when the yeast can no longer function in the sugar-rich environment, leaving very high RS (180-220 g/l) and relatively low alcohol.

Quality is very good -- a clean, well-made Niagara Vidal Icewine with the explosive tropical fruit concentration and racy acidity the category demands. The wine is youthful and vibrant, drinking well now with its primary fruit intensity. It can improve for 5-10 years as secondary honeyed complexity develops, and hold for 15-20 years. Price band approximately CAD 40-60 for 200ml.

**Wine 5**
Sweetness is achieved through **botrytis cinerea (noble rot)** combined with the traditional Tokaji production method. Aszú berries -- individual grapes shrivelled by noble rot (botrytis) -- are hand-selected over multiple vineyard passes (up to 30 passages). These berries are then macerated in or added to a base dry Furmint wine. The traditional measurement was puttonyos (hods/baskets of aszú berries added per barrel), with 5 Puttonyos indicating approximately 120-150 g/l residual sugar under current regulations. The wine is then aged in small Hungarian oak casks (220 litres, 30% new in the case of Chateau Pajzos) for a minimum of 18 months in underground cellars with ideal humidity.

Quality is very good to outstanding -- Tokaji Aszu 5 Puttonyos represents the benchmark sweet wine of Hungary, and this shows the classic complexity of orange peel, saffron, dried apricot, and marzipan with electric acidity providing freshness and extraordinary length. The wine is beginning to approach its drinking window but will improve for 10-20 years and hold for 30+ years. The volcanic terroir of Tokaj and the preservative combination of high acidity, sugar, and botrytis tannins give exceptional ageing potential. Price band EUR 30-50 for 500ml.

### b) Discuss the method of production, giving sensory evidence for your answer. (4 x 9 marks)

**Wine 6**
**Traditional method (methode champenoise).** Base wine is produced from 100% Chardonnay (Blanc de Blancs), fermented in tank with full malolactic fermentation. Reserve wine (approximately one-third of the blend) is kept in bottles, an unusual technique that preserves freshness. The cuvee undergoes second fermentation in bottle with the addition of liqueur de tirage (yeast and sugar). Extended ageing on lees (minimum 15 months for NV, likely 2-3 years for quality grower Champagne) develops the autolytic character -- brioche, toasted almond, and biscuit notes evident on the nose and palate. Riddling (remuage) and disgorgement remove the yeast sediment. A low dosage (approximately 5-6 g/l based on the restrained sweetness) is added at disgorgement.

*Sensory evidence:* The fine, persistent mousse (tiny bubbles, long-lasting) confirms bottle fermentation. The autolytic notes (brioche, almond) confirm extended lees contact. The integrated complexity and chalky texture confirm traditional method over transfer or Charmat. The low dosage is evidenced by the dry, mineral-driven finish without any cloying sweetness.

**Wine 7**
**Traditional method adapted for red wine.** The Shiraz is vinified as a still red wine first -- hand-harvested, foot-trodden or gently crushed in open fermenters with hand-plunging, then aged in French oak barrels. The still base wine then undergoes secondary fermentation in bottle (liqueur de tirage addition) and ages on lees for an extended period (likely 2-4 years for a vintage-dated sparkling Shiraz), developing autolytic complexity layered over the red fruit and spice. Disgorgement removes the lees sediment.

*Sensory evidence:* The fine persistent bead confirms bottle fermentation (tank-method sparkling reds are extremely rare). The complex layering of dark fruit, pepper, and aniseed with autolytic charcuterie and leather notes confirms extended lees ageing. The tannin structure, unusual in sparkling wine, confirms red-wine vinification of the base. The deep colour confirms full skin contact during primary fermentation -- this is not a rose or blanc de noirs.

**Wine 8**
**Ancestral method (Col Fondo / methode ancestrale).** The Glera grapes are harvested and undergo primary fermentation. Before fermentation is complete, the wine is bottled with its residual sugars and natural yeast still active. The remaining fermentation finishes in the sealed bottle, producing the natural effervescence. Crucially, unlike traditional method, there is no disgorgement -- the wine is left on its lees (col fondo literally means "with its sediment"), resulting in the characteristic slight cloudiness. No dosage is added; the wine is naturally dry.

*Sensory evidence:* The gentler, softer mousse (lower pressure, approximately 3-3.5 bars, classified as frizzante rather than spumante) confirms ancestral method rather than Charmat (which typically produces 5-6 bars). The slight haze and yeasty, bready character come from the retained lees. The cidery, slightly oxidative notes and textural complexity distinguish this from the clean, fruity profile of tank-method Prosecco. The bone-dry finish with no residual sweetness confirms natural completion of fermentation without dosage.

**Wine 9**
**Traditional method (Cava methodology).** Xarel-lo, Macabeu, and Parellada are harvested and vinified separately. Some base wines ferment and age in oak barrels for added structure. After blending, the wine undergoes secondary fermentation in bottle. For Gran Reserva classification, the wine must age on its lees for a minimum of 30 months (this wine aged 34 months). The wine is then riddled and disgorged. As Brut Nature, zero dosage is added -- a signature of premium Cava producers like Recaredo. The ageing under cork (rather than crown cap) and hand-riddling/disgorgement without neck-freezing are traditional Recaredo practices.

*Sensory evidence:* The fine persistent mousse confirms bottle fermentation. The pronounced autolytic character (dried herbs, almond, toast, biscuit) confirms extended lees ageing well beyond minimum requirements. The Brut Nature profile -- completely dry, with a slight bitter almond finish from the Xarel-lo -- confirms zero dosage. The broader, warmer fruit profile (Mediterranean stone fruit, dried herbs) compared to Champagne confirms warmer-climate origin. The complexity and integration exceed what Charmat method could achieve.

## Question 3

**Wines 10 to 12 are from the same category of fortified wine and the same country of origin, presented in ascending order of quality.**

Wines:
10. Late Bottled Vintage Port, Quinta do Crasto, 2019. Douro Valley, Portugal. (20%)
11. Colheita Tawny Port 2010, Niepoort, bottled 2023. Douro Valley, Portugal. (20%)
12. Vintage Port, Quinta do Vesuvio, 2017. Douro Valley, Portugal. (20%)

---

### a) Identify the category of fortified wine and the country of origin. (15 marks)

Port. Portugal (Douro Valley).

The three wines are all Port, identified by the combination of spirit warmth at approximately 20% ABV, residual sweetness (approximately 90-115 g/l RS), and the characteristic dark fruit intensity married to grape-spirit fortification. All three show the hallmarks of Port production: fermentation of red grape varieties (Touriga Nacional, Touriga Franca, Tinta Roriz, and others) halted partway through by the addition of aguardente (grape spirit at 77% ABV), which kills the yeast and preserves approximately half the grape sugar as residual sweetness.

The country is unambiguously Portugal: the Douro Valley is the only legally permitted region for Port production. The combination of grape spirit warmth, characteristic dried-fruit and floral complexity (violets, dark chocolate, cassis), and the high tannin-sweetness-acid framework is distinct from other fortified wines. Sherry (Spain) is eliminated by the sweetness and fruit profile; Madeira (Portugal but distinct) is eliminated by the absence of volatile acidity, caramelized lift, and the characteristic searing acidity of Madeira. Australian fortified Shiraz is eliminated by the higher tannin grip, drier fruit profile, and absence of the rancio/raisined character typical of Rutherglen.

The three wines represent three different Port styles spanning the ruby-to-tawny spectrum: one youthful ruby-family wine (LBV), one oxidatively aged single-vintage tawny (Colheita), and one premium ruby-family wine intended for long bottle-ageing (Vintage/Vintage Port).

### b) Identify the specific style, quality designation, and method of production and ageing as precisely as possible. (3 x 12 marks)

**Wine 10 -- Late Bottled Vintage (LBV) Port**

*Style and designation:* Late Bottled Vintage Port. This is a single-vintage Port from a declared year (2019), bottled between four and six years after the vintage. LBV occupies the quality tier between Ruby Reserve and Vintage Port -- it offers Vintage Port character at a more accessible price point and earlier drinking window.

*Method of production and ageing:* Grapes from old vines are hand-harvested, sorted, lightly crushed, and transferred to traditional stone lagares where they are foot-trodden to extract colour, tannin, and flavour during a short, intense maceration of 2-3 days. Fermentation is halted partway through by the addition of aguardente (grape spirit at 77% ABV) at a ratio of approximately 1 part spirit to 4 parts must, raising alcohol to approximately 20% and preserving natural grape sugars (~90-100 g/l RS).

The wine is then aged in large oak vats (approximately 9,000 litres at Quinta do Crasto) for approximately four years before bottling. Importantly, this LBV is unfiltered (the producer confirms no fining or filtration), meaning it retains a deposit and will continue to develop in bottle. This "traditional" unfiltered LBV style contrasts with most commercial LBVs, which are fined and filtered for immediate consumption.

*Quality:* Good to very good -- a well-made, concentrated LBV showing dark berry fruit, chocolate, gum cistus, and firm tannin. It is accessible now but will reward 5-10 years of additional bottle age. Price band approximately EUR 15-22.

**Wine 11 -- Colheita Tawny Port (2010, bottled 2023)**

*Style and designation:* Colheita Port. This is a single-harvest Tawny Port -- all grapes from the 2010 vintage, aged exclusively in wood (small oak casks of 550-600 litres, known as pipes) for a minimum of seven years. The 2010 Colheita aged approximately 13 years in wood before bottling in 2023. Colheita is a distinct legal category from aged Tawny (10/20/30/40-year designations, which are blends); Colheita must state both the harvest year and bottling date.

*Method of production and ageing:* Initial vinification follows the standard Port method: fermentation halted by aguardente addition. The key distinction is the ageing regime. Wines selected for their fruit structure and tannin framework are transferred to small oak casks (pipes) and aged in above-ground lodges, where they undergo gradual oxidative ageing. Over 13 years in wood, the wine loses colour (from deep ruby to tawny amber), gains oxidative complexity (dried fruit, caramel, nuts, spice), and concentrates through slow evaporation. The wine is tasted regularly and moved between casks (the racking process) to manage oxidation. It is bottled ready to drink, with no further ageing required.

*Quality:* Very good to outstanding. 13 years of careful oxidative ageing from Niepoort -- one of the most quality-obsessed houses in Port -- has produced a Colheita with complex nutty, dried-fruit character, caramel, toffee, and a long, elegant finish. The balance between fruit, acidity, sweetness, and oxidative complexity is excellent. Comparable in quality to a good 20-Year Tawny but with single-vintage identity. Price band approximately EUR 35-55.

**Wine 12 -- Vintage Port (Vintage/Vintage Port, 2017)**

*Style and designation:* Vintage Port (also known as Single Quinta Vintage Port). This is from a declared vintage year (2017), representing the pinnacle of the ruby-style Port hierarchy. Vintage Port is made only in exceptional years and is intended for long bottle ageing. The 2017 vintage was widely declared across the Douro.

*Method of production and ageing:* Grapes from a single estate (Quinta do Vesuvio) are hand-harvested and foot-trodden in traditional granite lagares -- the Symington family (owners) deliberately retained this traditional method for its superior extraction. The fermentation is halted by aguardente addition when approximately half the sugar remains. The key difference from LBV is the ageing trajectory: Vintage Port spends only approximately 18-24 months in seasoned oak vats (to allow the wine to settle and clarify) before being bottled unfiltered and unfined. The wine is intended to undergo decades of reductive ageing in bottle, during which it develops extraordinary complexity, throws a heavy sediment, and requires decanting.

The 2017 blend includes 33% Touriga Nacional, 35% Touriga Franca, 15% Sousao, and 12% Alicante Bouschet. The RS is approximately 115 g/l. The wine has tremendous concentration, dense tannin, and the structure for very long ageing.

*Quality:* Outstanding. Quinta do Vesuvio is one of the greatest single-quinta estates in the Douro, and the 2017 vintage is considered excellent. The wine shows the hallmarks of top-tier Vintage Port: intense dark fruit (cassis, blackberry, fig), floral lift (violets, rockrose), spice (ginger, mint), and a massively structured but balanced palate with the characteristic schist-derived tannins of the Upper Douro. This will need 15-20 years before approaching its peak drinking window and will hold for 40-60 years. Price band approximately EUR 50-80.

### c) Rank the wines in quality order and justify your ranking with reference to the applicable classification or designation. (15 marks)

**Ascending quality order: Wine 10 (LBV) < Wine 11 (Colheita) < Wine 12 (Vintage Port)**

The Port classification hierarchy places these wines in a clear ascending quality ladder:

**Wine 10 -- LBV Port (lowest of the three)**
LBV is positioned between Ruby Reserve and Vintage Port in the Port quality hierarchy. It is a single-vintage wine, but from a year that is not a universally "declared" vintage (or from fruit not selected for the top Vintage Port cuvee). It spends 4-6 years in large wood before bottling, making it more approachable earlier than Vintage Port. While Quinta do Crasto's unfiltered LBV is at the premium end of the LBV spectrum, the category itself occupies a tier below both Colheita and Vintage Port. LBV is designed to offer Vintage Port character at a more accessible price and drinking window.

**Wine 11 -- Colheita Tawny Port (middle)**
Colheita represents the single-vintage expression of the aged Tawny tradition. With 13 years of oxidative ageing in small casks, this Niepoort Colheita sits at a quality level comparable to a 10-20 Year Tawny but with the additional interest of single-vintage identity. Colheita is a prestigious category -- it requires longer minimum ageing than LBV (7 years vs 4-6 years) and the small-cask ageing regime demands more careful cellar management. However, in the overall Port hierarchy, Colheita (while excellent) is generally positioned below the very finest declared Vintage Ports, which represent the absolute pinnacle of Port production.

**Wine 12 -- Vintage Port (highest)**
Vintage Port is the apex of the ruby-style hierarchy and, for most Port houses, represents their finest wine. It is declared only in exceptional vintages (typically 3-4 times per decade), made from the best fruit of the best vineyards, and intended for multi-decade bottle ageing. Quinta do Vesuvio 2017 -- a single-quinta Vintage Port from one of the Douro's greatest estates in an excellent declared year -- represents the very top of the Port quality pyramid alongside multi-quinta Vintage Ports from the major houses. The concentration, structure, and ageing potential dramatically exceed both the LBV and Colheita.

The quality ranking is reinforced by price: LBV (~EUR 15-22) < Colheita (~EUR 35-55) < Vintage Port (~EUR 50-80), and by ageing potential: LBV (5-15 years) < Colheita (drink now, ready) < Vintage Port (20-60 years).
