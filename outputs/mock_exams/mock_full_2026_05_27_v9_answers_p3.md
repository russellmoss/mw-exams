## Question 2 — Sherry Styles

*Wines 5 to 8 are all from the same country and the same wine region. Each represents a different style within a single legally defined category.*

### a) Country, region, and legally defined wine category (10 marks)

Spain. Jerez de la Frontera / Sanlúcar de Barrameda. The legally defined category is **Jerez-Xérès-Sherry DO** (encompassing the Manzanilla-Sanlúcar de Barrameda DO for Wine 6).

All four wines share the fundamental Sherry markers: bone-dry to dry finish, aldehydic complexity, oxidative and/or flor-derived character, Palomino grape, and fortified alcohol levels ranging from 15% to 21.5%. The geographic triangle of Jerez, El Puerto de Santa María, and Sanlúcar de Barrameda is the only region in the world that produces this complete spectrum of biologically and oxidatively aged wines under one legal framework.

### b) Specific style and method of production (4 x 10 marks)

**Wine 5 — Fino**

Pale lemon-gold colour with a green-tinged rim. Intense nose of flor-derived character: bruised apple, salted almonds, dried herbs, chalk. Palate is bone dry, with piercing salinity, moderate body, and a long mineral finish. 15% ABV.

**Method:** After pressing, the base wine (Palomino from albariza soil) is fermented dry to ~11-12% ABV, then fortified to exactly 15% — the upper threshold at which flor (a film of Saccharomyces yeast) can survive. The wine enters a solera system of American oak butts (600L), filled five-sixths full to leave a headspace where flor can form. The flor protects the wine from oxidation while it ages biologically, consuming alcohol and glycerol, producing acetaldehyde (the characteristic bruised-apple/almond note). This wine likely passes through an extensive criadera system (possibly 8-10 stages), reaching an average age of 8-10 years before bottling. The flor also prevents colour change, keeping the wine pale. The saline, mineral character comes from the albariza chalk soils.

**Wine 6 — Manzanilla**

Very pale lemon colour, even lighter than Wine 5. Nose: chamomile, sea salt, iodine, green olive, lighter almond note than Wine 5. Palate: bone dry, extremely saline, lighter body than Fino, with a briny, maritime finish. 15% ABV.

**Method:** Identical biological aging process to Fino — fortified to 15%, aged under flor in a solera system. The critical difference is **geography**: Manzanilla must be aged in Sanlúcar de Barrameda, where the cooler, more humid Atlantic maritime climate produces a thicker, more active flor layer year-round. This thicker flor consumes more glycerol, producing a leaner, lighter, more saline wine. The chamomile note is specifically associated with Sanlúcar's microclimate and is the classic Manzanilla marker. Average solera age likely 4-6 years. The lighter body and increased salinity versus Wine 5 reflect Sanlúcar's distinct conditions.

**Wine 7 — Amontillado**

Deep amber-gold colour. Nose: complex layers of toasted hazelnuts, walnut, dried apricot, salted caramel, polished oak, bitter orange peel. Palate: completely dry, intense concentration, fierce acidity, layered nutty complexity extending to an extraordinarily long finish. 21.5% ABV.

**Method:** Amontillado is the product of **two-phase aging** — biological then oxidative. The wine begins life as a Fino, aging under flor at 15% ABV. After the flor naturally thins and dies (or is deliberately killed by further fortification to ~17-18% ABV), the wine transitions to oxidative aging in partially filled barrels without flor protection. The high ABV (21.5%) reflects additional fortification at this stage. During oxidative aging, the wine darkens dramatically through acetaldehyde polymerisation and contact with oxygen, developing the nutty, caramelised complexity. This wine's extraordinary concentration and layered character suggest a VORS designation (Vinum Optimum Rare Signatum — average age 30+ years). The dry, intense, saline finish retains the memory of biological aging while the oxidative phase has added depth and colour.

**Wine 8 — Palo Cortado**

Deep amber colour similar to Wine 7 but with a slightly warmer, tawny hue. Nose: elegant and complex — candied orange, hazelnuts, cigar ash, dried herbs, a delicate floral lift absent in Wine 7. Palate: dry, with the aromatic finesse and delicacy of an Amontillado but the fuller body and rounder texture of an Oloroso. 20% ABV.

**Method:** Palo Cortado is the rarest and most enigmatic Sherry style. It begins as a Fino under flor, but the flor spontaneously dies during biological aging — not through deliberate intervention, but through natural failure. The wine is then fortified to ~17-20% and transferred to an oxidative solera. The result combines the **aromatic refinement** of biological aging (the delicate, lifted nose) with the **body and structure** of purely oxidative aging (the weight and roundness on the palate). This is why Palo Cortado occupies a unique position: the nose of an Amontillado, the palate of an Oloroso. This example shows 30+ years average age, with the complexity and elegance that define the category. The slightly lower ABV than Wine 7 (20% vs 21.5%) and the more elegant, less austere profile distinguish it from Amontillado.

## Question 3 — Sweet Wine Mechanism and Quality

*Wines 9 to 12 all have residual sugar. They come from four different countries.*

### Wine 9 — Leitz Riesling Spätlese "Dragonstone" 2023, Rheingau, Germany (9%)

**a) Grape variety, country, region (6 marks)**

Riesling. Germany. Rheingau.

The lime, white peach, and green apple aromatics with a distinctive petrol/kerosene undertone are unmistakable Riesling markers. The very low alcohol (9%) combined with off-dry sweetness (~50 g/l RS) and electric, nerve-jangling acidity is the signature of German Prädikat Riesling. The Rheingau is distinguished from Mosel by the slightly riper fruit profile (more peach, less austere slate minerality) and marginally higher alcohol than a Mosel Spätlese (which might be 7.5-8.5%). The quartzite-driven minerality is consistent with the Drachenstein vineyard site. Youthful primary character with the 2023 vintage confirms a young wine still in its primary fruit phase.

**b) Mechanism of sweetness and method of production (6 marks)**

**Natural residual sugar through arrested fermentation (Prädikat Spätlese).**

The grapes were late-harvested (Spätlese = "late picking") at elevated must weights, reaching the legal minimum of 76° Oechsle for Rheingau Spätlese. The grapes were not affected by botrytis — the clean, pure fruit profile confirms this. After gentle whole-cluster pressing, the must was fermented in stainless steel at cool temperatures. Fermentation was halted naturally or by chilling/filtration when the desired balance of alcohol (~9%) and residual sugar (~50 g/l) was achieved. The low alcohol results from arresting fermentation early while retaining natural grape sugars. No chaptalisation is permitted under Prädikat regulations. No oak was used. The wine was bottled early to preserve primary fruit character and acidity.

### Wine 10 — Château Rieussec 2019, Sauternes, Bordeaux, France (14%)

**a) Grape variety, country, region (6 marks)**

Sémillon dominant (with Sauvignon Blanc). France. Sauternes, Bordeaux.

The lusciously sweet, waxy, unctuous palate with flavours of candied orange peel, apricot jam, beeswax, and honeycomb is classic Sauternes. The 14% alcohol with extremely high residual sugar (~130 g/l) places this firmly in the botrytis sweet-wine category. Sémillon's lanolin-waxy texture and susceptibility to noble rot are evident. The hint of Sauvignon Blanc's grapefruit zest and lemongrass lifts the blend. The oak influence (vanilla, toasted brioche, subtle spice) from barrel aging in new French oak is evident. The weight, concentration, and length suggest a classified Sauternes estate.

**b) Mechanism of sweetness and method of production (6 marks)**

**Botrytis cinerea (noble rot).**

The Sauternes microclimate — morning mists from the confluence of the Ciron and Garonne rivers, followed by warm afternoons — creates ideal conditions for Botrytis cinerea. The noble rot perforates grape skins, allowing water to evaporate and concentrating sugars, acids, and flavour compounds (sotolon, the signature botrytis marker creating honey/saffron notes). Grapes were harvested in multiple passes (tries) over approximately one week in October, selecting only botrytised berries at peak concentration. The juice, already concentrated to very high sugar levels, was fermented slowly in 50% new French oak barrels for approximately 18-24 months. Fermentation naturally slows and stops as alcohol reaches ~14% and the remaining sugar (~130 g/l) cannot be converted. No fortification; the sweetness is entirely from concentrated natural grape sugar. Extended barrel maturation (likely 18-30 months) adds vanilla, toast, and integrates the botrytis character with the oak.

### Wine 11 — Avignonesi Vin Santo del Chianti Classico 2011, Tuscany, Italy (15%)

**a) Grape variety, country, region (6 marks)**

Sangiovese and Malvasia (traditional Vin Santo blend). Italy. Tuscany (Chianti Classico zone).

The deep amber-gold colour, oxidative complexity (walnut, caramel, dried fig, candied citrus peel), and viscous, raisinated sweetness are classic Vin Santo markers. The 15% alcohol without spirit heat confirms a non-fortified passito wine that achieved high alcohol through concentration of the dried grapes. The underlying bitter-almond and dried-herb character is consistent with Sangiovese/Malvasia in the Vin Santo tradition. The extraordinary complexity and development from 10+ years of aging distinguish this from lesser examples.

**b) Mechanism of sweetness and method of production (6 marks)**

**Passerillage (appassimento — dried grape method).**

After harvest, bunches of Sangiovese and Malvasia grapes are hung on racks or laid on straw mats (graticci) in a well-ventilated vinsantaia (drying loft) for 3-6 months over autumn and winter. During this period, the grapes desiccate slowly, losing up to 50-60% of their water content. This concentrates sugars, acids, and flavour compounds while allowing partial oxidation and the development of raisinated, nutty flavours. In late winter or early spring, the shrivelled grapes are pressed, yielding a tiny quantity of intensely concentrated must. This must is transferred into small sealed oak barrels called caratelli (typically 50-100 litres). A madre — a culture of indigenous yeasts from previous vintages — may be present in the barrel to initiate fermentation. Fermentation is extremely slow due to the high sugar concentration and cold cellar temperatures, and proceeds intermittently over months or even years. The caratelli are sealed and left undisturbed for a minimum of 8 years (and often 10+ years for premium examples like this 2011). During this extended aging, the wine undergoes oxidative development within the sealed barrel, producing the deep amber colour, nutty complexity, and caramelised flavours. No fortification. The 15% alcohol is naturally achieved through the concentrated must.

**b) Mechanism of sweetness and method of production (6 marks)**

The base grapes are harvested at full maturity in the Douro Valley. Fermentation begins in lagares (traditional stone treading tanks) or autovinifiers, extracting colour and tannin from the skins during a short maceration of 2-3 days. When fermentation has consumed approximately half of the natural sugar — typically when the must reaches ~7-8% alcohol — neutral grape spirit (aguardente at 77% ABV) is added to arrest fermentation. This kills the yeast, leaving approximately 80-100 g/l of unfermented residual sugar and raising alcohol to ~19-20%. The spirit addition is the critical mechanism: it both preserves sweetness AND increases alcohol simultaneously.

For the Tawny style, the young fortified wine is then transferred to small oak casks (pipas, ~550-630 litres) and aged oxidatively in the cooler lodges of Vila Nova de Gaia. Over 20+ years of oxidative aging in small wood, the wine loses its youthful red-fruit character and develops the tawny colour, nutty complexity, rancio, and dried-fruit profile. Evaporation concentrates the wine further. The final wine is a multi-vintage blend assessed by an independent panel to verify it matches the character expected of a 20-year-old indicative age. The wine is ready to drink on release with no further aging required.
