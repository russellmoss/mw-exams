## Question 1

**Wines 1 to 4 are from the same country but come from four different wine regions. Each wine is made from a different single grape variety closely associated with its region of origin.**

a) Identify the country of origin. (10 marks)
b) Identify the grape variety and the specific region and appellation as closely as possible. (4 x 8 marks)
c) Comment on the quality of each wine and discuss the importance of natural factors versus winemaking in shaping its character. (4 x 9 marks)
d) Compare and contrast the commercial position of these wines in key export markets. (18 marks)

### Wines

1. Barolo Cascina Francia, Giacomo Conterno, 2018. Serralunga d'Alba, Piedmont, Italy. (14.5%)
2. Chianti Classico Gran Selezione "San Martino", Isole e Olena, 2020. Tuscany, Italy. (14%)
3. Etna Rosso "Feudo di Mezzo", Passopisciaro, 2022. Etna, Sicily, Italy. (13%)
4. Taurasi DOCG "Radici", Mastroberardino, 2019. Campania, Italy. (14%)

---

### Model Answer — Question 1

**Word count: ~420**

## a) Country of origin

**Italy.**

Four wines of markedly different structure, colour, and aromatic profile, yet all sharing high natural acidity, savoury tannin architecture, and a distinctly European, food-oriented sensibility. The combination of pale garnet with fierce tannin (Wine 1), sour cherry and dried herb (Wine 2), volcanic transparency (Wine 3), and dark, brooding tannic grip (Wine 4) points unambiguously to Italy — the one country that fields four world-class indigenous red varieties from four geographically distinct regions.

## b) Grape variety and origin

**Wine 1:** Nebbiolo; Barolo DOCG, Piedmont.
Pale garnet colour belying immense tannic structure. Rose, tar, dried cherry, liquorice, and menthol on the nose. The combination of translucent colour with savage, fine-grained tannin and piercing acidity is the hallmark of Nebbiolo from the Langhe hills. The depth and concentration suggest Serralunga — one of Barolo's most structured communes.

**Wine 2:** Sangiovese; Chianti Classico DOCG, Tuscany.
Medium ruby. Sour cherry, dried herbs, leather, and earthy minerality. High acidity and dusty, drying tannins that grip without astringence. The combination of cherry fruit, herbal complexity, and firm acid-tannin backbone places this squarely as Sangiovese from central Tuscany. The concentration and polish suggest Gran Selezione level.

**Wine 3:** Nerello Mascalese; Etna DOC, Sicily.
Light ruby with an almost Pinot-like translucency. Red fruits — wild strawberry, cranberry — overlaid with volcanic ash, smoke, dried herbs, and a faint floral lift. The paradox of delicate colour with firm, chalky tannins and brisk acidity on volcanic soils is the fingerprint of Nerello Mascalese from Etna's north face. This is a curveball within the Italian frame, but the volcanic mineral signature is unmistakable.

**Wine 4:** Aglianico; Taurasi DOCG, Campania.
Deep ruby-garnet. Intense dark fruit — blackberry, plum — layered with leather, tobacco, earth, and a note of tar. Massive tannins with a brooding, almost impenetrable structure. High acidity reinforces the tannic grip. This is Aglianico at its most serious — Taurasi, the "Barolo of the South." The 2019 vintage shows youthful power with the spice and resinous character typical of the variety.

## c) Quality and natural factors vs winemaking

**Wine 1 — Barolo Cascina Francia:**
Outstanding quality, comparable to top single-vineyard Barolo (£150–250). Natural factors dominate: Serralunga's calcareous-clay soils give the wine its iron backbone and longevity. The altitude and south-east exposure of the Francia vineyard provide full ripeness at 14.5% while retaining acidity. Winemaking is deliberately traditional and non-interventionist — extended maceration in large Slavonian oak botti rather than new barriques allows the terroir to speak. The decision by Roberto Conterno to blend all casks into this wine in 2018 (foregoing Monfortino) only enhances its quality. Natural factors account for at least 70% of the wine's character; winemaking serves to preserve rather than shape.

**Wine 2 — Chianti Classico Gran Selezione:**
Very good to outstanding quality, upper echelon of Chianti Classico (£35–55). Natural factors — the galestro and alberese soils of the Classico zone, altitude of 300–500m, and continental-Mediterranean climate — give the wine its hallmark cherry-herbal character and structural acidity. Winemaking adds complexity: selective use of new French oak (likely 20–30% new), controlled extraction, and the Gran Selezione designation requires estate-grown fruit and extended ageing. The balance between terroir purity and judicious oak integration suggests roughly equal importance of natural and human factors.

**Wine 3 — Etna Rosso:**
Very good quality, premium Etna single-contrada level (£30–50). Natural factors are paramount: the volcanic soils (ancient lava flows), altitude (800–1000m), and diurnal temperature variation create the wine's signature combination of translucent colour, aromatic complexity, and chalky tannin. Winemaking at Passopisciaro is deliberately minimal — fermentation in steel, ageing in large neutral oak, no filtering. This is a terroir-driven wine where human intervention is deliberately restrained. Natural factors dominate overwhelmingly — the contrada system exists precisely because site differences are so marked.

**Wine 4 — Taurasi Radici:**
Very good to outstanding quality, top Taurasi DOCG (£30–50). Natural factors shape the wine's massive tannic structure: Campania's volcanic and clay soils, the continental-influenced climate of Irpinia at 400–600m elevation, and Aglianico's inherently tannic character create a wine that demands time. Winemaking must manage this raw material — Mastroberardino employs extended maceration and 18–24 months in French and Slavonian oak to civilise the tannins without masking the variety's brooding character. The winemaker's hand is more visible here than in Wine 3, perhaps 40% of the final character.

## d) Commercial position in export markets

These four wines represent a cross-section of Italian fine wine, each occupying a distinct commercial niche.

**Wine 1 (Barolo):** The most commercially established. Giacomo Conterno is among the most coveted Barolo producers; Cascina Francia commands £150–250+ at retail and is allocated through specialist importers and fine wine merchants. The primary market is collector-driven: UK, US, German, and Asian fine wine channels. On-trade placement in Michelin-starred restaurants. Limited availability creates secondary-market demand. Competes with top Burgundy and classified Bordeaux for collector attention.

**Wine 2 (Chianti Classico Gran Selezione):** The most commercially accessible of the four at £35–55. Isole e Olena has strong distribution in UK and US specialist channels. Gran Selezione is still building recognition but benefits from the broader Chianti Classico brand. Both on-trade (Italian restaurants, wine bars) and off-trade (specialist retailers). Competes with Brunello, premium Rioja Reserva, and mid-tier Burgundy. Challenge: Gran Selezione designation is not yet universally understood by consumers.

**Wine 3 (Etna Rosso):** The trendiest. Etna has captured sommelier and wine-press attention disproportionate to production volume. Passopisciaro is a flagship estate. £30–50 retail, primarily through specialist importers and on-trade (wine-bar and restaurant by-the-glass programmes that prize discovery). Strong in US, UK, Scandinavian, and Japanese markets. Challenge: consumer awareness outside wine-trade circles remains limited; the "volcanic wine" story helps but requires handselling.

**Wine 4 (Taurasi):** The most undervalued. Despite outstanding quality, Taurasi lacks the global brand recognition of Barolo or Chianti. Mastroberardino is the region's reference producer, yet Radici retails at £30–50 — far below comparably structured wines from Piedmont. Distribution is narrower: specialist Italian wine importers, some on-trade. Strongest in domestic Italian market and Germany; growing in US and UK. The opportunity lies in the value proposition — this offers Barolo-level structure at a fraction of the price. The challenge is that "Aglianico" and "Taurasi" remain unfamiliar to most consumers.

The flight illustrates Italy's unique breadth: from blue-chip collectible (Wine 1) to emerging-region darling (Wine 3) to hidden gem (Wine 4), all within one country.

## Proposed annotation

**Q1 — Italy same-country, four regions, four indigenous varieties:**
This is a classic IMW same-country question testing depth of Italian knowledge beyond Piedmont and Tuscany. The question rewards candidates who can identify four distinct indigenous varieties from their structural and aromatic profiles. The "natural factors vs winemaking" sub-question rewards those who can articulate how Italy's diverse terroirs (calcareous clay, galestro, volcanic, clay-tuff) shape fundamentally different wines from fundamentally different grapes. The curveball is Wine 3 (Nerello Mascalese/Etna) — Pinot-like translucency from Sicily will confuse candidates who associate Sicily with rich, warm-climate reds. Wine 4 (Aglianico/Taurasi) tests whether candidates know Italy south of Tuscany. Commercial sub-question rewards specificity about the very different market positions of these four categories. Mark allocation is generous (18 marks) for commercial, signalling depth is expected.

## Reasoning trace

- **Stem signals:** "Same country," "four different wine regions," "different single grape variety closely associated with its region." This is a same-country, region-diversity question — textbook F2 in the P2 tree.
- **Universe:** Italy is the STRONG SIGNAL for a 4-region, 4-variety same-country question in P2. France could field this but would more likely use Bordeaux blends rather than "single grape variety closely associated." Spain could work (Tempranillo/Garnacha/Monastrell/Mencia) but the four-region depth is harder. Italy is the only country that routinely fields four completely different indigenous varieties from four world-class regions.
- **Rule-outs:** France — Pinot Noir (Burgundy), Syrah (Rhone), Cabernet/Merlot (Bordeaux) works but the fourth variety is awkward (Gamay? Mourvèdre?). Spain — possible but less canonical. Australia — doesn't have four indigenous varieties.
- **In-glass confirmation:** Wine 1's pale garnet + tar + rose = Nebbiolo. Wine 2's sour cherry + herbs = Sangiovese. Wine 3's translucent ruby + volcanic smoke = Nerello Mascalese. Wine 4's dark brooding tannin + leather = Aglianico. All four lock to Italy.
- **Conclusion:** Italy, Piedmont/Tuscany/Sicily/Campania. High confidence.

## Question 2

**Wines 5 to 8 are all made predominantly from the same single grape variety. Each wine comes from a different country.**

a) Identify the grape variety. (12 marks)
b) Identify the origin as closely as possible. (4 x 8 marks)
c) Comment on how climate and winemaking have shaped the structure, quality, and style of each wine. (4 x 11 marks)

### Wines

5. Crozes-Hermitage "Les Meysonniers", M. Chapoutier, 2023. Northern Rhone Valley, France. (13%)
6. Syrah "Thea's Selection", Lemelson Vineyards, 2022. Willamette Valley, Oregon, USA. (13.5%)
7. Shiraz "The Beekeeper", Hentley Farm, 2023. Barossa Valley, South Australia. (14.5%)
8. Syrah "Porseleinberg", Boekenhoutskloof, 2022. Swartland, South Africa. (13.5%)

---

### Model Answer — Question 2

**Word count: ~410**

## a) Grape variety

**Syrah / Shiraz.**

All four wines share a core aromatic profile of dark fruit, pepper, and violet, with varying expressions of savoury complexity. The structural signatures — moderate-to-high tannin, medium-to-full body, and a dark-fruited aromatic framework — are consistent across all four despite significant stylistic variation. Wine 5 is the most overtly peppery and medium-bodied; Wine 7 is the most generous and full-bodied. This is the classic Syrah/Shiraz spectrum from cool to warm climate. Pinot Noir is eliminated by tannin weight and colour depth; Cabernet by the absence of cassis-cedar and the presence of pepper-violet; Grenache by the darker fruit profile and firmer structure.

## b) Origin

**Wine 5:** Crozes-Hermitage, Northern Rhone, France.
Medium ruby-purple. White pepper, blackcurrant, raspberry, violet, and a subtle smoky minerality. Medium-bodied with fine tannins and pronounced freshness. The combination of pepper, restraint, and mineral drive at 13% alcohol places this firmly in the Northern Rhone. The accessible, fruit-forward style with moderate concentration suggests Crozes-Hermitage rather than the more intense Hermitage or Cornas.

**Wine 6:** Willamette Valley, Oregon, USA.
Medium ruby. Cool-climate Syrah showing red and blue fruit — raspberry, blueberry — with spice, black tea, and a floral lift. Bracing acidity with silky tannins. At 13.5%, this is a lean, elegant Syrah from a notably cool New World site. The combination of Northern-Rhone-like pepper with New World fruit purity suggests Oregon or possibly Hawke's Bay. The restrained structure and floral aromatics lean Oregon.

**Wine 7:** Barossa Valley, South Australia.
Deep purple-black. Intense blackberry, liquorice, dark chocolate, vanilla, and sweet spice from oak. Full-bodied with plush, ripe tannins and high alcohol (14.5%). This is unmistakably warm-climate Shiraz. The lavish fruit concentration, sweet American and French oak integration, and generous palate weight are hallmarks of the Barossa. The density and richness go beyond McLaren Vale's typically more savoury profile.

**Wine 8:** Swartland, South Africa.
Deep ruby-purple. White pepper, crushed violets, fynbos, dried lavender, dark berries, and a distinctive smoky, herbal complexity. Medium-to-full body with chalky, elegant tannins at 13.5%. This combines Northern-Rhone structure with a distinctly South African aromatic signature — the fynbos/herbal note is diagnostic. The Swartland's old-vine, dryland viticulture produces concentrated but refined Syrah with lower alcohol than comparable Barossa examples.

## c) Climate and winemaking shaping structure, quality, and style

**Wine 5 — Crozes-Hermitage:**
Very good quality; strong Village/satellite appellation level (£15–20).
**Climate:** Continental-influenced Mediterranean with cool mistral winds. Moderate heat accumulation ripens Syrah to 13% — lower than warmer sites — preserving acidity and producing the characteristic peppery, savoury style. The granite and alluvial soils of Crozes add mineral nuance without the weight of Hermitage's granite hill.
**Winemaking:** Biodynamic viticulture (Chapoutier). Vinified predominantly in concrete (90%) with 10% used puncheons for 12 months. Minimal new oak preserves varietal purity. The choice of concrete retains freshness and bright fruit. The result is a mid-weight, approachable Northern Rhone Syrah — designed for relatively early drinking rather than long cellaring.

**Wine 6 — Willamette Valley, Oregon:**
Good to very good quality; premium cool-climate Syrah (£20–30).
**Climate:** Maritime-influenced with long, cool growing seasons. Willamette Valley is at the extreme cool end for Syrah — the variety is rare here and grown only in warmer microclimates. The cool climate explains the lower alcohol (13.5%), the high acidity, and the red-fruit-dominated rather than black-fruit profile. Diurnal temperature variation preserves aromatic complexity.
**Winemaking:** Likely whole-cluster inclusion (common in Oregon) contributing floral lift and spice. Ageing in French oak (perhaps 20–30% new) adds structure without overwhelming the delicate fruit. Possible Viognier co-fermentation (a technique used by Oregon producers) would explain any floral aromatics. The winemaker must work to extract sufficient colour and tannin from marginally ripe Syrah in this climate.

**Wine 7 — Barossa Valley:**
Very good to outstanding quality; premium single-vineyard Barossa Shiraz (£30–50).
**Climate:** Warm continental with hot, dry summers. The Barossa's warmth drives alcohol to 14.5%, produces dense black fruit, and creates the plush, generous palate structure. Old vines (common in Barossa) add concentration and depth. Limited rainfall necessitates dry-farming or deficit irrigation, concentrating flavours.
**Winemaking:** The lavish oak signature (sweet vanilla, chocolate, spice) suggests extensive use of new American and French barriques — likely 50%+ new oak for 18+ months. Extended maceration builds the firm but ripe tannin structure. This is a deliberately hedonistic, oak-driven winemaking style that amplifies the warm climate's generosity. The winemaker's hand is very visible — this is as much a product of cellar choices as of terroir.

**Wine 8 — Swartland, South Africa:**
Outstanding quality; top-tier Swartland single-vineyard Syrah (£40–60).
**Climate:** Mediterranean with warm, dry summers tempered by maritime breezes from the Atlantic. The Swartland's schist and granite soils combined with old, dryland bush vines produce naturally low yields and concentrated fruit at moderate alcohol (13.5%). The Porseleinberg site on the Paardeberg adds mineral complexity and chalky tannin texture.
**Winemaking:** This is one of South Africa's most non-interventionist wines. Approximately 95% whole-bunch fermentation, aged in large foudres and 15% concrete. No new small oak. Wild yeast fermentation. The result is a Syrah where terroir expression is maximised — the fynbos and herbal aromatics come from the vineyard, not the cellar. Winemaking deliberately steps back to let the Swartland granite speak.

The flight beautifully illustrates how climate shifts the Syrah spectrum from peppery restraint (Wine 5, cool) through elegant middle-ground (Wines 6 and 8) to opulent generosity (Wine 7, warm), while winemaking choices — oak regimes, whole-bunch decisions, extraction levels — either amplify or restrain each climate's natural expression.

### Wines

9. Bourgogne Rouge "La Croix de Pierre", Domaine Drouhin-Laroze, 2023. Burgundy, France. (12.5%)
10. Gevrey-Chambertin, Domaine Drouhin-Laroze, 2022. Cote de Nuits, Burgundy, France. (13%)
11. Gevrey-Chambertin 1er Cru "Lavaux Saint-Jacques", Domaine Drouhin-Laroze, 2021. Cote de Nuits, Burgundy, France. (13.5%)
12. Mazis-Chambertin Grand Cru, Domaine Drouhin-Laroze, 2020. Cote de Nuits, Burgundy, France. (14%)

### Model Answer — Question 3

**Word count: ~415**

## a) Grape variety and broad region

**Pinot Noir; Burgundy, France (Cote de Nuits).**

All four wines share a translucent ruby-to-garnet colour palette with increasing depth and concentration from Wine 9 to Wine 12. The aromatic profile across the flight — red cherry, raspberry, earth, mushroom, and subtle spice — is unmistakably Pinot Noir. The high natural acidity, fine-grained silky tannins, and moderate alcohol (12.5–14%) confirm Burgundy. The ascending concentration, complexity, and tannin refinement from a simple, fresh style (Wine 9) through to a dense, multi-layered expression (Wine 12) maps precisely to Burgundy's legally defined quality hierarchy. The earthy, savoury character and terroir transparency are Cote de Nuits rather than Cote de Beaune (which would show more generosity and less structure at equivalent quality levels).

## b) Specific appellation or quality designation

**Wine 9:** Bourgogne Rouge (Regional appellation).
Light ruby. Simple, fresh red cherry and raspberry. Light body, moderate acidity, soft tannins, short finish. This is entry-level Burgundy — pleasant but uncomplicated. The 12.5% alcohol and lack of concentration are consistent with regional-level Pinot Noir from declassified or young-vine fruit.

**Wine 10:** Gevrey-Chambertin Village.
Medium ruby. More concentrated cherry and dark berry fruit with earthy undertones, leather, and subtle spice. Medium body, firmer tannins, better length. A clear step up in concentration and complexity at 13%. The savoury, structured character is typical of Gevrey-Chambertin's village-level wines — more masculine than Chambolle or Volnay.

**Wine 11:** Gevrey-Chambertin Premier Cru.
Medium-deep ruby. Intense red and dark fruit — morello cherry, blackcurrant — with violet, spice, and leather. Denser tannin structure, greater palate weight and length. At 13.5%, this shows the additional concentration and complexity that a Premier Cru site delivers. The tannins are more refined and integrated, the finish longer, the aromatic complexity richer.

**Wine 12:** Grand Cru (Mazis-Chambertin or equivalent Gevrey Grand Cru).
Deep ruby-garnet. Profound nose — dark cherry, rose, truffle, earth, iron, smoke. Full body (for Pinot Noir), powerful yet silk-textured tannins, exceptional length and persistence. At 14%, this shows Grand Cru concentration without losing Burgundian elegance. The layers of flavour, the mineral depth, and the finish that extends for 60+ seconds place this unambiguously at Grand Cru level.

## c) Style and quality within the context of designation

**Wine 9 — Bourgogne Rouge:**
Good quality for its designation. A well-made regional Burgundy that delivers fresh, approachable Pinot Noir character. Clean fruit, no faults, balanced acidity. Within the context of Bourgogne Rouge, this is above average — it shows more varietal character and purity than many generic examples. However, it lacks the concentration and complexity to suggest anything higher. Drink now to 2–3 years. The gentle extraction and likely minimal new oak (0–10%) are appropriate for the level.

**Wine 10 — Gevrey-Chambertin Village:**
Very good quality for Village level. This shows the concentration uplift that village-classified fruit from Gevrey-Chambertin delivers: darker fruit, more earth, firmer structure. Within Gevrey Village context, this is a solid example — not entry-level Gevrey but not pushing into Premier Cru territory either. The structured, savoury style is true to commune character. Drink 2025–2032. Likely 18 months in oak with 15–25% new barrels.

**Wine 11 — Gevrey-Chambertin Premier Cru:**

**Q3 — Pinot Noir, Burgundy, same-producer quality hierarchy (Bourgogne → Village → 1er Cru → Grand Cru):**
This is a textbook F7 hierarchy question using the world's most transparent quality ladder. The four-step Burgundy classification is the cleanest legal hierarchy in wine, making this a fair and unambiguous ranking question. Same-producer (Drouhin-Laroze) eliminates stylistic noise from different winemaking philosophies — differences are purely terroir- and classification-driven. Vintage variation (2023/2022/2021/2020 — youngest to oldest for lowest to highest tier) adds a subtle maturity dimension: the Grand Cru is the oldest and most evolved, the Regional the youngest and most primary.

## Reasoning trace

- **Stem signals:** "Same single grape variety," "same broad region," "ascending order of quality within a legally defined classification hierarchy." This is unambiguously F7 Hierarchy.
- **Universe:** The "legally defined classification hierarchy" with four ascending tiers points overwhelmingly to Burgundy (Regional → Village → 1er Cru → Grand Cru). The only other four-step system would be Germany's Pradikat levels, but those are whites. Bordeaux has a classification but not a four-tier ascending system in one region. Burgundy is STRONG SIGNAL.
- **In-glass:** Translucent ruby, red fruit, earth, high acid, fine tannin = Pinot Noir. Ascending concentration from 12.5% to 14% maps perfectly to Burgundy's hierarchy. The savoury, structured character says Cote de Nuits over Cote de Beaune.
- **Conclusion:** Pinot Noir, Burgundy, Bourgogne → Gevrey Village → Gevrey 1er Cru → Gevrey Grand Cru. Very high confidence.
