Distinguishing from alternatives: Chardonnay (excluded -- lacks the specific waxy, pear-skin phenolic grip; Chardonnay is more neutral at base and shows more lees/MLF malleability); Riesling (excluded -- no petrol, no electric acidity, alcohol too high in Wines 1, 3, 4); Viognier (excluded -- no apricot blossom intensity, no heavy phenolic weight); Chenin Blanc (excluded -- Chenin shows lanolin/quince and a more angular acid architecture). ### Part c) Method of production (4 x 7 marks)

**Wine 1 (Alsace Grand Cru, Weinbach):**

Vineyard: Altenbourg vineyard below Grand Cru Furstentum, biodynamic viticulture (Domaine Weinbach certified). Late harvest with significant botrytis selection. Fermentation: gentle, progressive pressing of whole clusters; long, slow fermentation in old oak foudres (large-format traditional Alsatian vessels), likely with indigenous yeast. The slow fermentation in foudre builds textural complexity and contributes the smoky, nutty character. Post-fermentation: extended lees contact in foudre (6--12 months). No MLF -- the preservation of acidity is essential to balance the RS. The RS (likely 15--35 g/l) is a consequence of deliberately arrested fermentation rather than sweetening; the low-intervention approach allows the wine to find its natural balance point. Oak influence is subtle -- the foudres are old and neutral, contributing texture rather than flavour. The wine's deep colour and viscosity confirm the concentration achieved through late harvest and possibly partial botrytis.

**Wine 2 (Alto Adige, Zemmer):**

Vineyard: high-elevation sites in Alto Adige (600--700m), hand-harvested. Early harvest to preserve acidity and aromatic freshness. Fermentation: stainless steel at controlled cool temperatures (15--17C typical), cultured yeast selected for aromatic preservation. This is a zero-intervention white winemaking model: no oak, no MLF, no lees stirring, no skin contact. The winemaking philosophy is entirely about fruit protection and terroir expression through restraint. Cold stabilisation and gentle filtration before bottling. The stainless steel and temperature control produce the clean, bright, mineral character visible in the glass. The wine's pale colour and crisp acidity confirm minimal oxidative contact throughout production.

**Wine 3 (Oregon, Adelsheim):**

Vineyard: Willamette Valley estate vineyards, LIVE-certified sustainable. Fermentation: mixed method -- approximately 25--30% barrel-fermented in French oak (low new-oak percentage) with the remainder in stainless steel. This partial-oak approach is characteristic of premium Oregon Pinot Gris and gives the wine its textural richness without oak dominance. Possible partial MLF to soften the mid-palate (the creamy texture suggests some MLF activity). Lees contact for several months in both barrel and tank portions before blending. The blending of tank and barrel portions is the key winemaking decision: it creates a wine with more weight and complexity than pure stainless steel but less overt oak than full barrel fermentation. Bottled dry with balanced acidity.

**Wine 4 (Marlborough, Nautilus):**

Vineyard: Marlborough estate fruit. Fermentation: stainless steel at cool temperatures, with reductive handling throughout (inert gas blanketing, minimal oxygen contact). The reductive approach preserves the variety's aromatic freshness and the ginger/baking-spice notes that are Pinot Gris's varietal markers. No oak, no MLF. Brief lees contact for palate texture. Early bottling to capture primary fruit character. The lifted aromatic intensity and the pale-gold/copper tint are consistent with careful reductive handling of Pinot Gris -- the slight copper colour is a varietal characteristic (Pinot Gris has grey-pink skins that can impart a faint copper tone even without skin contact). The wine is a fruit-forward, technically clean expression designed for immediate enjoyment.

### Part b) Natural factors vs human factors (3 x 5 marks)

**Wine 8 (Albarino, Pazo de Senorans, Rias Baixas):**

**Natural factors dominate.** The defining character of this wine -- searing acidity, saline minerality, citrus freshness -- derives overwhelmingly from Rias Baixas' cool Atlantic maritime climate and granite soils. The cool, humid conditions (average July temperatures 18--20C) preserve acidity naturally; the proximity to the Atlantic delivers the saline quality; the poor, sandy granite soils stress the old vines (45+ years) into producing small, concentrated berries. The winemaker's primary intervention is the decision to extend lees ageing (36 months), which adds complexity but does not alter the fundamental terroir-driven character. The wine is fermented in stainless steel -- no oak, no MLF, no barrel influence. The human contribution is essentially custodial: careful fruit selection, temperature-controlled fermentation, and the patience to age on lees. Nature writes the story; the winemaker edits it.

**Wine 9 (Verdejo, Jose Pariente, Rueda):**

**Human factors play a greater role.** While Rueda's extreme continental climate (hot summers, cold winters at 700--800m altitude) and limestone soils provide the raw material, the winemaker's decisions are more consequential here. The night harvest (a conscious human choice to preserve freshness in a warm climate), the use of concrete egg vessels and 500-litre foudres for fermentation and ageing (building texture and micro-oxygenation), the 11-month lees ageing, and the blending decisions all shape the wine's character significantly. Without these interventions, Verdejo from Rueda would tend toward the flat, broad, oxidation-prone style that characterised the region before the modern winemaking revolution of the 1980s--90s. Jose Pariente's innovation -- particularly the concrete egg introduced in 2008 -- represents a deliberate human choice to transform Verdejo into a premium variety.

**Wine 10 (Godello, Rafael Palacios, Valdeorras):**

**Both factors are in creative tension.** The terroir of Valdeorras -- terraced slate and schist slopes at 600m+, inland Galician climate with hot days and cool nights -- provides a raw material of genuine potential: concentrated fruit, mineral complexity, natural acidity. But the transformation of Godello from a forgotten, oxidised bulk variety into a premium wine capable of rivalling fine white Burgundy is almost entirely a human achievement. Rafael Palacios' decisions -- barrel fermentation in specific-size French oak, four months of lees contact, biodynamic viticulture, long skin contact, low yields from old terraced vines -- are what create the textural complexity visible in the glass. Without the terroir, the wine would lack its mineral backbone; without the winemaker, the terroir would produce unremarkable wine. Of the three wines in this flight, Wine 10 most clearly demonstrates the interdependence of natural and human factors.

### Part c) Commercial position and market opportunities (5 marks for all three)

These three wines collectively represent Spain's emerging white wine identity -- a sector gaining rapid momentum as the global market seeks alternatives to Burgundy, Loire, and Italian whites.

**Wine 8 (Albarino, Rias Baixas):** The most commercially established of the three. Albarino from Rias Baixas has strong global brand recognition, particularly in the UK, USA, and Northern European markets.
