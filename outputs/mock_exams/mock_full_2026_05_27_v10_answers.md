**Wines:**

2. Savennieres "Bellerive", Chateau de Chamboureau, 2022. Loire Valley, France. (13%)
3. Vouvray Sec "Cuvee Silex", Francois Pinon, 2024. Loire Valley, France. (13%)
4. Cour-Cheverny "Les Fougerons", Francois Cazin, 2023. Loire Valley, France. (12.5%)

### a) Region of origin

**Loire Valley, France.**

Four different single white grape varieties from one French region points strongly toward the Loire, the only French region that regularly showcases four or more distinct white varieties at this quality level. The spread of alcohol levels (12--13%), the uniformly high acidity across all four wines, and the stylistic range from lean and saline (Wine 1) to structured and phenolic (Wine 2) to aromatic and mineral (Wines 3 and 4) is characteristic of the Loire's diverse terroir and varietal palette. No other French region -- not Alsace (dominated by Germanic aromatics), not Burgundy (Chardonnay-centric), not the Rhone (blend-oriented) -- deploys four distinct single-variety whites with this combination of freshness, moderate alcohol, and individuality.

### b) Grape varieties

**Wine 1: Melon de Bourgogne (Muscadet)**
Pale lemon, lean, saline, with lemon-lime citrus, green herbs, and a chalky mineral finish. The 12% alcohol, absence of oak character, and pronounced yeasty breadth from extended lees contact point unmistakably to Muscadet from the Pays Nantais. The textural grip and salt-brushed finish from sur lie ageing distinguish this from a simple Sauvignon Blanc.

**Wine 2: Chenin Blanc**
Medium lemon with golden tints. Quince, beeswax, lemon oil, and a pronounced phenolic grip on the finish. High acidity with a structured, almost austere dry palate at 13%. This is classic Savennieres Chenin Blanc -- the combination of waxy texture, schist-driven minerality, and austere dryness rules out Vouvray (typically softer) and points to the Anjou sub-region. Considered Chardonnay briefly but the lanolin/wax character and phenolic finish eliminate it.

**Wine 3: Chenin Blanc (Vouvray)**
Pale to medium lemon. Green apple, sherbet citrus, quince, and a flinty mineral core. Dry with high acidity, some residual textural richness from lees ageing in old oak. The 13% alcohol and the combination of fruit purity with chalky tension is classic dry Vouvray Chenin. Distinguished from Wine 2 by its brighter fruit profile, less phenolic grip, and the flint-driven minerality characteristic of Vouvray's silex soils rather than Savennieres's schist.

**Wine 4: Romorantin**
Pale gold. Honeysuckle, apricot, quince paste, and white pepper with an underlying smoky minerality. High acidity with a slightly bitter, textured finish. The 12.5% alcohol and the unusual combination of exotic fruit with angular acidity and bitterness is diagnostic of Romorantin, the sole permitted variety of Cour-Cheverny AOC. This is the curveball wine -- considered Chenin Blanc and Pinot Gris briefly, but the bitter mineral finish, smoke character, and distinctive quince-apricot profile narrow to Romorantin. This variety exists virtually nowhere else in the world.

### c) Style, quality, and winemaking

**Wine 1 (Muscadet / Melon de Bourgogne)**
*Style:* Dry, lean, mineral-driven, saline. A serious expression of Muscadet that transcends the appellation's commercial image.
*Quality:* Very good for the appellation -- comparable to top-tier Muscadet Sevre-et-Maine single-cru level. Concentrated and austere rather than simple and fruity. Retail ~EUR15--25. Outstanding for its category.
*Winemaking:* Hand-harvested, whole-bunch pressed, fermented with indigenous yeasts in stainless steel. Extended sur lie ageing in glass-lined cement tanks (8+ months) providing yeasty breadth and textural complexity without oak influence. No malolactic fermentation, preserving razor-sharp acidity. Bottled with minimal SO2. The extended lees contact on old-vine fruit from granite soils gives atypical concentration for Muscadet.

**Wine 2 (Chenin Blanc / Savennieres)**
*Style:* Dry, powerful, austere, with significant ageing potential. This is Chenin at its most structured and intellectual.
*Quality:* Very good to outstanding -- a quality reference for the Savennieres appellation. Comparable to mid-tier Savennieres, below the pinnacle of Coulee de Serrant but well above generic Anjou Blanc. Retail ~EUR20--35.
*Winemaking:* Whole-cluster pressed, fermented with native yeasts. Likely a combination of stainless steel and old oak (no obvious new oak character). No or very limited malolactic fermentation, preserving the acidity framework. The phenolic grip suggests some extended skin contact during pressing. Aged on lees for several months. Schist soils contribute the mineral spine.

**Wine 3 (Chenin Blanc / Vouvray Sec)**
*Style:* Dry with a touch of residual richness (though technically sec), bright, mineral, and elegant.
*Quality:* Very good -- a quality-focused Vouvray Sec from a top grower. Single-vineyard level. Retail ~EUR15--25.
*Winemaking:* Hand-harvested, direct whole-bunch pressing, spontaneous fermentation with native yeasts at cool temperatures (long, slow 2--3 month ferment). Aged on fine lees for 4--5 months in old large-format oak (demi-muids and foudres) which adds textural complexity without oak flavour. No chaptalization. Minimal SO2. The silex (flint) soils of the vineyard impart the flinty mineral character. No malolactic fermentation.

**Wine 4 (Romorantin / Cour-Cheverny)**
*Style:* Dry, mineral, angular, with exotic aromatics belying a taut palate structure.
*Quality:* Very good for the appellation -- among the top expressions of Romorantin from one of the AOC's leading producers. Specialist quality, retail ~EUR12--20.
*Winemaking:* Hand-harvested, gently pressed, fermented in stainless steel and/or neutral oak with indigenous yeasts. No new oak. No malolactic. The naturally high acidity of Romorantin is preserved, with some lees ageing adding textural roundness. The smoky, honeyed complexity comes from the grape's inherent character rather than winemaking manipulation. Low-intervention approach.

### d) Commercial positioning and global marketplace importance

These four wines represent the Loire Valley's remarkable diversity but occupy very different commercial positions.

**Wine 1 (Muscadet)** is the most commercially significant by volume. Muscadet is a major Loire export category, sold through both on-trade (by-the-glass, seafood restaurants) and off-trade (supermarket and specialist). However, premium single-cru Muscadet like Clos des Briords occupies a niche within this: specialist wine shops, quality-focused on-trade, and educated export markets (UK, USA, Benelux, Scandinavia). Its challenge is the perception gap -- Muscadet's cheap image depresses willingness to pay EUR15+ despite the quality. Opportunity exists in the natural/minimal-intervention movement where low-alcohol, terroir-driven whites are increasingly valued.

**Wine 2 (Savennieres)** is a specialist wine with limited production (~300ha total appellation). Sold through specialist merchants and fine wine on-trade. Key markets: France (domestic), UK, USA, northern Europe. Its challenge is its austerity in youth -- it requires either ageing or an educated consumer. Competes with Chablis and white Burgundy at similar price points but with far less name recognition. Opportunity: growing interest in alternative premium whites and Chenin Blanc's rising global profile (boosted by South African Chenin).

**Wine 3 (Vouvray Sec)** has moderate commercial importance. Vouvray has better name recognition than Savennieres but is complicated by the appellation's wide sweetness spectrum (sec to moelleux), which confuses consumers. This producer-focused, artisanal style targets specialist merchants and sommelier-driven restaurant lists. Key markets: France, USA, UK, Japan. Price point (~EUR15--25) competes with entry-level Chablis and Sancerre.

**Wine 4 (Cour-Cheverny)** is a micro-niche: 71 hectares, 24 producers. Near-zero global brand recognition. Sold almost exclusively through specialist channels to curious wine enthusiasts and sommeliers seeking discovery. Its rarity is both its opportunity (unique selling point, story-driven hand-sell) and challenge (no marketing budget, consumer unfamiliarity). Export markets limited to specialist importers in USA, UK, and Benelux.

Collectively, these wines demonstrate the Loire's strength as France's most versatile white wine region, but none individually commands the global market power of Burgundy, Bordeaux Blanc, or even Alsace. Their importance is disproportionately high in the sommelier/specialist segment relative to their production volumes.

### c) Natural factors versus human inputs

This is one of wine's most compelling examples of natural factors dominating human inputs.

**Natural factors (dominant):**

*Climate:* The Hunter Valley's warm, humid, maritime-influenced climate is the fundamental shaping force. Warm days with afternoon cloud cover and sea breezes create a unique mesoclimate. The key paradox: despite warm temperatures, Semillon is picked early (before full phenolic ripeness) at low sugar levels, resulting in wines of just 10--11.5% alcohol. The climate's humidity encourages early harvesting to avoid botrytis pressure, which inadvertently produces the lean, low-alcohol style that defines the region.

*Soil:* The volcanic-origin soils of the Hunter Valley (particularly around Pokolbin and Brokenback) contribute mineral complexity and drainage. The sandy loam over clay subsoils moderate vigour and contribute to the wines' mineral spine.

*Grape variety:* Semillon's thin skin and susceptibility to botrytis makes early picking essential in the Hunter's humid climate. The variety's capacity to develop extraordinary complexity through bottle ageing -- without oak -- is an inherent genetic characteristic. No other variety transforms as dramatically in bottle from austerity to richness while maintaining structural integrity at such low alcohol levels.

*Vintage variation:* The Hunter's variable rainfall and storm patterns create significant vintage variation. Drier years (like 2014, 2017) produce more concentrated, longer-lived wines; wet years can compromise quality entirely.

**Human inputs (supportive but deliberately minimal):**

*Harvesting decisions:* The single most critical human decision is when to pick. Picking early (at 10--11 Baume) preserves acidity and produces the classic lean style. Picking later produces richer but less age-worthy wines. This timing decision is the primary human shaping force.

*Reductive winemaking:* The winemaking is deliberately non-interventionist: immediate crushing, cold-temperature fermentation in stainless steel, no oak, no malolactic fermentation, early bottling under screwcap. The human input is essentially the decision NOT to intervene. This negative space -- the absence of oak, MLF, lees stirring, and extended ageing -- is what allows the natural character to express itself.

*Cellaring and museum release:* The decision to hold wines for extended bottle ageing before release (Keith Tulloch's Museum Release programme) is a significant human input that determines when the consumer experiences the wine. However, the complexity that develops during this ageing is a natural (not human-driven) process.

**Balance assessment:** Natural factors are overwhelmingly dominant. The winemaker's role is essentially curatorial -- selecting the harvest date and then stepping back to let the variety, climate, and bottle ageing do the work. The absence of interventionist winemaking techniques (oak, MLF, batonnage) is itself the defining human choice, but it is a choice of restraint rather than manipulation. Compare this with heavily oaked, MLF-driven, lees-stirred Chardonnay from the same region, where human inputs fundamentally reshape the wine's character. Hunter Semillon is one of the wine world's purest expressions of natural factors working in concert with minimal human interference.

### a) Identify the category of fortified wine and the country of origin. (15 marks)

Port. Portugal (Douro Valley).

The three wines are all Port, identified by the combination of spirit warmth at approximately 20% ABV, residual sweetness (approximately 90-115 g/l RS), and the characteristic dark fruit intensity married to grape-spirit fortification. All three show the hallmarks of Port production: fermentation of red grape varieties (Touriga Nacional, Touriga Franca, Tinta Roriz, and others) halted partway through by the addition of aguardente (grape spirit at 77% ABV), which kills the yeast and preserves approximately half the grape sugar as residual sweetness.

The country is unambiguously Portugal: the Douro Valley is the only legally permitted region for Port production. The combination of grape spirit warmth, characteristic dried-fruit and floral complexity (violets, dark chocolate, cassis), and the high tannin-sweetness-acid framework is distinct from other fortified wines. Sherry (Spain) is eliminated by the sweetness and fruit profile; Madeira (Portugal but distinct) is eliminated by the absence of volatile acidity, caramelized lift, and the characteristic searing acidity of Madeira. Australian fortified Shiraz is eliminated by the higher tannin grip, drier fruit profile, and absence of the rancio/raisined character typical of Rutherglen.

The three wines represent three different Port styles spanning the ruby-to-tawny spectrum: one youthful ruby-family wine (LBV), one oxidatively aged single-vintage tawny (Colheita), and one premium ruby-family wine intended for long bottle-ageing (Vintage/Vintage Port).

### b) Identify the specific style, quality designation, and method of production and ageing as precisely as possible. (3 x 12 marks)

**Wine 10 -- Late Bottled Vintage (LBV) Port**

*Style and designation:* Late Bottled Vintage Port. This is a single-vintage Port from a declared year (2019), bottled between four and six years after the vintage. LBV occupies the quality tier between Ruby Reserve and Vintage Port -- it offers Vintage Port character at a more accessible price point and earlier drinking window.

*Method of production and ageing:* Grapes from old vines are hand-harvested, sorted, lightly crushed, and transferred to traditional stone lagares where they are foot-trodden to extract colour, tannin, and flavour during a short, intense maceration of 2-3 days. Fermentation is halted partway through by the addition of aguardente (grape spirit at 77% ABV) at a ratio of approximately 1 part spirit to 4 parts must, raising alcohol to approximately 20% and preserving natural grape sugars (~90-100 g/l RS).

The wine is then aged in large oak vats (approximately 9,000 litres at Quinta do Crasto) for approximately four years before bottling. Importantly, this LBV is unfiltered (the producer confirms no fining or filtration), meaning it retains a deposit and will continue to develop in bottle. This "traditional" unfiltered LBV style contrasts with most commercial LBVs, which are fined and filtered for immediate consumption.

*Quality:* Good to very good -- a well-made, concentrated LBV showing dark berry fruit, chocolate, gum cistus, and firm tannin. It is accessible now but will reward 5-10 years of additional bottle age. Price band approximately EUR 15-22.

**Wine 11 -- Colheita Tawny Port (2010, bottled 2023)**

*Style and designation:* Colheita Port. This is a single-harvest Tawny Port -- all grapes from the 2010 vintage, aged exclusively in wood (small oak casks of 550-600 litres, known as pipes) for a minimum of seven years. The 2010 Colheita aged approximately 13 years in wood before bottling in 2023. Colheita is a distinct legal category from aged Tawny (10/20/30/40-year designations, which are blends); Colheita must state both the harvest year and bottling date.

*Method of production and ageing:* Initial vinification follows the standard Port method: fermentation halted by aguardente addition. The key distinction is the ageing regime. Wines selected for their fruit structure and tannin framework are transferred to small oak casks (pipes) and aged in above-ground lodges, where they undergo gradual oxidative ageing. Over 13 years in wood, the wine loses colour (from deep ruby to tawny amber), gains oxidative complexity (dried fruit, caramel, nuts, spice), and concentrates through slow evaporation. The wine is tasted regularly and moved between casks (the racking process) to manage oxidation. It is bottled ready to drink, with no further ageing required.

*Quality:* Very good to outstanding. 13 years of careful oxidative ageing from Niepoort -- one of the most quality-obsessed houses in Port -- has produced a Colheita with complex nutty, dried-fruit character, caramel, toffee, and a long, elegant finish. The balance between fruit, acidity, sweetness, and oxidative complexity is excellent. Comparable in quality to a good 20-Year Tawny but with single-vintage identity. Price band approximately EUR 35-55.

**Wine 12 -- Vintage Port (Vintage/Vintage Port, 2017)**

*Style and designation:* Vintage Port (also known as Single Quinta Vintage Port). This is from a declared vintage year (2017), representing the pinnacle of the ruby-style Port hierarchy. Vintage Port is made only in exceptional years and is intended for long bottle ageing. The 2017 vintage was widely declared across the Douro.

*Method of production and ageing:* Grapes from a single estate (Quinta do Vesuvio) are hand-harvested and foot-trodden in traditional granite lagares -- the Symington family (owners) deliberately retained this traditional method for its superior extraction. The fermentation is halted by aguardente addition when approximately half the sugar remains. The key difference from LBV is the ageing trajectory: Vintage Port spends only approximately 18-24 months in seasoned oak vats (to allow the wine to settle and clarify) before being bottled unfiltered and unfined. The wine is intended to undergo decades of reductive ageing in bottle, during which it develops extraordinary complexity, throws a heavy sediment, and requires decanting.

The 2017 blend includes 33% Touriga Nacional, 35% Touriga Franca, 15% Sousao, and 12% Alicante Bouschet. The RS is approximately 115 g/l. The wine has tremendous concentration, dense tannin, and the structure for very long ageing.

*Quality:* Outstanding. Quinta do Vesuvio is one of the greatest single-quinta estates in the Douro, and the 2017 vintage is considered excellent. The wine shows the hallmarks of top-tier Vintage Port: intense dark fruit (cassis, blackberry, fig), floral lift (violets, rockrose), spice (ginger, mint), and a massively structured but balanced palate with the characteristic schist-derived tannins of the Upper Douro. This will need 15-20 years before approaching its peak drinking window and will hold for 40-60 years. Price band approximately EUR 50-80.

### c) Rank the wines in quality order and justify your ranking with reference to the applicable classification or designation. (15 marks)

**Ascending quality order: Wine 10 (LBV) < Wine 11 (Colheita) < Wine 12 (Vintage Port)**

The Port classification hierarchy places these wines in a clear ascending quality ladder:

**Wine 10 -- LBV Port (lowest of the three)**
LBV is positioned between Ruby Reserve and Vintage Port in the Port quality hierarchy.
