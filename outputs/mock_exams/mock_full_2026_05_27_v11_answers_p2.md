## Question 1

### Model answer

#### a) Grape variety

Cabernet Sauvignon, wholly or principally. The shared cassis/blackcurrant core, firm fine-grained tannin, cedar/graphite register and medium-plus to high alcohol point to the Cabernet family. Merlot would give softer plum flesh and lower structural severity; Syrah/Shiraz would show more pepper, violet and smoked meat. Wine 4 carries Shiraz warmth and spice, but the cassis frame and firm Cabernet spine keep the principal variety as Cabernet Sauvignon.

#### b) Origin

**Wine 1 -- Pauillac, Bordeaux, France.** Cedar, graphite, blackcurrant, gravelly tannin and 14% alcohol point to mature left-bank Bordeaux. The tannin is firmer and more linear than Margaux or St-Julien, making Pauillac the best call, at classified growth level.

**Wine 2 -- Napa Valley, California, USA.** Riper cassis, plush density, sweet oak polish and 14.5% alcohol move this away from Bordeaux. Coonawarra would show more eucalyptus and a cooler frame; the concentration and gloss point to Napa Valley.

**Wine 3 -- Puente Alto/Maipo Valley, Chile.** Ripe cassis, mint/eucalyptus, polished tannin and 14.5% alcohol indicate a warm New World Cabernet, but the savoury freshness and Andean graphite note fit Maipo more than Napa.

**Wine 4 -- South Australia, Australia.** The blackcurrant structure with blackberry, pepper and sweet spice suggests a Cabernet-Shiraz blend. The warmth, 14.5% alcohol and broad palate point to South Australia rather than Bordeaux or Chile.

#### c) Style, quality and maturity

**Wine 1** is excellent quality, equivalent to Pauillac classified growth. It has concentration, savoury complexity, integrated oak and long tannic persistence. From a warm recent vintage, it is still youthful; improve 10-15 years and hold 25+.

**Wine 2** is excellent Napa Cabernet, premium estate level, with dense fruit, polished oak and ripe tannins. It is youthful and primary; improve 8-12 years, then hold a further decade.

**Wine 3** is outstanding Chilean Cabernet, equivalent to the top icon tier, with ripe fruit balanced by freshness and graphite complexity. Young but approachable; improve 8-12 years and hold 20.

**Wine 4** is very good to excellent premium Australian Cabernet-Shiraz, below the rare icon tier but above regional commercial blends. Youthful, fruit-led and oak-framed; drink now to 10 years, holding a further 5.

#### d) Commercial position

These sit in different fine-wine lanes. Wine 1 sells through allocations, independent specialists and restaurant lists as a Bordeaux benchmark, competing with other left-bank classified growths at roughly $150-300+. Wine 2 is luxury New World Cabernet, strong in US direct-to-consumer, specialist retail and steakhouse/fine-dining lists, competing with top Napa and cult-adjacent Cabernet at about $100-200. Wine 3 offers top-tier Chilean Cabernet with global critic recognition and stronger value versus Napa or Bordeaux, usually $90-150 through fine-wine merchants. Wine 4 is premium Australian branded fine wine, more broadly distributed, with a strong domestic base and export appeal in the UK, Asia and North America, around $60-100.

### Proposed annotation

Same dominant-variety cross-origin question. The stem points strongly to Cabernet Sauvignon because the four-country spread supports Bordeaux, Napa, Chile and Australia, with one Cabernet-Shiraz variant to test whether candidates stay with principal variety rather than flipping to Syrah. Marks should reward structural Cabernet evidence, origin precision, and differentiated commercial positioning.

## Question 2

### Model answer

#### a) Country, region and grape variety

Spain, Rioja, Tempranillo-led reds. The shared red cherry, dried herb, vanilla/coconut oak, savoury leather development and medium-plus alcohol point to Rioja rather than Ribera del Duero, which would usually be darker, firmer and more powerful. Garnacha-led Spain would be warmer and softer; Sangiovese would have more sour cherry and Italian herbal austerity.

#### b) Ageing designations

**Wine 5 -- Crianza.** The fruit is still relatively primary, with red cherry, light vanilla and moderate oak integration. It has enough bottle age to soften but not the developed leather, spice and tertiary complexity of Reserva or Gran Reserva.

**Wine 6 -- Reserva.** More concentration, smoother oak integration, savoury spice and beginning leather notes place this above Crianza. It lacks the extended tertiary complexity and silky maturity of Gran Reserva.

**Wine 7 -- Gran Reserva.** Garnet rim, dried strawberry, leather, tobacco, vanilla, coconut and long savoury finish indicate extended barrel and bottle ageing. This is the most mature and complex wine.

#### c) Style, quality and maturity

**Wine 5** is good quality Rioja Crianza, mid-market bodega level around $15-25. Medium body, moderate tannin, clear red fruit and visible oak make it drink-now, with little need for ageing; hold 3-5 years.

**Wine 6** is very good quality Rioja Reserva, traditional premium level around $25-45. The balance of ripe fruit, oak spice and savoury development gives more complexity and length. Ready now but will improve 3-5 years and hold 8-10.

**Wine 7** is excellent quality Rioja Gran Reserva, top traditional house level around $70-120. Long, layered, tertiary and oak-integrated, it is at early maturity. Drink now or over 10-15 years; it will gain more tobacco, dried fruit and forest floor.

#### d) Maturation choices and commercial positioning

Maturation is the commercial ladder. Crianza uses enough oak and bottle time to signal Rioja typicity while keeping fresh fruit and accessible pricing for supermarkets, casual on-trade and by-the-glass. Reserva shifts toward independent retail and restaurant lists: more barrel and bottle age justify a higher price and a more traditional consumer. Gran Reserva is a deliberate fine-wine proposition: late release, tertiary complexity and extended cellar cost create scarcity and prestige, competing with mature Bordeaux, Brunello and classic Rioja collectors' wines. The challenge is cash tied up in stock; the opportunity is that Rioja can sell mature, ready-to-drink complexity at lower prices than many equivalent French classics.

### Proposed annotation

Formal Rioja ageing ladder. The key is not producer recognition but legal ageing designation: Crianza, Reserva, Gran Reserva. Strong answers should connect visible maturity, oak integration and tertiary development to both quality and commercial position.

### Reasoning trace

- Stem signals: same country and region, same grape variety, three different formal ageing designations.
- Universe: Rioja is the cleanest red-wine region with three familiar formal ageing designations in MW Paper 2.
- Rule-outs: Ribera del Duero has ageing terms but less classic three-step exam identity; Portugal/Italy/France do not use this exact Spanish Crianza/Reserva/Gran Reserva ladder.
- Conclusion: Spain, Rioja, Tempranillo-led wines, ordered Crianza -> Reserva -> Gran Reserva.

## Question 3

### Model answer

#### a) Identity

**Wine 8 -- Douro, Portugal, Touriga Nacional-led blend.** Deep colour, black fruit, violet, schist-like grip, firm tannin and 14.5% alcohol point to a dry Douro red rather than Port or Spanish Tempranillo.

**Wine 9 -- Swartland, South Africa, Syrah-led/Rhone-style blend.** Dark fruit, pepper, savoury olive, whole-bunch-like spice and firm but fresh tannin suggest Syrah-based Swartland rather than Barossa Shiraz.

**Wine 10 -- Mendoza, Argentina, Malbec.** Purple colour, plush plum/black cherry, violet, smooth tannin and 13.5% alcohol point clearly to Mendoza Malbec.

**Wine 11 -- Naoussa, Greece, Xinomavro.** Pale-to-medium garnet, high acid, firm drying tannin, tomato leaf, dried herbs and red fruit point to Xinomavro rather than Nebbiolo.

**Wine 12 -- Morgon, Beaujolais, France, Gamay.** Translucent ruby, juicy red fruit, floral lift, high acidity, low tannin and a mineral/granitic grip indicate cru Beaujolais, specifically Morgon.

#### b) Quality and development

**Wine 8** is excellent quality Douro DOC, top estate level, with depth, tannic architecture and length. Youthful; improve 8-12 years and hold 15+.

**Wine 9** is excellent quality premium Swartland, comparable to top old-vine/Rhone-blend benchmarks. Young, structured and savoury; improve 8-10 years and hold 15.

**Wine 10** is good to very good quality Mendoza Malbec, regional premium but not icon tier. Youthful and fruit-driven; drink now to 5 years.

**Wine 11** is very good to excellent Naoussa Xinomavro, serious appellation level. Still youthful and tannic; improve 5-8 years and hold 10-12.

**Wine 12** is very good to excellent cru Beaujolais, Morgon lieu-dit level. Young but expressive; improve 3-6 years and hold 8-10.

#### c) Structure, winemaking and reputation in commercial position

Wine 8 is sold on structure: tannin, depth and ageability let dry Douro compete with premium Iberian and Bordeaux blends, but market reputation still trails Port and the top French regions. Wine 9 relies on both structure and low-intervention winemaking signals: savoury restraint, freshness and texture appeal to specialist merchants and restaurants, while South African fine-wine reputation is rising from a lower price base. Wine 10 is reputation-led at a broader commercial level: Mendoza Malbec is easy to understand globally, with plush fruit and moderate tannin suiting premium supermarkets and casual on-trade. Wine 11 is structurally serious but commercially niche; high acid and tannin appeal to sommeliers, but Greek origin and Xinomavro need hand-selling. Wine 12 has strong regional reputation among specialists; lighter structure limits mass-market positioning, while cru identity, freshness and drinkability support independent retail and restaurant lists.

Breadth flight of five different countries and five different grapes/blends. The intended skill is anti-overlinking: identify each wine independently, then compare how commercial position is driven by structure, winemaking choices and market reputation rather than fame alone.

- Conclusion: Portugal/Douro blend; South Africa/Swartland Syrah-led blend; Argentina/Malbec; Greece/Xinomavro; France/Morgon Gamay.
