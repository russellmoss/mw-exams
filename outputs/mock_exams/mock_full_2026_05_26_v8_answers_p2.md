### a) Identify the grape variety. (16 marks)

**Grenache / Garnacha**

The structural fingerprint common to all four wines points decisively to Grenache. The shared profile is: elevated alcohol (13.5–14.5%), moderate-to-low acidity, soft to moderate fine-grained tannin, and a fruit expression ranging from kirsch and dried strawberry through to garrigue and meat. Grenache is characteristically low in natural acidity and tannin yet accumulates sugar rapidly, producing wines of density and warmth without the structural weight of Syrah or the tannic grip of Cabernet Sauvignon. The wines span France, Spain, Australia, and Italy, consistent with the global reach of this variety under its regional synonyms (Garnacha in Spain, Cannonau in Sardinia). Alternative varieties with warm-climate weight at 14–14.5% — Mourvèdre, Monastrell, Zinfandel — were considered and ruled out: Mourvèdre shows darker fruit and more savage tannin; Zinfandel brings higher volatile acidity and jammy fruit at this alcohol; Monastrell is denser and more extracted. ### d) Compare and contrast the commercial prospects for this variety in each country represented. (20 marks)

Grenache's commercial trajectory differs markedly across these four countries, reflecting contrasting levels of consumer awareness, production philosophy, and export infrastructure.

**France (Wine 1):** Châteauneuf-du-Pape is the commercially established benchmark for Grenache globally. Campo de Borja, Calatayud, Cariñena, and Priorat all play in this space, with Priorat commanding €80–200+ for its finest wines and the other DOs offering value alternatives at €15–50. US, UK, and Nordic markets are the primary exports. The opportunity is in capitalizing on the value story relative to French equivalents; the challenge is differentiating sub-appellations in consumer minds.

**Australia (Wine 3):** Australian Grenache's commercial renaissance is newer but accelerating. McLaren Vale Grenache from producers like Yangarra, d'Arenberg, and Bekkers is finding serious on-trade listings and critic attention globally. The key challenge is overcoming Australian wine's persistent value-rather-than-fine-wine commercial positioning in major markets; the opportunity is in the old-vine story and the sustainability/low-intervention narrative that resonates with younger on-trade buyers.

**Italy (Wine 4):** Cannonau di Sardegna has intermittent export visibility driven by two narratives: the longevity/health story (the Blue Zone association with Sardinian centenarian drinkers) and emerging fine-wine interest from natural producers. However, the DOC lacks globally recognized brand anchors beyond Sella & Mosca's volume tier. Export prices are modest (£12–25 for most Riserva). The opportunity lies in leveraging Sardinia's terroir uniqueness and the health narrative for differentiated positioning; the challenge is that the appellation spans the entire island without sub-regional identity, limiting premium pricing power. Specialist Italian off-trade and natural-wine channels are the realistic route to market.

However, Touriga Nacional as a variety name has limited consumer awareness outside specialist circles. The commercial hook for the Douro is typically the appellation name or the estate name (Crasto, Niepoort, Prats & Symington) rather than the variety. Within the fine-wine and specialist trade, Touriga Nacional single-varietal wines represent a growth opportunity — distinctive, age-worthy, competitive at €25–50 with equivalent Southern Rhône or Barolo. Export markets of highest relevance: UK, USA, Brazil (large Portuguese diaspora), Switzerland.

**Blaufränkisch / Mittelburgenland (Wine 10)** operates in a narrow commercial niche. Austria's total wine export volume is modest (~50–60 million litres, heavily Grüner Veltliner and Riesling), and red wine accounts for a small fraction of this. Blaufränkisch has achieved specialist recognition in the US (particularly the natural-wine and somm-led on-trade community), UK, Germany, and Scandinavia, but consumer awareness of the variety name remains low. The commercial story is compelling for those who know it — serious, age-worthy, mineral, food-compatible — but it requires on-trade advocacy and specialist distribution. Export pricing is strong for the quality (€20–60 for serious single-vineyard wines), but volume is structurally limited. The wine is best placed in sommelier-driven fine-dining lists, specialist wine bars, and importer-focused merchant lists rather than retail multiples.

**Tannat / Uruguay (Wine 11)** is the smallest commercial presence of the four globally, despite Uruguay being South America's fourth-largest wine producer. Tannat's structural challenge — extreme tannin — limits consumer accessibility and requires education to sell. However, the variety has an emerging narrative: the longest-lived of South America's signature reds, unique to Uruguay in the New World, with the authenticity story that resonates with premium-wine buyers seeking the unfamiliar. Pisano exports to the USA, UK, Canada, and Brazil (Uruguay's most important export markets). Retail at €15–25 positions this beneath Malbec's premium tier but above commodity South American red. The commercial opportunity is in the niche: somm-led menus, wine-curious consumers seeking South American depth beyond Argentina, and pairings with rich meat dishes (Uruguay's asado culture is a coherent export story). The challenge is that Tannat requires active selling — it will never be a self-selecting supermarket variety.
