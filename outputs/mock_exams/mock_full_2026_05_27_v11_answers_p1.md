# Mock Answers -- Paper 1 (Whites)

## Question 1

### Model answer

**a) Country:** Spain. The common thread is dry white wine with high-to-medium acidity, moderate alcohol and Mediterranean or Atlantic fruit, but with two clearly different Rioja expressions. France and Italy were considered, but the combination of Albarino-like salinity, Verdejo-like fennel and two Viura-led Rioja styles points most strongly to Spain.

**b) Region and variety**

**Wine 1:** Rias Baixas, Galicia; Albarino. The pale colour, high acid, citrus, peach skin, white flowers and saline finish point to an Atlantic, unoaked white. Muscadet was considered, but the riper stone fruit and 13% alcohol are more consistent with Albarino.

**Wine 2:** Rueda; Verdejo. The grapefruit, fennel, bay-leaf bitterness and dry medium-bodied palate point to Verdejo. Sauvignon Blanc is possible from the pungency, but the waxier texture and bitter almond finish are more Rueda.

**Wine 3:** Rioja Blanco; Viura-led, possibly with small Malvasia/Garnacha Blanca. The yellow apple, citrus, savoury wax and subtle nutty breadth point away from reductive aromatic whites and toward white Rioja. The 13.5% alcohol and dry, textured palate suggest a serious but not fully oxidative style.

**Wine 4:** Rioja Blanco Reserva; Viura-led. The deeper gold colour, dried apple, almond, vanilla-spice, wax and firm acidity indicate deliberate extended ageing in old oak and bottle. This is not oxidised through fault; it is a traditional dry, cask-aged white Rioja style.

**c) Method and style**

Wine 1 is a modern protective, stainless-steel Atlantic white, probably with some lees contact to give texture but no oak. Wine 2 is also protective and aromatic, fermented cool to preserve fennel/citrus Verdejo character, with the phenolic bitterness suggesting controlled skin or press fraction management. Wine 3 shows more oxygen and likely barrel or large-vessel handling, giving savoury texture without the old-Rioja depth of Wine 4. Wine 4 is the most human-input-led: fermentation and long maturation in old oak, then late release, producing nutty, waxy, oxidative complexity.

**d) Commercial position**

Wine 1 sits in premium specialist retail and seafood-led on-trade, around $25-40, strong in Spain, the UK, US and export markets seeking crisp coastal whites. Wine 2 is more volume-premium, $15-25, widely exportable as an accessible alternative to Sauvignon Blanc. Wine 3 is specialist/on-trade, $25-45, selling on Rioja brand recognition and food versatility. Wine 4 is niche fine-wine/sommelier territory, $60+, with strong appeal in Spain, the UK, US and Japan, but its oxidative profile is a challenge for mainstream consumers.

## Proposed annotation

Same-country Spain flight testing whether candidates can hold Spain open beyond Albarino. The intended route is Atlantic Galicia, inland aromatic Rueda, modern/classic Rioja Blanco, then traditional extended-aged Rioja Blanco. Production and commercial marks protect candidates who identify Spain but miss one region.

## Reasoning trace

- Stem signal: same country, different regions and dry-white production approaches.
- Universe: France is the default tree answer for same-country P1, but Spain is the deliberate curveball country in the P1 tree.
- Key unlock: Wines 3 and 4 both point to Rioja/Viura, with Wine 4 showing the traditional aged style.
- Wine 1 then becomes Albarino/Rias Baixas rather than Loire/Muscadet; Wine 2 becomes Verdejo/Rueda rather than Sauvignon Blanc.
- Research support used: near/direct briefs for Albarino/Rias Baixas, CVNE Monopole, Allende Rioja Blanco and Lopez de Heredia-style aged white Rioja.

## Question 2

### Model answer

**a) Identity:** Austria, Kamptal, Gruner Veltliner. The shared white pepper, green herb, citrus and dry medium-bodied structure point clearly to Gruner Veltliner. Riesling was considered because of the high acidity and mineral line, but the peppery, lentil-like savouriness and lack of petrol/lime purity rule it out. Both wines sit in Kamptal rather than Wachau: they have precision and loess-derived spice without Smaragd breadth.

**b) Quality ranking:** Wine 6 is higher quality than Wine 5. Wine 5 is a very good regional/estate Kamptal DAC level wine. It is pure, fresh and varietally clear, but the finish is moderate and the fruit is relatively direct. Wine 6 is Erste Lage equivalent: greater dry extract, more concentration, layered citrus, peach, white pepper and mineral length, with a more persistent, structured finish.

**c) Style, quality and commercial position**

**Wine 5:** Dry, unoaked, crisp-to-medium-bodied Gruner Veltliner for early drinking. Cool fermentation and protective handling are suggested by the clean citrus and green-herb profile; short lees contact may explain the slight palate breadth. Quality is very good at Kamptal DAC/estate level, retailing around $20-30. Commercially this works in premium supermarkets, independent retail and by-the-glass lists as a food-friendly alternative to Sauvignon Blanc or dry Riesling.

**Wine 6:** Dry, more concentrated single-vineyard Gruner Veltliner, with 13.5% alcohol, phenolic grip and a long pepper-mineral finish. No obvious new oak; the weight comes from ripeness, site and lees, not barrel flavour. Quality is excellent, Erste Lage/Premier Cru equivalent, retailing around $45-70 and suited to independent specialists, fine dining and collectors of Austrian whites. It should drink now but improve for 5-8 years, gaining honey, lentil and savoury spice.

## Proposed annotation

A clean Austrian hierarchy pair. The grape should be locked by white pepper and savoury herb rather than generic citrus. The examiner wants candidates to connect formal/regional quality level with concentration and length, not just say one wine is "better".

## Reasoning trace

- Stem signal: same country, same variety, different quality levels within one regional system.
- Likely P1 hierarchy candidates: Burgundy Chardonnay, German/Austrian Riesling, Loire Chenin, Austrian Gruner.
- In-glass unlock: white pepper + green herb + dry palate = Gruner Veltliner.
- Country/region: Austria, Kamptal. Wine 6's Ried Lamm/Erste Lage style is higher than the regional Terrassen style.
- Research support used: Schloss Gobelsburg Ried Lamm brief and regional Kamptal Gruner briefs.

## Question 3

### Model answer

**Wine 7:** Swartland, South Africa; Chenin Blanc-led old-vine white blend, likely with Grenache Blanc/Clairette/Viognier/Semillon support. The wax, quince, yellow apple, preserved lemon, dry extract and savoury texture point first to Chenin. The modest oak signature, old-vine concentration and 13.5% alcohol fit modern Swartland rather than Vouvray or Savennières. Quality is excellent, Cape fine-wine level, around $35-60, with complexity from old dry-farmed vines, natural fermentation and neutral vessels rather than obvious oak flavour.

**Wine 8:** Paso Robles, California; southern Rhone white blend, principally Roussanne/Grenache Blanc/Picpoul/Clairette/Viognier. The ripe peach, pear, honeysuckle, fennel, glycerol texture and moderate acidity point to a warm-climate Rhone-family blend. Chateauneuf-du-Pape Blanc was considered, but the more generous New World fruit and polished texture suggest California. Quality is very good to excellent, premium estate/Tablas-style level, around $45-65.

**Wine 9:** Cotes du Rhone Blanc, France; Grenache Blanc/Clairette/Viognier/Marsanne/Roussanne family. The stone fruit, white flowers, garrigue, moderate acidity and dry, rounded palate point to southern Rhone. It lacks the concentration and length for Chateauneuf-du-Pape Blanc, so regional Cotes du Rhone is the better quality call: good to very good, $15-25, reliable branded on-trade/off-trade.

**Natural factors vs human inputs:** Wine 7 is most nature-led: old vines, dry-farmed Swartland sites and naturally high Chenin acidity drive the style, with neutral cellar work preserving texture. Wine 8 balances nature and human input: warm Paso ripeness is shaped by Rhone-variety blending and controlled cellar polish. Wine 9 is the most commercial blend-led wine, where variety selection, large-scale blending and clean handling matter more than single-site expression.

## Proposed annotation

Three-country dry white blend question. Candidates should identify blend families rather than chase single varieties too narrowly. Wine 7 is Chenin/Swartland old-vine logic; Wines 8 and 9 are Rhone-family blends separated by New World ripeness and appellation/quality level.

## Reasoning trace

- Stem signal: dry white blends from different countries, with natural vs human-input comparison.
- Universe: Bordeaux Blanc, Rhone Blanc, Swartland old-vine Cape white, Rioja/Jura oxidative blends.
- Wine 7's wax/quince points to Chenin-led Swartland rather than Rhone.
- Wines 8 and 9 share Rhone-blend markers; origin and quality split them.
- Research support used: Swartland old-vine Chenin brief, Chateauneuf/Cotes du Rhone white blend briefs, and exam wine facts for the exact mock wines.

## Question 4

### Model answer

**a) Grape variety:** Riesling. All three show lime/citrus, very high acidity, no new oak, dry palate and mineral length. Chardonnay was considered for the concentration in Wine 10, but the absence of oak/MLF and the petrol-lime line rule it out.

**b) Origins**

**Wine 10:** Rheingau, Germany; Grosses Gewachs level, likely a top Kiedrich/Rheingau site. The dry 13% alcohol, firm extract, peach-lime fruit and long mineral finish point to German GG rather than Mosel Kabinett or Austrian Smaragd.

**Wine 11:** Alsace Grand Cru, France. The fuller dry palate, 13% alcohol, ripe citrus, spice, stone fruit and slight phenolic breadth fit Alsace Riesling. It is less austere than Rheingau and less lime-cordial pure than Australia.

**Wine 12:** Frankland River, Western Australia. The youthful pale colour, intense lime, blossom, piercing acidity and dry, linear palate point to Australian Riesling. It is less petrol-led than Clare/Eden and the slightly more textural, cool-fruited profile fits Great Southern/Frankland River.

**c) Quality, maturity and development**

**Wine 10:** Outstanding, Grosses Gewachs/Grand Cru equivalent, $70-120. Young at 2-3 years, already balanced but will improve 8-12 years, developing honey, petrol, smoke and deeper mineral complexity, then hold a further decade.

**Wine 11:** Excellent, Alsace Grand Cru equivalent, $50-90. At 4-5 years it is moving from primary citrus into spice and early petrol. Drink now, but it should improve 5-8 years and hold another 5-7, gaining wax, dried citrus and savoury complexity.

**Wine 12:** Very good to excellent, premium regional Australian Riesling, $25-45. Very young, 1-2 years, dominated by lime and acid. It will improve 6-10 years, developing toast, kerosene and preserved lemon, then hold well if screwcap and acid are intact.

**d) Global commercial position**

Premium dry Riesling remains a sommelier and specialist-retail category rather than a mainstream one. Germany owns the fine-wine GG/Pradikat reputation; Alsace offers powerful gastronomic dry styles; Australia gives high-quality, lower-priced dry Riesling with strong value. The opportunity is food compatibility, longevity and climate-change resilience in cool sites. The challenge is consumer confusion around sweetness and weaker broad-market demand versus Chardonnay or Sauvignon Blanc.

## Proposed annotation

Classic same-variety Riesling flight across three countries. The question rewards locking the grape from acid/lime/no-oak evidence, then separating dry GG Germany, Alsace Grand Cru and premium Australian Riesling by alcohol, texture, fruit profile and commercial ladder.

## Reasoning trace

- Stem signal: same single grape variety, three countries, premium dry white commercial discussion.
- Candidate set from tree: Chardonnay/Riesling strongest; Sauvignon Blanc plausible.
- In-glass unlock: lime/petrol/electric acid/no oak = Riesling.
- Origin split: 13% dry power and GG-like extract = Rheingau; broad dry spice = Alsace; youthful lime and piercing acidity = Frankland River/Great Southern.
- Research support used: Robert Weil Kiedrich GG, Alsace Riesling and dry Australian/Austrian Riesling comparison briefs, plus exam wine facts for Frankland Estate.
