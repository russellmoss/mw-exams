### Part c) Method of production (4 x 7 marks)

**Wine 1 (Alsace Grand Cru, Weinbach):**

Vineyard: Altenbourg vineyard below Grand Cru Furstentum, biodynamic viticulture (Domaine Weinbach certified). Late harvest with significant botrytis selection. Fermentation: gentle, progressive pressing of whole clusters; long, slow fermentation in old oak foudres (large-format traditional Alsatian vessels), likely with indigenous yeast. The slow fermentation in foudre builds textural complexity and contributes the smoky, nutty character. Post-fermentation: extended lees contact in foudre (6--12 months). No MLF -- the preservation of acidity is essential to balance the RS. The RS (likely 15--35 g/l) is a consequence of deliberately arrested fermentation rather than sweetening; the low-intervention approach allows the wine to find its natural balance point. Oak influence is subtle -- the foudres are old and neutral, contributing texture rather than flavour. The wine's deep colour and viscosity confirm the concentration achieved through late harvest and possibly partial botrytis.

**Wine 2 (Alto Adige, Zemmer):**

Vineyard: high-elevation sites in Alto Adige (600--700m), hand-harvested. Early harvest to preserve acidity and aromatic freshness. Fermentation: stainless steel at controlled cool temperatures (15--17C typical), cultured yeast selected for aromatic preservation. This is a zero-intervention white winemaking model: no oak, no MLF, no lees stirring, no skin contact. The winemaking philosophy is entirely about fruit protection and terroir expression through restraint. Cold stabilisation and gentle filtration before bottling. The stainless steel and temperature control produce the clean, bright, mineral character visible in the glass. The wine's pale colour and crisp acidity confirm minimal oxidative contact throughout production.

**Wine 3 (Oregon, Adelsheim):**

Vineyard: Willamette Valley estate vineyards, LIVE-certified sustainable. Fermentation: mixed method -- approximately 25--30% barrel-fermented in French oak (low new-oak percentage) with the remainder in stainless steel. This partial-oak approach is characteristic of premium Oregon Pinot Gris and gives the wine its textural richness without oak dominance. Possible partial MLF to soften the mid-palate (the creamy texture suggests some MLF activity). Lees contact for several months in both barrel and tank portions before blending. The blending of tank and barrel portions is the key winemaking decision: it creates a wine with more weight and complexity than pure stainless steel but less overt oak than full barrel fermentation. Bottled dry with balanced acidity.

**Wine 4 (Marlborough, Nautilus):**

Vineyard: Marlborough estate fruit. Fermentation: stainless steel at cool temperatures, with reductive handling throughout (inert gas blanketing, minimal oxygen contact). The reductive approach preserves the variety's aromatic freshness and the ginger/baking-spice notes that are Pinot Gris's varietal markers. No oak, no MLF. Brief lees contact for palate texture. Early bottling to capture primary fruit character. The lifted aromatic intensity and the pale-gold/copper tint are consistent with careful reductive handling of Pinot Gris -- the slight copper colour is a varietal characteristic (Pinot Gris has grey-pink skins that can impart a faint copper tone even without skin contact). The wine is a fruit-forward, technically clean expression designed for immediate enjoyment.

The juice, already concentrated to very high sugar levels, was fermented slowly in 50% new French oak barrels for approximately 18-24 months. Fermentation naturally slows and stops as alcohol reaches ~14% and the remaining sugar (~130 g/l) cannot be converted. No fortification; the sweetness is entirely from concentrated natural grape sugar. Extended barrel maturation (likely 18-30 months) adds vanilla, toast, and integrates the botrytis character with the oak.

### Wine 11 — Avignonesi Vin Santo del Chianti Classico 2011, Tuscany, Italy (15%)

**a) Grape variety, country, region (6 marks)**

Sangiovese and Malvasia (traditional Vin Santo blend). Italy. Tuscany (Chianti Classico zone).

The deep amber-gold colour, oxidative complexity (walnut, caramel, dried fig, candied citrus peel), and viscous, raisinated sweetness are classic Vin Santo markers. The 15% alcohol without spirit heat confirms a non-fortified passito wine that achieved high alcohol through concentration of the dried grapes. The underlying bitter-almond and dried-herb character is consistent with Sangiovese/Malvasia in the Vin Santo tradition. The extraordinary complexity and development from 10+ years of aging distinguish this from lesser examples.

**b) Mechanism of sweetness and method of production (6 marks)**

**Passerillage (appassimento — dried grape method).**

After harvest, bunches of Sangiovese and Malvasia grapes are hung on racks or laid on straw mats (graticci) in a well-ventilated vinsantaia (drying loft) for 3-6 months over autumn and winter. During this period, the grapes desiccate slowly, losing up to 50-60% of their water content. This concentrates sugars, acids, and flavour compounds while allowing partial oxidation and the development of raisinated, nutty flavours. In late winter or early spring, the shrivelled grapes are pressed, yielding a tiny quantity of intensely concentrated must. This must is transferred into small sealed oak barrels called caratelli (typically 50-100 litres). A madre — a culture of indigenous yeasts from previous vintages — may be present in the barrel to initiate fermentation. Fermentation is extremely slow due to the high sugar concentration and cold cellar temperatures, and proceeds intermittently over months or even years. The caratelli are sealed and left undisturbed for a minimum of 8 years (and often 10+ years for premium examples like this 2011). During this extended aging, the wine undergoes oxidative development within the sealed barrel, producing the deep amber colour, nutty complexity, and caramelised flavours. No fortification. The 15% alcohol is naturally achieved through the concentrated must.

### Wine 12 — Ramos Pinto 20 Year Old Tawny Port NV, Douro Valley, Portugal (20%)

**a) Grape variety, country, region (6 marks)**

Touriga Nacional, Touriga Franca, Tinta Roriz, and other traditional Douro varieties (field blend). Portugal. Douro Valley.

The warm amber-tawny colour with orange rim, spirit-driven warmth (20% ABV), and complex dried-fruit/nut profile (fig, date, apricot, caramel, toasted almond, coffee) are unmistakable 20-year-old Tawny Port markers. The fortified sweetness (~80 g/l RS) balanced by spirit warmth and well-integrated tannin confirms Port. The rancio character (dried orange peel, curry-like spice) indicates extended oxidative aging. The 20-year age indication means the average age of the blend is 20 years.

**b) Mechanism of sweetness and method of production (6 marks)**

**Fortification (addition of grape spirit during fermentation).**

The base grapes are harvested at full maturity in the Douro Valley. Fermentation begins in lagares (traditional stone treading tanks) or autovinifiers, extracting colour and tannin from the skins during a short maceration of 2-3 days. When fermentation has consumed approximately half of the natural sugar — typically when the must reaches ~7-8% alcohol — neutral grape spirit (aguardente at 77% ABV) is added to arrest fermentation. This kills the yeast, leaving approximately 80-100 g/l of unfermented residual sugar and raising alcohol to ~19-20%. The spirit addition is the critical mechanism: it both preserves sweetness AND increases alcohol simultaneously.

For the Tawny style, the young fortified wine is then transferred to small oak casks (pipas, ~550-630 litres) and aged oxidatively in the cooler lodges of Vila Nova de Gaia. Over 20+ years of oxidative aging in small wood, the wine loses its youthful red-fruit character and develops the tawny colour, nutty complexity, rancio, and dried-fruit profile. Evaporation concentrates the wine further. The final wine is a multi-vintage blend assessed by an independent panel to verify it matches the character expected of a 20-year-old indicative age. The wine is ready to drink on release with no further aging required.
