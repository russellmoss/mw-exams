### Part d) Quality ranking with reference to legal classification (18 marks)

Ranking in ascending quality order, with reference to the Prädikat classification system:

**Third (lowest):** Wine 5 — Kabinett. By the legal architecture of the QmP system, Kabinett represents the lightest tier of Prädikat wines and the minimum required natural sugar content. The quality of the individual wine (Bernkasteler Doctor) is exceptional within the tier, but the Kabinett designation establishes a structurally lighter, less concentrated style than either Spätlese or Auslese. A Kabinett, however great the site, does not aspire to the richness or complexity that the higher Prädikat designations achieve by harvesting later-ripened fruit.

**Second:** Wine 6 — Spätlese. The Spätlese designation requires 76–95 Oechsle minimum (vs. 70 for Kabinett) — the later harvest delivers greater concentration, more extract, more complex aromatics, and higher natural RS. Reinhold Haart's Goldtröpfchen Spätlese is one of the greatest wines at this tier anywhere in Germany, but by classification the Spätlese tier sits below Auslese in the formal hierarchy. The wine shows greater depth and length than Wine 5, confirming its superior position.

**First (highest):** Wine 7 — Auslese. The Auslese tier requires minimum 83–105 Oechsle; the selection of individually botrytis-affected and fully-ripened bunches at harvest produces the most concentrated, complex, and age-worthy category within the table wine segment of QmP (above Auslese sits the rare Beerenauslese and Trockenbeerenauslese, which are dessert wines in concentration). Christoffel's Würzgarten Auslese demonstrates the peak of what Mosel Riesling can achieve as an off-sweet wine: extraordinary aromatic complexity, impeccable balance, and the concentration to develop for 20–30 years.

The examiner should note that the IMW quality ranking here is a reflection of official German wine law's own hierarchy, which defines Auslese as superior to Spätlese as superior to Kabinett. The quality of each individual wine (all three are from excellent sites and estates) could in theory blur this ranking — a Bernkasteler Doctor Kabinett from a great year (e.g., 2021, 2017) can surpass a mediocre Auslese from a lesser site. However, within this flight — same region, same vintage year, three different designation tiers — the Prädikat ladder is the correct and legally supported ordering framework.

### Part d) Variety, production method, quality and commercial appeal (24 marks)

These three wines represent the Northern Rhône's white variety landscape: Viognier (Condrieu), Marsanne (dominant in both Crozes and Saint-Péray), and the Marsanne-Roussanne blend. The production approach differs across the three appellations in ways that profoundly shape both quality expression and commercial appeal.

**Condrieu (Wine 8) — Viognier:** Viognier is among the world's most aromatic white varieties and among the most site-specific — it performs as a premium variety only on the granite terraces of Condrieu and in a small handful of sites elsewhere globally. Its commercial proposition is based entirely on this uniqueness: no other wine smells exactly like a great Condrieu. The production challenge is the variety's difficulty in the vineyard (low yields, susceptibility to disease, narrow harvest window) and in the cellar (aromatic intensity fades quickly; timing of harvest is critical). Quality potential: exceptional when the site-variety-vintage combination aligns. The Guigal/Rozay expression is a reliable, commercially visible benchmark at £35–55 retail. Commercial market: specialist on-trade (sommeliers value it as a by-the-glass prestige white), UK and USA export markets, Japanese market (phenolic whites have cultural appeal). The primary challenge: consumer unfamiliarity with Condrieu outside specialist circles, and the short drinking window (3–5 years) limits the fine wine collector market.

**Crozes-Hermitage Blanc (Wine 9) — Marsanne-dominant:** Marsanne produces wine of unusual weight and texture; its low acidity and almond/beeswax character require careful winemaking management (oak, lees, MLF all play important roles in building complexity and preventing flat, flabby results). Crozes-Hermitage is the commercial-volume Northern Rhône white — the appellation's larger production enables consistent restaurant-list and retailer supply at accessible price points (£18–30). Quality: good to very good at best; the appellation lacks Hermitage's concentration and longevity but offers accessible, textural white wine at a compelling price-quality ratio. Commercial appeal: the Jaboulet brand (despite its post-2006 change of ownership) remains globally recognised and drives restaurant sales across UK, USA, continental Europe. The risk: Crozes-Hermitage Blanc is not a category that commands consumer loyalty or cellar-worthy excitement; it needs on-trade placement to find its audience.

**Saint-Péray (Wine 10) — Marsanne-dominant:** Saint-Péray's smaller appellation and steeper, more mineral sites produce still whites of greater structural interest than most Crozes-Hermitage. The variety-site interaction (Marsanne on granite) yields a wine capable of genuine ageing (10–15+ years for the best cuvées) — a proposition that Condrieu and most Crozes-Hermitage cannot match. Quality potential: high, but the category is underrecognised outside the Northern Rhône specialist community. Commercial challenge: Saint-Péray's name is most associated in popular wine culture with sparkling wine (historically the appellation produced mainly sparkling Saint-Péray mousseux); the still white category competes against this historical identity. The Voge "Fleur de Crussol" at £25–40 retail is a value proposition for quality Northern Rhône white — it rewards specialist merchants and restaurant sommeliers who champion unusual Rhône varieties. Growth opportunity: as consumers increasingly seek alternatives to Burgundy (cost and availability), structured Marsanne-based Northern Rhône whites offer a distinctive, intellectually engaging proposition.

Collectively, this flight demonstrates that the Northern Rhône white category encompasses three quite different commercial profiles: Condrieu (luxury, aromatic, brief window), Crozes-Hermitage Blanc (commercial volume, accessible pricing), and Saint-Péray still (specialist, structured, ageworthy). The production method — particularly the treatment of oak, MLF, and lees — shapes how each variety expresses its terroir and determines the commercial tier. The greatest wines of the zone (Château-Grillet, Hermitage Blanc from Chave or Jaboulet La Chapelle) show what the varieties can achieve at the ceiling; this flight sits at the conscientiously-made, quality-focused tier just below.

### Part c) Natural versus human inputs in shaping style and quality (16 marks)

Hunter Valley Semillon represents one of the wine world's most compelling case studies in the co-dependence of natural and human inputs; neither factor alone explains the wine's existence as a style.

**Natural inputs — the determining framework:**

The Hunter Valley's climate is warm and humid by Australian standards, yet Semillon ripens early here — paradoxically, producers harvest at low Brix (approximately 10–11% potential alcohol) in late January and February, well before the late-summer rains and disease pressure that characterise the Hunter's difficult vintage conditions. This early harvest timing — which is a response to climate, not a purely chosen winemaking philosophy — preserves the extraordinary natural acidity that defines the style. The Hunter Valley's free-draining loam and clay soils (around the Broke Fordwich and Pokolbin sub-regions) retain enough moisture for the vine through the growing season but encourage deep root systems that add mineral complexity. The Lovedale vineyard (Wine 12) is planted on particularly well-drained alluvial soils that add a distinctive textural refinement. Critically, the chemistry of Hunter Valley Semillon under bottle-reduction — the Maillard reactions and phenolic development that produce the toasty/honeyed character over time — are entirely natural processes; no winemaking intervention creates these characters.

**Human inputs — the enabling layer:**

The style is also entirely a human invention. The decision to harvest at 10–11% natural alcohol is a choice — a producer who waited for phenological ripeness would produce a 13–14% wine without the distinctive tension. The decision to make the wine completely bone dry (no RS) is a choice that makes the high acidity bearable at young age and essential to the long-term structure. Both Tyrrell's Vat 1 and McWilliam's Lovedale are made with minimal winemaking intervention — no new oak, no MLF, no lees ageing, no barrel; fermentation in temperature-controlled stainless steel followed by early bottling. This radical non-intervention is itself a highly deliberate human choice that creates the conditions for the wine's natural development in bottle. Without the human decision to make wine this way — harvesting early, fermenting cleanly, adding nothing — the natural potential of the Hunter site would be masked by conventional winemaking.

**Relative importance:**

In the context of young Hunter Semillon (Wine 11), the human inputs (harvest timing, clean fermentation, early bottling, no oak) dominate what you taste — the wine's current character is largely a winemaking construct, because the natural development has not yet occurred. In the context of mature Hunter Semillon (Wine 12), the natural inputs (the Maillard reactions, the acid preservation, the site-driven mineral complexity) dominate what you experience — years of bottle development have overwritten the winemaking fingerprint. The genius of the style is that human and natural factors operate in sequence rather than simultaneously: human choices in the cellar set the conditions for natural transformations in the bottle to deliver the final, extraordinary wine. This makes Hunter Valley Semillon arguably the world's most perfect illustration of winemaker restraint as the prerequisite for nature's full expression.

### c) Discuss the relative commercial importance of these wine styles in the global export market. (16 marks)

The four wines represent very different levels of commercial visibility in global export markets, ranging from an established category leader to niche emerging varieties.

**Malbec / Mendoza (Wine 12)** is the dominant commercial force in the group. Argentine Malbec is the most successful varietal-variety export narrative in the Southern Hemisphere over the past 20 years, with an estimated 75–80 million litres exported annually. The key export markets — USA, UK, Canada, Brazil, Germany — are all well-developed, with Malbec holding the second-largest imported red wine category share in the US market after Pinot Noir. Clos de los Siete, while a premium rather than volume producer, benefits from this wave of category recognition. The variety is understandable to consumers, widely distributed through off-trade multiples and specialist merchants alike, and carries a consistent brand story (altitude, terroir, elegance). The commercial challenge at premium price points is differentiation in an increasingly crowded upper tier.

**Touriga Nacional / Douro (Wine 9)** is commercially significant but not yet at category scale. Export markets of highest relevance: UK, USA, Brazil (large Portuguese diaspora), Switzerland.

**Blaufränkisch / Mittelburgenland (Wine 10)** operates in a narrow commercial niche. Austria's total wine export volume is modest (~50–60 million litres, heavily Grüner Veltliner and Riesling), and red wine accounts for a small fraction of this. Blaufränkisch has achieved specialist recognition in the US (particularly the natural-wine and somm-led on-trade community), UK, Germany, and Scandinavia, but consumer awareness of the variety name remains low. The commercial story is compelling for those who know it — serious, age-worthy, mineral, food-compatible — but it requires on-trade advocacy and specialist distribution. Export pricing is strong for the quality (€20–60 for serious single-vineyard wines), but volume is structurally limited. **Tannat / Uruguay (Wine 11)** is the smallest commercial presence of the four globally, despite Uruguay being South America's fourth-largest wine producer. Tannat's structural challenge — extreme tannin — limits consumer accessibility and requires education to sell. However, the variety has an emerging narrative: the longest-lived of South America's signature reds, unique to Uruguay in the New World, with the authenticity story that resonates with premium-wine buyers seeking the unfamiliar. Pisano exports to the USA, UK, Canada, and Brazil (Uruguay's most important export markets). Retail at €15–25 positions this beneath Malbec's premium tier but above commodity South American red. The commercial opportunity is in the niche: somm-led menus, wine-curious consumers seeking South American depth beyond Argentina, and pairings with rich meat dishes (Uruguay's asado culture is a coherent export story). The challenge is that Tannat requires active selling — it will never be a self-selecting supermarket variety.

## Question 1 — Wines 1–3: Sparkling cross-origin

### Wine 1 — Bollinger Special Cuvée NV, Champagne, France (12%)

#### a) Origin and grape variety (8 marks)

**Champagne, France. Pinot Noir-dominant blend (with Pinot Meunier and Chardonnay).**

The combination of fine, persistent mousse, pronounced autolytic development (brioche, toast, hazelnut), complex red apple and stone fruit character over a base of citrus zest, high but finely integrated acidity, and full body at ~12% ABV places this firmly in Champagne. The wine's weight and structured mid-palate, with a hint of red fruit and vinous depth, indicate Pinot Noir dominance. The evident complexity and evident reserve wine character — dried fruit, some oxidative nuttiness alongside the autolytic notes — confirm a prestige NV blend rather than a grower or basic entry-level cuvée. English sparkling is eliminated by the broader body, lower acid tension, and obvious reserve-wine vinosity; Franciacorta by the heavier body and more assertive autolysis. This is unambiguously Champagne.

#### b) Method of production, with specific reference to dosage and lees ageing (10 marks)

Traditional method (méthode champenoise). Grapes — primarily Pinot Noir, with Meunier and Chardonnay from across the Bollinger house's estate and grower contracts — are pressed, statically settled, and fermented to base wine in a combination of older oak casks and stainless steel (a Bollinger house signature distinguishing it from the majority of Champagne houses). Malolactic fermentation is typically partial or blocked to preserve freshness. Multiple harvest years are blended with a significant proportion of reserve wines (Bollinger typically holds 5–10 years of reserve wines, giving Special Cuvée approximately 40% reserve content) to achieve stylistic consistency. The blended base wine undergoes secondary fermentation in bottle via addition of tirage liqueur (sugar and selected yeast), with bottles held on lees for a minimum of three years — well above the NV minimum of 15 months — which accounts for the prominent autolytic character. Riddling and disgorgement are followed by dosage addition. For Special Cuvée the dosage is approximately 8–9 g/L (extra brut to brut threshold), contributing moderate sweetness balanced by the wine's structure.

#### c) Quality within the context of wine globally (7 marks)

Outstanding quality within the category. Bollinger Special Cuvée occupies the prestige NV tier — the house's flagship non-vintage product — and represents one of the benchmark examples of full-bodied, Pinot-dominant Champagne. Benchmarked globally, it competes with Krug Grande Cuvée and Louis Roederer NV at the top of the ~£50–60 retail tier; its combination of autolytic complexity, reserve wine depth, and structural precision earns a quality level clearly above average Champagne NV and comfortably within the upper échelon of the category. It is not a prestige cuvée (that would be Bollinger RD or Vieilles Vignes), but it exceeds the quality of any entry NV cuvée.

### Wine 2 — Graham Beck Blanc de Blancs 2019, Robertson, Western Cape, South Africa (12.5%)

#### a) Origin and grape variety (8 marks)

**Robertson, Western Cape, South Africa. Chardonnay (Blanc de Blancs).**

This wine shows the hallmarks of traditional method Chardonnay: fine persistent mousse, tightly integrated acidity, green apple, citrus, and white stone fruit with a subtle autolytic note of fresh brioche. The relatively higher alcohol at 12.5% compared to Champagne NV, the slightly fuller body, and the riper citrus character (lemon curd, nectarine rather than chalk-and-green apple) indicate a warmer-climate Blanc de Blancs. This is South African cap classique, Blanc de Blancs style.

#### b) Method of production, with specific reference to dosage and lees ageing (10 marks)

Traditional method (méthode cap classique). Chardonnay is harvested at lower sugar levels than still wine to preserve acidity, whole-bunch pressed, cold-settled, and fermented in stainless steel with selected yeasts. Base wines may undergo partial MLF depending on vintage requirements; in cooler years MLF may be blocked to preserve green-apple freshness. Under MCC regulations, vintage wines must spend a minimum of 12 months on lees; Graham Beck's Blanc de Blancs is typically aged on lees for 36+ months, accounting for the toasty autolytic character evident at this stage. Post-disgorgement, a dosage of approximately 8–10 g/L (brut) is added, contributing softness without masking fruit character. The wine is sealed with a crown cap during lees ageing and corked at disgorgement.

#### c) Quality within the context of wine globally (7 marks)

Very good to excellent quality, genuinely competitive at the upper end of the global sparkling category. Graham Beck Blanc de Blancs 2019 is a benchmark cap classique: the autolytic development, structural precision, and variety expression place it clearly above entry-level sparkling wine and at a quality level comparable to mid-tier Champagne Blanc de Blancs (~£25–35 equivalent). Globally, this represents a serious fine wine argument for South African sparkling at a price point (~ZAR 350–450, ~£15–22 export) that undercuts equivalent-quality Champagne significantly. It is not Grand Marque level, but it is outstanding within its own category.

### Wine 3 — Bisol Crede Prosecco Superiore Brut 2024, Conegliano Valdobbiadene, Veneto, Italy (11%)

#### a) Origin and grape variety (8 marks)

**Conegliano Valdobbiadene Prosecco Superiore DOCG, Veneto, Italy. Glera (principal grape variety).**

The soft, frothy mousse, relatively short persistence, pear, white peach, acacia, and green apple aromatics, light body at 11% ABV, and clean, refreshing rather than autolytic palate are diagnostic of tank-method (Charmat/Martinotti) sparkling wine from Glera. It is the best argument for Prosecco as a serious category, rather than a peer of the great sparkling wines of the world. Retail at approximately €12–18, it offers outstanding value in its category.

### Wine 4 — Demeter Zoltán Tokaji Aszú 3 Puttonyos 2019 (11%)

#### b) Specific quality designation and mechanism of sweetness (12 marks)

**Tokaji Aszú 3 Puttonyos.** Under the current Hungarian Tokaj regulations (revised in the 2010s), Aszú wines must have a minimum residual sugar of 120 g/L and minimum total sugar (RS + titrable acidity expressed as total sugar equivalent) to qualify; the historic Puttonyos system (referring to the number of 20–25 kg hods of Aszú berries added per 136-litre Gönci barrel) has been retained as a label quality signal though now measured by analytical parameters rather than literal hod counts. At 3 Puttonyos, the wine sits at the entry level of Aszú: minimum ~60 g/L RS (historically; currently the 3 Puttonyos tier was abolished and reinstated by some producers as a category signal — Demeter Zoltán's use indicates a lighter, more restrained style). This wine's RS is approximately 80–100 g/L, reflected in its sweetness level, which is high but counterbalanced by Furmint's characteristic high acidity to give a less cloying impression than its sugar content implies.

**Mechanism of sweetness:** Sweetness in Tokaji Aszú is achieved through noble rot (Botrytis cinerea). Furmint and Hárslevelű berries are selectively harvested when individually shrivelled and colonised by Botrytis — these are the Aszú berries. The fungus penetrates the berry skin, dehydrating the grape and concentrating sugars, acids, and flavour compounds while producing glycerol (which adds texture) and botrytis-specific aromatics (honey, saffron, ginger). Aszú berries are macerated or directly pressed with base wine or must (aszúzás — the process of soaking dried berries in base wine for 12–60 hours), extracting concentrated sugary juice. Fermentation of the resulting high-sugar must is arrested naturally by the increasing alcohol level and/or by yeast exhaustion, leaving residual sugar. The wine is aged in traditional Gönci barrels in Tokaj's famous underground cellars.

### d) Commercial opportunities and challenges in key export markets (15 marks)

**Opportunities:**

Tokaji Aszú's UNESCO and first-classified status (the Tokaj Classification predates Bordeaux's by over 130 years) provides a powerful narrative for sommelier-led on-trade positioning in the UK, USA, Germany, and Japan — Tokaj's key export markets. The relatively modest production and the prestige-tier pricing of 5 and 6 Puttonyos mean that for on-trade fine wine lists, these wines compete with Sauternes Premier Grand Cru Classé and German TBA at similar or lower price points while offering extreme rarity and strong origin stories. The premium NV Hungarian Tokaj category (3 Puttonyos especially) has strong by-the-glass opportunity in the fine dining sector as a dessert wine course. The US market is particularly receptive to the "world's first classified wine region" narrative. Japan has historically shown strong interest in dessert wines at the premium tier. Growing interest in Hungarian wines (driven partly by the Vega-Sicilia/Oremus profile and by younger natural winemakers) is creating new interest in the sub-£30 tier.

**Challenges:**

Sweet wine consumption in export markets has declined significantly since the 1990s; the on-trade dessert wine slot is contracting, with many restaurant lists cutting dessert wine by-the-glass programmes. Tokaji Aszú faces a consumer education challenge: the Puttonyos system (and the 2013 regulatory changes that altered the system) is poorly understood by consumers and many trade buyers, making value communication difficult. Lower tiers (3 Puttonyos) compete against a broad field of affordable sweet wines (Sauternes, Monbazillac, German QbA Auslese) from better-known regions. At the premium tier (5–6 Puttonyos), the competition from first-growth Sauternes and GG Trockenbeerenauslese is severe, and both have higher brand recognition in export markets. Exchange-rate volatility affecting the Forint makes pricing unpredictable for importers. Vintage release lags (minimum 2–3 years, often 5+) mean market windows can close before the wine arrives — the 2013 and 2016 vintages are only now entering prime drinking.

### Wine 7 — Fritz Haag Riesling Kabinett Brauneberger Juffer 2024, Mosel, Germany (8%)

#### a) Approximate residual sugar (g/l) (2 marks)

Approximately **20–35 g/L.** Kabinett Riesling from the Mosel must have a minimum must weight of 70 Oechsle (minimum 6.3% potential alcohol), and fermentation is typically arrested at ~8% ABV to retain residual sugar. At 8% ABV the sugar/acid balance is delicate — the wine reads as off-dry to medium-sweet, with sugar masked by Mosel's characteristically high tartaric acidity.

#### b) Approximate alcohol level (%) (2 marks)

**8%.** This is exactly the stated ABV, and consistent with Mosel Kabinett style. Low alcohol is a fundamental characteristic: the cool Mosel climate, the steep slate slopes, and the deliberate arrest of fermentation combine to produce wines at 7.5–9% ABV. Any reading above 9.5% should prompt reconsideration of the Kabinett designation.

#### c) Origin and grape variety (7 marks)

**Mosel, Germany. Riesling.** The wine's signature lime, green apple, wet slate, and delicate petrol precursor aromatics, combined with piercing acidity (the Mosel's signature), off-dry sweetness, and the lightest possible body at 8% ABV, are diagnostic of Mosel Riesling Kabinett. The Brauneberger Juffer is a classified Erste Lage (Grosse Lage equivalent) steep-slate vineyard in the Middle Mosel, producing wines of exceptional mineral precision. Fritz Haag is a benchmark producer for this vineyard. Alsace Riesling is eliminated by the very low ABV and sweetness; Rheingau Riesling by the typically broader body; Austrian Riesling by the fuller body and different mineral signature.

#### d) Mechanism of sweetness; quality and maturity (8 marks)

**Mechanism:** In Mosel Kabinett production, fermentation of the relatively low-must-weight juice is arrested before completion to retain residual sugar. Practically, this is achieved by chilling the fermenting wine to near-freezing temperatures — typically 0–2°C — which inactivates yeast activity. The cold-stabilisation effectively stops fermentation and the wine is then racked off its lees, filtered, and bottled with the desired RS. Sulphur dioxide additions at bottling further inhibit any resumption of fermentation. The result is a wine where residual sugar derives entirely from unfermented grape sugar (natural grape sweetness), not from any addition or concentration technique. This is the most delicate of all sweet wine production methods, preserving maximum primary fruit character. The wine is certified as Prädikatswein at the Kabinett level under the German Weingesetz — the lowest of the six quality Prädikat tiers (Kabinett → Spätlese → Auslese → Beerenauslese → Eiswein → Trockenbeerenauslese).

**Quality:** Outstanding quality within its category. Fritz Haag's Brauneberger Juffer Kabinett is a Erste Lage-level wine producing one of the Mosel's most compelling expressions of the Kabinett style — racily acidic, crystalline, precisely delineated. In the context of sweet wines globally it sits at the lighter-bodied, lower-sugar end, but the quality of fruit expression, mineral precision, and potential for evolution over 10–20 years is exceptional.

**Maturity:** This is a 2024 vintage Kabinett, currently 1–2 years old. It is drinking beautifully now due to its natural freshness, but Mosel Kabinett from premier vineyards and top producers will improve over the next 5–10 years as petrol and mineral notes develop. Peak drinking: 2028–2035. Well-cellared examples will hold for 20–25 years.

### Wine 8 — Château Coutet 2016, Barsac/Sauternes, France (14%)

#### a) Approximate residual sugar (g/l) (2 marks)

Approximately **120–140 g/L.** Sauternes requires a minimum 45 g/L RS, but First Growth Barsac operates at 100–160 g/L for classic vintages. 2016 is a benchmark Sauternes year and Coutet's concentration reflects this; the RS is firmly in the 120–145 g/L range, balanced by the wine's vibrant acidity.

#### b) Approximate alcohol level (%) (2 marks)

**14%.** Higher alcohol is a structural requirement of Sauternes: the extensive concentration of sugar means fermentation can run further before arrest, producing 13–15% ABV wines. At 14% this sits in the central range for a classic Sauternes vintage. The warmth on the palate confirms this — it is a significantly fuller-bodied sweet wine than either the Tokaji or the Kabinett.

#### c) Origin and grape variety (7 marks)

**Barsac (Sauternes), Bordeaux, France. Sémillon (principal), Sauvignon Blanc, Muscadelle (blend).** The combination of beeswax, pineapple, apricot, ginger, and honey aromatics over a rich, unctuous palate with full body, relatively high alcohol (14%), and the signature lanolin-waxy character of botrytis-affected Sémillon is diagnostic of Premier Grand Cru Classé Sauternes/Barsac. Château Coutet is a Premier Grand Cru Classé, located in Barsac — the designated sub-appellation within the Sauternes AC. Barsac wines can label as either Barsac or Sauternes. The typical Coutet character — lighter, more citrus-inflected and elegant than Château d'Yquem, slightly more lime-fresh than the richest Bommes or Preignac properties — is recognisable. Compared with Tokaji Aszú (lower ABV, orange peel/saffron, tea tannin) or German BA/TBA (lower ABV, more concentrated acid), Sauternes stands apart by its higher alcohol and beeswax-dominant botrytis profile.

#### d) Mechanism of sweetness; quality and maturity (8 marks)

**Mechanism:** Sweetness in Sauternes results from noble rot (Botrytis cinerea) selectively affecting ripe Sémillon, Sauvignon Blanc, and Muscadelle grapes. Morning mists from the Ciron river create humid conditions encouraging Botrytis growth; afternoon sun desiccates the colonised berries without causing grey rot. Multiple successive harvests (tries) — sometimes 5–8 passes through the vineyard — collect berries at their peak of botrytis concentration. The dehydrated berries have concentrated sugars at 30–40% Brix; pressing extracts a must with potential alcohol of 18–20%+ or more. Fermentation in barrel (often new oak for Premier Crus) is arrested naturally when alcohol reaches approximately 13–14.5% and the yeast become inhibited by alcohol concentration in the high-sugar medium, or by chilling/SO₂ addition. Residual sugar remains at 120–160 g/L.

**Quality:** Outstanding. Château Coutet is a Premier Grand Cru Classé (1855 Bordeaux classification of Sauternes and Barsac — the only classification applying to this region). It ranks immediately behind d'Yquem (Premier Cru Supérieur) among the Premiers Crus. The 2016 vintage is widely regarded as one of the finest recent Sauternes vintages, producing wines of great concentration, freshness, and balance. This is a great wine — perhaps 97–98 points in context — and among the finest expressions of botrytis-affected Sémillon in the world.

**Maturity:** 2016 is approximately 9–10 years old. Premier Cru Sauternes of this quality is currently entering its secondary development phase but is far from its peak. The beeswax and botrytis characters are integrating; the fruit is transitioning from fresh pineapple toward honey and dried apricot. It will improve significantly over the next 10–20 years and will hold for 30–50 years. Drink 2028–2060+.

### Wine 9 — Isole e Olena Vin Santo del Chianti Classico 2012, Tuscany, Italy (16.5%)

#### a) Approximate residual sugar (g/l) (2 marks)

Approximately **80–120 g/L.** Vin Santo exhibits a wide range depending on the producer and the year of harvest; Isole e Olena produces a medium-rich style with significant RS but not at the full extreme. The sweetness is pronounced but integrated with the wine's oxidative character and very high alcohol.

#### b) Approximate alcohol level (%) (2 marks)

**16.5%.** Very high alcohol — the combination of partial drying (concentrating sugars) and extended fermentation produces Vin Santo typically at 14–18% ABV. This is a critical diagnostic: if a wine shows amber colour, dried fig and walnut character, and 16.5% ABV without obvious spirit warmth suggesting fortification, Vin Santo (or similar passito) is strongly suggested. This ABV is high enough that a candidate unfamiliar with Vin Santo might mistakenly suspect fortification.

#### c) Origin and grape variety (7 marks)

**Chianti Classico, Tuscany, Italy. Trebbiano Toscano and Malvasia Bianca (traditional blend), possible inclusion of Sangiovese-related whites.** Vin Santo del Chianti Classico is a DOC category produced within the Chianti Classico zone of Tuscany. Isole e Olena is one of the Chianti Classico zone's most respected estates. The wine's amber colour deepening to brown, dried fig, walnut, caramel, orange marmalade, and honey aromatics, combined with the very high ABV, oxidative complexity, and concentrated sweetness, are diagnostic of Vin Santo. This is a curveball wine for candidates unfamiliar with the style. However, the structural evidence is clear: high ABV without spirit warmth (distinguishing from Madeira or Port), significant RS (distinguishing from dry oxidative wines like Oloroso), amber passito colour (distinguishing from Sauternes), and the dried-fruit/nut character (distinguishing from icewine). If the exact identity is unknown, a candidate who describes "a high-alcohol Italian passito-style sweet wine, non-fortified, oxidatively aged, consistent with Vin Santo di Toscana" earns substantial marks.

#### d) Mechanism of sweetness; quality and maturity (8 marks)

**Mechanism:** Vin Santo production involves appassimento — the drying/raisining of harvested grapes on cane mats (arelle) or hanging in well-ventilated rooms (vinsantaie) for 2–6 months from harvest (typically October–November) through winter. During this period grapes lose 30–50% of their weight through water evaporation, concentrating sugars, acids, and flavour compounds. The dried grapes are pressed — typically in late winter or spring after the traditional "new wine" period — producing a small volume of extremely concentrated must. This high-sugar must ferments slowly, typically in small sealed barrels (caratelli) of 50–225 litres, often using the previous year's Vin Santo as starter (madre — the "mother"), at slow ambient temperatures in the vinsantaia (the attic-level room that heats in summer and cools in winter). Fermentation may take 2–6 years to complete, often arresting at 14–18% ABV with significant RS remaining. The combination of high alcohol, small barrel size, and slow oxidation over the extended ageing period (minimum 3 years under DOC regulations; Isole e Olena typically ages 6+ years) creates the characteristic amber-brown oxidative complexity. The wine is NOT fortified.

**Quality:** Outstanding quality for the category. Isole e Olena's Vin Santo is consistently cited as one of Italy's finest examples — concentrated, complex, and perfectly balanced between sweetness, acidity, and oxidative development. Within the tiny world of quality Vin Santo production it competes with Avignonesi and San Giusto a Rentennano at the very top. The 2012 vintage (a warm, concentrated Tuscany year) has produced exceptional material.

**Maturity:** At 14 years old (2012), this is at or approaching peak complexity. The combination of appassimento concentration, extended barrel ageing, and bottle evolution has fully developed the walnut, fig, and caramel tertiary characters. It will hold well for another 5–15 years but is drinking very well now. Drink 2025–2035.

### Wine 10 — Cave Spring Gewürztraminer Icewine 2022, Niagara Peninsula, Ontario, Canada (9%)

#### a) Approximate residual sugar (g/l) (2 marks)

Approximately **180–250 g/L.** Canadian Icewine regulations require a minimum of -8°C (currently -8°C harvest temperature), producing must at a minimum 35 Brix. This yields RS typically in the 180–280 g/L range. The very pronounced sweetness, despite low ABV, indicates high RS — significantly above Sauternes and Tokaji Aszú and closer to the German BA/TBA range.

#### b) Approximate alcohol level (%) (2 marks)

**9%.** Low alcohol is diagnostic of icewine: the very high sugar concentration makes full fermentation extremely difficult, and most icewines ferment only partially. The 9% stated ABV is consistent with typical Canadian icewine at the low-to-mid range (most range 7–11%). The sweetness-to-alcohol contrast — intense sweetness at very low alcohol — is the structural fingerprint.

#### c) Origin and grape variety (7 marks)

**Niagara Peninsula, Ontario, Canada. Gewürztraminer.** The intense rose petal, lychee, ginger, and Turkish delight aromatics over a background of tropical fruit and acacia honey, combined with extreme sweetness at very low alcohol and high acidity, are diagnostic of Gewürztraminer Icewine. The variety is far more common in Alsace in a dry table wine context, but its intensely aromatic profile translates dramatically well to icewine — where the concentration amplifies its floral/spice characteristics. Cave Spring is one of Niagara's most respected producers, specialising in cool-climate varieties. Canadian Icewine regulations (VQA Ontario) require grapes to be harvested and pressed frozen at -8°C or below, with no artificial freezing permitted — distinguishing it from cryoextraction ("ice wine" produced by artificial freezing, which is permitted in the EU but not under VQA Canada). The Niagara Peninsula's continental climate with cold winters makes it ideally suited to icewine production. Vidal is the more common Canadian icewine grape (producing tropical and peachy character); Gewürztraminer produces this identifiable floral-spice fingerprint.

#### d) Mechanism of sweetness; quality and maturity (8 marks)

**Mechanism:** Canadian Icewine sweetness derives from cryoconcentration of grape juice in situ on the vine. Grapes are left on the vine well past normal harvest into deep winter (typically December–January in Ontario) until temperatures drop to -8°C or below. At these temperatures the water in the grape berries freezes while the sugar-rich juice remains liquid. Harvesting frozen berries and pressing them immediately — still frozen — means the ice crystals (frozen water) are retained in the press, and only the concentrated, sugar-rich juice flows free. The resulting must is extraordinarily high in sugar, acidity, and flavour compounds. This must ferments at cold temperatures over weeks to months; fermentation arrests at approximately 7–10% ABV when yeast are inhibited by alcohol concentration and cold. Residual sugar of 180–280 g/L remains. The wine is not filtered or processed aggressively — its natural acidity acts as a preservative.

**Quality:** Very good to outstanding quality. Cave Spring is a VQA Ontario benchmark producer; this Gewürztraminer Icewine is at the upper quality tier of Canadian icewine, a category where Canada has no peer globally (no other country produces VQA-equivalent icewine at this scale or consistency). Within the world of sweet wines it represents a unique flavour profile — the variety's aromatics amplified by cryoconcentration — but lower complexity than botrytis-affected wines due to the single-mechanism concentration. Price approximately CAD 60–90 per 375 ml, internationally exported at ~£35–55.

**Maturity:** Icewines age very well due to their extreme sugar/acid balance. This 2022 is young — 3–4 years old — and is drinking beautifully now with vibrant primary aromatics. It will develop honey and marmalade notes over 5–10 more years and will hold for 15–20 years. Drink now to 2040.

### Wine 11 — Stanton & Killeen Rutherglen Muscat Classic NV, Rutherglen, Victoria, Australia (17.5%)

#### c) Origin and grape variety (7 marks)

**Rutherglen, Victoria, Australia. Muscat à Petits Grains Rouge (Brown Muscat / Muscat Blanc à Petits Grains — the dark-skinned clone used for Rutherglen Muscat).** This is an extreme curveball. The deep brown-amber colour, extraordinary sweetness, raisin, fig, caramel, coffee, toffee, and orange marmalade character, very high RS and ABV, and the viscous, almost syrup-like texture are unique to Rutherglen Muscat and the closely related Rutherglen Muscadelle (Topaque). #### d) Mechanism of sweetness; quality and maturity (8 marks)

**Mechanism:** Rutherglen Muscat production begins with Brown Muscat (Muscat à Petits Grains, dark-skinned) grapes harvested at extreme ripeness — typically 30+ Brix — in the hot, continental climate of Rutherglen. The "Classic" and higher tiers are produced via a solera-like blending system (though not a formal Spanish solera — it is a fractional blending system where older material is drawn off and replenished with younger wine) that imparts the concentrated complexity of very old wines. The combination of fortification, heat-accelerated ageing, and fractional blending is unique to Rutherglen.

**Quality:** Outstanding quality within its unique category. Stanton & Killeen is a benchmark producer. The Classic tier represents very good to outstanding quality — significantly more concentrated and complex than the entry Rutherglen tier, with clearly discernible age and depth. Grand and Rare tiers above it offer even more concentration and extreme age, but Classic is a reference-quality wine within its classification. In the global context of fortified sweet wines, Rutherglen Muscat competes for complexity at the premium tier with aged Tawny Port, PX Sherry, and Tokaji Eszencia — though its flavour profile is entirely its own.

**Maturity:** NV blended product with indefinite shelf life; the solera system ensures ongoing stylistic consistency. Currently at drinking maturity — this style does not improve significantly in bottle once purchased, as the cellar ageing has already occurred. Drink now to 5 years from purchase.

### Wine 12 — Braida Brachetto d'Acqui 2024, Piedmont, Italy (5.5%)

#### a) Approximate residual sugar (g/l) (2 marks)

Approximately **70–100 g/L.** This is a sweet sparkling wine at very low ABV — the sweetness is pronounced but not as intense as Aszú or icewine. The Brachetto d'Acqui DOCG style (Spumante Dolce) typically ferments only to 5–6% ABV with high RS. The approximate RS of 70–100 g/L is consistent with this style.

#### b) Approximate alcohol level (%) (2 marks)

**5.5%.** Extremely low — one of the lowest ABVs of the flight. This is the definitive structural marker: a sweet wine at 5.5% ABV must have either stopped fermentation very early or never fully fermented. There is no evidence of fortification at this ABV level. The delicate mousse and light body confirm a low-alcohol sweet sparkling wine.

#### c) Origin and grape variety (7 marks)

**Acqui Terme, Piedmont, Italy. Brachetto (Brachetto d'Acqui DOCG).** This is the flight's most significant curveball. The wine's delicate, soft pink/light red colour, red fruit (strawberry, raspberry), rose petal, and floral aromatics, combined with a gentle frothy mousse, very low ABV (5.5%), pronounced sweetness, and moderate acidity, are distinctive. The profile resembles a sweet Lambrusco in structure but the aromatic character — intensely floral, rose-driven rather than dark fruit — and the pink/light red colour point toward Brachetto. Braida is one of the most prominent estates in Piedmont for this variety. Brachetto d'Acqui DOCG includes both a Spumante (sparkling) and a Passito style; this is clearly the Spumante. Red Moscato d'Asti could be confused, but Asti is typically white/golden; Lambrusco Rosé would be darker and less aromatic. If the variety is unknown, a candidate describing "a sweet, low-alcohol, lightly sparkling red/rosé wine, floral and strawberry-scented, likely Italian, probably produced by arrested fermentation" earns most available marks.

#### d) Mechanism of sweetness; quality and maturity (8 marks)

**Mechanism:** Brachetto d'Acqui Spumante is produced by the Asti method (cuve close / autoclave / tank method), which is the same process used for Moscato d'Asti. Freshly extracted and cold-stabilised Brachetto must is stored under pressure and at cold temperature to inhibit fermentation until ready for processing. When needed, the must is warmed and inoculated to begin fermentation in a sealed, pressurised autoclave (the same Charmat-style tank used for Prosecco, but the process is managed to produce a sweeter, lower-alcohol product). Fermentation is stopped — by chilling the tank — when the wine reaches approximately 5.5% ABV and the desired residual sugar level. The resulting wine under pressure is then filtered and bottled isobarically. This method preserves the delicate primary aromatics (rose, strawberry) that would be lost in secondary in-bottle fermentation. Crucially, this wine is NOT fortified — the low ABV is entirely due to arrested natural fermentation, not spirit addition.

**Quality:** Good to very good quality within its category. Brachetto d'Acqui is an obscure but genuinely charming wine style producing one of Italy's most distinctive sweet sparkling wines. Braida is the reference producer for the DOCG. The wine's quality ceiling is inherently moderate — the style is designed for accessibility and immediate pleasure rather than complexity or ageing — but within those parameters, Braida's example is excellent. In the context of the flight as a whole, this is a wine that rewards honest description over confident misidentification.

**Maturity:** This is a 2024 vintage, 1–2 years old. Brachetto d'Acqui is designed for immediate consumption — within 1–2 years of vintage. The aromatic freshness (rose, strawberry) fades quickly; there is no benefit to further cellaring. Drink now.
