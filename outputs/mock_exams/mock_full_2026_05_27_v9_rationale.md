### Paper 1 design rationale

**Q1 — Pinot Gris/Grigio cross-origin (F1: same-variety cross-origin)**
The variety has never served as the F1 subject in any prior mock suite. The four wines span a stylistic spectrum from opulent Alsace Grand Cru (W1: high residual sugar, foudre ageing, concentrated, phenolic) through the light crisp northern Italian commercial model (W2: stainless, 0 g/l RS, early-harvest aromatic) to the mixed-method Oregon premium model (W3: partial oak, dry, 13%) and finally the fresh reductive Marlborough interpretation (W4: stainless, clean aromatic, dry). Winemaking marks are generated by the RS + oak/foudre question on W1, the stainless/zero-intervention question on W2, the partial-MLF/barrel question on W3, and the reductive handling on W4. The Alsace wine is the benchmark anchor; the Alto Adige is the commercial foil; Oregon and NZ are developing-market references.

Identification difficulty gradient: W1 (Alsace Grand Cru PG) = STRONG SIGNAL; W2 (Alto Adige PG) = STRONG SIGNAL; W3 (Oregon PG) = PLAUSIBLE; W4 (Marlborough PG) = PLAUSIBLE (could confuse with SB at first nose).

Method hook ledger — W1: RS + old foudre + late pressing. W2: stainless + early harvest. W3: partial oak + mixed method. W4: reductive stainless. All four have distinct hooks. PASS.

**Q2 — Wachau Riesling quality ladder (F7: quality calibration)**
Three legally distinct Wachau quality tiers from the same producer (Alzinger) ensure the ranking question has one objectively defensible answer. Steinfeder (max 11.5% ABV) → Federspiel (11.5–12.5%) → Smaragd (12.5%+) are formal legal designations enforced by the Vinea Wachau Nobilis Districtus association. The ABV difference alone provides sensory evidence for ranking. Candidates earn marks by identifying the Wachau DAC classification, by naming the three tier labels, and by discussing how ripeness, extraction, and site selection cascade into the quality pyramid. Commercial sub-question rewards candidates who can articulate the specialist-retailer and fine-dining positioning of Smaragd vs the on-trade/supermarket positioning of Steinfeder and Federspiel.

**Q3 — Spain white varieties (F2: same-origin comparative, novel question type)**
Spain is under-represented as the subject of P1 whites questions in the historical corpus (primarily used as an origin signal via Rioja Blanco oxidative style curveball). This question tests breadth of knowledge beyond Albariño: Verdejo (increasingly important Rueda variety) and Godello (the curveball — a variety with genuine complexity potential in Valdeorras but unknown to many candidates). The "natural vs human factors" sub-question (Q3b) echoes the 2025 novel question type while giving candidates a structured way to earn marks even without identifying W10 Godello. Albariño and Verdejo are the anchors; Godello is the curveball.

Method hook for Q3b: Albariño (granite-driven mineral expression; unoaked; tank; cool Atlantic climate dominates terroir character) vs Verdejo (high-altitude continental limestone; some oxidative handling; Rueda's human choice of early-harvest acid preservation) vs Godello (slate and schist soils of Galicia interior; Rafael Palacios' choice of barrel ferment and long lees contact to build texture). Candidates can argue convincingly for terroir dominance in all three, or for human choices being decisive in Godello's transformation from traditional bulk to premium varietal.

**Q4 — South Africa Chenin Blanc maturity axis (F6: maturity axis)**
Two wines, same variety and country, different vintages and sub-regions (Swartland vs Tulbagh). The 2024 Badenhorst Secateurs represents young-primary Chenin: Swartland bush vine, tank-fermented, fresh yellow apple and citrus, under 2 years old. The 2021 Rijk's Signature Old Vine represents developing Chenin: Tulbagh old vines (40–60 years), barrel-fermented, showing honeycomb and developing stone fruit. The contrast tests whether candidates can read maturity signals while also identifying an unexpected sub-region (Tulbagh = medium curveball). The 4-mark vintage sub-question rewards candidates who deduce vintage from development state. Both wines are under $60 retail; price ceiling compliant.

### Paper 2 design rationale

**Q1 — Cabernet Sauvignon cross-origin (F1: same-variety cross-origin)**
Cabernet Sauvignon as the F1 subject has NOT appeared in any previous mock in this suite, making it fresh ground. The four wines represent the four most important Cabernet Sauvignon archetypes globally: classic Médoc Left Bank (W1: Cantenac Brown Margaux 2018, predominantly Cabernet, structured tannin, cedary oak); California mountain Cabernet (W2: Ridge Monte Bello 2021, Santa Cruz Mountains, high-elevation, restrained style); Australian terra rossa Cabernet (W3: Wynns Black Label Coonawarra 2021, red fruit, eucalyptus, medium body); and Chilean Maipo premium Cabernet (W4: Almaviva 2021, warm Puente Alto, Rothschild-Concha y Toro collaboration, full body). The quality spread spans Cru Classé/iconic-benchmark to regional benchmark to commercial-premium, generating strong commercial sub-question marks.

Method hook ledger: W1 — new French oak barrique (~50% new, 18 months), MLF, traditional Médoc maceration; W2 — American and French oak mix, long maceration, indigenous yeast, minimal intervention; W3 — French oak hogsheads, traditional maceration, relatively accessible style; W4 — new French oak (~70% new), extended maceration, selected yeast. All distinct. PASS.

**Q2 — Burgundy Pinot Noir quality ladder (F7: quality calibration)**
Three legally distinct Burgundy appellation tiers: Bourgogne Pinot Noir (Regional AC) → Gevrey-Chambertin Village AC → Gevrey-Chambertin 1er Cru Les Cazetiers. The ranking is legally unambiguous (Regional < Village < 1er Cru within the Burgundy classification). Using two separate producers (Chanson for Bourgogne, Sérafin for Village, Bruno Clair for 1er Cru) tests whether candidates can identify quality gradients across producers, which is the harder and more realistic exam question. Prices: ~€20-25 (Bourgogne) / ~€55-65 (Village) / ~€85-95 (1er Cru) — defensible spread within ceiling.

The maturity sub-question (c) rewards candidates who identify that the Bourgogne 2022 is drinking in its window now, the Village 2021 has 5-8 more years, and the 1er Cru 2020 has 10-15+ years ahead.

**Q3 — Spain reds same-country (F2: same-origin comparative)**
Five wines from across Spain's key red wine regions, testing regional depth beyond the Rioja/Ribera duopoly. The flight progresses from commercial Rioja Crianza (W8: value/mainstream, American oak, accessible) through classic Rioja Reserva (W9: French oak, 5-year age, benchmark Imperial) to the benchmark prestige of Ribera del Duero (W10: Valbuena 5° — Vega Sicilia's second wine, aged 5 years, Spanish icon) through the mineral schist-driven Grenache-blend Priorat (W11: Álvaro Palacios Les Terrasses, new-wave premium, Garnacha/Cariñena/Cabernet blend) and finally the curveball Mencía from Bierzo (W12: Pétalos, low-intervention, slate-driven, Pinot-like tannin structure). The curveball Mencía is anchored by four well-known Spanish red categories. It tests depth — candidates who know only Rioja and Ribera will struggle with W11 and W12.

Mark redistribution for W12: identification marks are reduced; discussion of style (cool-climate character, floral aromatics, low tannin relative to Spanish reds) can earn marks even without naming Mencía or Bierzo.

### Paper 3 design rationale

**Q1 — Sparkling method diversity (F5: sparkling method)**
The flight moves away from the overused Champagne/Cava/Franciacorta/English trio used repeatedly in prior mocks. The four wines represent genuinely distinct sparkling categories: W1 (Jacquesson 742 NV: non-vintage Champagne, traditional method, 3-4 years lees ageing, multi-vintage blend with small-production artisan character, predominantly Chardonnay); W2 (Albert Boxler Crémant d'Alsace BdB: Pinot Blanc-dominant traditional method, no Champagne AOC designation, earlier-disgorge profile, lighter autolytic character); W3 (Bellavista Alma Franciacorta Brut NV: Italian traditional method DOCG, 18+ months lees minimum, Chardonnay/Pinot Nero, warmer-climate base fruit than Champagne); W4 (Seppelt Original Sparkling Shiraz NV: RED sparkling, traditional method from Grampians Victoria, deep ruby, tannin, 13%, sweet-finish style, 2-3% RS typical — the HIGH curveball).

The curveball (W4) earns marks from candidates who can describe what is in the glass: red sparkling wine, full body, Shiraz character (black fruit, pepper, firm tannin), Australia origin from climate and variety, traditional method from bead quality and autolytic character. Even without naming "Grampians" or "Seppelt," most marks are accessible. Identification sub-marks for W4 are weighted toward method (traditional) and broad country (Australia) rather than specific sub-region.

Countries: France (2), Italy (1), Australia (1) = 3 countries. NB: France 2 is permissible as Champagne and Alsace are distinct regions with distinct legal categories.

**Q2 — Sherry styles (F2: same-origin comparative)**
Four wines from the Jerez/Manzanilla Sanlucar geographic triangle, all within the Sherry/Manzanilla DO, each representing a different aging style on the biological-oxidative spectrum:
- W5 Fino Inocente (Valdespino): fully biological (flor), ultra-dry, 15% ABV, pale lemon, sharp almonds, acetaldehyde, saline
- W6 Manzanilla La Cigarrera: fully biological (flor under oceanic conditions in Sanlúcar), lighter color than Fino, more camomile and maritime character, same ABV profile
- W7 Amontillado del Duque (González Byass): starts biologically then transitions to oxidative after flor dies; amber-orange color, hazelnut and dried fruits, 21.5%, complexity from both phases
- W8 Palo Cortado Cardenal (Hidalgo-La Gitana): THE MEDIUM CURVEBALL — begins as Fino (biological), loses flor spontaneously, develops oxidatively but retains finesse and elegance; tertiary amber, complex aromatic bouquet, 20%, structurally between Amontillado and Oloroso. Many candidates will not know the Palo Cortado category — marks available for describing the sensory character accurately and inferring the aging method from what is in the glass.

The question stem says "different style within a single legally defined category" — the category being "Jerez-Xérès-Sherry" (the DO). Candidates who know only Fino and Oloroso can still earn significant marks from biological-vs-oxidative analysis.

**Q3 — Sweet wine mechanism and quality calibration (F7: quality calibration with F5 mechanism)**
The gold-standard 5-mechanism-minimum structure applied to 4 wines (all 4 mechanisms distinct, all 4 varieties distinct, 4 countries distinct). Mechanism inventory: natural RS Prädikat (W9 Leitz Spätlese, 9% ABV, ~50 g/l RS, Riesling, Germany); botrytis noble rot (W10 Rieussec 2019, ~130 g/l RS, Sémillon-dominant blend, Sauternes 1er Cru Classé); passerillage/dried grape (W11 Avignonesi Vin Santo 2011, ~15 years on the vine + 8+ years in caratelli, Sangiovese/Malvasia blend, ~100-140 g/l RS); fortification-oxidative-Tawny (W12 Ramos Pinto 20yr Tawny, blended multi-vintage, oxidative maturation in small oak barrels, ~80 g/l RS typical, Douro Touriga blend).

Quality ranking (c): W10 Sauternes 1er Cru > W11 Avignonesi Vin Santo > W9 Leitz Spätlese > W12 Ramos Pinto Tawny. This is defensible by classification (Sauternes Premier Grand Cru Classé outranks regional premium Portuguese NV Tawny and a commercial Rheingau Spätlese) while acknowledging that Avignonesi Vin Santo is a benchmark for that style. Candidates who argue W12 should rank higher than W9 based on complexity and price can earn marks with good reasoning. The question is designed so the ranking discussion is the main mark-generator, not the "right answer."

Price spread: W9 ~€18-25 / W11 ~€45-65 / W12 ~€45-55 / W10 ~€70-90. Ratio ~4:1 (well within 20:1 limit). All within the ~$100 sweet wine ceiling. PASS.

Sweetness spectrum: W9 off-dry/light sweet (~50 g/l) / W12 medium-sweet (~80 g/l) / W11 sweet (~120 g/l) / W10 lusciously sweet (~130 g/l). 4 distinct sweetness levels for 4 wines. RULE S5 PASS.

Mechanism count: 4 wines, 4 distinct mechanisms. Rule S1 (4 wines = min 3 mechanisms) = PASS with 4.
Variety count: Riesling / Sémillon blend / Sangiovese-Malvasia / Touriga blend = 4 entirely distinct varieties. Rule G1 PASS.
Country count: Germany / France / Italy / Portugal = 4 distinct countries. PASS.
