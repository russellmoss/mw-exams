## b) Compare and contrast the winemaking of the two wines.
Wine 3
Manual harvest into 300kg crates. Pneumatic whole-bunch pressing, static gravity settling. Spontaneous fermentation with indigenous yeast. Malolactic in 350L barrels.  12–24 months on fine lees in 350L barrels with weekly topping (ouillé). Light plate filtration. Unfined. Total SO2: ~33 mg/L.
These are the decisive production choices because they explain the still dry, bone dry, medium balanced, neutral old style.
Wine 4
Hand-harvested. Fermented in stainless steel cuves with indigenous yeasts.  Racked into old foudres, demi-muids, and small barrels (virtually no new oak). Never racked during aging. **Not topped off** (non-ouillé) — wines develop partial voile (flor). Aged 24–30 months. No fining or filtration mentioned.
These are the decisive production choices because they explain the oxidative, full rich, neutral old style.
## c) Comment on the quality and commercial potential of each wine.
Wine 3
good to very good quality in context: balance/freshness, length, oak handling, ageing capacity. It is young but developing; it has the structure to improve further and hold in the long term.
Wine 4
outstanding quality in context: balance/freshness, length, complexity, typicity. It is developing with some early tertiary complexity; it has the structure to improve further and hold in the long term.
